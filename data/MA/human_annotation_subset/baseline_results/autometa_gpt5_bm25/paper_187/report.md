## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
From 138 records screened, three primary studies met inclusion criteria (publication years: 2015, 2017, 2025). Designs comprised two quasi-experiments (a within-cohort before–after comparison in 2015 and a within-course format comparison in 2017) and one cross-sectional study (2025). Across studies, sample sizes, participant demographics, and setting details were not reported (N=NR/NR in all three), and comparators were broadly defined as “other pedagogies” (e.g., lectures, problem-based learning, enquiry-based learning, case-based learning) but not consistently specified per study in the extracted data. Only one study provided an extractable effect estimate (odds ratio [OR] 0.78), with no confidence intervals or p-values available; two studies did not provide computable effect sizes. Consequently, no meta-analysis was conducted, and findings are synthesized narratively.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The central question—whether team-based learning (TBL) improves undergraduate dental students’ satisfaction and academic performance compared with other pedagogies—remains unresolved based on the limited and incomplete evidence identified. The only extractable quantitative estimate was OR 0.78 from the 2025 cross-sectional study. Without additional detail on outcome coding, this estimate is ambiguous: if the outcome was academic success or satisfaction, an OR <1 would suggest lower odds of success/satisfaction with TBL relative to the comparator; if the outcome was failure or dissatisfaction, an OR <1 would instead suggest a benefit of TBL. The absence of confidence intervals and p-values precludes inferences about precision or statistical significance. The two quasi-experimental studies (2015; 2017) provided no computable effect measures, preventing cross-study synthesis.

Heterogeneity is likely substantial. First, interventions labeled as “TBL” can vary in structure (e.g., frequency and duration of TBL sessions, readiness assurance testing formats, composition and stability of teams, grading weight assigned to TBL components, and facilitation quality). Second, comparators ranged broadly—from traditional lectures to PBL/CBL/EbL—pedagogies that differ in student autonomy, assessment strategies, and cognitive demands. Third, outcomes likely spanned both subjective (satisfaction) and objective (exam scores, pass rates) measures, which are not directly comparable and are variably susceptible to bias. Finally, differences across curriculum year (preclinical vs clinical courses) and discipline may influence both engagement and performance, yet such contextual moderators were not reported.

Risk of bias concerns are inherent to the included designs and reporting. The quasi-experiments lacked randomization, raising selection bias and confounding risks (e.g., cohort effects, concurrent curriculum changes). Within-course format comparisons are susceptible to order effects and differential instructor enthusiasm. Cross-sectional designs cannot establish temporality and are highly vulnerable to confounding by prior ability, motivation, or instructor differences. Across all studies, key data (sample sizes, baseline equivalence, outcome definitions, psychometric properties of satisfaction instruments, blinding of assessors for performance outcomes) were not available in the extracted records, limiting interpretability and increasing risk of biased estimates. The net effect is very low confidence in any direction of effect.

We could not juxtapose our synthesis with primary authors’ interpretations because author conclusions were not available from the extracted records. This is important: in education research, authors’ narrative interpretations can be optimistic despite small, imprecise, or non-significant effects, and discrepancies between narrative conclusions and quantitative findings are common. In the absence of detailed study-level findings, CIs, or p-values, and with only one ambiguous OR, we cannot determine whether the primary studies collectively favored TBL, favored comparators, or were neutral.

Given the sparse and inadequately reported evidence, we did not perform sensitivity analyses (e.g., fixed- vs random-effects models), subgroup analyses by comparator type, or publication bias assessments. With fewer than ten studies and only one extractable effect, funnel plot or Egger’s tests would be inappropriate in any case. Overall, the body of evidence is insufficient to make confident claims about the effect of TBL on student satisfaction or academic performance in undergraduate dental education.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on three small, poorly reported studies (2015, 2017, 2025)—two quasi-experiments and one cross-sectional study—with only one extractable but directionally ambiguous effect estimate (OR 0.78; no CI/p-value), there is insufficient evidence to determine whether team-based learning improves, harms, or has no effect on undergraduate dental students’ satisfaction or academic performance compared with other pedagogies. The certainty of evidence is very low due to design limitations, missing data, and heterogeneity.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on incomplete extracted study information: sample sizes, detailed comparators, outcome operationalization, and quantitative results (CIs, p-values) were unavailable, precluding pooling and precision assessment. We could not perform formal risk of bias assessments or explore heterogeneity quantitatively. The small number of included studies (n=3) and lack of extractable data constrained subgroup analyses (e.g., by comparator type, curriculum phase, or discipline).

Study-level limitations likely include selection bias (non-randomized designs), confounding (unmeasured differences in cohorts, instructors, or assessments), measurement bias (non-validated satisfaction scales, unblinded grading), and reporting bias (selective reporting of favorable outcomes). The single extractable OR lacked context on outcome coding, precision, and adjustment for confounders, limiting its interpretability. Variation in TBL implementation and comparators further reduces external validity.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current evidence does not robustly support or refute adopting TBL over other pedagogies in undergraduate dental education to improve satisfaction or academic performance. Programs considering TBL should do so based on local pedagogical goals, resource availability, and faculty expertise, and should implement internal evaluation plans (e.g., pre-specified outcomes, blinded assessment where feasible, and routine collection of both objective performance and validated satisfaction measures). Hybrid approaches that combine TBL with targeted didactics or case-based learning may be defensible, but decisions should be guided by local data rather than expectations of superior effectiveness.

For research: There is a clear need for adequately powered, pre-registered comparative studies—ideally cluster-randomized or well-controlled quasi-experiments—directly comparing TBL with specific alternatives (e.g., lectures, PBL, CBL) in undergraduate dental cohorts. Studies should report: sample sizes; baseline equivalence; detailed TBL implementation; assessor blinding; standardized, validated satisfaction instruments; and objective performance outcomes (e.g., MCQ exam scores, OSCE results, pass/fail, item-level analyses). Effect estimates (ORs, mean differences) with CIs and adjusted analyses for key confounders should be provided, along with raw or sufficient summary data for meta-analysis. Multisite trials and head-to-head comparisons across preclinical and clinical years would help address heterogeneity and improve generalizability.
