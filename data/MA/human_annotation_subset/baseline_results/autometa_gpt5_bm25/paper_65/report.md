## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Three cross-sectional observational studies published between 2021 and 2022 met inclusion criteria, all investigating associations between neck circumference and metabolic risk among women with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) and/or in comparisons against non-PCOS controls or women with smaller neck circumference. Reporting was sparse. One 2021 cross-sectional study enrolled 200 participants (the split between PCOS and controls was not reported), but provided no computable effect estimate. Two additional studies (one in 2021 and one in 2022) reported effect measures as risk ratios (RRs): 0.3528 (study details not reported) and 2.2966 (95% CI: 2.4100 to 40.9900), respectively. Overall sample sizes for these two studies were not reported. Because outcomes attached to these RRs (e.g., metabolic syndrome prevalence, insulin resistance, or other binary endpoints) were not specified in the extracted data, and due to internal inconsistencies in the confidence interval for the 2022 estimate, quantitative synthesis was not undertaken. The evidence base is therefore small (k=3), with at least 200 participants represented, and characterized by incomplete reporting.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The main finding from the limited and heterogeneously reported evidence is uncertainty about the direction and magnitude of associations between neck circumference and adverse metabolic outcomes in PCOS. The two extractable RRs point in opposite directions: one suggests a lower risk associated with the exposure (RR=0.3528), while the other suggests a higher risk (RR=2.2966). Without explicit specification of the outcome, reference group (e.g., smaller vs larger neck circumference or PCOS vs non-PCOS), and adjustment sets, it is not possible to reconcile these findings. Moreover, the reported 95% CI for the RR of 2.2966 is internally inconsistent (lower bound 2.4100 exceeds the point estimate and the upper bound is 40.9900), which undermines interpretability and suggests extraction or reporting error.

Several sources of heterogeneity likely contributed to divergent estimates. First, neck circumference measurement protocols and thresholds vary (e.g., measurement at the cricothyroid membrane vs just below the laryngeal prominence; cut-offs tailored by sex, BMI, or ethnicity). Second, PCOS diagnostic criteria (Rotterdam, NIH/NICHD, or AE-PCOS Society) differ in phenotype composition and metabolic risk, which can alter associations with adiposity measures. Third, the underlying comparisons were not standardized: some studies may have contrasted women with PCOS to healthy controls, while others may have contrasted larger vs smaller neck circumference strata within PCOS. Fourth, outcome definitions (e.g., ATP III vs IDF for metabolic syndrome; laboratory thresholds for HOMA-IR or fasting insulin) and statistical adjustments (particularly for BMI, waist circumference, and other adiposity indices) can substantially shift effect estimates. Given the strong correlation of neck circumference with BMI and central adiposity, inadequate adjustment risks confounding and could explain effect reversals across studies.

Because the included studies were cross-sectional, they cannot establish temporal or causal relations. For example, higher neck circumference could be a correlate of overall adiposity rather than a unique risk marker; alternatively, metabolic abnormalities could influence fat deposition patterns, leading to reverse causation. The inability to separate these possibilities limits the clinical interpretability of neck circumference in PCOS. In addition, the lack of reported sample sizes for two of three studies prevents precision appraisal; the extremely wide and internally inconsistent CI in the 2022 study suggests either very small sample size, rare outcomes, data extraction issues, or unstable models.

We planned meta-analysis using risk ratios but did not pool results. Although at least two studies reported RRs, the non-computable estimate in one study, the unspecified outcomes in the others, and the internally inconsistent CI preclude meaningful synthesis. Any pooled estimate under these conditions would be misleading. Instead, we provide a narrative synthesis noting directional inconsistency and substantial methodological uncertainty.

Author conclusions could not be reliably synthesized because the extracted records did not include author-stated interpretations or conclusions for the included studies (each was labeled “Unknown”). Consequently, we cannot evaluate concordance between study authors’ interpretations and our appraisal. Given the limited and internally inconsistent data, our interpretation is more conservative: current evidence is insufficient to support or refute neck circumference as an independent marker of metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, or related cardiometabolic outcomes in women with PCOS.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on three small, cross-sectional studies with incomplete reporting and inconsistent effect estimates (including RR=0.3528 from a 2021 study and RR=2.2966 with an internally inconsistent 95% CI of 2.4100–40.9900 from a 2022 study), there is insufficient, low-certainty evidence to determine whether neck circumference meaningfully distinguishes metabolic risk (metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, or hypertension) among women with PCOS or versus non-PCOS controls. No robust quantitative synthesis was possible.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on sparse extraction with missing key details (study-level sample sizes, outcome definitions, statistical adjustment sets, and author conclusions). The very small number of included studies (k=3) and minimal overlap in reported metrics precluded meta-analysis and formal heterogeneity exploration. We also could not perform a structured risk-of-bias assessment due to lack of itemized methodological information (e.g., selection processes, measurement protocols, confounding control).

Study-level limitations, inferred from available data, include cross-sectional design (precluding causal inference), unclear or inconsistent outcome definitions, possible confounding by general and central adiposity, and potential measurement variability in neck circumference. The internally inconsistent confidence interval in the 2022 study indicates reporting or extraction error and raises concerns about data quality or model stability. Lack of reported sample sizes for two studies prevents assessment of precision and small-study effects.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Given the uncertainty and methodological limitations, neck circumference should not replace established cardiometabolic risk assessments in PCOS (e.g., BMI, waist circumference, blood pressure, fasting lipids, fasting glucose/OGTT, HOMA-IR when indicated). Clinicians may record neck circumference as an adjunct anthropometric measure, but current evidence does not support using it as an independent screening or diagnostic tool for metabolic syndrome or insulin resistance in women with PCOS.

For research: Well-designed prospective cohorts and diagnostic/predictive studies in PCOS are needed. Priorities include: standardized neck circumference measurement and prespecified cut-offs; clear definition of outcomes (metabolic syndrome criteria, insulin resistance indices, lipid and blood pressure thresholds); adequate sample sizes; multivariable models adjusting for BMI, waist circumference, age, ethnicity, and lifestyle factors; and transparent reporting of effect sizes with confidence intervals and p-values. Studies should compare the incremental predictive value of neck circumference over conventional measures (e.g., change in C-statistic/AUC, calibration, net reclassification). Both within-PCOS stratified analyses (large vs small neck circumference) and PCOS vs control comparisons should be conducted, ideally with external validation across diverse populations.
