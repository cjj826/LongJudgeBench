## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Four cross-sectional case–control neuroimaging studies met inclusion criteria, all enrolling medication-free patients with major depressive disorder (MDD) and healthy controls (HC), published between 2014 and 2025. Sample sizes varied: one study enrolled 27 MDD and 27 HC (2015), another 123 MDD and 81 HC (2022), a third did not report exact sample sizes (2014), and a fourth included 86 MDD and 81 HC (2025). Across studies with reported counts, there were at least 236 medication-free MDD participants and 189 HC. All studies used MRI-derived cortical thickness as the outcome; however, none reported extractable statistics (e.g., group means/SDs per region or comparable vertex-wise effect estimates) sufficient to compute standardized mean differences (SMDs), so quantitative pooling was not possible. 

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Main finding. The primary result of this review is that, despite identifying four relevant studies in medication-free MDD versus HC, the reporting of cortical thickness findings was insufficiently granular to derive effect sizes for meta-analysis. Specifically, no study provided a complete set of group-wise means and standard deviations for predefined cortical regions or equivalent statistics enabling SMD calculation, and none provided a harmonized set of effect estimates across a common parcellation that would allow pooling. As a result, we cannot determine the magnitude or direction of cortical thickness differences between medication-free MDD and HC from the available reports, nor can we evaluate overall statistical significance (e.g., pooled SMDs, confidence intervals, or p-values). The choice of SMD as the intended effect measure underscores that even ROI-level means/SDs or test statistics would have sufficed; their absence indicates an important reporting gap.

Heterogeneity and likely sources. Although quantitative heterogeneity cannot be estimated, several methodological differences likely contributed to variability across studies. First, sample sizes ranged from small (27 MDD/27 HC in 2015) to moderate (123 MDD/81 HC in 2022; 86 MDD/81 HC in 2025), which affects power and precision. Second, cortical thickness can be derived using different pipelines and versions (e.g., FreeSurfer), parcellations (e.g., Desikan–Killiany vs. Destrieux or vertex-wise analyses), and smoothing kernels; these choices influence effect estimates and spatial specificity and complicate cross-study comparison. Third, “medication-free” may include drug-naïve individuals or those with variable washout periods and prior exposure; without standardized definitions and detailed histories, residual medication effects or illness chronicity could confound comparisons. Fourth, covariate adjustment (e.g., age, sex, total intracranial volume or global mean thickness, site/scanner) and multiple-comparison procedures differ across teams and can yield differing inferences even with similar raw effects. Finally, clinical heterogeneity (first-episode vs. recurrent, illness duration, severity, comorbidity) likely varied across studies, further limiting direct comparability.

Risk of bias implications. Formal risk-of-bias assessment was not performed due to lack of extracted data. Nevertheless, common risks for cross-sectional neuroimaging studies of cortical thickness warrant consideration: selection bias (e.g., recruitment settings, exclusion criteria), potential residual confounding (e.g., age-by-group interactions, education, smoking, BMI), and analytic flexibility (e.g., cluster-defining thresholds, correction methods). Small studies (e.g., 27/27) are especially susceptible to imprecision and effect-size inflation; even the larger samples (123/81; 86/81) may be underpowered for subtle regional effects, especially after stringent multiple-comparison control. Without uniform reporting of quality control (QC) procedures (e.g., surface reconstruction QC), scanner characteristics, or protocol harmonization, measurement bias cannot be ruled out. These issues collectively temper confidence in any unreported positive or negative findings and further justify the need for extractable, standardized metrics.

Robustness and comparison with author conclusions. Because no extractable effects were available, we could not test robustness (e.g., fixed- vs. random-effects models) or perform subgroup/sensitivity analyses (e.g., by medication history, first-episode vs. recurrent). The extracted records did not preserve the original author conclusions in a form suitable for synthesis; therefore, we cannot compare study-level interpretations to pooled results. The inability to reconcile study-level claims with quantitative estimates is itself informative: it suggests that current reporting standards in this niche (medication-free MDD cortical thickness) often emphasize thresholded statistical maps or qualitative summaries over reproducible effect metrics. 

Context from screening. Of 118 screened records, 114 were excluded—most commonly due to unclear medication status at the time of MRI, absence of healthy control comparisons, and use of non-thickness modalities (e.g., resting-state fMRI) or different anatomical measures (e.g., gray matter volume or morphological similarity networks). This underscores both the importance and the rarity of medication-free cortical thickness studies with appropriate controls and the need for precise reporting of medication exposure. The fact that only four studies met criteria—and none allowed effect-size extraction—highlights a substantive evidence gap rather than a clear pattern of cortical thickness differences in medication-free MDD.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on four cross-sectional studies (at least 236 medication-free MDD and 189 healthy controls), current evidence on MRI-derived cortical thickness differences in medication-free MDD versus healthy controls is insufficiently reported to support meta-analysis or to establish the presence, magnitude, or direction of case–control differences. No extractable effect sizes were available; therefore, no confident, quantitative conclusion about cortical thinning or thickening in medication-free MDD can be drawn from the included literature.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations:
- The analysis relied on a local corpus and on what could be extracted from available records; it is possible that additional eligible studies exist or that full texts contain extractable data not captured here. We did not perform author contact for missing data, which could have improved effect-size availability.
- Heterogeneity in reporting (e.g., vertex-wise maps without ROI means/SDs) restricted quantitative synthesis. The use of “unknown” study identifiers and incomplete metadata (e.g., NR/NR sample sizes in one study) further limited contextualization and risk-of-bias assessment.

Study-level limitations:
- All included studies were cross-sectional, limiting causal inference and the ability to distinguish state (episode-related) from trait effects. Small to moderate sample sizes (e.g., 27/27; 86/81; 123/81) limit power for subtle, regionally specific cortical effects after multiple-comparison correction.
- Methodological variability likely spanned MRI field strengths, processing pipelines and versions, parcellations, smoothing, QC procedures, and covariate handling. Definitions of “medication-free” (drug-naïve vs. washed out; duration of washout; prior lifetime exposure) and clinical heterogeneity (first-episode vs. recurrent, illness duration, severity, comorbidities) were not consistently detailed, increasing the risk of confounding and limiting comparability.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice:
- There is no robust, quantitative evidence from medication-free samples to support using cortical thickness measures for diagnosis, prognosis, stratification, or treatment selection in MDD. Clinicians should not alter practice based on cortical thickness differences in unmedicated MDD at this time.
- Any individual study claims should be interpreted cautiously in the absence of standardized, reproducible effect metrics and independent replication with consistent reporting.

For research:
- Reporting standards: Future studies should provide extractable statistics enabling meta-analysis, including ROI definitions, group means and SDs (or SEs) for each ROI and global mean thickness, sample sizes per ROI, and effect estimates with 95% CIs. For vertex-wise analyses, share unthresholded effect maps and cluster-level statistics (e.g., via NeuroVault) and indicate multiple-comparison procedures.
- Harmonization and design: Pre-register primary ROIs/hypotheses; adopt standardized pipelines (e.g., ENIGMA protocols), document FreeSurfer versions and QC; report scanner parameters; and adjust for key covariates (age, sex, global thickness/ICV, site/scanner). Carefully define “medication-free” (drug-naïve vs. washout) and report prior exposure and washout duration.
- Sampling and confounding: Use adequately powered, multi-site designs with harmonization (e.g., ComBat) and transparent QC. Enrich phenotyping (illness duration, episode count, severity, comorbidity, lifestyle factors) to enable stratified analyses (e.g., first-episode vs. recurrent) and reduce residual confounding.
- Longitudinal and mechanistic work: Incorporate longitudinal designs to distinguish state versus trait effects and to isolate potential impacts of initiating or discontinuing antidepressants from intrinsic illness-related cortical changes.

Until such data are consistently reported and shared, quantitative synthesis will remain limited, and uncertainty about cortical thickness differences in medication-free MDD will persist.
