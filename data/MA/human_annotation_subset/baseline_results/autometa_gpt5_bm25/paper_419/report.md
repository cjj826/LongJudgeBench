A further subset did not enroll adults with both overweight/obesity and comorbid depressive symptoms—either focusing on generalized populations with depression regardless of BMI, or on weight-management cohorts without specified depressive symptomatology. ## 8. Conclusions and Implications

8. Conclusions and Implications

8.1 Main Conclusions
There is insufficient evidence to determine whether internet- or mobile-based interventions primarily targeting mental health improve depressive symptoms or reduce weight in adults with overweight/obesity and comorbid depressive symptoms, relative to standard care or no intervention. No eligible RCTs were identified; therefore, the strength of evidence is rated as absent/insufficient for both outcomes.

8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations: Our conclusions are constrained by the absence of eligible trials. Because all 82 records were excluded at title/abstract screening, we did not conduct full-text assessments; misclassification at screening is possible, though dual-review or verification was not described here. The local corpus may not capture all relevant primary RCTs (e.g., very recent, non-indexed, or unpublished studies). Additionally, the requirement that the intervention be primarily mental health–focused and delivered via web/app may have excluded multi-component digital programs where mental health is a secondary or integrated component. Finally, the planned use of odds ratios may be suboptimal for inherently continuous outcomes and could limit comparability if future trials report continuous measures; however, this did not affect results given the absence of data.

Study-level limitations: None can be directly appraised because no trials were included. Nonetheless, typical challenges in this space should be anticipated for future studies: potential selection bias in recruiting a comorbid sample; difficulties ensuring intervention adherence and engagement over time; reliance on self-reported depressive symptoms or weight; contamination with concurrent treatments (e.g., antidepressants, diet/exercise programs); and attrition bias. These anticipated issues underscore the importance of rigorous trial design and transparent reporting.

8.3 Implications
For practice: No practice recommendations can be made specific to IMIs primarily targeting mental health for adults with overweight/obesity and comorbid depressive symptoms. Clinicians should continue to follow established guidelines for the management of depression and obesity separately and consider digital mental health tools as adjuncts based on general evidence of efficacy in broader populations, recognizing that their effects on weight—and their effectiveness in patients explicitly selected for both elevated BMI and depressive symptoms—are unproven. Patients should be counseled that mood-focused IMIs, on their own, may not produce clinically meaningful weight change, and weight-focused apps may not ameliorate depressive symptoms unless they include robust, evidence-based psychological components.

For research: There is a clear need for adequately powered, preregistered RCTs that enroll adults with overweight/obesity and comorbid depressive symptoms and test web/app-based mental health interventions against standard care or no-intervention comparators. Trials should:
- Define comorbidity at baseline using validated thresholds (e.g., PHQ-9/BDI-II cutoffs; BMI criteria) and specify concurrent treatments.
- Use standardized, validated depression measures and objective weight outcomes (kg, BMI, and percent weight loss), with clinically meaningful thresholds (e.g., ≥5% weight loss; depression remission/response).
- Include sufficient follow-up (e.g., 3, 6, and 12 months) to capture durability of both mood and weight effects.
- Evaluate intervention features likely to moderate effects (guided vs. unguided, CBT vs. other approaches, integration with diet/physical activity modules).
- Apply intention-to-treat analyses, predefined handling of missing data, and independent outcome assessment where feasible.
- Report both continuous and dichotomous outcomes to facilitate future meta-analyses using multiple effect metrics, and share data to enable pooled analyses.
- Consider equity and implementation outcomes (digital access, usability, adherence, and cost-effectiveness) relevant to real-world deployment in primary care, bariatric, and behavioral health settings.
