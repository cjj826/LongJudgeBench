## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
We included 13 randomized trials (parallel-group and crossover designs) published between 2016 and 2025 that evaluated time-restricted eating (TRE; 4–12 hour feeding windows) combined with structured exercise versus an exercise-matched control with unrestricted eating windows in adults. Although 13 trials met inclusion criteria, only two provided extractable comparative data suitable for meta-analysis of fat mass (FM). These two comparisons enrolled at least 69 participants in total (Unknown 2021: n=10/7; Unknown 2025, four-arm RCT: n=26/26). Several additional trials reported outcomes relevant to body composition (including body fat percentage [BF%] and fat-free mass [FFM]) but did not provide sufficient statistics to compute study-level effects for pooling. Consequently, the quantitative synthesis focuses on FM, with narrative consideration of other outcomes.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The primary quantitative finding is that TRE combined with structured exercise produced a significantly greater reduction in FM than unrestricted eating with matched exercise. The pooled mean difference (fixed-effects) across two trials was -2.53 kg (95% CI: -3.47 to -1.59; p<0.001; I²=0.0%). Individually, Unknown (2021) reported an FM reduction of -1.92 kg (95% CI: -3.52 to -0.32) with TRE versus control in a small RCT (n=10/7), and the four-arm RCT (Unknown 2025; n=26/26 for the relevant comparison) observed a larger reduction of -2.85 kg (95% CI: -4.01 to -1.69). These effects are clinically meaningful over a short-to-medium time horizon and were observed despite matched exercise training between groups, suggesting the eating-window intervention, as implemented, contributed to greater fat loss.

Heterogeneity in the pooled analysis was low (I²=0%), but this estimate is inherently imprecise with only two studies contributing to the model. Potential sources of true heterogeneity that could not be explored include variation in TRE window length (e.g., 16/8 vs shorter/longer windows), feeding-window timing (early vs late), exercise modality (resistance, aerobic, or concurrent), and energy intake control (isocaloric vs ad libitum within the eating window). Notably, two additional RCTs in the dataset reported directionally similar FM effects (-2.00 kg and -1.867 kg), but missing precision data prevented inclusion in the meta-analysis; nevertheless, their point estimates qualitatively support the pooled direction and magnitude. Several included crossover trials were designed for acute or short-term mechanistic outcomes and did not provide body composition data suitable for synthesis; their designs are not optimal for detecting changes in FM or FFM, which typically require longer follow-up.

Formal risk-of-bias assessment could not be performed because necessary domain-level information was not consistently available in the extracted records. Nonetheless, several trial-level features may influence internal validity and interpretation. Most included studies appear small, increasing the potential for imprecision and baseline imbalances. Dietary intake and adherence are often self-reported in TRE literature; if TRE leads to spontaneous caloric restriction, the observed FM reductions may reflect differences in energy intake rather than meal timing per se. Differences in body composition assessment (e.g., DXA vs bioimpedance) could also contribute to measurement variability. The four-arm RCT design (Unknown 2025) suggests more complex allocation schemes; without full reporting, risks related to allocation concealment, deviations from intended interventions, and missing data remain uncertain.

Author conclusions, where available, generally align with the pooled estimate: resistance-training TRE trials commonly reported decreased FM without adverse effects on FFM or strength. For example, the 2016 16/8 RCT in resistance-trained adults (title fragment: “Effects of eight weeks of time-restricted feeding (16/8) on basal metabolism, maximal strength, body composition, inflam…”) concluded that TRE reduced FM while maintaining strength and fat-free mass compared with a normal-diet control. This pattern is consistent with the negative pooled FM effect and with the two additional RCT point estimates that favored TRE. However, for BF% and FFM, quantitative synthesis was not possible, and absence of pooled data should not be interpreted as evidence of no effect. The overall direction of author interpretations suggests preservation of FFM when TRE is combined with resistance training, but stronger inferences will require better-reported trials.

Given the small number of analyzable studies, we could not conduct sensitivity analyses, explore subgroup effects, or assess publication bias. Fixed- and random-effects estimates are identical with I²=0%, but this concordance is not a strong test of robustness with only two studies. Overall, the evidence indicates that TRE plus structured exercise reduces FM by approximately 2–3 kg versus unrestricted eating windows with matched training in the short term, while effects on BF% and FFM remain less certain due to reporting gaps.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 13 randomized trials, with two contributing extractable comparative data, time-restricted eating combined with structured exercise produced a statistically and clinically meaningful short-term reduction in fat mass compared with unrestricted eating windows under matched exercise conditions (pooled MD: -2.53 kg; 95% CI: -3.47 to -1.59; p<0.001; I²=0%). Available author conclusions generally indicate that FFM and strength are preserved when TRE is combined with resistance training, but we could not quantitatively synthesize BF% or FFM due to incomplete reporting. The overall strength of evidence is limited by small samples, short durations, and sparse analyzable data.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include incomplete reporting in the primary studies, which restricted meta-analysis to two trials for FM and precluded synthesis for BF% and FFM. Key details necessary for risk-of-bias assessment (randomization, allocation concealment, blinding, missing data handling) were inconsistently available, and study identifiers/authors were not fully retrievable from the extracted records, preventing author contact for missing data. We could not evaluate publication bias or conduct prespecified subgroup and sensitivity analyses.

Study-level limitations likely include small sample sizes, short intervention periods (typical TRE-plus-training trials run 4–12 weeks), and heterogeneous TRE protocols (window length and timing) and exercise programs (resistance, aerobic, concurrent). Many trials appear to permit ad libitum intake within the feeding window; without isocaloric controls or objective intake measurement, it is difficult to disentangle effects of meal timing from energy restriction. Body composition assessment methods may vary (DXA vs BIA), introducing measurement heterogeneity, and training and dietary adherence are often self-reported.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: In adults undertaking structured exercise, adopting a TRE pattern with a 4–12 hour daily feeding window may facilitate additional fat mass loss of roughly 2–3 kg over several weeks compared with an unrestricted eating window, without clear evidence of harm to fat-free mass when resistance training is included. Given the limited and short-term nature of the evidence, TRE should be considered an optional strategy rather than a standard recommendation. If implemented, ensuring adequate protein intake, distributing protein across the feeding window, and prioritizing resistance training may help preserve FFM. Individual tolerance, training goals, and lifestyle constraints should guide use.

For research: Priority studies include adequately powered, preregistered RCTs with longer follow-up (≥6–12 months) that isolate meal-timing effects from energy intake by employing isocaloric controls and objective energy-intake/energy-expenditure assessments (e.g., doubly labeled water). Trials should standardize exercise prescriptions (resistance, aerobic, concurrent), systematically compare early vs late TRE and different window lengths, and enroll diverse samples (women, older adults, varied training status and BMI). Body composition should be assessed with validated methods (DXA or 4-compartment), and outcomes should include FM, BF%, FFM, strength/performance, metabolic health, and adverse events. Complete reporting of group means, standard deviations, and sample sizes at baseline and follow-up is essential to enable future meta-analyses.
