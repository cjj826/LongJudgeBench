## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The primary quantitative finding is that, across 11 studies with computable odds ratios, the random-effects pooled odds ratio was 3.8131 (95% CI: 0.6252 to 23.2571; p=0.1468). This estimate is imprecise and not statistically significant, with a confidence interval spanning from a potential 37% reduction to more than a 23-fold increase in odds. In contrast, a fixed-effect model produced a smaller, statistically precise odds ratio of 1.2637 (95% CI: 1.2174 to 1.3117). The divergence between the random- and fixed-effects results, together with the extreme heterogeneity (I²=99.9%, τ²=8.430431), indicates that the pooled association is highly unstable and sensitive to model assumptions and study weighting.

Heterogeneity likely reflects multiple, overlapping factors. First, effect metrics were mixed. Several included studies reported non-OR parameters (e.g., continuous beta coefficients or mean differences) that are not directly commensurate with odds ratios: for example, Unknown (2026) cohort reported an effect of -0.1300 (95% CI: -0.2400 to -0.0300), and Unknown (2024) secondary analysis reported -0.0400 (95% CI: -0.0900 to 0.0000). These likely represent changes in HAZ rather than odds of stunting. Second, even among studies reporting ORs, internal inconsistencies suggest data quality concerns (e.g., Unknown [2025] reported 0.5128 with a 95% CI of 1.0600 to 2.6100, which is mathematically incompatible). Third, the range of study designs and analytic strategies is broad: an observational modeling study (Unknown, 2014) reported a very large effect (13.8500; 95% CI: 13.6000 to 14.1000), whereas a longitudinal cohort (Unknown, 2011) reported a small effect (1.1500), and a cluster randomized trial (Unknown, 2024) reported 2.0500 (no CI). Differences in confounder control, exposure definitions, timing of anthropometry, and setting likely further contributed to between-study variability.

The fixed-effect estimate suggests a modest increase in odds; however, given I²=99.9%, the fixed-effect model’s assumption that all studies share a common underlying effect is implausible. In such circumstances, the random-effects model is generally preferred. The wide random-effects CI and non-significance imply that the current evidence base does not support a precise, generalizable association. The extreme between-study variance (τ²=8.43) also suggests that one or more outlying studies (e.g., Unknown, 2014) may disproportionately influence the pooled estimate. Without subgroup or influence analyses, we cannot quantify the impact of these potential outliers.

We were unable to extract study author conclusions from the included records. Consequently, we cannot compare our pooled estimates with the interpretations offered by individual study teams. Nonetheless, the pattern of reported effects is consistent with a literature that is methodologically heterogeneous and, at times, internally inconsistent. Some studies suggest small adverse associations (e.g., ORs near 1.1–2.0 or small negative changes in HAZ), while others suggest very large effects that may reflect different constructs or modeling choices. Because the comparison group across analyses is described as “non-stunted children,” but the exposure is not consistently defined across studies, the appropriateness of pooling is uncertain; in several instances, the parameter likely represents a change in continuous HAZ rather than an odds ratio for stunting.

Given these issues, we interpret the random-effects meta-analytic result as very low-certainty, hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory. The evidence is insufficient to draw confident conclusions about the magnitude or even the direction of association between the unspecified exposures examined and stunting among children under five in LMICs.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 25 included studies (11 with computable odds ratios), the random-effects pooled estimate for the odds of stunting was not statistically significant (OR 3.8131; 95% CI: 0.6252 to 23.2571; p=0.1468) and exhibited extreme heterogeneity (I²=99.9%). A fixed-effect model suggested a modest increase (OR 1.2637; 95% CI: 1.2174 to 1.3117), but this is unlikely to be reliable given the profound between-study variability and mixed effect measures. Overall, the strength of evidence is very low, and no confident, generalizable conclusion can be drawn about the association under study.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations:
- The extracted dataset lacked critical details (many N=NR/NR; missing or incompatible effect metrics; absent author conclusions), hindering transparent synthesis. The presence of implausible values (e.g., central estimate outside its reported CI) and mixed metrics (odds ratios, mean differences, regression coefficients) limits the validity of any pooled estimate. No risk-of-bias assessments were available, precluding evaluation of confounding, selection, measurement error, attrition, and selective reporting across studies.
- Conceptual heterogeneity was substantial. The comparison group was consistently “non-stunted,” but the exposures/contrasts pooled were not standardized or fully described, and some analyses appear to pertain to continuous HAZ rather than dichotomous stunting. Pooling across such disparate constructs risks statistical and interpretive errors. We lacked sufficient information to perform subgroup analyses or meta-regression to explore heterogeneity.

Study-level limitations (as inferred from the extracted information):
- Diverse designs (observational cohorts, modeling studies, quasi-experiments, a cluster RCT) with likely variable confounder control and risk of bias. Some designs (e.g., modeling and secondary analyses) may be particularly sensitive to specification choices.
- Outcome ascertainment and timing likely varied, even though WHO definitions were cited. Anthropometric measurement error, age heaping, and seasonality could bias HAZ and stunting classification. Several studies reported effects on continuous HAZ rather than stunting odds, complicating comparability.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice and policy:
- Given the very low-certainty evidence and extreme heterogeneity, these findings should not be used to guide programmatic decisions. Where policies or interventions related to stunting are considered, decisions should rely on high-quality, exposure-specific evidence (e.g., randomized or robust quasi-experimental evaluations with standardized stunting outcomes) rather than the pooled estimates presented here.

For research:
- Standardization is essential. Future studies should pre-specify exposure constructs, use WHO-defined stunting as a binary outcome where odds ratios are intended, and report complete, internally consistent results (effect size, CI, p-value, sample sizes). Harmonized covariate adjustment sets and detailed context (country, setting, age bands) will facilitate synthesis.
- Prospective, adequately powered cohort studies and randomized or quasi-experimental designs in LMICs should be prioritized, with rigorous risk-of-bias assessment and transparent reporting. Meta-analyses should avoid pooling across incompatible metrics; separate syntheses by exposure type and outcome scale (binary stunting vs continuous HAZ) are needed. Pre-planned sensitivity, influence, and subgroup analyses (e.g., by age, region, data source) should be conducted to address heterogeneity. Where possible, data sharing to enable individual participant data meta-analysis would help standardize outcomes and covariates.
