## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
We included 12 controlled studies evaluating biofeedback for adults with diagnosed hypertension, spanning publications from 1979 to 2013. Designs ranged from randomized controlled trials (including several double‑blind/sham- or placebo‑controlled comparisons) to other controlled group comparisons. Sample sizes were generally small; where reported, individual trials enrolled on the order of tens of participants per arm (e.g., 20/18 in Unknown 2007; 57/20 in Unknown 1989; 28/28 in a trial with year not reported; 16/14 in Unknown 1998). Three studies contributed extractable data for quantitative synthesis. The meta-analysis used mean difference (MD, mmHg) in blood pressure; by dataset coding, positive values were treated as favoring biofeedback, although sparse reporting warrants caution in interpreting directionality.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Across the three trials with computable outcomes, the random-effects pooled estimate suggested a modest, statistically non‑significant advantage of biofeedback over control for blood pressure reduction: MD 3.43 mmHg (95% CI −2.97 to 9.83; p=0.2933). Under a fixed‑effects model, the pooled MD was 0.31 mmHg (95% CI −0.72 to 1.34), indicating essentially no average benefit when assuming a common effect. These contrasts imply that any potential effect, if present, is small and highly uncertain.

Individual trial estimates varied widely. The Unknown (2007) randomized controlled trial (N=20/18) reported an MD of 8.50 mmHg (95% CI 3.83 to 13.17), suggesting a clinically meaningful reduction in blood pressure with biofeedback. In contrast, a randomized, double‑blind, sham‑controlled trial from 2013 reported an MD of 2.35 mmHg (95% CI −6.50 to 11.20), compatible with no effect and with imprecise estimation. A randomized, placebo‑controlled, double‑blind trial (year not reported; N=28/28) yielded an MD of −0.15 mmHg with an internally inconsistent 95% CI (0.30 to 2.50), which, taken at face value, implies a null or trivial effect and highlights data quality/reporting issues in the available records. The divergence between the relatively large, favorable effect in the 2007 trial and the null estimates in the sham-/placebo‑controlled trials drives the substantial between‑study heterogeneity.

Heterogeneity was considerable (I²=84.3%, τ²=25.12), indicating that the observed effects differ more than expected by chance. Potential contributors include: biofeedback modality (e.g., heart rate variability [HRV] biofeedback versus thermal or EMG biofeedback), comparator type (usual care vs sham feedback), participant characteristics (medication status, baseline blood pressure), outcome definition (systolic vs diastolic blood pressure; office vs ambulatory measures), intervention dose and adherence, and trial conduct (e.g., blinding effectiveness). Notably, the trials with more rigorous sham/placebo controls tended to cluster near the null, whereas the trial without a clearly described sham control showed a larger effect. This pattern raises the possibility that nonspecific factors (expectancy, attention, regression to the mean) may account for part of the observed benefit in less controlled settings.

We could not perform a formal risk‑of‑bias synthesis, and the extracted records did not include the original author conclusions for most trials. Consequently, we cannot systematically compare our pooled estimates with the investigators’ interpretations. Nevertheless, given the null or near‑null point estimates and wide confidence intervals in the 2013 double‑blind sham‑controlled trial and the placebo‑controlled double‑blind trial (year NR), it is reasonable to infer that those authors likely judged their findings as inconclusive or not supportive of a clinically important effect. Conversely, the 2007 RCT’s statistically significant MD of 8.50 mmHg would typically be interpreted by authors as supportive of efficacy—though without details on blinding, measurement methods, or medication adjustments, that result remains vulnerable to bias.

The marked difference between random‑effects (3.43 mmHg) and fixed‑effects (0.31 mmHg) pooled estimates underscores the instability of the summary effect and the influence of small, positive trials. With only three studies contributing quantitative data and with evident reporting inconsistencies, these meta-analytic results should be considered exploratory. No formal assessment of publication bias was possible; the preponderance of small studies and the earlier publication era of several trials (1979–1989) heighten concerns about selective reporting.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Current evidence from controlled trials of biofeedback for hypertension is limited, heterogeneous, and imprecise. A random‑effects meta‑analysis of three small studies suggested a non‑significant average reduction of 3.43 mmHg (95% CI −2.97 to 9.83), while a fixed‑effects model showed essentially no effect (0.31 mmHg; 95% CI −0.72 to 1.34). Trials employing double‑blind/sham controls largely showed null or trivial differences. On balance, the evidence is insufficient to conclude that biofeedback produces a reliable, clinically meaningful reduction in blood pressure in adults with diagnosed hypertension.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on a local corpus and incomplete reporting in the included records. Many studies lacked basic details (sample sizes by arm, outcome definitions, measurement methods), precluding confirmation of whether the meta-analyzed outcomes were systolic, diastolic, or a composite; the sign convention for mean differences required assumptions; and only three studies yielded extractable data, limiting precision and precluding subgroup or sensitivity analyses. We were unable to perform a formal risk‑of‑bias assessment or to synthesize author conclusions because these data were not available in the extracted records.

Study-level limitations are substantial. Several trials date from the 1979–1989 era, when randomization, allocation concealment, and blinding were often suboptimally reported or implemented. Biofeedback modalities varied (e.g., HRV vs thermal/EMG), as did comparators (usual care vs sham/placebo feedback), treatment dose, and follow‑up duration. Small sample sizes (often tens per arm) and short interventions increase susceptibility to random error, regression to the mean, and Hawthorne/expectancy effects. Medication status and changes were not consistently described, and ambulatory blood pressure monitoring—more robust against white‑coat effects—was not clearly reported. Finally, one trial presented internally inconsistent effect/CI values, highlighting data integrity concerns.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Given the small, non‑significant pooled effects and stronger trials with sham controls tending toward null findings, biofeedback cannot be recommended as a stand‑alone antihypertensive therapy. Clinicians may consider biofeedback (particularly HRV biofeedback) as an adjunctive stress‑management tool for selected patients who are motivated and interested in behavioral interventions, but it should not replace guideline‑directed pharmacologic therapy and proven lifestyle measures (e.g., sodium reduction, physical activity, weight loss). Patients should be counseled that any blood pressure benefit is uncertain and likely small.

For research: Future trials should be adequately powered, preregistered randomized controlled studies using standardized biofeedback protocols (e.g., clearly defined HRV biofeedback dosing and fidelity monitoring) and rigorous comparators (credible sham feedback). Primary outcomes should include 24‑hour ambulatory systolic and diastolic blood pressure, with blinded outcome assessment and stable, documented medication regimens. Trials should stratify or adjust for baseline blood pressure, medication use, and stress/anxiety profiles; collect mechanistic measures (e.g., baroreflex sensitivity, HRV indices) to link physiological targets to clinical effects; and include longer follow‑up to assess durability. Comparative effectiveness versus other behavioral interventions (slow breathing without feedback, relaxation training) would clarify the value added by feedback itself. A core outcome set and transparent reporting (CONSORT, TIDieR) are needed to reduce heterogeneity and enable more definitive synthesis.
