## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
From 100 records screened, four cross-sectional studies conducted between 2010 and 2020 met inclusion criteria (final included: 4). All were set in dental care contexts but were sparsely reported in the extraction. Sample sizes were available for two studies (N=161 and N=232), while two reported N as not recorded (NR). Reported effect measures were odds ratios (ORs): 1.0682 with a 95% CI of 1.1130 to 7.6110 (2010), 0.2700 (2020), 85.8000 (year not reported), and one study with non-computable effects (2018). Due to heterogeneity and incomplete reporting, quantitative pooling was not undertaken.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The principal finding is that the extractable evidence on sexual misconduct in dental settings is limited, internally inconsistent, and highly heterogeneous across studies, precluding a meaningful meta-analysis. Effect sizes ranged from a large inverse association (OR 0.27 in 2020, N=161) to an extremely large positive association (OR 85.8 in a study with N=232). One study reported an OR of 1.0682 with a 95% CI of 1.1130 to 7.6110 (2010), which is internally inconsistent because the point estimate lies outside the stated CI; taken at face value, the CI implies elevated odds (lower bound >1.0), but the misalignment undermines confidence in that result. A 2018 survey provided insufficient numeric detail to compute an effect. None of the studies provided p-values in the extraction, and outcomes (e.g., prevalence, underreporting, trauma, disengagement) were not consistently mapped to the reported ORs.

Interpreting the individual effects is constrained by unclear exposure and comparator definitions. An OR of 0.27 in 2020 could indicate lower odds of an outcome (e.g., experiencing misconduct or reporting it) among an “exposed” group, but the exposure (e.g., training, code-of-conduct implementation, role, or demographic factor) is unspecified. Conversely, an OR of 85.8 suggests a very strong association between exposure and outcome—values of this magnitude often reflect sparse data (small cell counts), case definition heterogeneity, or model instability (e.g., quasi-complete separation) rather than a true effect of that size. The 2010 study’s CI (1.11 to 7.61) implies a positive association of moderate-to-large magnitude if accurate, but the point/CI inconsistency signals possible data entry or reporting error.

Heterogeneity is likely driven by differences in populations (practitioners vs staff vs students vs patients vs educators), outcomes (prevalence of harassment vs assault; reporting behavior; emotional trauma; professional disengagement), and instruments used to assess misconduct or its consequences. Cross-sectional surveys are susceptible to recall and social desirability biases, and underreporting is common in this topic area; both would bias prevalence estimates downward and could distort associations if reporting differs by role, gender, power differentials, or organizational culture. Without standardized definitions and validated measures, study-level findings are not readily comparable. The missing sample size data in two studies further impedes assessment of precision and external validity.

Robustness checks (e.g., fixed- vs random-effects models, sensitivity analyses) were not possible given the scant and inconsistent data. We planned to synthesize author conclusions to contextualize the numeric results; however, author interpretations were not captured in the extraction for any included study, preventing triangulation between our synthesis and original conclusions. Given the topic, it is plausible that authors emphasized the presence of sexual misconduct in dental environments and advocated for preventive measures, but we cannot confirm this from the available data.

In sum, while the existence of sexual misconduct in dental settings is supported by the very fact that targeted surveys were conducted and associations were reported, the magnitude, direction, and determinants of outcomes such as underreporting, emotional trauma, or professional disengagement remain uncertain. No included study evaluated the effectiveness of interventions, leaving that portion of the PICO unanswered.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
The current evidence base on sexual misconduct in dental care settings is sparse, cross-sectional, and inconsistently reported. Four surveys (2010–2020) yielded heterogeneous and, in one case, internally inconsistent effect estimates (ORs 0.27; 1.0682 with CI 1.11–7.61; 85.8; and one not computable), with incomplete sample size and outcome information. We cannot confidently quantify prevalence, underreporting rates, emotional trauma, professional disengagement, or intervention effectiveness. The overall strength of evidence is very low, and any inferences about magnitude or causality would be speculative.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations:
- The corpus was limited to 100 records, with only four meeting criteria. While our screening was systematic, the small yield and reliance on a local corpus raise the possibility of missed literature (e.g., grey literature, disciplinary reports, regulatory data).
- Data extraction was incomplete for key variables (sample sizes, confidence intervals, outcome definitions, author conclusions), and one effect estimate was internally inconsistent (point estimate outside CI), constraining both evidence grading and meta-analysis.
- Planned risk of bias assessment could not be performed due to insufficient reporting of study methods and outcomes in the extracted records.

Study-level limitations:
- All included studies were cross-sectional, precluding causal inference and temporal ordering (e.g., whether professional disengagement follows misconduct exposure).
- Potential for selection bias (nonresponse, convenience samples), measurement bias (non-validated instruments, varying definitions of misconduct), and social desirability/recall bias that likely understate prevalence and could distort associations.
- Sparse data and small cell counts may have produced unstable ORs (e.g., OR 85.8), and missing N values in two studies prevented assessment of precision and power.
- No study in the set evaluated interventions; thus, effectiveness of prevention or reporting strategies remains unaddressed.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice:
- Despite limited quantification, sexual misconduct in dental settings is a plausible and serious risk. Organizations should implement evidence-informed safeguards commonly recommended in healthcare: clear definitions and codes of conduct; confidential, trauma-informed reporting pathways; anti-retaliation protections; bystander and supervisor training; and regular climate assessments. However, practitioners and administrators should recognize that the present review cannot verify the effectiveness of specific interventions in dental settings.
- Data infrastructure should be strengthened: anonymous climate surveys using validated instruments; standardized incident categorization; and linkage (with safeguards) to administrative and complaint data to monitor trends and identify high-risk contexts (e.g., student clinics, one-on-one operatories).

For research:
- Foundational work is needed: consensus definitions of sexual misconduct across roles (patient-on-staff, staff-on-staff, staff-on-patient, faculty-on-student); validated measurement tools; and standardized reporting (including base rates, denominators, and CIs).
- Study designs should include: multicenter cross-sectional studies with representative sampling; longitudinal cohorts to estimate incidence and downstream outcomes (e.g., PHQ-9 for depression, PCL-5 for trauma, Maslach Burnout for disengagement); and mixed-methods qualitative studies to understand barriers to reporting and underreporting.
- Intervention evaluations are a priority: cluster-randomized or stepped-wedge trials of policies and trainings (e.g., bystander intervention, supervisor accountability, environmental modifications) with predefined, validated outcomes (incident rates, reporting uptake, psychological outcomes) and equity analyses by role, gender, and power gradients.
- Analytical rigor: preregistration; adequate sample sizes; handling of sparse data (e.g., Firth correction or exact logistic regression); transparent reporting of denominators, effect sizes with CIs, and sensitivity analyses.
