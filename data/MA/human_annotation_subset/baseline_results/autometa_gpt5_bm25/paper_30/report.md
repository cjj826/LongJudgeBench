## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Five randomized controlled trials (RCTs) with offspring follow-up met inclusion criteria (publication years spanned 2021–2023 for four studies; one not reported). Across the three trials reporting group sizes, at least 176 offspring contributed outcome data (31/39, 17/29, and 24/36 in intervention/control groups, respectively); two studies did not report offspring numbers. One RCT included an additional non-randomized reference cohort for contextual comparisons. Outcomes encompassed offspring cardiac structure/function and related cardiovascular parameters (blood pressure, heart rate, vascular structure/stiffness) assessed from infancy (<2 months) through early childhood (3–7 years). Four studies contributed to quantitative synthesis using mean differences (MDs); one study’s effect was not computable and was summarized narratively. Effects were coded so that negative values indicate lower (more favorable) levels of adverse cardiovascular parameters in the intervention arm.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The primary finding from random-effects meta-analysis was no clear overall effect of preconception/antenatal lifestyle interventions on offspring cardiac/cardiovascular parameters in early life: pooled MD −0.2996 (95% CI −0.8113 to 0.2121; p=0.2511). Considerable heterogeneity was present (I²=90.5%, τ²=0.2255), indicating that between-study differences dominate the observed variance. In contrast, the fixed-effect model suggested a small statistically significant benefit, MD −0.0343 (95% CI −0.0553 to −0.0134). Given the high heterogeneity and diverse outcomes/ages, the random-effects estimate is the more appropriate summary and does not support a consistent effect across trials.

Individual trials varied widely in magnitude and precision. One 2022 offspring follow-up (N=24/36) reported a large, precise benefit (MD −0.8800, 95% CI −1.1800 to −0.5800). Another 2022 study with an additional non-randomized reference group (N=31/39) reported a very small but statistically precise benefit (MD −0.0300, 95% CI −0.0500 to −0.0080). A 2021 antenatal diet and physical activity trial with 3-year follow-up (offspring N not reported) found no clear effect (MD −0.3000, 95% CI −0.9200 to 0.3100). A further RCT with offspring follow-up (N=17/29) yielded an imprecise estimate spanning potential benefit and harm (MD 0.0632, 95% CI −0.5357 to 0.6620). One 2023 RCT contributed offspring data but the effect was not computable for pooling. The spread of point estimates—from a large favorable effect (−0.88) to near-null or slightly positive values—explains the high I².

Potential sources of heterogeneity likely include: (1) the nature of the offspring outcome (cardiac structure/function versus peripheral blood pressure or arterial stiffness), (2) timing of assessment (neonatal vs preschool/school-age), (3) intervention content and intensity (dietary counseling, physical activity programs, combined approaches), and (4) differences in adherence and maternal mediators (e.g., gestational weight gain, glycemia). The inclusion of a non-random reference cohort in one study may aid external benchmarking but can complicate interpretation if used beyond contextualization. Although analyses used MDs, the underlying measurement scales likely differed (e.g., mm for wall thickness, mmHg for blood pressure, m/s for pulse wave velocity), and the dataset did not specify whether outcomes were harmonized (e.g., via standardized metrics or z-scores). This limits clinical interpretability of pooled magnitudes, though the directionality was aligned such that negative values favored the intervention.

Risk of bias could not be formally assessed with the provided data. Nonetheless, common issues in offspring follow-ups include attrition, potential selection bias if follow-up is differential by treatment, and outcome assessor blinding (e.g., echocardiographic readings). Small sample sizes in several studies (e.g., N=17/29) yield wide confidence intervals and increase the influence of single studies on pooled results. The fixed-effect significance with a very small MD (−0.0343) contrasted with the non-significant random-effects estimate underscores that apparent precision may be driven by one or two studies with narrow CIs rather than a consistent effect across diverse contexts. With fewer than 10 studies, small-study effects and publication bias could not be assessed reliably.

Direct author conclusions were not captured in the extracted dataset, limiting our ability to quote study interpretations. Based on reported estimates, it is plausible that the 2022 trial with MD −0.8800 concluded a beneficial offspring effect, whereas studies with MDs spanning zero likely concluded no statistically significant differences. The aggregate evidence, however, especially under random-effects modeling and in the presence of substantial heterogeneity, does not support a consistent impact of antenatal lifestyle interventions on early offspring cardiac remodeling or cardiovascular parameters.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across five RCTs with offspring follow-up, evidence for an effect of preconception/antenatal lifestyle interventions on early-life cardiac remodeling and related cardiovascular parameters is inconsistent and heterogeneous. The random-effects meta-analysis showed no clear overall effect (MD −0.2996, 95% CI −0.8113 to 0.2121; p=0.2511) despite a small fixed-effect signal (MD −0.0343, 95% CI −0.0553 to −0.0134). On balance, current evidence does not support a reliable, generalizable benefit for offspring cardiovascular structure or function up to early childhood.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include incomplete reporting of study identifiers, offspring sample sizes (two studies), and author conclusions in the extracted dataset; a formal risk-of-bias assessment was not feasible with available information. The pooled effect was expressed as a mean difference without detailed specification of the underlying scales or whether outcomes were standardized; this limits clinical interpretability of pooled magnitudes. Considerable heterogeneity (I²=90.5%) reduces confidence in the pooled estimate and suggests that true effects likely vary across populations, interventions, outcomes, and ages at assessment.

Study-level limitations likely include small sample sizes (e.g., N=17/29), attrition and potential selection bias in offspring follow-ups, and variability in intervention fidelity/adherence. Differences in outcome domains (echocardiographic measures vs blood pressure vs arterial stiffness), measurement methods, and follow-up timing (from neonatal to school age) further complicate synthesis. One RCT included a non-randomized reference group for contextual comparisons, which—if not carefully separated from randomized contrasts—could introduce confounding. With fewer than 10 studies, we could not evaluate small-study effects or publication bias.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current RCT evidence does not support changing clinical practice on the basis of expected benefits to offspring cardiac remodeling or early cardiovascular parameters from antenatal lifestyle interventions. Lifestyle counseling and physical activity/diet programs should continue to be offered for established maternal benefits (e.g., gestational weight gain control, glycemic management), but clinicians should avoid overpromising downstream cardiovascular benefits for offspring based on present data.

For research: Larger, well-characterized RCTs (or extended follow-ups of existing RCTs) with prespecified offspring cardiovascular endpoints are needed. Priorities include: standardized outcome batteries (e.g., LV mass, interventricular septal thickness, systolic/diastolic function, carotid intima-media thickness, pulse wave velocity, resting blood pressure/heart rate), harmonized measurement protocols and units, and age-specific follow-up windows (neonatal, preschool, school-age) to map developmental trajectories. Trials should report intention-to-treat analyses, minimize loss to follow-up, ensure blinded outcome assessment, and provide complete data (means, SDs, units, and directionality) to enable meta-analysis. Investigations should examine mediators (gestational weight gain, maternal glycemia, placental function), intervention adherence/dose-response, and the potential added value of preconception interventions. Stratified analyses by maternal obesity severity and metabolic status may clarify who benefits. Data sharing of individual participant data could facilitate standardized re-analysis and meta-analysis across heterogeneous outcomes.
