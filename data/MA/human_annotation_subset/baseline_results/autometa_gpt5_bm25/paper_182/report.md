## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
We included six randomized trials published between 2004 and 2018 evaluating extraction of the primary maxillary canine as interceptive treatment for palatally displaced maxillary canines (PDC) in mixed dentition (ages 10–13). Study designs included randomized prospective longitudinal trials, randomized clinical trials, and one split-mouth randomized controlled trial. Reporting of sample sizes was incomplete: one split-mouth trial enrolled 24 participants (with contralateral sides serving as within-patient controls; N=24/24), one randomized clinical trial reported N=35 with a non-reported comparator size, and four trials did not report group sizes in the information available to us. Extractable quantitative data suitable for calculating an effect size were available from only one trial; the remaining five trials reported outcomes in formats that did not permit computation of odds ratios for successful eruption or normalization. Consequently, a meta-analysis was not performed.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The single trial with extractable data (split-mouth design; N=24/24) yielded an odds ratio (OR) of 1.03 (95% CI 0.87 to 9.06) for successful eruption/normalization when extracting the primary canine compared with no/delayed interceptive treatment. This point estimate is near null and the confidence interval is very wide, spanning both potential harm and substantial benefit. Statistically, this result is inconclusive and primarily reflects imprecision associated with a small sample rather than evidence of no effect. With only one computable estimate, we were unable to examine the consistency of effects across studies, nor could we assess dose–response by initial displacement severity or age within the 10–13 range.

Across the remaining five randomized trials (2004, 2008, 2015, 2015, 2018), outcomes were not reported in a way that allowed calculation of effect sizes for this review. Differences in outcome definitions (e.g., “normalization” on radiographs versus “successful eruption” into the arch), variable follow-up durations, and potential use of intermediate outcomes (angulation changes, sector movements) likely contributed to non-computability. Heterogeneity in trial design—parallel-group versus split-mouth—and incomplete reporting of participant characteristics (unilateral versus bilateral PDC, baseline canine angulation/sector, dental age, and any delayed co-interventions) further limit cross-trial comparability. These sources of clinical and methodological heterogeneity would have complicated pooling even if multiple computable estimates had been available.

We did not have access to formal risk-of-bias details (e.g., allocation concealment, sequence generation, attrition, or selective reporting) for the included trials, which constrains interpretation. Blinding of operators and outcome assessors is generally challenging in surgical or extraction trials; lack of blinded assessment, particularly when outcomes rely on clinical judgment or non-standardized imaging, can introduce detection bias. Split-mouth designs mitigate inter-individual confounding but can introduce carry-over or measurement issues if bilateral PDC severity differs, and they may limit generalizability to unilateral cases.

Author conclusions were not available from the extracted records for these trials; thus, we cannot compare original investigators’ interpretations with our synthesis. Given the single inconclusive OR and the pervasive reporting limitations, we cannot determine whether primary canine extraction increases the probability of spontaneous eruption or normalization of PDC compared with no or delayed interceptive treatment in children aged 10–13 years. Any inference about benefit or lack thereof would be speculative and not supported by the available data.

Robustness checks (e.g., comparing fixed versus random-effects models, subgroup analyses by baseline severity, sensitivity analyses excluding high-risk-of-bias studies) were not possible. The available estimate’s precision is inadequate to guide practice, and the lack of pooled analysis prevents assessment of heterogeneity or exploration of effect modifiers.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across six randomized trials (2004–2018), quantitative evidence on whether extraction of the primary maxillary canine improves spontaneous eruption/normalization of palatally displaced maxillary canines in 10–13-year-olds is insufficient. The only computable effect (split-mouth RCT; N=24) yielded an OR of 1.03 (95% CI 0.87 to 9.06), an imprecise and inconclusive result. Given incomplete reporting and absence of pooled data, we cannot conclude that interceptive extraction confers benefit over no or delayed treatment.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on incompletely reported trial data in our corpus. For five of six trials, outcome reporting did not allow calculation of dichotomous effect sizes; author conclusions and detailed methods were not available, precluding cross-verification and formal risk-of-bias assessment. The inability to pool across studies, perform subgroup analyses, or conduct sensitivity analyses substantially limits the strength of inference.

Study-level limitations, as inferred from available metadata, include small sample sizes (e.g., N=24 in the split-mouth RCT; another trial with N=35 and unspecified comparator size), heterogeneous designs (split-mouth versus parallel-group), and likely variability in definitions of “normalization” and “successful eruption,” imaging modalities, and follow-up periods. Blinding is inherently difficult in extraction trials, raising risk of detection bias. Without explicit reporting on allocation concealment, attrition, crossovers, and co-interventions (even if active orthodontics at baseline was excluded), internal validity is uncertain. Generalizability may be limited if bilateral PDC predominated in split-mouth studies.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current randomized evidence as available to this review does not provide a reliable estimate of the benefit or harm of extracting the primary maxillary canine to manage PDC in children aged 10–13. Clinicians should not base routine prophylactic extraction decisions solely on this evidence. Instead, decisions should be individualized, considering initial canine displacement severity, patient age/dental development, risk of incisor root resorption, arch space, bilateral versus unilateral involvement, and family preferences. Close monitoring and shared decision-making remain essential.

For research: There is a clear need for adequately powered, multicenter randomized trials with standardized, patient-important outcomes. Future studies should:
- Pre-register protocols and follow CONSORT.
- Use standardized radiographic classifications (e.g., sector, angulation) and define primary outcomes as spontaneous full eruption into functional occlusion by a pre-specified time, with time-to-event analysis.
- Report dichotomous outcomes and raw counts to enable effect size computation, along with continuous measures of positional change.
- Stratify or adjust for baseline severity, unilateral/bilateral PDC, dental age, and presence of space adequacy.
- Include harms and downstream resource use (need for surgical exposure/orthodontic traction, treatment time, adverse events, impact on adjacent teeth).
- Ensure blinded outcome assessment where feasible (centralized radiographic readings) and transparent reporting of allocation concealment, attrition, and crossovers.
Sample size calculations should target detection of clinically meaningful absolute risk differences (e.g., 15–20%) with sufficient power, anticipating spontaneous eruption rates in control groups and potential clustering in split-mouth designs.
