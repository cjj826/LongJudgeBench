7.2 Discussion of Findings
The single included controlled feeding RCT provides limited, short-term evidence on the effect of lower versus higher sodium intake in the general adult population. The reported effect estimate was BETA = -0.96 (95% CI: -2.74 to 0.83). Because the outcome unit was not specified in the extraction and the effect measure was labeled generically as BETA, we interpret this as indicating a small, statistically non-significant reduction (the 95% CI crosses the null) in the studied outcome with lower sodium intake relative to higher sodium intake. If this estimate pertains to a continuous physiological outcome such as blood pressure, the magnitude appears modest; however, without a defined outcome and units, any clinical interpretation must be restrained.

No between-study heterogeneity could be assessed, as only one study was included. Conceptually, important sources of heterogeneity in sodium trials typically include the magnitude of sodium contrast achieved, baseline participant characteristics (e.g., normotensive vs hypertensive), study duration, and co-interventions (such as diet patterns that co-vary with sodium). The included trial used a sequential sodium-level (crossover-like) feeding protocol, which generally strengthens internal validity by reducing between-person confounding and improving adherence and exposure control. At the same time, sequential designs can be vulnerable to period and carryover effects if washout is inadequate or if outcomes have delayed responses to sodium changes; we could not evaluate these risks with the information provided.

Risk-of-bias details were not available from our extraction, and therefore a formal assessment was not performed. Nonetheless, controlled feeding trials typically have strengths in exposure measurement (precise sodium delivery) and adherence, but may have limitations in external validity, shorter durations, and selective participant samples. The absence of a reported outcome definition and the lack of detail on randomization, sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding, and attrition prevent us from judging the credibility of the effect estimate beyond noting that it is derived from a randomized design with controlled intake.

We did not conduct meta-analysis, fixed- vs random-effects comparisons, or sensitivity analyses, given the single study with one extractable effect size. The original study authors’ conclusions were not available in our data extraction; we therefore cannot directly compare our synthesis to their interpretation. Based solely on the effect size and confidence interval reported, the study does not provide clear evidence of a clinically meaningful benefit of lower versus higher sodium intake on the unspecified outcome. Importantly, no data from this trial were available for hard clinical endpoints (cardiovascular events, chronic kidney disease progression, fractures, or mortality), nor for other outcomes specified in our protocol (e.g., composite renal outcomes or physical performance).

The screening process excluded most records due to lack of a clear comparative sodium exposure (e.g., non-comparative or no defined lower vs higher sodium groups), or because they were reviews/editorials or focused on excluded populations. This pattern suggests that, within the corpus we searched, comparative human studies directly contrasting lower (<5 g/day salt or <2 g/day sodium) versus higher intakes and reporting extractable effect sizes for our outcomes were scarce.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

8. Conclusions and Implications

8.1 Main Conclusions
Within the constraints of this corpus, evidence linking lower versus higher sodium intake to our prespecified outcomes is insufficient. One controlled feeding RCT (2024; N=207 randomized, 202 analyzed) reported a small, statistically non-significant effect favoring lower sodium (BETA = -0.96; 95% CI: -2.74 to 0.83) on an unspecified continuous outcome. No conclusions can be drawn regarding effects on cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, osteoporosis, all-cause mortality, or physical performance.

8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations: Only one study met inclusion criteria, precluding meta-analysis and limiting the precision and generalizability of conclusions. Extracted data lacked key details: the specific outcome corresponding to the effect size, the unit of measurement, the magnitude of sodium contrast achieved, intervention duration, and risk-of-bias domains. We could not locate or extract the original authors’ conclusions, limiting our ability to compare interpretations. Although we screened 107 records, our evidence base derives solely from the local corpus provided; studies outside this corpus or published after the search window could alter conclusions.

Study-level limitations: The included trial’s sequential feeding design likely ensured exposure control but may have limited external validity and duration, reducing insight into long-term clinical endpoints. Without clarity on the outcome, measurement methods, and trial conduct (randomization, blinding, attrition), internal validity cannot be fully appraised. The sodium intake levels relative to recommended thresholds (<5 g/day salt or <2 g/day sodium) were not reported, so it is unclear whether the “lower” arm achieved guideline-relevant targets. The sample size (207 randomized, 202 analyzed) may be underpowered for small effects, and short-term physiologic changes (if that was the outcome) may not translate to long-term health outcomes.

8.3 Implications
For practice: This review, based on a single controlled feeding RCT, does not provide sufficient evidence to change practice regarding sodium intake for the general adult population. The isolated, non-significant effect estimate and the absence of outcome specificity and event-based data mean that policy and clinical guidance should continue to rely on the broader body of evidence not captured here. Clinicians and public health practitioners should interpret these findings as inconclusive rather than contradictory to existing guidance.

For research: There is a clear need for well-designed, adequately powered randomized trials and prospective cohort studies that: (1) prespecify and measure clinically meaningful outcomes (blood pressure with standardized protocols, cardiovascular and renal events, fractures, mortality, and validated physical performance measures); (2) achieve and verify sodium contrasts using high-quality methods (e.g., multiple nonconsecutive 24-hour urinary sodium collections); (3) report effect sizes with units and confidence intervals, and provide sufficient detail for meta-analysis; (4) include diverse populations (age, sex, baseline BP, comorbidities, and different dietary contexts) and assess effect modification; (5) examine dose-response relationships across sodium intakes, particularly below 2 g/day sodium versus higher levels; and (6) ensure transparent reporting of randomization, allocation concealment, blinding, adherence, attrition, and adverse events. Longer follow-up is essential to determine whether short-term physiologic changes translate into differences in clinical endpoints.
