## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
We identified 11 primary case–control, cross‑sectional laboratory studies published between 1991 and 2014 assessing intracellular calcium ion concentrations ([Ca2+]i) in peripheral cells among individuals with bipolar disorder (BD) compared with healthy controls, and in some analyses with unipolar depression or schizophrenia comparators. All studies used ex vivo platelet or lymphocyte assays to quantify basal free [Ca2+]i and/or stimulated [Ca2+]i responses to serotonin (5‑HT) or thrombin, with two studies also incorporating within-sample pharmacologic modulation or in vitro incubation. Extractable quantitative data were limited: only one study provided sufficient statistics to compute a standardized mean difference (SMD). Across the five studies that reported group sizes explicitly, at least 303 participants were included (e.g., 9/7; 25/15; 7/26; 15/17; 152/30 for BD/controls, respectively), suggesting that the actual total across all 11 studies was higher, but incomplete reporting prevented precise enumeration. Because only one effect size (SMD) was computable, meta-analysis was not feasible; we present a narrative synthesis.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The one study with extractable data (1995, N=7 BD / 26 controls) reported a moderate-to-large difference favoring higher [Ca2+]i in the BD group (SMD 1.45, 95% CI 0.55 to 2.36). Although the specific assay (basal vs stimulated, platelet vs lymphocyte) was not explicitly retrievable from our corpus, the direction and magnitude suggest a substantial case–control separation in at least one calcium-related outcome. This effect size, if representative, would support the long-standing hypothesis that peripheral calcium homeostasis is altered in BD. However, the confidence interval is wide and compatible with a range of large to modest effects, reflecting the small BD sample (n=7) and underscoring imprecision.

Across the remaining 10 studies, formal effect estimates could not be computed due to absent or incomplete reporting (e.g., means without SDs, figures without extractable data, or statistics insufficient for transformation). Nevertheless, study descriptions consistently indicate measurements of basal platelet [Ca2+]i and agonist-evoked responses (5‑HT, thrombin), with some studies also examining lymphocytes. The two studies employing within-sample pharmacologic modulation and incubation experiments suggest assay sensitivity to receptor-level or second messenger manipulations, which is consistent with a mechanistic interpretation (i.e., altered signaling rather than purely systemic calcium differences). A 2001 cross-sectional study with a comparatively large BD sample (N=152 BD / 30 controls) implies that larger-scale assessments have been attempted; however, in the absence of extractable summary statistics, its contribution to the magnitude or direction of association cannot be quantified here.

Potential sources of heterogeneity span multiple domains. Biological heterogeneity likely arises from cell type (platelets vs lymphocytes), analyte (basal vs stimulated [Ca2+]i), and agonist choice (5‑HT vs thrombin), each reflecting distinct receptor systems and signaling cascades (e.g., 5‑HT2A vs protease-activated receptors). Clinical heterogeneity includes illness phase (mania, depression, euthymia), lifetime course features, and—critically—medication status (lithium, valproate, antipsychotics), which can directly influence calcium signaling. Methodological heterogeneity is also plausible: older studies (early 1990s) likely used ratiometric dyes such as fura‑2 with varied calibration protocols, buffering conditions, and temperature control, all of which affect absolute [Ca2+]i estimates and stimulated responses. Selection of controls, matching procedures, and blinding of laboratory personnel were not extractable from our dataset and may have varied.

Although we did not perform a formal risk of bias assessment, the predominance of small, cross-sectional case–control designs indicates susceptibility to selection bias, confounding (particularly by medication and mood state), and measurement bias. The inability to pool results precludes any assessment of between-study heterogeneity or small-study effects. Fixed- vs random-effects robustness checks were not possible.

Regarding author interpretations, while direct quotations were not available from our corpus, the study designs and outcomes targeted (basal and agonist-stimulated [Ca2+]i) indicate that authors generally framed their work as tests of the hypothesis that BD is associated with altered intracellular calcium regulation in peripheral cells. The single quantifiable study’s effect size aligns with that hypothesis. However, without pooled data and with incomplete statistical reporting in most studies, we cannot determine the consistency, specificity (vs unipolar depression or schizophrenia), or magnitude of effects across the literature. Consequently, any alignment between individual authors’ interpretations and a summarized, quantitative conclusion must be regarded as tentative.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Limited and heterogeneously reported evidence from 11 cross-sectional laboratory studies suggests that individuals with bipolar disorder may exhibit altered intracellular calcium handling in peripheral cells, with one small study showing a moderate-to-large elevation in [Ca2+]i compared with healthy controls (SMD 1.45, 95% CI 0.55 to 2.36). However, the overall strength of evidence is low: most studies lacked extractable data, meta-analysis was not possible, and key confounders (medication, mood state) and methodological variability limit confidence. Current evidence does not support clinical application of platelet or lymphocyte [Ca2+]i measures as diagnostic or monitoring biomarkers in BD.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include dependence on a local corpus with incomplete statistical reporting in most included studies, precluding quantitative synthesis despite 11 eligible studies. We were unable to compute effect sizes for 10 studies because essential parameters (e.g., standard deviations, standard errors, or group-wise numeric summaries) were missing. We did not contact authors for additional data, and a formal risk-of-bias assessment could not be conducted due to insufficient reporting of design and conduct details.

Study-level limitations likely include small sample sizes (e.g., the only computable study had 7 BD participants), cross-sectional case–control designs prone to selection and confounding biases, and heterogeneity in laboratory methods (dyes, calibration, temperature, buffering), cell types, and stimuli. Medication exposure and current mood state—both capable of altering calcium signaling—were not consistently documented in the retrievable data. The inclusion of comparator psychiatric groups (unipolar depression, schizophrenia) in some studies without extractable subgroup data prevents conclusions about the specificity of findings to BD.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: The available evidence is insufficient to recommend measurement of platelet or lymphocyte [Ca2+]i—either basal or agonist-evoked—as a clinical biomarker for diagnosing BD, differentiating it from other psychiatric conditions, or guiding treatment decisions. Any observed differences in research settings should be interpreted cautiously given potential confounding by medications and mood state, and methodological variability across assays.

For research: Future work should prioritize adequately powered, preregistered studies with standardized laboratory protocols (e.g., ratiometric dye calibration, temperature and pH control, platelet preparation and activation control) and transparent, complete statistical reporting (means, SDs, sample sizes, and raw/individual-level data where feasible). Designs should stratify by mood state and rigorously document and adjust for medication exposure, with planned analyses comparing BD to unipolar depression and schizophrenia to assess diagnostic specificity. Harmonization of outcomes—clearly distinguishing basal [Ca2+]i from agonist-evoked responses (5‑HT, thrombin) in predefined primary endpoints—is essential. Multicenter efforts and independent replication are needed, and longitudinal designs could clarify state vs trait effects. Where ethical and feasible, medication-naïve or washout cohorts and within-subject designs can help disentangle pharmacologic from illness-related effects on calcium signaling.
