## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Six primary clinical studies (total N=200) met inclusion criteria, spanning 2015–2025 and comprising mainly pre–post, single-arm cohorts (n=4) and one small parallel-group controlled study (n=1), plus one retrospective single-center cohort (n=1). All evaluated catheter-based radiofrequency renal denervation (RDN) in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and treatment-resistant hypertension, comparing post-procedure outcomes with baseline. Extractable quantitative effect sizes were available for blood pressure (BP) from three studies; data on estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), serum creatinine, and procedural complications were not extractable in a form suitable for synthesis. Because fewer than two studies provided comparable, clearly defined effect sizes for the same outcome and metric, no meta-analysis was conducted; a narrative synthesis is presented.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Across studies, the direction of effect generally favored a reduction in BP after RDN, but the magnitude and certainty were variable and limited by design and reporting. Among studies with computable effects, two single-arm cohorts suggested substantial decreases from baseline: one prospective cohort (N=15) reported a mean difference (MD) of −33.0 (unit and BP measure not specified in the extract, plausibly systolic BP), and one cohort (N=40) reported MD −19.7. In contrast, the only controlled study (2020; N=12 RDN/12 control) showed a between-group MD of −6.0 (95% CI −18.9 to 6.9), an imprecise estimate spanning no effect. Together, these patterns are consistent with the hypothesis that uncontrolled pre–post designs may overestimate treatment effects due to regression to the mean, placebo effects, and co-interventions, whereas controlled comparisons provide more conservative and uncertain estimates.

Heterogeneity was substantial and multifactorial. First, study designs ranged from prospective single-arm to retrospective cohorts and a small controlled clinical study, each with differing risks of bias and susceptibility to confounding. Second, the BP outcome metric was not consistently specified (office vs ambulatory, systolic vs diastolic), and units were not always explicit, which precluded meaningful pooling and limits interpretability of the absolute effect sizes. Third, CKD severity, definitions of treatment-resistant hypertension, medication regimens, and follow-up durations likely varied, but these characteristics were insufficiently reported for stratified synthesis. Finally, three studies (2015 observational pre–post, N=27; 2022 cohort, N=54; 2025 retrospective cohort, N=40) lacked extractable effect sizes, implying incomplete reporting or incompatible outcome formats; notably, these three studies comprise over half of the total sample (121/200), meaning the quantitative picture is dominated by smaller cohorts with available data.

The implications of bias are material. Single-arm pre–post studies are inherently vulnerable to regression to the mean in patients selected for severe, resistant hypertension, and to changes in background care (e.g., medication optimization) after an invasive intervention. Without sham control or at least a contemporaneous comparator, observed BP decreases cannot be attributed confidently to RDN alone. The sole controlled study was underpowered, with a wide 95% CI (−18.9 to 6.9), compatible with a moderate benefit, no effect, or even a small increase in BP. Thus, while point estimates from uncontrolled cohorts appear promising (e.g., MD −33.0 and −19.7), the controlled evidence remains inconclusive.

Renal function and safety outcomes were insufficiently detailed for synthesis. Although these outcomes were prespecified (eGFR, creatinine, procedural complications), extractable data were not available in the included reports. As a result, we cannot quantify changes in eGFR or creatinine, nor can we estimate complication rates (e.g., renal artery injury, access-site complications, acute kidney injury). Author-level conclusions summarized in the corpus commonly characterize the procedures as technically feasible and generally well tolerated, and several reports suggest no major deterioration in renal function; however, those statements could not be verified with standardized denominators, definitions, or time points in this review. Accordingly, any apparent renal safety signal should be considered hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory.

Overall robustness is low. We could not perform a meta-analysis, nor compare fixed- versus random-effects models. The largest included cohort (N=54) did not yield extractable outcome data, which further reduces confidence in the representativeness of the quantitative findings. The available controlled evidence does not establish superiority of RDN versus medical management in CKD with treatment-resistant hypertension, and the preponderance of evidence rests on small, uncontrolled studies with high potential for bias. Authors of individual studies generally concluded that RDN was associated with BP reductions and was feasible in CKD populations, but those interpretations appear stronger than what the aggregate, methodologically limited evidence can support.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
In patients with CKD and treatment-resistant hypertension undergoing radiofrequency endovascular renal denervation, the limited and heterogeneous evidence (six studies; N=200) suggests directional reductions in blood pressure relative to baseline, with single-arm cohorts reporting mean decreases of approximately −20 to −33 (likely mmHg for systolic BP), contrasted by an inconclusive small controlled study (MD −6.0; 95% CI −18.9 to 6.9). Data on eGFR, serum creatinine, and procedural complications were insufficient for quantitative synthesis. Certainty is low due to study design limitations, inconsistent outcome reporting, and small sample sizes.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on incompletely reported studies within a local corpus, precluding extraction of key prespecified outcomes (ambulatory vs office BP, eGFR, creatinine, complications). We could not pool results due to outcome heterogeneity and insufficiently defined effect measures, and we were unable to formally assess risk of bias. Publication bias and selective outcome reporting could not be evaluated.

Study-level limitations are substantial: most included studies used uncontrolled pre–post designs vulnerable to regression to the mean, placebo effects, and concurrent care changes. Sample sizes were small (the only controlled study enrolled 24 participants total), confidence intervals were wide, and CKD staging, antihypertensive regimens, and follow-up durations were not consistently described. Outcome definitions (office vs ambulatory BP; systolic vs diastolic; units; time points) were not standardized, and safety events and renal function trajectories were not reported in a manner that allows reliable synthesis.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current evidence does not provide high-certainty support for routine use of radiofrequency RDN in CKD patients with treatment-resistant hypertension. While reductions from baseline BP are frequently observed in uncontrolled cohorts, the lone controlled comparison was inconclusive, and renal safety cannot be characterized from available data. Decisions about RDN in this population should be individualized, ideally within multidisciplinary programs and, when possible, in the context of clinical trials or registries with systematic outcome collection. Ensuring optimized pharmacotherapy, adherence, and management of secondary causes remains essential.

For research: There is a clear need for adequately powered, randomized (preferably sham-controlled) trials specifically in CKD populations with rigorously defined treatment-resistant hypertension, using contemporary radiofrequency RDN systems. Studies should prespecify standardized outcomes and time points: office and ambulatory systolic and diastolic BP, eGFR and creatinine trajectories, acute kidney injury, renal artery imaging for stenosis/dissection, access-site complications, and longer-term outcomes (≥12–24 months). Trials should stratify by CKD stage and albuminuria, control for medication changes with adherence monitoring, and report device/procedural details (ablation pattern, energy delivery) to allow assessment of dose–response. Parallel prospective registries with core outcome sets could complement trials by capturing real-world safety and effectiveness in diverse CKD subgroups.
