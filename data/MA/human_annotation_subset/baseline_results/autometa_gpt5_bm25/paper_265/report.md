### 7.1 Summary of Evidence

Consequently, no randomized or non-randomized human studies evaluating the effect of specified psychoplastogens (ketamine, esketamine, LSD, psilocybin, ayahuasca, DMT, MDMA, scopolamine, or rapastinel) on peripheral BDNF levels (serum or plasma) versus baseline or versus a placebo/control condition were included. ### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The principal finding is the absence of eligible human studies in this corpus that report pre–post or between-group effects of the specified psychoplastogens on peripheral BDNF. Without extractable effect sizes, we cannot estimate the magnitude or direction of any acute, subacute, or sustained changes in serum or plasma BDNF following these interventions. Meta-analytic pooling could not be attempted (target effect measure: SMD), and no inferences can be drawn regarding heterogeneity, dose–response, or subgroup effects.

The screening record indicates that the retrieved literature was dominated by non-original publications (reviews or meta-analyses), reports where the specified agents were not administered as the primary intervention, and at least one case report/series without a comparator. These exclusion patterns suggest that, within the available corpus, BDNF was often discussed conceptually (e.g., as a proposed mechanism of action for rapid-acting antidepressants or classic psychedelics) rather than measured as a prespecified biochemical endpoint in controlled human studies. Other likely reasons for exclusion include studies focusing on central measures (e.g., CSF), genetic or expression markers (e.g., BDNF mRNA, Val66Met polymorphism), or biomarker panels that did not report peripheral BDNF protein results in a way compatible with effect size calculation or with our agent list and comparison criteria.

Although we could not assess heterogeneity empirically, substantial heterogeneity would be expected if eligible studies existed. Key sources include: differences in agents (short- vs long-acting; dissociative vs serotonergic vs empathogen; inclusion of scopolamine/rapastinel), doses, routes (IV, intranasal, oral), and timing of sampling (minutes–hours for acute effects, 24–72 hours for subacute effects, weeks for sustained effects). Clinical populations (e.g., major depressive disorder, PTSD, substance use disorder) versus healthy volunteers may differ at baseline and in reactivity of BDNF to stress or pharmacologic challenge. Pre-analytical and analytical variability is a major concern for BDNF: serum (strongly platelet-derived) versus plasma (lower concentrations, sensitive to anticoagulant and processing), time from draw to processing, freeze–thaw cycles, and assay platform/kit differences can shift absolute concentrations and variability. Even in well-designed trials, these factors can obscure small-to-moderate true effects or generate spurious signals if not standardized.

Risk of bias considerations, had studies been available, would likely hinge on allocation concealment and blinding (particularly challenging for psychoactive interventions due to prominent subjective effects), completeness of outcome data, and selective reporting of biomarker results. Additional confounding arises from factors known to influence peripheral BDNF (diurnal variation, fasting status, recent exercise, smoking, stress of venipuncture, platelet count, concurrent medications such as SSRIs or benzodiazepines). Without study-level data, we cannot judge these risks, but they would be critical determinants of credibility and would shape interpretation even if statistically significant effects were observed.

We were unable to synthesize or contrast study-level author conclusions because no eligible studies were included. Notably, while narrative and systematic reviews in the field often posit that psychoplastogens may modulate neurotrophic pathways, our results indicate that this proposition has not, within the confines of our corpus and criteria, been tested and reported in a manner permitting quantitative synthesis of peripheral BDNF outcomes in humans. Thus, any claims about increases or decreases in serum/plasma BDNF following these agents remain unsubstantiated by extractable comparative human data in this review.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Within the scope of this review (71 records screened; 0 included), there is no directly extractable human evidence to quantify the effect of ketamine, esketamine, LSD, psilocybin, ayahuasca, DMT, MDMA, scopolamine, or rapastinel on peripheral BDNF (serum or plasma) relative to baseline or placebo/control. We cannot conclude whether these agents increase, decrease, or do not change circulating BDNF at any timepoint. Any assertion regarding peripheral BDNF modulation by psychoplastogens would be speculative based on the evidence available to this review.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations: The analysis relied on a local corpus; it is possible that relevant primary studies exist outside this source, were indexed under unqueried synonyms (e.g., proprietary names, alternative agent descriptors, or biomarker terminology), or were missed due to title/abstract screening constraints. We did not assess any full texts, limiting opportunities to identify eligible biomarker outcomes reported only in the methods or results sections. No contact with authors was attempted to obtain unreported BDNF data. As with any biomarker-focused review, decisions about eligible matrices (serum/plasma), comparators, and prespecification of agents may have excluded otherwise informative studies.

Study-level limitations (anticipated): Even if studies had been eligible, peripheral BDNF is a challenging endpoint. Pre-analytical variables (serum vs plasma, anticoagulant choice, platelet activation, processing delays, storage conditions), analytical variability (assay platform, calibration, lot-to-lot differences), and participant-level confounders (diurnal rhythm, fasting, recent physical activity, BMI, smoking, medications, menstrual phase) can substantially affect measured concentrations. Psychoactive interventions introduce additional issues: unblinding, expectancy effects, and co-interventions (e.g., psychotherapy integrated with dosing) that may independently influence BDNF.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: There is no evidence base here to support measuring peripheral BDNF to guide or monitor treatment with the specified psychoplastogens. Clinicians should not use serum or plasma BDNF as a biomarker for predicting response, tracking efficacy, or making dosing decisions for ketamine/esketamine, classic psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD), ayahuasca/DMT, MDMA, scopolamine, or rapastinel. Patient counseling should avoid claims that these treatments reliably elevate circulating BDNF, pending robust human data.

For research: There is a clear need for well-powered, preregistered trials that prespecify peripheral BDNF as an outcome, include appropriate controls (placebo or active comparator), and collect samples at standardized timepoints capturing acute (e.g., 30–240 minutes), subacute (24–72 hours), and sustained (1–8 weeks) windows. Key design elements include:
- Standardization of matrix (serum vs plasma) with detailed pre-analytical protocols; consideration of both matrices to disentangle platelet contributions; blinded laboratory analysis with validated assays.
- Adequate sample sizes powered to detect small-to-moderate effects (e.g., SMD ~0.3–0.5), with crossover designs for short-acting agents (ketamine, DMT) and parallel-group designs for long-acting agents (psilocybin, LSD).
- Inclusion of both healthy volunteers and clinical populations, with stratified or adjusted analyses for sex, age, BMI, smoking, exercise, menstrual phase, and concomitant medications; explicit management of psychotherapy co-interventions.
- Transparent reporting of absolute concentrations, change scores, variance (SD/SE), and correlation structures (for crossover designs), enabling future meta-analysis; public sharing of de-identified data.
- Examination of dose, route, and agent class as moderators; exploration of clinical correlates (e.g., depressive symptom change) and genetic moderators (e.g., BDNF Val66Met), with prespecified analysis plans.
- Adherence to CONSORT and biomarker reporting standards; publication of null findings to reduce reporting bias.

Until such data are available, mechanistic narratives linking psychoplastogens to peripheral BDNF changes should be treated as hypotheses rather than established facts.
