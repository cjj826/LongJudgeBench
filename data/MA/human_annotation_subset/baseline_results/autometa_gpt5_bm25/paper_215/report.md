## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
We identified 158 records, screened all titles/abstracts, and included 23 observational studies after full-text assessment. Most were cohort studies (prospective where stated), with two cross-sectional analyses. Publication years spanned 2009–2025. Sixteen studies provided quantitative estimates that could be synthesized; seven lacked computable data. Participant counts were not consistently reported in the extraction (N=NR across studies), precluding a reliable total sample size. PFOS exposure was typically assessed in maternal or cord blood during pregnancy; the outcome was birth weight, though several studies reported results on non-gram scales (e.g., standardized units or risk ratios for low birth weight), complicating comparability.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The main quantitative finding is that, under a random-effects model, higher PFOS exposure was not significantly associated with lower birth weight. The pooled mean difference was -0.0380 (95% CI: -0.3355 to 0.2595; p=0.8021) across 16 studies with extractable data. Interpreted at face value, this estimate is near the null and imprecise, and we cannot rule out small decreases or increases in birth weight. A fixed-effects model yielded a small inverse estimate, -0.0712 (95% CI: -0.1120 to -0.0304), suggesting a very modest adverse association under the assumption of a common true effect. Given the considerable heterogeneity (I²=86.5%; τ²=0.1448), the random-effects result is more appropriate and indicates no consistent effect.

Individual study results were mixed and reflect differences in scale and precision. Several cohorts that reported effects in grams suggested inverse associations. For example, a 2009 prospective hospital-based cohort in Sapporo, Japan (“Prenatal exposure to PFOS/PFOA and birth size in a hospital-based cohort in Sapporo, Japan”) reported a mean difference of -148.8 g (95% CI: -297.0 to -0.5), consistent with lower birth weight at higher PFOS exposure. A 2012 cohort reported -140.0 g (95% CI: -238.0 to -42.0), and a 2023 prospective cohort reported -89.13 g (95% CI: -166.84 to -11.42). Other cohort estimates in grams were smaller and not statistically significant, such as -50.0 g (95% CI: -123.0 to 23.0) in a 2016 cohort. Several studies reported associations on non-gram scales; for instance, a 2017 cohort reported a beta of -0.10 (95% CI: -0.34 to 0.13) on a standardized scale, while a 2021 cohort reported -0.27 (95% CI: -0.51 to -0.02), suggesting a modest adverse association in standardized units. Two prospective cohorts reported risk ratios for adverse birth size outcomes (e.g., low birth weight), including 1.53 (95% CI: 1.18 to 1.99) in a 2020 study and 1.2528 (95% CI: 1.10 to 11.50) in a 2016 study; these are not directly comparable to a mean difference in grams and likely contributed to heterogeneity when synthesized.

Heterogeneity is substantial and plausibly driven by several factors. Exposure assessment varied (maternal versus cord serum; timing across trimesters), as did exposure scaling (e.g., per doubling, per IQR, log-transformed), and whether co-exposures (notably PFOA) were included in the model. The magnitude of PFOS exposure also varied across calendar time and populations; earlier cohorts (e.g., circa 2009–2012) tended to report larger inverse associations in grams (-148.8 g, -140.0 g), which could reflect higher exposure levels or different confounding structures. Outcome modeling differed: some studies analyzed continuous birth weight in grams, others reported standardized scores or dichotomous outcomes (low birth weight/SGA), and model adjustment sets varied (e.g., inclusion of gestational age, which can be on the causal pathway). These differences, along with potential sex-specific effects that were not uniformly reported, likely underlie the high I².

Risk of bias was not formally assessed in this review, but typical concerns apply. Residual confounding by maternal smoking, parity, pre-pregnancy BMI, socioeconomic factors, and co-exposures is likely. Exposure misclassification is possible due to single-time-point measurement and laboratory variability. Selection bias (e.g., recruitment from hospital-based samples) and differences in covariate adjustment across studies can distort estimates. The lack of consistent reporting of sample sizes and effect scaling limits our ability to judge precision and assess small-study effects or publication bias.

Author conclusions extracted from individual studies were not consistently available in the dataset; where interpretable from the effect estimates, they ranged from null to modest inverse associations. For example, studies reporting -140 g to -149 g differences likely concluded an inverse association between prenatal PFOS and birth weight, whereas cohorts with near-zero standardized betas (e.g., -0.10, 95% CI: -0.34 to 0.13) likely concluded no meaningful association. Studies reporting elevated risk ratios for low birth weight (e.g., 1.53, 95% CI: 1.18 to 1.99) likely inferred increased risk of adverse size outcomes at higher PFOS exposure. The pooled random-effects result reconciles these divergent findings as overall null, with high between-study variability and possible context-specific effects.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 23 observational studies (16 with quantitative data), there is no consistent evidence that higher prenatal PFOS exposure is associated with clinically meaningful reductions in birth weight. The random-effects pooled estimate is near null (-0.0380; 95% CI: -0.3355 to 0.2595; p=0.8021), with considerable heterogeneity (I²=86.5%). Some cohorts reported moderate inverse associations in grams (approximately -90 g to -150 g), while others were null or used non-gram scales, resulting in mixed findings overall.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include incomplete extraction of essential study characteristics (notably sample sizes and, in many cases, study identities), lack of a formal risk-of-bias assessment, and inclusion of effect estimates expressed on different scales (mean differences in grams, standardized units, and risk ratios). Although the planned effect measure was mean difference in grams, the underlying heterogeneity in scaling and outcome definitions likely reduced the interpretability of the pooled estimate. The high I² and the discrepancy between fixed-effects (small, statistically significant inverse association) and random-effects models underscore instability of the summary effect.

Study-level limitations likely include residual confounding (maternal smoking, BMI, parity, socioeconomic status), exposure misclassification (single-time-point PFOS measurements, differing matrices and timings), and inconsistent adjustment for potential mediators (e.g., gestational age). Co-exposure to other PFAS (e.g., PFOA) and mixtures complicates attribution to PFOS. Differences in exposure levels across cohorts and calendar periods, potential nonlinearity, and effect modification (e.g., by infant sex) were not uniformly assessed. Hospital-based sampling in some cohorts may limit generalizability.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice and policy: Current evidence does not support changes to individual clinical management of pregnant women based solely on PFOS levels with respect to birth weight. However, given broader toxicological and epidemiological concerns about PFAS, general exposure reduction policies remain prudent from a public health perspective. Clinicians should continue standard risk assessment for fetal growth without relying on PFOS measures as a determinant of birth weight.

For research: Harmonization is needed. Future studies should report effects in grams per clearly defined exposure increment (e.g., per doubling of PFOS), alongside standardized units when used, to facilitate synthesis. Pre-registration of analyses, standardized covariate adjustment sets (with sensitivity analyses excluding potential mediators like gestational age), and consistent handling of co-exposures are essential. Large, contemporary, multi-cohort studies or individual participant data meta-analyses should examine dose–response, nonlinearity, critical windows of exposure, mixture effects, and sex-specific associations. Transparent reporting of sample sizes, precision, and model specification, along with exploration of heterogeneity sources, will improve the interpretability and credibility of future syntheses.
