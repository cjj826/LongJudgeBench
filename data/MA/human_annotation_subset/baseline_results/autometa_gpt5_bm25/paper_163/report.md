### 7.2 Discussion of Findings

7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

7.1 Summary of Evidence
We included eight observational studies published between 2012 and 2015, spanning cohort designs (prospective and retrospective) and one population-based nested case–control study. The primary effect measure was the hazard ratio (HR). Sample sizes were incompletely reported: one 2012 cohort enrolled 30,606 bisphosphonate users and 124,424 non-users, while the remaining studies reported sample sizes as not reported (NR). Across the eight studies, individual HRs ranged from 0.56 to 1.69. A random-effects meta-analysis yielded a pooled HR of 0.85 (95% CI 0.74 to 0.97; p=0.0139), with considerable heterogeneity (I²=85.2%; τ²=0.027). A fixed-effect model produced an HR of 0.83 (95% CI 0.80 to 0.87).

7.2 Discussion of Findings
The main quantitative finding is a modest association between bisphosphonate use and lower overall cancer risk compared with non-use (random-effects pooled HR 0.85). On an absolute scale, this would correspond to a small relative reduction in incidence, but the precision and interpretation are limited by substantial between-study heterogeneity. Individual study estimates varied: several cohorts suggested lower risk (e.g., HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.60 to 0.79 in the large 2012 cohort; HR 0.87, 95% CI 0.82 to 0.92; HR 0.78, 95% CI 0.65 to 0.94), two were statistically compatible with no difference (HR 0.88, 95% CI 0.72 to 1.07; HR 0.80, 95% CI 0.64 to 1.00; HR 1.04, 95% CI 0.82 to 1.33), and one study reported increased risk (HR 1.69, 95% CI 1.39 to 2.04). The latter indicates that an elevated risk cannot be excluded in some contexts or for certain cancer sites. The fixed-effect estimate (HR 0.83, 95% CI 0.80 to 0.87) suggests a protective association under a common-effect assumption, but the high I² indicates that a common effect is unlikely; thus, the random-effects summary is more appropriate but also more uncertain and context-dependent.

Potential sources of heterogeneity include differences in: (1) outcome definitions (mixing all-cancer incidence with site-specific cancers such as colorectal, breast, endometrial, liver, or pancreatic cancer), (2) exposure definitions (ever vs current use; oral vs intravenous bisphosphonates; individual agents), (3) dose/duration and latency windows, (4) underlying populations (osteoporosis patients vs broader general-population samples), and (5) adjustment sets and analytic designs (e.g., handling of time-varying exposure and immortal time). The single substantially increased HR (1.69) may reflect a specific cancer site, a subpopulation at elevated baseline risk, or methodological bias; without harmonized outcome categories and study-level covariates, we could not explore this heterogeneity further.

All included studies were observational. Confounding by indication (e.g., differences in age, sex, bone health, comorbidities), healthy adherer effects, and screening behaviors (e.g., mammography or colonoscopy) are plausible threats to validity. For example, higher healthcare engagement among bisphosphonate users could either increase detection (biasing toward higher observed incidence) or be correlated with healthier behaviors (biasing toward lower incidence). Additionally, if exposure is mis-specified (e.g., not using new-user designs or not modeling time-varying drug use), immortal time bias can artifactually lower cancer risk among “users.” The diversity of point estimates—especially the presence of both protective and harmful associations—underscores the likelihood that residual confounding and design differences are material.

Author conclusions from the original reports were not available in the extracted data, so we could not directly compare our pooled findings with the studies’ stated interpretations. Based on the direction and precision of the reported HRs, several studies likely concluded no clear association or a modest reduction in risk, while at least one likely reported an increased risk in its particular setting. The discordance among studies suggests that site-specific effects, exposure details, and methodological rigor may strongly influence observed associations.

Robustness is limited. The statistically significant random-effects summary (p=0.0139) indicates that, on average, bisphosphonate use is associated with lower overall cancer risk; however, the high heterogeneity and lack of risk-of-bias assessment reduce confidence in a causal interpretation. The fixed-effect and random-effects estimates are directionally consistent (HR 0.83 vs 0.85), but the heterogeneity argues against overreliance on the fixed-effect result. Without stratified meta-analyses by cancer site, exposure duration, or design quality, we cannot determine whether the observed average effect is driven by specific cancers or study features.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

8. Conclusions and Implications

8.1 Main Conclusions
Across eight observational studies (2012–2015), bisphosphonate use was associated with a modestly lower overall risk of cancer compared with non-use (random-effects pooled HR 0.85, 95% CI 0.74 to 0.97), but with considerable heterogeneity (I²=85.2%) and no formal risk-of-bias assessment. These findings suggest that bisphosphonates are unlikely to increase overall cancer risk and may be associated with a small reduction in risk, though causality and site-specific effects remain uncertain.

8.2 Limitations
Study-level limitations include observational designs vulnerable to confounding by indication, healthy-user effects, and differential screening; potential immortal time bias and exposure misclassification; heterogeneity in outcome definitions (all-cancer vs specific sites) and exposure metrics (agent, route, dose, duration); and incomplete reporting of key covariates and sample sizes. The presence of at least one study with increased risk (HR 1.69, 95% CI 1.39 to 2.04) indicates that specific contexts or cancer types may diverge from the overall average.

Review-level limitations include incomplete metadata (most studies reported sample size as NR, limiting calculation of a precise total sample), absence of extracted author conclusions for triangulation, and no risk-of-bias assessment, which constrains certainty grading. We pooled across mixed outcomes and designs due to limited detail, likely inflating heterogeneity and obscuring site-specific patterns. We lacked data to perform subgroup analyses (e.g., by cancer site or duration of use), sensitivity analyses (e.g., excluding high-risk-of-bias studies), or publication-bias assessments.

8.3 Implications
For practice: Current evidence does not support using bisphosphonates to prevent cancer. The pooled association suggests no increase in overall cancer risk and possibly a small reduction, which is reassuring for patients receiving bisphosphonates for approved indications (e.g., osteoporosis). However, given heterogeneity and potential biases, clinicians should not infer a protective anticancer effect nor alter prescribing solely on anticipated cancer risk modification. Counseling should focus on established benefits and risks of bisphosphonates for bone health.

For research: Future studies should use new-user, active-comparator cohort designs with robust control for confounding (including bone health, comorbidities, concomitant medications, and screening behaviors), time-varying exposure modeling, and clearly defined latency windows. Standardized, site-specific cancer outcomes with adequate follow-up are essential, as are dose–response analyses and stratification by bisphosphonate agent and route. Large, population-based datasets with linkage to cancer registries can support these aims. Meta-analyses should prespecify cancer-site strata, include formal risk-of-bias assessments, and, where possible, incorporate individual participant data to harmonize exposure/outcome definitions and adjustment sets. Clarifying whether any cancer-site–specific risks exist—especially in settings that yielded elevated HRs—should be a priority.
