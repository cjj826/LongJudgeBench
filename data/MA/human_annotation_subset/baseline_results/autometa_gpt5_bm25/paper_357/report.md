## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Fifteen postmortem case–control studies met inclusion criteria, spanning publications from 1986 to 2014 (with several undated), all cross-sectional and focused on neuroinflammatory features in schizophrenia versus non-psychiatric controls. Designs ranged from immunohistochemical and morphometric assessments to molecular analyses; two studies incorporated parallel rodent experiments. Reported sample sizes were incomplete for many studies; among those reporting, there were at least 166 schizophrenia cases and 179 controls (e.g., 62/62, 57/57, 15/15, 12/10, 11/20, 9/15). Outcomes encompassed glial and neuroimmune markers, including microglial markers, glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP), glial cell densities, cytokines, arachidonic cascade markers, and selected proteins such as substance P, SERPINA3, and IFITM. Effect estimates were heterogeneously presented: several studies provided non-computable results; a minority reported numeric effects, sometimes as standardized mean differences (SMDs), and sometimes as large unscaled contrasts that could not be harmonized.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Across the included literature, the balance of computable effects is consistent with dysregulation of neuroinflammatory pathways in schizophrenia, but the direction and magnitude vary substantially by study and (presumably) by marker and brain region. Large positive case–control differences were reported in a 2000 study (SMD 3.5195, 95% CI 2.0410 to 4.9981; sample size not reported) and a 2014 study (SMD 2.9780; sample size not reported), indicating markedly higher levels of at least some neuroinflammatory markers in schizophrenia than in controls. Two larger studies reported extremely large positive effects (114.0000 with N=57/57; 379.0000 with N=62/62), but the scale and definition of these contrasts were not standardized to SMD units, limiting interpretability and comparability. Conversely, one 2002 morphometric study (N=9/15) reported a large negative effect (SMD −32.0000), implying lower levels in cases for the outcome assessed. Most other studies did not yield computable contrasts from the available extraction.

The heterogeneity is substantial and likely multi-factorial. First, outcomes targeted different facets of neuroinflammation (e.g., microglial markers, GFAP expression, cell densities, cytokines, arachidonic cascade components, and proteins such as SERPINA3 and IFITM). Second, brain regions and cell compartments almost certainly varied across studies, which is critical given regional specificity in glial phenotypes and inflammatory signaling. Third, methodological diversity (immunohistochemistry vs. qPCR vs. radioligand or protein assays; qualitative vs. quantitative morphometry) and variable normalization procedures complicate synthesis. The presence of implausibly large “SMDs” (e.g., 114 and 379) suggests either unit mislabeling or effect measures that are not directly comparable to SMDs, reinforcing the decision not to pool. Only one study reported a confidence interval (SMD 3.5195, 95% CI 2.0410 to 4.9981), precluding formal heterogeneity statistics or sensitivity analyses.

Risk of bias was not formally assessed in this review, and author conclusions were not captured for most studies in the extraction. This limits our ability to compare study authors’ interpretations with the numerical results. Nonetheless, the pattern of large positive case–control differences in several studies suggests that some neuroinflammatory readouts are elevated in schizophrenia postmortem tissue. At the same time, the presence of a large negative result (−32.0000) indicates that specific markers or compartments may show reductions, potentially reflecting compensatory changes, region-specific effects, or methodological factors. Routine postmortem confounders—antipsychotic exposure, smoking, agonal state, brain pH, postmortem interval (PMI), comorbid medical illnesses, and cause of death—can influence glial and immune marker expression and may differ across case–control groups, affecting direction and magnitude of effects.

Given these constraints, the robustness of any single estimate is low without consistent replication across the same marker and region using comparable methods and thorough adjustment for confounders. Fixed- versus random-effects modeling was not attempted due to the lack of harmonized effect sizes and the small number of studies with extractable variance. The overall narrative indicates neuroinflammatory dysregulation in schizophrenia but underscores that the size and even direction of changes likely depend on which marker and brain region are assessed, and on methodological and clinical covariates.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 15 postmortem case–control studies (≥166 schizophrenia cases and 179 controls with reported Ns), there is evidence consistent with dysregulation of neuroinflammatory processes in schizophrenia, with several studies reporting large case–control differences favoring higher levels in schizophrenia (e.g., SMD 3.5195, 95% CI 2.0410 to 4.9981; SMD 2.9780). However, results are heterogeneous, including at least one large negative contrast (−32.0000) and multiple non-computable or non-standardized effect estimates (e.g., 114.0000; 379.0000). Given incomplete reporting, lack of pooled analysis, and no formal risk-of-bias assessment, the strength of evidence is low to moderate at best, and conclusions must be considered provisional and marker- and region-dependent.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include incomplete extraction of study-level data (notably missing sample sizes for many studies, absent variance measures for nearly all, and lack of author conclusions), which prevented meta-analysis and formal heterogeneity estimation. The inconsistency in effect metrics—with some labeled as SMD and others clearly on different scales—precluded harmonization. We did not perform a formal risk-of-bias assessment, reducing our ability to contextualize findings with respect to selection, measurement, and confounding biases.

Study-level limitations, inferred from typical postmortem research constraints and sparse reporting, likely include: cross-sectional designs vulnerable to confounding; variable PMI, brain pH, and agonal factors; differences in antipsychotic exposure, smoking, and comorbidities; region- and layer-specific sampling that may not be comparable across studies; small sample sizes in several analyses; and heterogeneous assays (IHC, morphometry, mRNA/protein quantification) with differing normalization and thresholds. These factors can inflate variance, bias effect estimates, or even reverse directions of association when not balanced or adjusted.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice and policy, postmortem evidence of neuroinflammatory dysregulation in schizophrenia does not translate directly to clinical decision-making. The current body of evidence does not support using specific neuroinflammatory markers (e.g., GFAP, microglial markers, cytokines, arachidonic cascade components, SERPINA3, IFITM) as diagnostic, prognostic, or treatment-monitoring tools in routine care. Any clinical extrapolation would be premature without in vivo corroboration (e.g., imaging, peripheral biomarkers) and standardized postmortem-in vivo bridging studies.

For research, priorities include:
- Standardized protocols for tissue selection, region matching, covariate capture (antipsychotics, smoking, PMI, pH, cause of death), and assay methods, with pre-registered analysis plans.
- Harmonized reporting of effect sizes with measures of variance, enabling meta-analysis (e.g., SMDs with 95% CIs for each marker and region).
- Replication across multiple brain regions and cell compartments to resolve marker- and region-specific patterns, with particular attention to glial markers (e.g., GFAP, microglial activation indices), cytokine panels, and pathways such as arachidonic metabolism, as well as proteins like SERPINA3 and IFITM.
- Larger, multi-brain-bank studies to improve power and generalizability, ideally integrating multi-omic layers (transcriptomic, proteomic) with quantitative histology.
- Sensitivity analyses to parse medication effects and confounding, and parallel in vivo studies to establish translational relevance.

Overall, the field would benefit from collaborative data-sharing consortia to reanalyze raw data under common pipelines, which could substantially clarify the magnitude and consistency of neuroinflammatory alterations in schizophrenia.
