## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
We included 13 pre–post (within-subject) cohorts published between 2010 and 2025 that evaluated physiologic esophageal outcomes before and after laparoscopic vertical sleeve gastrectomy (LVSG) in patients with morbid obesity. Designs were predominantly prospective before–after single-arm cohorts; two were retrospective pre–post within-subject cohorts. Sample sizes where reported ranged from 15 to 73 participants per study (e.g., N=20 in a 2010 prospective before–after study; N=73 in a 2017 observational cohort; N=15 in a 2025 prospective cohort). Outcomes targeted included lower esophageal sphincter pressure (LESP), lower esophageal sphincter length (LESL), DeMeester score (DMS), and BMI. Five studies contributed computable data to a quantitative synthesis using standardized mean differences (SMDs); eight studies were narratively described but lacked extractable pre–post statistics. No formal risk-of-bias assessments were available.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The primary quantitative finding was that, under a random-effects model, the pooled standardized mean difference across five studies was -0.5044 (95% CI: -2.5165 to 1.5077; p=0.6232), indicating no statistically significant overall change in the composite of esophageal physiologic measures after LVSG. Heterogeneity was considerable (I²=95.5%; τ²=4.386), indicating that study effects varied widely beyond chance and limiting the interpretability of a single pooled estimate across disparate physiologic endpoints. By contrast, a fixed-effects model suggested a statistically significant negative summary effect (SMD -0.8871; 95% CI: -1.2524 to -0.5219), but in the setting of extreme heterogeneity the random-effects model is more appropriate, and the fixed-effects estimate is likely overly precise and potentially misleading.

Direction and magnitude of individual study effects were inconsistent. Among the five meta-analyzed cohorts:
- A 2021 prospective cohort reported a large negative SMD of -2.6434 (95% CI: -3.2089 to -2.0778), indicating a marked postoperative reduction in the targeted metric.
- A 2010 before–after study (N=20) suggested little to no change (SMD -0.5114; 95% CI: -1.1412 to 0.1185).
- A 2017 cohort (N=73) reported a positive SMD of 1.7566 (95% CI: 2.6331 to 12.7448 as provided), implying a substantial postoperative increase in the measured parameter.
- A 2025 prospective cohort (N=15) also suggested a postoperative increase (SMD 1.6275; 95% CI: 0.4957 to 52.2852 as provided).
- Another 2017 cohort (N=18) reported a large negative SMD of -4.0000 (95% CI: -8.3000 to 0.2000).

Because individual studies assessed different physiologic outcomes (e.g., LESP vs DMS) with nonuniform “beneficial” directions (higher LESP may be favorable; lower DMS indicates less reflux), the sign of the SMD is not uniformly interpretable as benefit or harm when pooled. This mixing of outcome constructs likely contributes materially to the very high heterogeneity and cautions against clinically overinterpreting the combined effect as evidence of overall improvement or worsening after LVSG.

Narratively, author interpretations, where reported, also diverged. Studies with positive SMDs (e.g., the 2017 N=73 and 2025 N=15 cohorts) typically concluded that LVSG was associated with physiologic changes consistent with increased reflux burden (e.g., higher DMS or impaired antireflux barrier), plausibly related to altered sleeve geometry, increased intragastric pressure, or hiatal factors. Conversely, studies with large negative SMDs (e.g., 2021; 2017 N=18) concluded that physiologic parameters improved postoperatively, often attributing this to substantial BMI reduction and decreased intra-abdominal pressure. The 2010 small study (N=20) reported no material change. These divergent author conclusions mirror the quantitative inconsistency and suggest that local factors (operative technique, bougie size, antral preservation, concomitant hiatal hernia repair, timing of postoperative testing, and baseline GERD phenotype) may drive study-level differences more than LVSG per se.

The robustness of the synthesis is constrained. Only five of the 13 cohorts provided computable data; we could not perform subgroup analyses by specific endpoint (LESP, LESL, DMS) or by timing of assessment. The discrepancy between fixed- and random-effects estimates underscores model sensitivity in the presence of extreme heterogeneity and limited contributing studies. Publication bias could not be assessed. Although BMI likely decreased substantially across cohorts, extractable BMI change data were insufficient to meta-analyze or to test correlations between weight loss magnitude and physiologic outcomes.

In sum, across small, heterogeneous pre–post cohorts, we did not find statistically significant evidence under random effects that LVSG reliably improves or worsens esophageal physiologic measures. Individual studies report both clinically meaningful worsening and improvement; the balance likely reflects methodological and clinical heterogeneity rather than a uniform physiologic effect of LVSG.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on 13 pre–post cohorts (five with computable data), evidence is very uncertain regarding the effect of LVSG on esophageal physiologic measures (LESP, LESL, DMS). The random-effects pooled SMD was -0.5044 (95% CI: -2.5165 to 1.5077; p=0.6232) with extreme heterogeneity (I²=95.5%). We cannot conclude with confidence that LVSG consistently improves or worsens esophageal physiology; findings vary across studies and likely hinge on patient selection, operative technique, and measurement protocols.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations:
- Data sparseness: Only 5/13 studies contributed extractable statistics; many reported outcomes without sufficient pre–post variance data, precluding comprehensive meta-analysis or planned subgroup analyses by endpoint (LESP vs DMS) or by timing post-surgery.
- Outcome mixing: Pooling across different physiologic constructs with opposing “beneficial” directions (e.g., higher LESP vs lower DMS) reduces clinical interpretability and inflates heterogeneity. We lacked standardized coding to harmonize directions across outcomes.
- No risk-of-bias assessments were available for included studies, limiting our ability to evaluate internal validity and to perform sensitivity analyses by risk domains. Publication bias could not be assessed.

Study-level limitations commonly observed across the included literature:
- Nonrandomized, single-arm pre–post designs are susceptible to regression to the mean, maturation effects, and confounding (e.g., concurrent hiatal hernia repair, PPI use, or behavioral changes postoperatively).
- Small sample sizes (N=15–73), variable and sometimes short follow-up, and heterogeneity in measurement methods (conventional manometry vs high-resolution, 24-hour pH-metry vs MII-pH) and operative technique (bougie size, antral resection extent, hiatal repair) likely account for between-study differences.
- Incomplete reporting (missing standard deviations/standard errors) limited effect-size computation; few studies examined or reported the relationship between BMI change and physiologic outcomes.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice:
- Current evidence does not support assuming uniform improvement or deterioration in objective esophageal physiology after LVSG. Given the inconsistency and wide uncertainty, clinicians should counsel patients that postoperative reflux physiology may improve, worsen, or remain unchanged.
- In patients with significant pre-existing GERD or hiatal hernia, careful preoperative evaluation (symptom assessment, endoscopy, and physiologic testing when indicated) and surgical planning are prudent. Postoperative surveillance should be individualized, especially if symptoms emerge or persist. The present synthesis does not establish superiority or inferiority of LVSG for reflux physiology and should not be used to justify or contraindicate LVSG on this basis alone.

For research:
- Priorities include larger, prospective cohorts with standardized protocols: prespecified primary endpoints (e.g., LESP on HRM and DMS on 24-hour pH), uniform direction coding, and fixed time points (e.g., 6 and 12 months) with transparent reporting of means, variances, and within-subject change.
- Studies should stratify by operative technique (bougie size, antral length, fundus mobilization, hiatal repair), baseline GERD phenotype, and presence of hiatal hernia; and examine dose–response relationships between BMI change and physiologic outcomes.
- Comparative designs (e.g., pragmatic trials or well-matched cohorts) contrasting LVSG techniques and adjuncts (e.g., systematic hiatoplasty) would clarify modifiable drivers of postoperative physiology. Pre-registered protocols, blinded outcome assessment, and complete case reporting are needed to reduce bias and enable future meta-analysis.
