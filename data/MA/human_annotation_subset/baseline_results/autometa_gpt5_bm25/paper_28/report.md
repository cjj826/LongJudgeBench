## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
From 115 records screened (title/abstract), two primary studies met the inclusion criteria for synchronous, real-time remote weight management among adults with BMI ≥27.5 kg/m², with 113 records excluded at screening and no exclusions at full text (final included n=2). The two studies were single-arm interventional designs published in 2019 and 2021, respectively: one pragmatic nonrandomized single-arm study (N=37) and one single-arm interventional study (N=53), for a combined sample of 90 participants. Neither study provided extractable quantitative effect sizes for engagement-related barriers or facilitators, and no comparative or multivariable data were available to compute odds ratios (ORs) or confidence intervals. As a result, only narrative synthesis was possible, and planned quantitative pooling could not be performed.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The principal finding of this review is the paucity of extractable evidence on barriers and facilitators to engagement with synchronous remote weight management interventions in adults with excess weight or obesity. Although two small single-arm studies (2019: N=37; 2021: N=53) met eligibility criteria, neither contributed quantifiable engagement outcomes (e.g., standardized attendance, retention, adherence, or participation metrics) nor comparative analyses linking specific barriers/facilitators to engagement. Consequently, the pre-specified effect measure (OR) was not computable, and no meta-analysis could be conducted. The immediate implication is that the strength, direction, and magnitude of any association between capability-, opportunity-, or motivation-related factors (COM-B framework) and engagement with synchronous remote interventions remain indeterminate in this dataset.

Heterogeneity could not be formally assessed, but contextual variability likely exists. The publication years (2019 vs 2021) straddle the COVID-19 pandemic onset, a period associated with rapid shifts in telehealth delivery and uptake. If one study was conducted pre-pandemic and the other during the pandemic, differences in digital access, platform familiarity, and service organization could have influenced both barriers (e.g., technology access, bandwidth) and facilitators (e.g., increased comfort with video calls). However, the included records did not describe the intervention modalities (e.g., one-to-one vs group sessions; telephone vs video), session frequency or duration, or participant support for technology onboarding—factors that typically shape engagement. Without standardized reporting of these elements, heterogeneity in implementation and measurement of engagement cannot be disentangled.

Risk of bias considerations weigh heavily on interpretation. Both studies used single-arm designs without comparators, raising concerns about selection bias, confounding, and regression to the mean. Small sample sizes (N=37 and N=53) limit precision and the ability to characterize subgroup differences (e.g., by age, sex, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, or baseline digital literacy). The lack of reported analytic detail (e.g., handling of missing data, definitions of engagement, or adjusted analyses) further impedes inference. Reporting bias is plausible: without prespecified engagement outcomes or protocol registration (not reported in the extracted data), selective outcome reporting cannot be ruled out.

Robustness checks (e.g., fixed vs random effects) were infeasible given the absence of computable effect sizes and the heterogeneity of unreported intervention components. Critically, mapping to COM-B requires explicit identification of barriers (e.g., limited digital literacy, scheduling constraints, privacy concerns) and facilitators (e.g., technical support, flexible scheduling, motivational interviewing) with evidence of their association with engagement outcomes. The included studies, as extracted, did not present such data, precluding a meaningful COM-B synthesis. We also sought to compare the studies’ author-stated conclusions with our findings; however, author conclusions were not available in the extracted dataset. Therefore, we cannot determine whether the primary investigators reported feasibility, acceptability, or engagement outcomes aligned with our PICO nor whether their interpretations supported any particular barriers or facilitators.

In summary, despite identifying two eligible studies totaling 90 participants, the current evidence base does not allow estimation of the strength of association between specific barriers/facilitators and engagement with synchronous remote weight management interventions in adults with BMI ≥27.5 kg/m². The observed evidence gap likely reflects both limited primary research in this specific modality and suboptimal reporting of engagement constructs.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
The available evidence is insufficient to determine which capability-, opportunity-, or motivation-related factors facilitate or hinder engagement with synchronous remote weight management interventions in adults with BMI ≥27.5 kg/m². Two small single-arm studies (2019, N=37; 2021, N=53) lacked extractable engagement data and did not permit computation of effect sizes or mapping to COM-B. No confident, evidence-based recommendations can be made regarding barriers or facilitators specific to synchronous remote weight management.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on a local corpus and the information available in extracted records. Although 115 records were screened, most were excluded for not focusing on weight management or being non-primary research (e.g., systematic reviews). For the two included studies, essential details (author conclusions, engagement definitions, data for effect size calculation, and risk-of-bias elements) were not accessible, constraining synthesis. We pre-specified ORs as effect measures, but the absence of comparative analyses made this approach infeasible.

Study-level limitations are substantial. Both included studies used single-arm designs without control groups, had small samples (N=37 and N=53), and provided limited methodological detail. Engagement was not reported in standardized, extractable ways, preventing quantification and COM-B mapping. Potential sources of bias include selection bias, confounding, missing data, and reporting bias. Lack of detail on the synchronous modality (telephone vs video), frequency/intensity, participant support (e.g., technical onboarding), and contextual factors (e.g., pandemic-related service changes) further limits interpretability and generalizability.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: There is no robust, direct evidence from these studies to guide clinicians or program designers on which specific factors most influence engagement in synchronous remote weight management for adults with elevated BMI. Practitioners should therefore rely on general telehealth best practices (e.g., ensure technology access and literacy support, flexible scheduling, privacy assurance, culturally responsive communication) while monitoring engagement locally and adapting iteratively. However, such actions are not substantiated by effect estimates in this review.

For research: Future studies should explicitly measure and report engagement outcomes using standardized definitions (e.g., session attendance rate, attrition/retention, adherence to intervention components) and link them to a priori barriers/facilitators mapped to COM-B (capability: digital literacy, health literacy; opportunity: device/internet access, scheduling, environment; motivation: autonomous motivation, self-efficacy). Recommended designs include:
- Prospective, adequately powered comparative studies (e.g., randomized or well-controlled cohorts) with multivariable modeling to estimate adjusted associations (reporting ORs/95% CIs) between candidate barriers/facilitators and engagement.
- Mixed-methods process evaluations to elucidate mechanisms and contextual determinants, especially across diverse populations and settings.
- Transparent reporting (protocol registration, prespecified outcomes, handling of missing data), and inclusion of equity-relevant variables (age, gender, race/ethnicity, socioeconomic status, rurality) to detect disparities (e.g., digital divide).
- Detailed intervention descriptions (synchronous modality, frequency/dose, group vs individual delivery, coaching approaches) to enable replication and synthesis.

Until such data are available, any assertions about key barriers or facilitators for synchronous remote weight management in this population remain speculative.
