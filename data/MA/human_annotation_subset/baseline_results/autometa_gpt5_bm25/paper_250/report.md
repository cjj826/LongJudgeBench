### 7.2 Discussion of Findings

Because the 95% confidence interval excludes 0, this difference is statistically significant at the conventional two‑sided alpha of 0.05. On conventional benchmarks, an SMD of 0.65 indicates a moderate effect size, but the clinical meaning depends on the underlying instrument (e.g., MADRS or HDRS) and baseline severity—details that were not available. Moreover, our review’s primary outcome was response (typically defined as ≥50% symptom reduction), which is not directly inferable from the reported SMD without additional data (e.g., scale SDs, baseline distributions, or responder counts). Accordingly, while the direction of effect favors the active treatment on continuous symptoms, the evidence is insufficient to quantify effects on response rates as planned.

With only one study and inadequate reporting of study characteristics, between‑study heterogeneity cannot be assessed. Potential sources of heterogeneity that commonly influence antidepressant and neuromodulation trials in TRD include: variability in TRD definitions (e.g., two vs ≥3 failed trials), concomitant treatments (augmentation vs monotherapy), differences in scales and timing of outcome assessment, and distinctions between modalities (pharmacologic vs device-based). Because the corpus for this review encompassed 25 candidate treatments, high heterogeneity would be expected in a future meta‑analysis; however, the present dataset does not permit formal exploration of heterogeneity, subgroup effects, or meta‑regression.

For example, in antidepressant and neuromodulation RCTs, risks commonly arise from incomplete blinding (e.g., in device trials where sensory experiences differ between arms), selective outcome reporting (e.g., response vs continuous scales), attrition bias in longer trials, and small‑study effects. Without information on randomization procedures, concealment, blinding integrity, attrition rates, and outcome ascertainment, the internal validity of the reported effect remains uncertain.

Robustness checks were not feasible. With only one study, there is no meta‑analytic estimate, and fixed‑ versus random‑effects comparisons are inapplicable. The mismatch between our prespecified primary outcome (odds ratios for response) and the available effect measure (SMD for continuous symptoms) further constrains interpretability and limits comparability to prior TRD syntheses that report response/remission odds ratios.
