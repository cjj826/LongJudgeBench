### 7.2 Discussion of Findings

7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

7.1 Summary of Evidence
We included 12 comparative observational studies (prospective or retrospective cohorts, a nested case–control, and other observational designs) published from 2003 to 2025, comparing birth outcomes among offspring of women with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) versus those without PCOS. Sample sizes varied widely, from small hospital-based cohorts (e.g., 38/136 and 47/180) to a very large population-based cohort (390 PCOS vs 68,708 non-PCOS). Nine studies contributed computable effect estimates to a quantitative synthesis using odds ratios (ORs); three studies did not yield computable estimates. Some studies reported continuous outcomes (e.g., mean birthweight), while others reported dichotomous outcomes (e.g., preterm birth, low birth weight, small for gestational age [SGA], NICU admission, or perinatal mortality).

7.2 Discussion of Findings
Overall, pooling nine studies that reported dichotomous outcomes yielded a higher odds of adverse birth outcomes in offspring of women with PCOS: random-effects OR 1.484 (95% CI 1.081 to 2.038; p=0.0146). Given the range of outcomes encompassed across studies and the differences in outcome definitions, this pooled estimate should be interpreted as indicating a general elevation in risk across adverse perinatal endpoints rather than a specific effect on any single outcome. The observed association was statistically significant under a random-effects model, but the high between-study heterogeneity (I²=91.1%; τ²=0.147) suggests substantial variability in effect size magnitude and possibly outcome-specific differences that were not captured by our aggregate analysis.

The heterogeneity is plausibly explained by several factors. First, outcomes varied (preterm birth, SGA, low birth weight, NICU admission, perinatal death), and effects may differ by endpoint. Second, exposure definitions likely differed (e.g., varying diagnostic criteria for PCOS across eras and settings), and a proportion of PCOS pregnancies may have been conceived with assisted reproductive technologies (ART), which themselves influence perinatal risks. Third, confounding structures varied; maternal age, parity, BMI, metabolic comorbidities (insulin resistance, diabetes, hypertension), and smoking may not have been uniformly measured or adjusted, especially in older or smaller studies. Indeed, earlier small cohorts showed large and imprecise associations—for example, a 2003 retrospective cohort reported an OR of 1.426 with a very wide upper CI (95% CI 1.360 to 12.736), and a 2005 cohort reported OR 1.634 (95% CI 1.490 to 17.604)—consistent with outcome sparsity and residual confounding. A 2018 prospective cohort reported OR 4.20 (95% CI 0.70 to 7.70), again imprecise and consistent with both null and markedly elevated risk. In contrast, a very large 2024 cohort reported a lower mean birthweight in PCOS pregnancies (mean difference approximately −0.260; 95% CI −0.380 to −0.140), suggesting worse fetal growth, while a small 2025 prospective cohort also reported lower birthweight (effect −0.405; 95% CI −0.752 to −0.057). These continuous results, while not directly combinable with dichotomous outcomes, are directionally concordant with increased risks of SGA or low birth weight.

The robustness analysis comparing random- and fixed-effects models revealed meaningful model dependence. Under a fixed-effects model, the pooled OR was 1.084 (95% CI 1.021 to 1.151), indicating a modest but statistically significant 8% increase in odds. The larger random-effects estimate (OR 1.484) suggests that smaller or more heterogenous studies reported stronger associations than larger and more homogeneous ones. This discrepancy raises the possibility of small-study effects and/or publication bias, although we did not perform formal assessments (e.g., funnel plot or regression tests). Some extracted records showed internal inconsistencies between the point estimate and confidence intervals (e.g., an effect of 0.412 with a CI of 1.26 to 1.82), limiting their interpretability and potentially contributing to heterogeneity if included.

Author conclusions were not consistently available from our extraction. Nonetheless, the direction of reported effects generally aligned with a higher risk of adverse perinatal outcomes among offspring of women with PCOS, particularly for fetal growth metrics and composite adverse neonatal endpoints. A minority of studies—especially those with larger samples and more contemporary data—appeared to suggest a smaller magnitude of risk, which could reflect better confounding control (e.g., adjustment for BMI/metabolic status), improvements in obstetric care, or differences in PCOS phenotype and treatment. Without standardized outcome definitions and adjustment sets, it is difficult to reconcile these differences definitively.

Given the breadth of outcomes and data inconsistencies, outcome-specific meta-analyses (e.g., separate pooling for preterm birth, SGA, low birth weight, NICU admission, and perinatal mortality) would be preferable. Our aggregate estimate should be viewed as an indicative signal of increased risk, with the caveat that the magnitude likely varies by outcome and context (ART use, maternal metabolic health, and PCOS phenotype). Continuous outcomes (mean birthweight) were consistently lower in PCOS groups across two studies, which supports the plausibility of increased SGA/low birth weight risk, but the units and standardization were variably reported.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

8. Conclusions and Implications

8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 12 observational studies (2003–2025), offspring of women with PCOS experienced higher odds of adverse birth outcomes overall, with a pooled random-effects OR of 1.484 (95% CI 1.081 to 2.038; p=0.0146). However, heterogeneity was considerable (I²=91.1%), outcome definitions varied, and confounding may be substantial. Fixed-effects estimates suggested only a modest increase (OR 1.084; 95% CI 1.021 to 1.151). The balance of evidence indicates increased risk, but the precise magnitude likely differs by specific outcome and study context.

8.2 Limitations
Study-level limitations include heterogeneous outcome definitions (e.g., variable cutoffs for SGA or low birth weight), mixed outcome types (continuous versus dichotomous), inconsistent PCOS diagnostic criteria, and incomplete adjustment for confounders such as maternal BMI, metabolic syndrome, smoking, parity, and ART. Several studies were small, with wide confidence intervals indicative of sparse data and potential selection biases. Older studies likely reflect different clinical practices and diagnostic standards than contemporary cohorts. Some extracted estimates showed internal inconsistencies (point estimates not aligning with CIs), suggesting reporting or extraction errors that reduce confidence in those specific results.

Review-level limitations include the inability to harmonize outcomes for outcome-specific meta-analyses, reliance on a single pooled estimate across varied endpoints, and the absence of a formal risk of bias assessment (not performed in the source data). We also lacked consistent access to author conclusions, limiting our ability to triangulate quantitative results with the original investigators’ interpretations. No formal assessment of publication bias was performed; given the model-dependent pooled results, small-study effects cannot be excluded.

8.3 Implications
For practice: The evidence cautiously supports heightened vigilance in pregnancies complicated by PCOS, particularly regarding fetal growth monitoring and preterm birth prevention strategies. Clinicians should consider that risks likely vary by PCOS phenotype and coexisting metabolic risk factors; targeted optimization of maternal weight, glycemic control, and blood pressure may mitigate risk. However, given heterogeneity and potential confounding, these findings should not prompt blanket changes to care pathways without consideration of individual risk profiles and local protocols.

For research: Priority areas include large, contemporary, population-based cohorts and registries with standardized PCOS definitions (e.g., Rotterdam or updated criteria), clear delineation of ART versus spontaneous conceptions, and pre-specified, harmonized perinatal outcomes (preterm birth, SGA, low birth weight, NICU admission, perinatal mortality). Rigorous confounding control (including maternal BMI, metabolic markers, smoking, parity, socioeconomic status, and comorbidities) is essential, as are sensitivity analyses by PCOS phenotype and metformin or other treatment exposures. Future meta-analyses should conduct outcome-specific pooling, assess small-study effects/publication bias, and incorporate risk-of-bias grading to strengthen inference.
