## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
From 128 records screened, two cross-sectional studies conducted in the ASEAN region met inclusion criteria and were assessed at full text (both included). Publication year was reported for one study (2017); the second had no year available in the extracted data. Both studies compared people living or working in agricultural land-use settings against persons unexposed to agricultural areas and reported human infectious disease prevalence outcomes. ### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Taken together, the two included cross-sectional studies offer insufficient and internally inconsistent evidence to estimate the association between agricultural land-use exposure and infectious disease prevalence in Southeast Asia. The analytic study with a reported OR of 0.6098 (95% CI: 1.2200–2.7700) presents a logical contradiction: an OR below 1.0 implies lower odds of infection in the agricultural exposure group, whereas a CI entirely above 1.0 implies higher odds. Without access to corrected numbers or study-level context (e.g., whether the OR corresponds to the same model as the CI, or a transcription error occurred), neither the direction nor magnitude of association can be inferred. The other study (2017, cross-sectional, N=NR/61) lacked sufficient data to compute an effect size. As a result, no pooled estimate could be generated, and the direction of any association remains uncertain.

Beyond these data limitations, important sources of heterogeneity likely exist and further complicate inference. Agricultural land-use is a broad exposure category that can encompass diverse practices (e.g., wet rice cultivation, plantation agriculture, mixed crop–livestock systems), ecological contexts (e.g., forest margins vs peri-urban fields), and occupational roles (field laborers, pesticide applicators, animal handlers). Infectious outcomes of interest—including helminths (e.g., hookworm), vector-borne diseases (e.g., malaria, scrub typhus, spotted fever group rickettsioses), and other zoonoses—differ in transmission pathways and environmental drivers. Without standardized exposure definitions, outcome ascertainment methods, and adjustment for key confounders (e.g., socioeconomic status, sanitation, housing quality, bed net use, animal contact, travel history), cross-sectional associations risk being biased and non-comparable across settings.

Risk of bias considerations are paramount. Both studies used cross-sectional designs, which can introduce selection bias and preclude causal inference about temporality. Exposure misclassification is plausible if “agricultural exposure” is assigned by residence alone rather than measured by proximity, intensity, or duration of occupational tasks. Outcome misclassification can arise if prevalence is based on self-report or non-validated diagnostics; even with laboratory confirmation, diagnostic sensitivity and specificity vary by pathogen and test platform. Unmeasured or residual confounding is likely substantial in rural–urban comparisons where agricultural activity, poverty, health care access, and vector ecologies covary. In this context, even large observed associations would require careful adjustment and sensitivity analyses to be credible. Here, the absence of consistent and interpretable effect sizes prevents such evaluation.

We also note an evidence gap between the number of records screened (n=128) and those ultimately analyzable (n=0 for meta-analysis; n=2 for narrative review). Common reasons for exclusion included lack of ASEAN-specific human data, absence of infection prevalence outcomes, and no appropriate unexposed comparator. This pattern suggests that while research on agriculture and infectious disease risk in the broader literature may exist, studies that directly compare agricultural versus non-agricultural groups with human prevalence endpoints in ASEAN settings are rare or poorly reported.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on two cross-sectional studies with incomplete and internally inconsistent quantitative reporting, we find insufficient evidence to determine whether agricultural land-use exposure is associated with higher or lower infectious disease prevalence among people living or working in Southeast Asia. One study reported an OR of 0.6098 with a CI of 1.2200–2.7700, a contradiction that prevents interpretation; the other did not provide computable data. No meta-analysis could be performed. Given likely heterogeneity in exposures, outcomes, and confounding structures, robust conclusions cannot be drawn from the available evidence.

### 8.2 Limitations
At the review level, the principal limitations are the paucity of eligible studies with extractable effect sizes and the incomplete reporting of study characteristics (including participant counts and author conclusions). Although 128 records were screened, only two met the inclusion criteria, and neither provided interpretable, meta-analysable data. The reliance on a local corpus may also have missed relevant studies not captured or not accessible in full detail, though full-text assessment was completed for both included records.

At the study level, both included studies used cross-sectional designs vulnerable to selection bias, exposure and outcome misclassification, and uncontrolled confounding. Reporting deficiencies were substantial: one study yielded a non-computable effect; the other presented an odds ratio inconsistent with its confidence interval. Lack of standardized exposure definitions (what constitutes “agricultural land-use”), unclear diagnostic methods for infections, and absent adjustment strategies further limit interpretability.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice and policy, current evidence does not support changing infectious disease prevention or control strategies on the basis of agricultural land-use exposure alone in ASEAN settings. Established, pathogen-specific interventions—such as vector control and bed net use for malaria, rodent control and chigger bite prevention for scrub typhus, and deworming and sanitation improvements for soil-transmitted helminths—remain the most defensible approaches. Local risk assessments should continue to consider the broader rural context (housing, sanitation, vector habitats) rather than agricultural status per se, until higher-quality comparative data become available.

For research, there is an urgent need for well-designed epidemiological studies that:
- Define agricultural exposure using standardized, quantitative metrics (e.g., land-use classification, proximity, crop type, livestock density, pesticide use, time in field tasks).
- Focus on specific pathogens with validated, comparable diagnostic methods and standardized prevalence endpoints.
- Include clear unexposed comparator groups and prespecified confounder adjustment (e.g., socioeconomic status, hygiene and sanitation, housing, vector control measures, animal contact, healthcare access, mobility).
- Employ prospective cohort or case-control designs where feasible to clarify temporality, or rigorously analyze cross-sectional data with sensitivity analyses.
- Report complete data (counts, effect sizes, confidence intervals, model specifications) and preregister protocols to reduce selective reporting.
Multi-country studies across the ASEAN region, with harmonized methods and data sharing, would be particularly valuable for assessing heterogeneity by ecology and agricultural practice.
