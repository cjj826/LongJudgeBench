## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Two studies met the inclusion criteria: Unknown (2011), a cluster randomised trial enrolling 12,391/11,443 participants across arms, and Unknown (2016), a prospective cohort nested within a cluster-RCT with not reported sample sizes (N=NR/NR). Both were conducted in low- and middle-income settings and involved newborns and young infants up to 59 days of age receiving home visits by trained community health workers (CHWs), compared with standard care without CHW home visits. Publication years spanned 2011 to 2016. Across included trials, the outcome reported was care seeking from health facilities; no included study reported diagnostic accuracy (sensitivity/specificity) for CHW identification of serious illness against a reference standard.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The primary synthesis focused on care seeking from health facilities. Under a random-effects model, the pooled risk ratio (RR) was 1.1742 (95% CI 0.5332 to 2.5857; p=0.6902), indicating no statistically significant effect of CHW home visits on the likelihood of facility-based care seeking in this age group. Heterogeneity was considerable (I²=77.5%, τ²=0.264005), suggesting that the true effects likely vary across settings or implementations and that a single pooled estimate should be interpreted with caution.

The two contributing trials had discordant point estimates and directions of effect. Unknown (2016) yielded a log RR of 0.6627, corresponding to an RR of about 1.94 with a 95% CI of 1.32 to 2.84, implying a substantial increase in the measured outcome in the CHW-visited group relative to control. Unknown (2011) reported a log RR of -0.1625, corresponding to an RR of about 0.85 (95% CI 0.76 to 0.96), implying a modest decrease. Without harmonized outcome definitions, it is unclear whether both trials measured the same construct (e.g., any facility care seeking among all live births, timely care seeking among those with danger signs, or care seeking from qualified providers only). Differences in outcome specification could plausibly account for the opposite directions observed. Other likely sources of heterogeneity include the content and intensity of home visits (counseling only versus active case detection and referral), timing and frequency of visits in the first weeks of life, training and supervision of CHWs, linkage to facilities (referral slips, transport facilitation), baseline care-seeking norms, and health system accessibility.

The robustness of the synthesis is limited. A fixed-effects model—implicitly assuming a common underlying effect—produced RR 0.8620 (95% CI 0.7807 to 0.9519), suggesting a statistically significant 14% reduction in the measured outcome in the intervention arm. However, given the substantial between-study heterogeneity (I²=77.5%), a fixed-effects summary is likely inappropriate and potentially misleading. The shift in direction between fixed- and random-effects underscores that the combined result is driven by model choice and that the true intervention effect may be context-dependent.

Risk-of-bias data were not available from the extraction; therefore, we cannot judge how internal validity might have influenced these results. Cluster-randomised designs are vulnerable to unit-of-analysis errors if clustering is not accounted for, contamination between clusters, and imbalances in co-interventions or baseline characteristics. The absence of arm-level Ns for the 2016 nested cohort further constrains appraisal. Small-study effects and publication bias could not be assessed with only two studies.

Critically, none of the included trials reported the diagnostic accuracy of CHW assessments in identifying serious illness (sensitivity, specificity) against a clinical or laboratory reference standard. This leaves a central part of the review question unanswered. Programs that aim to improve early identification rely on CHW recognition of danger signs; without accuracy data, we cannot determine whether observed changes in care seeking reflect improved recognition, enhanced caregiver motivation, or other mechanisms.

The extracted dataset did not include the authors’ own conclusions for either study. Based on the effect sizes, Unknown (2016) likely concluded that the CHW home-visit package increased the measured care-seeking outcome, whereas Unknown (2011) likely concluded a modest reduction; however, without the authors’ text, we refrain from inferring rationales or contextual explanations they may have provided.

In sum, the limited and inconsistent evidence base precludes a confident statement about the effect of CHW home visits on facility care seeking among neonates and young infants in LMICs. The high heterogeneity suggests that program design and context matter, and no inferences can be made regarding the accuracy of CHW identification of serious illness.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Evidence from two cluster-based studies (2011 and 2016) on CHW home visits for newborns and young infants in LMICs provides inconsistent findings for facility care seeking, with a non-significant random-effects pooled RR of 1.1742 (95% CI 0.5332 to 2.5857; p=0.6902) and considerable heterogeneity (I²=77.5%). No included study reported sensitivity or specificity of CHW identification of serious illness. Overall, the strength of evidence is very low and insufficient to draw firm conclusions on either outcome.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include the small number of included studies (2/142 screened) and missing core information (study identifiers, complete sample sizes for one trial, and author conclusions). We were unable to perform a risk-of-bias assessment or explore publication bias. Outcome heterogeneity was substantial, and we lacked the detail needed to harmonize definitions (e.g., “any facility care seeking,” “timely care seeking,” “care from qualified providers”) or to align denominators (all births vs. symptomatic infants).

Study-level limitations likely include variation in intervention content and intensity, timing and frequency of postnatal visits, CHW training and supervision, and referral linkages, which may explain divergent effects. Cluster-randomised designs risk contamination and require proper adjustment for clustering; without access to analytic details, residual confounding and unit-of-analysis errors cannot be excluded. Generalizability is unclear, as country contexts and health system characteristics were not reported in the extracted data.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Based on the current synthesis, there is no consistent evidence that CHW home visits, as implemented in the included trials, reliably increase facility-based care seeking for neonates and young infants, nor is there evidence on the diagnostic accuracy of CHW recognition of serious illness. Programs considering or scaling CHW postnatal home visits should do so on the basis of broader programmatic goals (e.g., postnatal counseling, breastfeeding support, thermal care) and ensure strong referral pathways, supervision, and monitoring. Local evaluation should track clearly defined care-seeking indicators and ascertain whether CHWs can accurately recognize danger signs.

For research: Priorities include (1) rigorously designed cluster randomised trials and quasi-experiments that prespecify and standardize care-seeking outcomes (e.g., care seeking within 24 hours of danger sign onset; care from qualified facilities), report arm-level denominators, and adjust for clustering; and (2) diagnostic accuracy studies comparing CHW assessments to clinical reference standards to estimate sensitivity and specificity for serious neonatal illness. Future studies should detail intervention components (visit timing/frequency, assessment algorithms, referral supports), context (facility access/readiness), and implementation fidelity; stratify by age bands within 0–59 days; and report process indicators (referral issuance, completion) to clarify mechanisms. Harmonization with WHO danger sign definitions and transparent preregistration and analysis plans would improve comparability and reduce risk of bias.
