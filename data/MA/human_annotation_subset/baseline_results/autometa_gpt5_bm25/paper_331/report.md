## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
We included 23 comparative prospective studies (predominantly randomized controlled trials) enrolling patients with Persistent Spinal Pain Syndrome Type 2 (PSPS-T2) and comparing neuromodulation (mainly spinal cord stimulation [SCS]), minimally invasive treatments (e.g., epidural injections), conservative care, and/or reoperation. Publication years spanned 2005–2022. Sample sizes varied widely, with several smaller trials (e.g., 2005 RCT N=24/26; 2010 double-blind RCT N=18/20) and at least one larger trial (2019 open-label, parallel-group RCT N=110/108). Only nine studies provided extractable comparative data for quantitative synthesis. Although the protocol specified pain intensity (standardized mean difference [SMD]) as the primary effect measure, the meta-analysis was conducted using responder-based relative measures (pooled effect reported as a risk ratio [RR]), indicating discrepancies in outcome reporting and data availability.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Across the nine studies with extractable data, the random-effects meta-analysis yielded a pooled RR of 4.2944 (95% CI 1.4302 to 12.8945; p=0.0094), suggesting the intervention groups (largely neuromodulation) had substantially higher odds of achieving a pain response than controls. However, heterogeneity was considerable (I²=91.8%; τ²=1.84), and the fixed-effects model was not statistically significant (RR 1.1645; 95% CI 0.9714 to 1.3960). The divergence between random- and fixed-effects estimates, coupled with extreme heterogeneity, indicates that any apparent benefit is not robust to analytical assumptions and may be driven by between-study differences (e.g., study design, comparators, outcome definitions) and small-study effects.

Individual trial estimates were heterogeneous and in several cases internally inconsistent with the pre-specified SMD outcome. For example, a 2022 placebo-controlled crossover RCT reported an SMD of -1.30 (95% CI -3.90 to 1.30), crossing the null and indicating no clear benefit versus sham stimulation. A 2022 assessor-blind trial reported a point estimate of -0.1082 with a confidence interval ranging from 0.5663 to 1.4222, which does not bound the point estimate, suggesting data extraction or reporting anomalies. Several earlier RCTs reported large relative effects: the 2005 RCT (N=24/26) showed an effect of 1.4123 (95% CI 1.2804 to 13.1621), and the 2012 RCT (N=60/60) showed an effect of 2.7932 (95% CI 5.3859 to 49.5329). In both examples, the confidence intervals are incongruent with the point estimates and/or with SMD metrics, implying that responder-based RRs were combined with continuous effect estimates across studies. A 2019 large open-label RCT (N=110/108) reported an effect of 1.0803 (95% CI 1.1091 to 7.8220), again with a confidence interval not centered on the point estimate. One 2019 open-label trial reported a very large negative effect (“30.0000” with CI -37.7 to -22.4), which is not interpretable as either SMD or RR. These anomalies limit interpretability and confidence in pooled results.

Synthesizing authors’ likely interpretations in the absence of consistent extraction: open-label RCTs and equivalence trials historically report that SCS improves pain outcomes versus conventional medical management in selected PSPS-T2 patients, consistent with the direction of several large RRs favoring intervention. Conversely, the 2022 placebo-controlled crossover RCT (SMD -1.30, CI spanning zero) suggests no clear superiority over sham in a blinded context, highlighting the role of placebo and expectancy effects and/or differences in patient selection, waveform, or stimulation targets. The 2022 assessor-blind RCT with a near-null estimate further tempers enthusiasm, suggesting that benefits may not generalize across all PSPS-T2 subgroups or methodological approaches.

Potential sources of heterogeneity include: (1) comparator differences (sham vs optimized conservative management vs reoperation), (2) variation in intervention modality (tonic vs high-frequency vs burst SCS; trial stimulation protocols), (3) mixed outcome metrics (responder RRs vs continuous pain SMDs), (4) differences in blinding (open-label vs assessor-blinded vs patient-blinded), and (5) small sample sizes and crossover designs (carryover effects). Notably, 14 of 23 included studies had non-computable pain outcomes for meta-analysis, raising the possibility of selective reporting or insufficiently standardized outcome reporting. Minimal extractable data were available for minimally invasive injections and reoperation; despite inclusion, we could not reliably compare these modalities to neuromodulation or to each other.

Taken together, while the random-effects pooled RR suggests a sizable benefit of neuromodulation on pain response in PSPS-T2, the mismatch between pre-specified SMD outcomes and analyzed responder data, high heterogeneity, inconsistent trial-level estimates, and absence of definitive blinded superiority evidence significantly limit the strength of inference. The most rigorous blinded evidence (2022 crossover) did not demonstrate clear benefit over sham, contrasting with open-label and pragmatic trials that reported improvements. This pattern suggests that any true analgesic effect may be smaller than suggested by unblinded studies and conditional on patient selection and stimulation parameters.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 23 comparative studies (nine with extractable data), evidence for neuromodulation—primarily SCS—in PSPS-T2 suggests possible reductions in pain versus conservative/sham comparators, with a random-effects pooled RR of 4.29 (95% CI 1.43 to 12.89). However, considerable heterogeneity (I²=91.8%), a non-significant fixed-effects estimate (RR 1.16; 95% CI 0.97 to 1.40), outcome metric inconsistencies, and at least one placebo-controlled crossover RCT without clear superiority (SMD -1.30; 95% CI -3.90 to 1.30) collectively reduce confidence in a large, generalizable analgesic effect. Evidence for minimally invasive injections and reoperation is insufficient for definitive comparative conclusions.

### 8.2 Limitations
Study-level limitations include small sample sizes in several RCTs, inconsistent blinding (many open-label designs), crossover designs with potential carryover, heterogeneity in SCS modalities and comparators, and substantial variability in outcome definitions (responder rates vs continuous pain intensity). Importantly, the most rigorous placebo-controlled evidence did not show clear benefit over sham stimulation, underscoring potential expectancy/placebo contributions. Reporting quality was uneven; many studies lacked sufficient detail for effect size computation, and several reported effect estimates and confidence intervals inconsistent with the pre-specified SMD outcome.

Review-level limitations also affect certainty. Risk-of-bias assessments were not available for the included studies in this dataset, precluding formal appraisal of bias domains. Only nine studies contributed to quantitative synthesis; 14 had non-computable outcomes, risking availability and reporting bias. Discrepancies between the planned effect measure (SMD for pain intensity) and the pooled metric (RR) indicate that the quantitative synthesis combined different outcome types, which can distort pooled estimates. Incomplete study identifiers and missing sample sizes limit transparency and reproducibility.

### 8.3 Implications
For clinical practice, these findings support that SCS may reduce pain for some PSPS-T2 patients compared with conservative care, but the magnitude and generalizability are uncertain. Shared decision-making should emphasize variability in response, the possibility that effects observed in open-label studies may overestimate true analgesia, and the limited blinded evidence of superiority over sham. Conservative therapies remain foundational; definitive evidence favoring neuromodulation over optimized noninvasive care or reoperation is not established within this dataset. Evidence regarding minimally invasive injections and reoperation relative to SCS is insufficient for strong recommendations.

For research, priorities include adequately powered, patient- and assessor-blinded, sham-controlled RCTs using standardized continuous pain intensity outcomes (and core outcome sets including function and quality of life), with harmonized reporting enabling SMD computation. Trials should delineate PSPS-T2 subgroups (e.g., predominant leg vs back pain, neuropathic features), compare modern SCS paradigms head-to-head and against optimized conservative care, epidural injections, and reoperation, and include long-term follow-up (≥12–24 months). Transparent reporting of responder and continuous outcomes, pre-registered analytic plans, and complete data sharing are essential to reduce heterogeneity and enable robust meta-analysis. Adverse event and explantation rates should be systematically captured to inform risk–benefit decisions.
