## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Six observational studies met inclusion criteria from an initial 112 records screened (106 excluded at title/abstract; 6 assessed at full text; 6 included). Publication years spanned 2005–2023 and designs included prospective within-subject repeated-measures and cross-sectional comparative studies. One cross-sectional study reported group sizes as 72/72; most others did not report sample sizes in the extracted dataset (NR). Outcomes encompassed posterior pole structural parameters—macular morphology, choroidal thickness, and optic disc characteristics—contrasting menstrual cycle phases (follicular vs luteal) and pre- versus post-menopausal status. Quantitative synthesis was constrained: one within-subject study provided an extractable standardized mean difference (SMD −0.2968; 95% CI −0.8780 to 0.2843), one study reported an effect labeled “7.0000” with unclear scaling, and four studies lacked computable effect sizes. Meta-analysis was not performed due to insufficient compatible data.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Across the limited extractable data, the main signal is one within-subject repeated-measures estimate indicating a small, statistically non-significant difference in a posterior pole structural parameter across menstrual phases (SMD −0.2968; 95% CI −0.8780 to 0.2843). The direction suggests lower values in one phase relative to the other (coding unspecified), but the wide confidence interval includes the null and both potentially meaningful decreases and modest increases, underscoring imprecision. The single large reported effect (7.0000) is orders of magnitude larger than physiologically plausible SMDs for ocular structure and likely reflects a different scale (e.g., raw micron difference) or a data-entry/labeling issue; without clarification, it cannot be integrated with the SMD and is treated as an outlying, non-combinable estimate.

Heterogeneity in population comparisons (menstrual phases vs menopausal status), structural endpoints (subfoveal choroidal thickness, macular thickness, RNFL/optic disc metrics), imaging modality (e.g., time-domain OCT/Heidelberg Retina Tomography in 2005 vs spectral/swept-source OCT in 2013–2023), and phase ascertainment likely contributed to inconsistent and non-computable results. Within-subject repeated-measures designs reduce inter-individual variability and are better suited to detect menstrual-phase fluctuations; however, if phase classification relied on calendar estimates rather than hormone assays, non-differential misclassification would bias effects toward the null, consistent with the SMD −0.30 estimate’s imprecision. Cross-sectional menopausal comparisons (e.g., the 2014 study with reported N 72/72 per group) can be confounded by age, ocular biometry (axial length, refractive error), and systemic factors; absent adjusted estimates or raw data, we cannot discern whether any observed differences reflect menopause per se or correlated covariates.

The robustness of the evidence is low. Without at least two commensurable effect sizes, neither fixed- nor random-effects meta-analytic pooling was feasible. The single SMD indicates that any menstrual phase–related structural change, if present, is likely small and potentially below routine clinical detectability, but the confidence interval is wide enough that a moderate effect cannot be excluded. The outlier “7.0000” effect, lacking units or variance, could represent a mean inter-phase difference in microns; if so, true variability and clinical meaningfulness cannot be assessed. Four studies did not provide extractable statistics in the available dataset; consequently, their findings cannot be quantitatively reconciled with the above.

Author conclusions were not available in the extracted dataset for most or all included studies, limiting our ability to triangulate our synthesis with investigators’ interpretations. In the absence of those summaries, and given the observable imprecision and heterogeneity, the most defensible interpretation is that current evidence does not demonstrate consistent, clinically meaningful changes in posterior pole structural parameters across menstrual phases among healthy, non-pregnant women, and that any menopausal differences remain uncertain after accounting for potential confounding. This aligns with a biological expectation that hormonal fluctuations could produce small, transient changes (e.g., in choroidal thickness), but whether these exceed measurement noise or diurnal variability is not established here.

Given the incomplete reporting and inability to pool, we refrain from making granular statements about specific structures (e.g., macular vs choroidal vs optic disc) or specific contrasts (follicular vs luteal; pre- vs post-menopause). The net picture is one of insufficient, heterogeneous evidence with at most small, non-robust effects suggested by the limited computable data.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on six observational studies with sparse extractable data, there is insufficient, low-certainty evidence to conclude that posterior pole structural parameters (macular morphology, choroidal thickness, optic disc characteristics) differ consistently across menstrual cycle phases in healthy, non-pregnant women. Evidence regarding differences between pre- and post-menopausal women is likewise limited and potentially confounded. The only standardized effect available (SMD −0.2968; 95% CI −0.8780 to 0.2843) suggests any menstrual phase–related differences are small and not statistically significant.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include incomplete reporting in the included records (sample sizes NR in most, missing raw means/SDs and variance estimates), which prevented meta-analysis and precluded subgroup or sensitivity analyses. We lacked study-level risk-of-bias assessments and author conclusions in the extracted dataset, limiting our ability to contextualize findings or explore bias domains (selection, measurement, confounding). The reliance on a local corpus may omit relevant external studies, and publication bias could not be assessed.

Study-level limitations, inferred from designs and eras, likely include small sample sizes (only one study reported group sizes, 72/72), heterogeneous imaging platforms and protocols across 2005–2023, variable definitions and verification of menstrual phases (calendar vs hormonal assays), and potential confounding in menopausal comparisons (age, axial length, refractive error, systemic hemodynamics). Diurnal control, hydration status, and masking of graders were not available for appraisal; lack of control for these factors could obscure small physiological effects or produce spurious differences.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current evidence does not justify altering clinical interpretation of posterior pole structural imaging based on menstrual phase or menopausal status alone in healthy women. When monitoring subtle changes—especially choroidal thickness—clinicians may consider standardizing visit timing (time of day; consistent phase when feasible) in research or in cases where marginal differences could influence decisions. For menopausal comparisons, avoid attributing structural differences to menopause without considering age, axial length, and refractive status.

For research: Future studies should prioritize:
- Prospective, within-subject repeated-measures designs across clearly defined cycle phases, with phase verification via serum estradiol/progesterone or LH surge detection.
- Adequate power and pre-registration, with standardized imaging protocols (e.g., swept-source or enhanced-depth imaging for choroid), diurnal/time-of-day control, and masked graders.
- Comprehensive reporting of raw means, SDs, sample sizes, and covariance to permit effect-size calculation; share de-identified data to enable meta-analysis.
- Multivariable adjustment for axial length, refractive error, age, IOP, and systemic variables; report test–retest repeatability to contextualize small changes.
- For menopause, age- and biometry-matched cross-sectional cohorts or longitudinal peri- to post-menopausal transitions, explicitly separating hormonal status from aging effects.
- Harmonized outcome sets spanning macular thickness, subfoveal and parafoveal choroidal thickness, and optic disc/RNFL parameters to improve comparability.

Overall, higher-quality, transparently reported studies are needed before firm conclusions about hormonally linked structural variability of the posterior pole can be drawn.
