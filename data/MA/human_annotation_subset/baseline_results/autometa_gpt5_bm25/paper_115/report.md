## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Seven retrospective cohort studies met inclusion criteria, spanning publication years 1997 to 2022 and including single-center and multi-institutional/multicenter series. Reported cohort sizes ranged from 9 to 153 patients; several records reported two numbers (e.g., 24/21, 36/29, 26/23), suggesting total enrolled versus analyzable stage IV subsets, but the denominators could not be confirmed from the extracted data. All included studies examined primary stage IV endometrial cancer and compared overall survival (OS) by completeness of cytoreductive surgery (CRS). However, effect sizes were largely not extractable; only one multicenter 2022 cohort provided a numeric estimate that was internally inconsistent (point estimate −1.5606 with 95% CI 13.7 to 26.4), precluding interpretation. As a result, meta-analysis was not performed and the evidence synthesis is narrative.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Across the seven cohorts (1997, 2000, 2004, 2012 multi-institutional [N reported 153/95], two studies with not-reported years, and a 2022 multicenter cohort), the central clinical question was whether complete or “optimal” CRS (commonly defined as no gross residual or ≤1 cm residual) confers longer OS than incomplete CRS in primary stage IV endometrial cancer. Despite the consistent framing, study-level data sufficient to compute hazard ratios (HRs) by residual disease status in a stage IV–restricted analysis were unavailable in six studies, and the sole numeric estimate available (2022) was incoherent (HR −1.5606; 95% CI 13.7–26.4), preventing both interpretation and pooling. In line with our protocol, we therefore provide a qualitative synthesis.

The directionality described in these retrospective reports generally favors more complete cytoreduction, consistent with broader gynecologic oncology principles; however, the absence of extractable HRs or median OS by residual disease strata from most studies severely limits quantification. The multi-institutional 2012 study (N reported 153/95) likely contributed the largest number of stage IV cases and would be expected to offer greater statistical power; yet, OS estimates stratified by complete/optimal versus incomplete CRS could not be abstracted in a form that permitted effect size computation. Earlier series (1997, 2000, 2004) reported small samples (e.g., 24/21; 36/29; 26/23), raising imprecision concerns even if directionally favorable associations were suggested. Two additional cohorts with not-reported publication years (N=9/49 and N=16/15) underscore the small-study problem and probable variability in definitions and supportive therapies over time.

Heterogeneity is likely substantial. First, definitions of “complete” or “optimal” cytoreduction may have varied (no gross residual vs ≤1 cm thresholds), and “incomplete” residual categories were not consistently specified. Second, staging criteria and patient selection evolved across eras (e.g., FIGO changes), as did systemic therapy standards (transition to platinum-taxane combinations and increasing use of neoadjuvant or adjuvant chemotherapy). Third, histologic mix (endometrioid vs serous, clear cell, carcinosarcoma) and metastatic patterns (IVa vs IVb; peritoneal vs extra-abdominal) likely differed across cohorts and influence both resectability and prognosis. These factors, combined with variations in surgical aggressiveness and institutional expertise, plausibly introduce clinical and methodological heterogeneity that would have complicated any pooled analysis even if effect sizes were available.

Risk of bias, while not formally rated here due to insufficient reporting, is an overarching concern inherent to all included retrospective cohorts. Confounding by indication is particularly salient: patients selected for complete/optimal CRS generally have lower tumor burden, more favorable disease distribution, and better performance status. Without robust multivariable adjustment (and preferably propensity-based or instrumental variable approaches), any observed survival advantage may reflect baseline prognostic differences rather than the causal effect of completeness of CRS. Other biases include immortal time/guarantee-time bias (if time zero differs between groups), misclassification of residual disease, and era effects. Small-sample imprecision and potential selective reporting further weaken inferences.

Author conclusions, as summarized from the included reports, typically emphasized the potential survival benefit of achieving no macroscopic residual or small-volume residual disease at primary cytoreduction in stage IV endometrial cancer, with caution regarding selection bias and the need for careful patient selection. However, the inability to abstract consistent, stage IV–specific HRs or medians by residual disease category from six studies, along with the unusable estimate in the 2022 multicenter study (−1.5606; 95% CI 13.7–26.4), means our synthesis cannot confirm the magnitude—or even the statistical significance—of any OS benefit. In short, while the narrative across studies aligns with the hypothesis that more complete CRS is associated with longer OS, the current dataset does not allow reliable quantification or a graded strength-of-evidence rating in favor of complete/optimal over incomplete CRS in primary stage IV endometrial cancer.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on seven retrospective cohorts (1997–2022), there is suggestive but unquantified evidence that complete or optimal cytoreduction may be associated with longer overall survival compared with incomplete cytoreduction in primary stage IV endometrial cancer. However, due to pervasive data limitations—absence of extractable effect sizes in six studies and an incoherent estimate in the seventh (−1.5606; 95% CI 13.7–26.4)—we cannot determine the magnitude, precision, or certainty of effect. No meta-analysis was feasible.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on retrospective cohorts, incomplete reporting in the source studies, and inability to compute or pool adjusted hazard ratios. We could not confirm total analyzable sample sizes across studies because several reported dual or unclear denominators (e.g., 24/21, 36/29) and two had not-reported cohort counts for key strata. The lack of standardized residual disease categories and inconsistent stage IV sub-stratification (IVa vs IVb) further constrained evidence extraction.

Study-level limitations likely include confounding by indication (patients with less extensive disease being more likely to achieve complete/optimal CRS), misclassification of residual disease, era effects (evolving systemic therapy), and small-sample imprecision, especially in earlier single-center series. Selection bias and potential immortal time bias may inflate the apparent association between completeness of CRS and OS in the absence of rigorous time-zero alignment and multivariable adjustment. Publication bias cannot be excluded, as small negative studies may be underrepresented.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: The current evidence base does not provide a precise or high-certainty estimate of the OS advantage associated with complete/optimal versus incomplete CRS in primary stage IV endometrial cancer. Nevertheless, consistent with oncologic principles and the qualitative direction of included reports, attempting complete or optimal cytoreduction in appropriately selected patients within experienced centers remains reasonable. Decisions should be individualized in a multidisciplinary setting, incorporating disease distribution, histology, patient fitness, anticipated surgical morbidity, and the availability of effective systemic therapy. Clinicians should counsel patients that any survival benefit specifically attributable to completeness of CRS in stage IV disease is not well quantified.

For research: Priority needs include prospective or carefully designed retrospective multicenter studies with standardized definitions of residual disease (e.g., no gross residual; ≤1 cm; >1 cm), explicit stage IV subgroups (IVa vs IVb), and histology-specific analyses. Studies should report adjusted HRs for OS comparing complete/optimal versus incomplete CRS with prespecified confounders (age, performance status, comorbidity, histology, disease burden/distribution, neoadjuvant/adjuvant therapy) and align time zero to minimize immortal time bias. Transparent reporting of numerators/denominators, median OS by strata, and confidence intervals is essential. A registry-based collaboration or pragmatic prospective cohort could deliver adequately powered, generalizable estimates and enable sensitivity analyses (e.g., propensity-score or instrumental-variable methods) to better approach causal inference.
