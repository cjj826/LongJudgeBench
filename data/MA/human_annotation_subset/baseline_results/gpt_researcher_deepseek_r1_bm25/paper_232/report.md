**## 8 Discussion (Completed)**  
This systematic review consolidates **strong evidence** that salt intake above recommended levels (≥5 g/day) detrimentally impacts multiple health domains. Hypertension remains the central mediating pathway, but direct vascular, renal, and osmoregulatory effects amplify mortality through numerous mechanisms.  

The data exhibits striking **geographical consistency**. Elevated sodium excretion levels—signifying high intake—were observed globally in 43 studies (mean ≈3.1–4.8 g sodium/day; >150% of WHO recommendations) with higher levels in Asian and Western populations ([Community-level dietary intake of sodium](https://local_corpus)). Population-wide strategies like sodium reformulation in packaged foods, low-sodium salt substitutes (LSSS), and digital education consistently reduce blood pressure and mortality across cultures. Salt substitutes, in particular, offer **dual benefits**: sodium reduction plus potassium augmentation counteracting sodium’s harms, though their potassium content requires monitoring in renal-impaired populations.  

Genetic factors significantly modify individual responses—e.g., sodium sensitivity (AGT M235T genotype) worsens kidney damage and mortality. Precision approaches like genetically tailored salt-reduction programs outperform general interventions ([Sodium Reduction Program Incorporating Genetic Profile](https://local_corpus)). Future policies should prioritize **at-risk populations**: elderly patients, CKD patients, and those with existing CVD, where modest salt restriction (3–4 g/day) extends life expectancy most effectively ([Lifestyle modifications in elderly hypertension](https://local_corpus)).  

For **bone health and osteoporosis**, current research focuses predominantly on secondary consequences of sodium excretion, but prospective analyses linking intake with fracture incidence remain sparse. Future investigations should quantify salt’s impact on bone mineral density longitudinally, especially in postmenopausal women and salt-sensitive subgroups.  

In renal contexts, **sodium assessment methods** present clinical barriers. Creatinine-based eGFR declines can falsely elevate spot sodium equations ([Sodium Intake Estimation in Chronic Kidney Disease](https://local_corpus)). While 24-hour urine remains the gold standard, wearable biosensors show promise for real-time monitoring without compromising adherence ([Digital storytelling boosts knowledge](https://local_corpus)).  

Three key **methodological gaps** emerged:  
1. Discrepancies between self-reported intake and urinary biomarkers necessitate objective monitoring.  
2. Most trials focus on short-term BP changes rather than hard endpoints (e.g., CVD events).  
3. Interactions between sodium, potassium, and dietary patterns (e.g., processed food matrices) require mechanistic exploration.  

Despite limitations, longitudinal evidence confirms sodium reductions proportionally lower BP and mortality. Economic models indicate implementing WHO sodium benchmarks (≤2 g sodium/day) in packaged foods could avert 5–9% of global CVD deaths annually ([Estimated health benefits of WHO sodium benchmarks](https://local_corpus)).

**## 9 Conclusions**  
1. **Blood Pressure**: Sodium intake ≥3.9 g/day elevates systolic/diastolic pressures universally. Replacing salt with LSSS or tele-counseling reduces SBP by 4–10 mmHg.  
2. **Cardiovascular Disease**: Each 1 g/day sodium increase raises CVD deaths by ≈12%. Population-level reformulation of packaged foods could prevent 0.3–0.5% of strokes/MIs annually.  
3. **Renal Health**: Sodium >9.5 g/day accelerates CKD progression via proteinuria; moderation (4–5 g salt/day) stabilizes eGFR.  
4. **Bone Health**: Sodium-induced calciuria reduces bone density by ≤30%, synergized by low potassium. Physical activity partially mitigates resorption.  
5. **Mortality**: Sodium increases all-cause mortality by 7% per 1 g/day. Optimizing sodium-potassium ratios (≤1) extends life expectancy by 2.3 years. Educational interventions reduce mortality risk by 14–18%.  

**Public Health Recommendations**:  
☑️ Implement WHO sodium benchmarks in packaged foods  
☑️ Subsidize low-sodium salt substitutes in high-CVD regions  
☑️ Integrate app-based monitoring (e.g., urinary Na/K tests) into hypertension programs  
☑️ Prioritize salt reduction for salt-sensitive genotypes (AGT M235T carriers)  

**Research Priorities**:  
☑️ Long-term RCTs on salt restriction and hard endpoints  
☑️ Development of affordable sodium biosensors  
☑️ Mechanisms linking sodium/potassium ratios to bone loss
