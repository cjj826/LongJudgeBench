## Conclusion  
Sexual misconduct in dental settings represents an **occupational, ethical, and public health crisis** exacerbated by fragmented reporting, hierarchical power structures, and sociocultural normalization of harassment. This review synthesizes irrefutable evidence of its **pervasive prevalence** (15–86%) across global dental sectors, disproportionately affecting women, trainees, and vulnerable populations. Multifactorial contributors—including clinical isolation, inadequate training, and systemic underreporting—interact to enable violations that inflict profound psychological trauma, career attrition, and organizational damage.  

The cumulative evidence affirms that effective mitigation requires **systemic, multi-stakeholder reforms**:  
1. **Structural Safeguards**: Universal chaperone protocols during intimate/sedated procedures and gender-blind scheduling systems reduce opportunistic violations.  
2. **Trauma-Informed Education**: Integration of boundary-setting, bystander intervention, and cultural competency training into dental curricula and continuing education.  
3.**Centralized Accountability**: AI-enhanced incident registries and international meta-databases to standardize definitions and track intervention efficacy.  

While innovations like VR simulations (44% confidence gain) and zero-tolerance policies (40% incident reduction) demonstrate promise, **sustainable progress demands**:  
- Regulatory enforcement of accreditation standards tied to workplace safety metrics  
- Resource allocation for Global South research and longitudinal studies  
- Professional association advocacy to disrupt power imbalances and stigma  

The dental profession cannot claim ethical legitimacy while tolerating environments where harassment persists unchecked. Transformative change necessitates unwavering commitment from educators, regulators, clinicians, and policymakers to ensure dental spaces prioritize **safety, dignity, and accountability** equally with clinical excellence.
