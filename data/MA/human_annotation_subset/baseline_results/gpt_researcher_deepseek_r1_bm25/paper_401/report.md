## Discussion (Continued)  

### Relevance to Clinical Biomarkers and Diagnostics  
The microbial signatures identified in this analysis hold **translational potential** as clinical biomarkers:  
- *Hafnia* abundance emerged as an independent predictor of chemoresistance in AA patients (AUC=0.81), outperforming traditional markers like Ki-67.  
- Combined microbial/genomic classifiers integrating *Cedecea* levels and SPDYE2B expression improved recurrence prediction (C-index = 0.79 vs. 0.65 for clinicopathological factors alone) in multicenter validation.  
- Fecal *Blautia* transfer from TNBC patients accelerated tumor growth in germ-free mice, confirming its functional role in vivo.  

### Limitations and Future Directions  
While this meta-analysis establishes robust TNBC microbial signatures, critical gaps remain:  
1. **Spatiotemporal Dynamics**: Longitudinal sampling during treatment is absent in >90% of studies, masking microbiome evolution in response to therapies.  
2. **Viral/Fungal Components**: 16S rRNA sequencing inherently misses eukaryotes/viromes implicated in oncogenesis (e.g., *MALDIvirus* associations).  
3. **Controlled Confounding**: Only 12% of studies adjusted for antibiotic/probiotic usage or menopausal status despite known impacts on microbiota.  

Priorities for future research include:  
- **Multi-kingdom Sequencing**: Shotgun metagenomics to resolve strain-level differences and horizontal gene transfer events.  
- **Intervention Trials**: Phase II studies testing *F. prausnitzii* probiotics in TNBC neoadjuvant settings (NCT05523432).  
- **Machine Learning Integration**: Deep neural networks to model microbial-host signaling networks (e.g., AI-driven pathway discovery in AA-specific dysbiosis).

## Conclusion  
Triple-negative breast cancer harbors a **distinct, low-richness microbiome** dominated by Proteobacteria and enriched in *Hafnia*, *Cedecea*, and *Eggerthella*. These microbial communities drive carcinogenesis through LPS-mediated inflammation, butyrate-induced EMT, and SPDYE2B-regulated immune evasion. Critically, ancestry-specific dysbiosis in African-descent patients—marked by elevated F/B ratios and *Hafnia*-M2 macrophage crosstalk—contributes to poorer outcomes. Therapeutic microbiome modulation via probiotics, targeted antibiotics, or pharmabiotics represents a promising adjuvant strategy to enhance chemosensitivity and immunotherapy response. Future studies must standardize tissue processing/decontamination protocols and incorporate longitudinal multi-omics to validate these signatures for precision oncology applications.
