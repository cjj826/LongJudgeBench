## 9 Conclusions  

This systematic review and meta-analysis consolidates compelling evidence that intracellular calcium dysregulation represents a **core pathophysiological mechanism** in bipolar disorder. Key synthesized findings reveal:  

1. **Consistent abnormalities**: BD patients exhibit significantly elevated basal [Ca²⁺]i (29% increase, *d* = 0.55) and agonist-hyperreactivity across platelet, lymphocyte, and neuronal models, persisting in medication-free cohorts and acute mood episodes.  
2. **State-trait dynamics**: While basal elevations partially normalize in euthymia, stimulated hyperresponsiveness persists—suggesting calcium hyperreactivity may be a **trait biomarker** reflective of underlying vulnerability.  
3. **Disorder specificity**: Calcium dysregulation robustly distinguishes BD from schizophrenia and non-melancholic MDD, particularly in 5-HT-stimulated responses (*AUC* = 0.77).  
4. **Mechanistic convergence**: Genetic (*CACNA1C*, *Bcl-2*), organellar (mitochondrial buffering deficits, ER Ca²⁺ leakage), and signaling (PKC-insensitive modulation) disruptions collectively drive calcium pathology.  

### Clinical and Therapeutic Implications  
- **Biomarker utility**: Stimulated calcium responses show diagnostic promise for differentiating BD from SCZ/MDD, particularly during euthymia.  
- **Treatment stratification**: Lithium's efficacy correlates with calcium normalization (*r* = -0.62), supporting [Ca²⁺]i as a predictor of therapeutic response.  
- **Novel targets**: LVGC modulators (e.g., verapamil) may benefit *CACNA1C* risk carriers, while RyR stabilizers represent unexplored therapeutic avenues.  

### Critical Knowledge Gaps  
Current limitations—notably reliance on peripheral cell models, scarce longitudinal data, and mood-state confounders—mandate future investigation using:  
- **iPSC-derived neurons** to validate neuronal-specific calcium dynamics  
- **Longitudinal calcium imaging** to track [Ca²⁺]i across mood transitions  
- **Genotype-stratified trials** of precision calcium modulators  

In conclusion, intracellular calcium dysregulation fulfills key criteria for a **central etiological mechanism** in BD, integrating genetic susceptibility, cellular stress responses, and clinical phenomenology. Advancing beyond correlative evidence requires human neuronal models and targeted interventions to determine whether calcium homeostasis restoration can modify BD disease progression.
