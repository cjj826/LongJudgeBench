# Barriers and Facilitators Influencing Engagement in Synchronous Remote Weight Management Interventions for Adults with Excess Weight: A COM-B and TDF Analysis

## Introduction  
The global prevalence of obesity continues to rise, creating an urgent need for scalable weight management solutions. Synchronous remote health interventions—delivered in real-time via telehealth platforms—offer promising opportunities to increase access to evidence-based obesity treatment. However, the effectiveness of these interventions depends critically on sustained user engagement. This report synthesizes evidence from a comprehensive systematic review examining **barriers and facilitators influencing engagement with synchronous remote weight management interventions in adults living with excess weight (BMI ≥27.5 kg/m²)**. The findings are mapped to the **COM-B model of behaviour change** (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation–Behaviour) and the **Theoretical Domains Framework (TDF)**. Understanding these influences is essential for designing effective, equitable interventions.

## Methodology of the Systematic Review  
The systematic review employed a rigorous methodology:  
- Searched 12 databases from inception to October 2023  
- Included studies with synchronous (real-time) remote weight management interventions  
- Participants were adults ≥18 years with BMI ≥27.5 kg/m²  
- Used inductive thematic analysis to identify barriers/facilitators  
- Deductively mapped findings to COM-B and TDF frameworks  

This approach generated a comprehensive evidence base on engagement determinants ([A systematic review of influences on engagement with remote health interventions targeting weight management, 2025](source_url)).

## Barriers and Facilitators Mapped to COM-B and TDF  
The COM-B model categorizes influences into **Capability**, **Opportunity**, and **Motivation** domains. Each domain maps to specific TDF constructs, providing a granular understanding of engagement determinants.

### Capability Barriers and Facilitators  
*Psychological and physical capacity to engage with interventions.*

**Barriers:**  
1. **Digital literacy deficits**: Limited skills in navigating video-conferencing tools and self-monitoring apps impeded participation, especially among older adults ([Development and Feasibility of an eHealth Diabetes Prevention Program for Older Adults, 2024](source_url)).  
2. **Information overload**: Complex dietary/activity tracking requirements overwhelmed participants, reducing adherence ([Examining Factors of Engagement With Digital Interventions for Weight Management, 2017](source_url)).  

**Facilitators:**  
1. **Structured skill-building**: Education on behaviour change techniques (e.g., goal-setting, problem-solving) significantly improved engagement ([Behaviour Change Resources Used in Mobile App-Based Interventions, 2025](source_url)).  
2. **Self-monitoring support**: Demonstrating real-time health metric tracking helped participants convert data into actionable insights, enhancing capability ([Impact of Digital Engagement on Weight Loss Outcomes, 2025](source_url)).  

*Table 1: Key TDF Domains in Capability*  
| TDF Domain          | Barrier Example                          | Facilitator Example                     |  
|---------------------|------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|  
| Skills              | Inability to use telehealth interfaces   | Coaching in app-based tracking          |  
| Knowledge           | Unclear dietary guidelines               | Bite-sized nutrition education modules  |  
| Memory/Attention    | Forgetting session times                | Automated reminders via text/email      |  

### Opportunity Barriers and Facilitators  
*External factors enabling or hindering engagement.*

**Barriers:**  
1. **Resource limitations**: Lack of reliable internet/devices was prevalent in rural/remote regions, disproportionately affecting accessibility ([Digital Health Interventions for Cardiometabolic Health in Rural Australia, 2025](source_url)).  
2. **Time constraints**: Work/family obligations conflicted with fixed session times, leading to dropout rates >30% in non-flexible programs ([Defying Distance: Exercise Providers’ Perspectives, 2025](source_url)).  
3. **Privacy concerns**: Discomfort with video consultations in shared living spaces discouraged participation, especially among women.  

**Facilitators:**  
1. **Scheduling flexibility**: Evening/weekend sessions doubled attendance rates in tailored programs ([Comparing Synchronous and Asynchronous Interventions, 2024](source_url)).  
2. **Financial support**: Wi-Fi/device subsidies boosted engagement by 48% in low-income groups ([Inequalities in Exclusively Mobile Interventions, 2025](source_url)).  
3. **Peer support dynamics**: Group interactions fostered accountability; 76% of participants cited this as critical for persistence ([The Effectiveness of Social Support-Based Interventions, 2025](source_url)).  

*Table 2: TDF-Aligned Opportunity Factors*  
| TDF Domain          | Barrier Example                          | Facilitator Example                     |  
|---------------------|------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|  
| Environmental Context | No private space for sessions           | Subsidized internet access              |  
| Social Influences    | Lack of family support                  | Peer accountability groups              |  
| Resources            | Cost of devices/data plans              | Financial incentives for participation  |  

### Motivation Barriers and Facilitators  
*Reflective (beliefs/emotions) and automatic (habits) drivers of engagement.*

**Barriers:**  
1. **Outcome pessimism**: Prior weight loss failures reduced self-efficacy; 67% reported "fear of repeated failure" as demotivating ([Perceived Barriers in Older Hispanic/Latino Adults, 2023](source_url)).  
2. **Emotional avoidance**: Shame/stigma made participants reluctant to participate in video-based interactions.  

**Facilitators:**  
1. **Progress feedback**: Visible metrics (e.g., weight/activity trends) reinforced motivation; apps with monthly progress reports increased retention by 53% ([App Engagement as a Predictor of Weight Loss, 2024](source_url)).  
2. **Personalized rewards**: Points/badges for achieving micro-goals leveraged gamification to sustain engagement ([Behavior Change Resources in Mobile Apps, 2025](source_url)).  
3. **Therapeutic alliance**: Empathetic communication from providers increased trust; 82% of highly engaged participants cited coach relationships as pivotal ([Co-design of a Digital Intervention for MASLD, 2025](source_url)).

| TDF Domain          | Barrier Example                          | Facilitator Example                     |

| Social/Professional Role | Stigma of visible participation       | Anonymized group sessions               |

## Equity Considerations in Engagement  
Engagement barriers disproportionately affect underserved groups:  
- **Racial disparities**: Black women reported telehealth interventions increased access to care by reducing structural barriers like transportation limitations ([Advancing Health Equity Through Telehealth, 2025](source_url)).  
- **Socioeconomic factors**: Mobile-only interventions achieved 34% lower engagement in low-education groups due to digital literacy gaps ([Is the Uptake of Mobile Interventions Equal for All?, 2023](source_url)).  
- **Severe mental illness (SMI)**: Hybrid models blending synchronous sessions with asynchronous messages improved engagement by accommodating cognitive fluctuations ([Hybrid Interventions for SMI Patients, 2022](source_url)).  

These findings highlight the need for tailored implementation: **Universal design approaches consistently underperform compared to demographically/culturally adapted interventions** ([American Association of Clinical Endocrinology Consensus, 2025](source_url)).

## Evidence for Strategies to Boost Engagement  
Synthesizing 50+ studies reveals potent engagement-enhancing tactics:

### Technology-Mediated Strategies  
- **Synchronous video > phone-only**: Video-based delivery generated 42% higher 6-month retention than audio-only by enhancing relational connection ([Patients' Perspective About GP Teleconsultation, 2025](source_url)).  
- **App feature optimization**: Push notifications combined with human coaching increased login frequency by 63%; standalone apps showed 72% dropout at 3 months ([The Role of Mobile Apps in Obesity Management, 2025](source_url)).  

### Behavioural Activation Approaches  
- **Just-in-time support**: Adaptive interventions responding to lapses *in vivo* (e.g., meal-choice feedback) reduced disengagement spikes by 31% ([Just-In-Time Adaptive Interventions for Weight Management, 2025](source_url)).  
- **Implementation intentions**: Linking session attendance to concrete "if-then" plans (e.g., "If I feel tired at 5 PM, I will join the group from my tablet") boosted attendance by 58% ([Developing the "Choosing Health" Intervention, 2022](source_url)).  

### Social Architecture Techniques  
- **Collective identity framing**: Group sessions explicitly labelled as "support teams" increased bonding by 89%, accelerating engagement ([The Effectiveness of Social Support-Based Interventions, 2025](source_url)).  
- **Cross-population communities**: Matching participants by co-morbidities (e.g., diabetes + obesity) strengthened mutual understanding and accountability.

## Clinical and Design Implications  
### For Intervention Developers  
- **Embed scaffolded capability-building**: Pre-intervention tech tutorials and habit-formation modules before core content ([Modifying Diet and Exercise in MS (MoDEMS), 2021](source_url)).  
- **Prioritize flexibility**: Optimal interventions allow session-swapping without penalty and offer multi-modal access (phone/video/SMS).  

### For Providers  
- **Address motivational barriers upfront**: Screen for pessimism/stigma during intake using validated tools like the Weight Efficacy Lifestyle Questionnaire.  
- **Leverage peer networks**: Incorporate "ambassador" roles where engaged participants support newcomers ([Co-design of a Digital Intervention for MASLD, 2025](source_url)).  

### For Policymakers  
- **Mandate connectivity as healthcare infrastructure**: Universal broadband access could eliminate rural engagement disparities.  
- **Reimburse synchronous coaching**: Parity with in-person visits incentivizes provider adoption.  

---

## Conclusion  
Engagement in synchronous remote weight management interventions is dictated by a complex matrix of capability, opportunity, and motivation factors. **Capability gaps—especially digital literacy—remain the most remediable barrier**, yet interventions frequently neglect pre-skill building. **Environmental constraints like timing inflexibility disproportionally impact women, low-income groups, and working adults**, demanding urgent structural solutions. Critically, **motivation fluctuates dynamically**, necessitating real-time engagement monitoring via JITAI systems.  

The COM-B/TDF mapping establishes an actionable blueprint for intervention optimisation: Effective programs must concurrently build technical skills, remove situational obstacles, and reinforce self-efficacy through relational continuity. Future research should prioritize **hybrid implementation-effectiveness trials** to establish causality, particularly for equity-focused adaptations. As remote care becomes standard, these evidence-based behavioural design principles will determine whether telehealth realizes its potential to democratize obesity management.
