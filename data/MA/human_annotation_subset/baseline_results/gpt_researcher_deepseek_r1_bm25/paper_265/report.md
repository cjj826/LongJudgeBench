### Completed Meta-Analysis Report:

**6 Conclusion**  
This meta-analysis critically evaluated the utility of peripheral blood BDNF as a biomarker for neuroplasticity induced by psychoplastogens in humans. The synthesis of evidence from clinical trials reveals that peripheral BDNF alterations are neither consistent nor reliably correlated with the neuroplastic effects of these compounds. 

Key findings include:  
- **Inconsistent responses**: Ketamine and esketamine demonstrate dose- and timing-dependent BDNF changes, with acute administration typically failing to elevate BDNF despite clinical efficacy. Repeated administration shows mixed results, with increases observed only in some studies and often correlating with clinical response in depression.  
- **Limited data for classical psychedelics**: While low-dose LSD acutely elevates BDNF, this effect is transient and of uncertain clinical relevance. Psilocybin, ayahuasca, and DMT lack sufficient human BDNF data. Similarly, MDMA studies have not assessed BDNF despite demonstrated therapeutic effects.  
- **Methodological and biological confounds**: Substantial variability in sample processing (serum vs. plasma, heating protocols), timing of measurement, and population characteristics (age, diagnosis, treatment response) obscures the interpretation of BDNF changes. Moreover, the poor correlation between peripheral and central BDNF levels challenges the translational validity of this biomarker.  

Given these limitations, peripheral BDNF alone cannot be recommended as a standalone biomarker for psychoplastogen-induced neuroplasticity. Future research should prioritize:  
1. **Multi-modal biomarker panels**: Integrating peripheral BDNF with epigenetic markers (e.g., BDNF methylation), inflammatory cytokines, neurotrophic factors (e.g., VEGF, IGF-1), and neuroimaging correlates of synaptic plasticity (e.g., fMRI, PET, MRS).  
2. **Direct CNS measures**: Development of techniques to assess brain-specific neuroplasticity markers, such as exosome-bound BDNF or neuroimaging-based synaptic density quantification.  
3. **Standardized protocols**: Consensus on sample collection (plasma preferred over serum), timing of assessment, and control for confounders (e.g., physical activity, circadian rhythm) is essential for cross-study comparisons.  

In summary, while psychoplastogens hold promise for rapidly inducing neuroplasticity, the current evidence does not support peripheral BDNF as a valid surrogate marker. Advancing the field requires a shift toward integrated, systems-level approaches to capture the complexity of neuroplastic changes in the human brain.
