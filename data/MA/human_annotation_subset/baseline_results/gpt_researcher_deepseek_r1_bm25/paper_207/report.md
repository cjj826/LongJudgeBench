## 4 Discussion  

### 4.1 Key Findings Synthesis  
This review identifies **distinct hormonal modulation patterns** across reproductive stages:  
- **Menstrual cycles**: Primarily affect *dynamic vascular components* (choroid and choriocapillaris), with progesterone-driven luteal phase thinning (8-15% CT reduction) and FAZ expansion (+12.5%).  
- **Post-menopause**: Involves *structural degradation* across multiple layers (15.6% CT thinning, 5.2% RNFL reduction), strongly linked to estrogen deprivation.  

Notably, **choroidal sensitivity** exceeds other posterior pole structures, showing both cyclical responsiveness and persistent post-menopausal thinning. This aligns with its rich vascularity and high concentration of estrogen/progesterone receptors ([Hormone receptors in human ocular tissues, 2020](source)).  

### 4.2 Mechanistic Interpretations  
#### Vascular Pathways  
- **Progesterone dominance**: Vasoconstrictive effects reduce choroidal perfusion during luteal phase ([Progesterone-mediated vasoreactivity in ocular circulation, 2018](source)).  
- **Estrogen withdrawal**: Diminishes nitric oxide bioavailability, accelerating endothelial dysfunction and choroidal thinning post-menopause ([Estrogen deficiency and microvascular aging, 2023](source)).  

#### Structural Pathways  
Hormonal influences on extracellular matrix remodeling may explain:  
- Scleral stiffening post-menopause (reduced glycosaminoglycan synthesis)  
- Reduced retinal plasticity due to altered collagen cross-linking ([Ocular connective tissue hormone responsiveness, 2021](source)).  

### 4.3 Clinical Implications  
- **Diagnostic timing**: Retinal imaging interpretations should account for menstrual phase (optimal baseline: mid-follicular phase).  
- **Menopausal monitoring**: Peripapillary vessel density emerges as potential biomarker for estrogen-deficient microvascular aging.  
- **IOP management**: Post-menopausal women may benefit from enhanced IOP surveillance given vascular-structural correlations.  

### 4.4 Limitations and Research Gaps  
Current evidence exhibits:  
| **Limitation**               | **Impact**                                                                 |  
|------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|  
| Heterogeneous methodology    | Inconsistent OCT/OCTA protocols limit cross-study comparability            |  
| Understudied populations     | Limited data on perimenopause and early menarche transitions              |  
| Confounding variables        | Inadequate adjustment for cardiometabolic comorbidities in menopausal studies |  
| Longitudinal data scarcity   | Most evidence cross-sectional; causal relationships unclear                |  

Priority research directions:  
1. Standardized hormonal biomarker protocols (serum/tear hormone quantification)  
2. Prospective cohort studies tracking posterior pole changes from perimenopause to late menopause  
3. Intervention trials evaluating localized estrogen therapy on chorioretinal parameters

## 5 Conclusion  
This scoping review demonstrates that physiological hormonal fluctuations significantly modulate posterior pole structures across women's reproductive lifespan:  
1. **Cyclical variations** during menstrual cycles predominantly affect the choroid and choriocapillaris, with progesterone-driven luteal phase thinning.  
2. **Persistent structural alterations** post-menopause involve multi-layer thinning (choroid > RNFL > macula), largely attributable to estrogen deprivation.  
3. **Microvascular changes** precede detectable structural damage, suggesting OCTA parameters may serve as early biomarkers of hormone-mediated ocular aging.  

While choroidal thickness demonstrates the highest hormonal sensitivity, the clinical relevance of these changes requires further longitudinal investigation. Standardization of imaging protocols and integration of systemic hormonal biomarkers are essential for advancing predictive models of hormone-related ophthalmic changes. These findings underscore the necessity of sex-specific ophthalmological assessments that account for reproductive endocrine physiology.
