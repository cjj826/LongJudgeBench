# Impact of Laparoscopic Vertical Sleeve Gastrectomy on Lower Esophageal Sphincter Function and Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease

## Executive Summary
This comprehensive analysis synthesizes findings from 34 clinical studies examining the effects of laparoscopic vertical sleeve gastrectomy (LVSG) on lower esophageal sphincter (LES) function and gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) in patients with morbid obesity. The evidence demonstrates that LVSG consistently reduces LES pressure (LESP) and compromises sphincter length (LESL), leading to significantly increased esophageal acid exposure as measured by DeMeester Score (DMS). Approximately 20-31% of patients develop de novo GERD postoperatively, with existing GERD worsening in most cases. These changes occur independently of successful weight loss, highlighting fundamental alterations in esophagogastric junction physiology requiring careful patient selection and postoperative management.

## Introduction
Laparoscopic vertical sleeve gastrectomy (LVSG) has become the predominant bariatric procedure worldwide due to its technical simplicity and effectiveness in weight reduction. However, concerns persist regarding its impact on gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) and esophageal physiology. This analysis examines the procedure's effect on lower esophageal sphincter pressure (LESP), sphincter length (LESL), and acid exposure (DeMeester Score - DMS) through systematic evaluation of pre- and postoperative esophageal function tests.

## Methodology of Included Studies
The evidentiary basis comprises 34 studies published between 2010-2026, including:
- Prospective cohort studies (n=15)
- Retrospective analyses (n=10)
- Systematic reviews/meta-analyses (n=4)
- Case series/reports (n=5)

Diagnostic modalities included high-resolution manometry (HRM), 24-hour pH monitoring, multichannel intraluminal impedance-pH testing (MII-pH), and endoscopic evaluation. Study populations ranged from 19 to 668 patients, with follow-up periods spanning 6 months to 11 years postoperatively.

## Impact on Lower Esophageal Sphincter Function

### Lower Esophageal Sphincter Pressure (LESP)
**Consistent reduction in LESP is documented across all studies:**

- The 2025 meta-analysis of 19 studies (668 patients) showed a significant mean reduction of 4.94 mmHg in LESP post-LVSG ([Impact of LVSG on LESP, LESL and GERD, 2025](local_corpus))
- Manometric studies demonstrated reductions from preoperative baselines:
  - 14.2 ± 5.8 mmHg to 11.8 ± 6.9 mmHg at 6 months ([Manometric changes after SG, 2010](local_corpus))
  - 12.26 ± 6.87 mmHg to 8.88 ± 6.28 mmHg in larger cohorts ([Persistent and De Novo GERD After SG, 2023](local_corpus))
- Prospective data from 45 patients showed LESP decreased proportionally to weight loss but remained below preoperative values despite BMI normalization ([Impact on esophageal physiology, 2021](local_corpus))

### Lower Esophageal Sphincter Length (LESL)
**Compromised sphincter dimensions are consistently reported:**

- Total LESL decreased significantly (p=0.002) in prospective manometric evaluation ([Esophageal motility after LSG, 2017](local_corpus))
- Abdominal LESL component decreased by mean 0.44 cm postoperatively ([Effect on antireflux mechanism, 2013](local_corpus))
- The 2025 meta-analysis reported moderate LESL reduction but noted significant methodological heterogeneity in measurement techniques ([Impact of LVSG on LESP, LESL and GERD, 2025](local_corpus))

## Impact on Gastroesophageal Reflux

### DeMeester Score (DMS) and Acid Exposure
**Significant increases in objective GERD measures:**

- Meta-analysis of 547 patients demonstrated a 15.2-point mean increase in DMS after LVSG ([Impact of LVSG on LESP, LESL and GERD, 2025](local_corpus))
- Evolution of GER after LVG (2019) documented a statistically significant increase in DMS (p=0.028) despite weight reduction ([Evolution of GER after LVG, 2019](local_corpus))
- MII-pH studies showed increased acid exposure time (AET), reflux index, and prolonged acid clearance ([Investigation using MII-pH, 2024](local_corpus))

### Clinical GERD Incidence
**Substantial increase in GERD prevalence postoperatively:**

- De novo GERD developed in 19-67.9% of patients without preoperative symptoms ([Predictive Factors GERD, 2020](local_corpus); [Impact on esophageal physiology, 2021](local_corpus))
- Existing GERD worsened in 71.4% of patients with preoperative symptoms ([Persistent and De Novo GERD After SG, 2023](local_corpus))
- Prevalence increased from 17.8% preoperatively to 31.1% postoperatively in prospective cohorts ([Impact on esophageal physiology, 2021](local_corpus))
