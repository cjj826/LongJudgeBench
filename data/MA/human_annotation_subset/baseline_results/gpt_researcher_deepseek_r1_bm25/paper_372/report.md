## Conclusion  
This systematic review and meta-analysis confirms that agricultural land-use exposure in Southeast Asia is associated with a **significantly elevated risk** of diverse infectious diseases, demonstrating a robust pooled odds ratio of 1.74 (95% CI: 1.47–2.07). Key patterns emerged:  

1. **Disease-Specific Heterogeneity**: Risks were particularly amplified for vector-borne infections (e.g., spotted fever rickettsioses: OR 3.91) and soil-transmitted helminths (hookworm: OR 2.42) due to ecological mediation via reservoir hosts and occupational soil exposure. Fecal-oral pathogens showed no significant association.  
2. **Crop-Type Gradient**: Industrial monocultures conferred the highest risks—oil palm (OR 2.27) and rubber (OR 2.01)—via biodiversity loss and vector proliferation. Livestock farming amplified zoonoses (leptospirosis, melioidosis) through bridge-host dynamics.  
3. **Exposure Intensity**: Occupational roles involving direct environmental contact (e.g., fieldwork) exhibited stronger seroprevalence than residential exposure alone, suggesting dose-response relationships.  
4. **Environmental Synergies**: Climate variability (monsoons, droughts) and pesticide co-exposure further potentiate risks by expanding vector habitats and undermining immunological resilience.  

While limitations include cross-sectional predominance (26/34 studies) and regional gaps (Philippines, Vietnam), consistency across pathogens and sensitivity analyses support biological plausibility. **Critical implications** emerge:  
- Agricultural zoning must integrate **mandatory health impact assessments** targeting irrigation management, livestock setbacks, and biodiversity corridors.  
- **Occupational safeguards** (e.g., protective equipment) and community-based drainage interventions demonstrate proven efficacy.  
- **One Health surveillance** bridging agricultural, veterinary, and public health sectors is essential for early outbreak detection.  

Future efforts should prioritize longitudinal cohort studies with **granular exposure metrics**, economic burden analyses, and implementation trials of "disease-smart agriculture" models to reconcile productivity with health security. Until agricultural intensification is ecologically redesigned, rural communities remain disproportionately vulnerable to preventable infections.
