## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Six observational studies met the inclusion criteria (final included = 6) from an initial 123 records screened. Publication years spanned 2013–2023. Designs included prospective within-subject repeated-measures across menstrual phases (n=2; years 2013 and 2022) and cross-sectional comparative analyses of menstrual-phase groups or pre- vs post-menopausal status (n=4; years 2014, 2022, and 2023; one NR year). Sample sizes were incompletely reported: four studies reported N per group of 72/72, 47/47, 50/50, and 15/15, totaling at least 184 participants; two studies did not report participant counts. Quantitative effect sizes (standardized mean differences, SMDs) were available or derivable for four studies, ranging from small magnitudes (SMD 0.2540; SMD −0.5410; SMD −0.2968 [95% CI −0.8780 to 0.2843]) to one extreme outlier (SMD −7.0000). Two studies had insufficient data for effect computation. Given heterogeneity in outcomes (macular morphology, choroidal thickness, optic disc characteristics), designs, and unclear directionality of comparisons, meta-analytic pooling was not performed and a narrative synthesis is presented.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Across the six studies, evidence for systematic differences in posterior pole structural parameters by menstrual cycle phase or menopausal status was inconsistent and generally imprecise. The one study that reported an effect estimate with a confidence interval found no statistically significant difference (SMD −0.2968; 95% CI −0.8780 to 0.2843), compatible with no effect and excluding any large phase- or status-related difference in the measured parameter for that study. Two additional studies produced small to moderate standardized effects in opposite directions (SMD 0.2540 vs SMD −0.5410), which, without harmonized directionality (i.e., which phase/status is the reference) or uniform outcomes, are difficult to interpret as evidence for a consistent biological pattern. A single study yielded an implausibly large standardized effect (SMD −7.0000), suggesting either extreme group separation on a particular metric with very small variability or, more plausibly, a data extraction/calculation artifact; this estimate is not commensurate with the remainder of the dataset and, if genuine, would imply a structural difference of unlikely magnitude for healthy eyes.

Heterogeneity likely arose from multiple sources. First, the outcome domains varied: some studies assessed macular morphology (e.g., thickness parameters), others choroidal thickness, and others optic disc characteristics. Differences in device type, segmentation protocols, and measurement locations would reasonably yield variability in effect estimates even if the underlying biological effect were constant. Second, study design differences matter: within-subject repeated-measures designs (two studies) can control for between-person confounding but are sensitive to timing (cycle day) and diurnal effects; cross-sectional comparisons (four studies) are more vulnerable to confounding by age (especially in menopausal comparisons), axial length, intraocular pressure, and systemic factors. Third, measurement timing across the menstrual cycle was not standardized in the abstracted dataset; luteal and follicular phases encompass broad hormonal milieus, and misclassification (without serum hormone confirmation) would attenuate true effects. The small sample sizes reported for some studies (e.g., N=15/15) exacerbate imprecision and susceptibility to outliers.

Risk-of-bias information was not available in the extracted dataset, and a formal assessment could not be completed. Nevertheless, plausible sources of bias include selection bias (non-representative volunteers), confounding (age, axial length, refractive error, blood pressure, hydration status), lack of cycle verification (absence of estradiol/progesterone assays), and inadequate control of diurnal variation, particularly for choroidal thickness. Reporting limitations were evident: two studies lacked sufficient data for effect size computation, and several did not report sample sizes, hindering interpretability and precluding pooling.

We sought to reconcile the extracted estimates with study authors’ interpretations; however, author conclusions were not captured in the dataset for the included reports. Consequently, we cannot compare pooled or narrative conclusions with the investigators’ stated interpretations. From the quantitative pattern alone, the balance of evidence does not support a consistent or clinically meaningful difference in posterior pole structural parameters across menstrual phases or between pre- and post-menopausal states. The small-to-moderate SMDs near zero, a null confidence interval (−0.2968; 95% CI −0.8780 to 0.2843), and the methodological heterogeneity argue for caution. If the outlying SMD −7.0000 is discounted as likely erroneous or not comparable, the remaining estimates cluster around negligible to modest effects with inconsistent direction, suggesting any true effect—if present—is likely small and highly parameter-specific.

Given these constraints, a meta-analysis would not have yielded a meaningful summary estimate. The lack of standardized outcomes and unclear comparator directionality would violate key assumptions for pooling and could produce misleading inferences. The current evidence base is better suited to a structured narrative emphasizing inconsistency, imprecision, and reporting gaps.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on six observational studies (2013–2023) with at least 184 participants across four reports with known sample sizes, there is no consistent, precise evidence that posterior pole structural parameters (macular morphology, choroidal thickness, optic disc characteristics) differ by menstrual cycle phase or menopausal status in healthy, non-pregnant women. Quantitative effects, where computable, were small and directionally inconsistent, with one implausible outlier (SMD −7.0000) and one clearly null result (SMD −0.2968; 95% CI −0.8780 to 0.2843). Heterogeneity and reporting limitations preclude firm conclusions.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on a single local corpus, which may omit relevant studies and is susceptible to selection and publication biases. Extracted data were incomplete: two of six included studies lacked sufficient statistics for effect computation, author conclusions were not available, and details necessary for risk-of-bias appraisal were missing. Outcomes were heterogeneous, and comparator directionality (e.g., follicular vs luteal as the reference) was not consistently captured, preventing meta-analysis.

Study-level limitations likely include small sample sizes (e.g., N=15/15 in one study), inconsistent timing within menstrual phases, probable absence of hormonal confirmation (estradiol/progesterone) for phase assignment, and inadequate control of confounders such as age, axial length, refractive status, blood pressure, hydration, and diurnal variation—factors that can materially influence choroidal thickness and macular measurements. Device/model differences and segmentation protocols add further measurement variability. The presence of an extreme SMD (−7.0000) suggests potential data or analytic issues in at least one study.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current evidence does not support adjusting interpretation of OCT-derived posterior pole structural parameters solely based on menstrual cycle phase or menopausal status in healthy women. Observed differences, where present, appear small and inconsistent relative to known sources of physiological and technical variation (e.g., diurnal changes, axial length, segmentation). Clinicians should continue to control for established factors and prioritize consistent measurement protocols; any cycle- or menopause-related adjustments are not justified by the available data.

For research: Priority gaps include well-powered, standardized, prospective studies with within-subject repeated measures across clearly defined cycle windows, verified by serum hormone levels, and controlled for time-of-day. Cross-sectional menopausal comparisons should match or adjust for age, axial length, refractive error, and systemic covariates. Studies should prespecify and harmonize posterior pole outcomes (e.g., macular subfield thicknesses, subfoveal choroidal thickness, Bruch’s membrane opening/minimum rim width), use consistent devices/segmentation, and report sufficient statistics (means, SDs, Ns, exact p-values, and confidence intervals) to enable effect-size computation. Pre-registration, clear reporting of masking and confounder adjustment, and multi-center designs would improve generalizability and reduce bias. Finally, exploring whether any effects are parameter-specific (e.g., choroid vs inner retinal layers) and clinically meaningful should be a focus.
