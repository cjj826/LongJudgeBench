## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Seventeen observational studies (2010–2020) met inclusion criteria: cohorts (including prospective and retrospective), nested case–control, and case–control designs. Sixteen studies contributed quantitative data to the meta-analysis; one reported insufficient data for pooling. Sample sizes were frequently not reported in the extracted records, precluding a reliable estimate of total participants; one cohort reported 6,906 vs 20,697 in exposed vs unexposed groups. Across studies, exposures were predominantly oral bisphosphonates used for osteoporosis in general populations, and outcomes included all-cause cancer or specified sites (most commonly colorectal and breast; some also assessed endometrial, and fewer liver or pancreatic). No randomized trials were identified.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The primary quantitative synthesis found that bisphosphonate use was associated with a modestly lower risk of cancer overall: random-effects pooled HR 0.84 (95% CI 0.77 to 0.91; p<0.001). The fixed-effect estimate was similar (HR 0.88, 95% CI 0.86 to 0.90), suggesting the direction and approximate magnitude of association were robust to model choice. However, between-study heterogeneity was considerable (I²=90.1%, τ²=0.022), indicating substantial differences across studies that likely reflect variation in cancer sites evaluated, exposure definitions (ever-use versus duration/intensity), populations (e.g., predominantly female osteoporosis cohorts versus mixed-sex general populations), and analytic control for confounding.

Individual study estimates spanned protective to harmful associations. Several studies reported inverse associations of meaningful magnitude. For example, a 2012 cohort reported HR 0.69 (95% CI 0.60 to 0.79); a 2011 case–control study reported HR 0.41 (95% CI 0.25 to 0.67); a 2014 case–control study HR 0.49 (95% CI 0.26 to 0.93); and a 2015 cohort HR 0.56 (95% CI 0.34 to 0.93). More modest inverse estimates included a 2012 cohort HR 0.87 (95% CI 0.82 to 0.92), a 2013 nested case–control HR 0.92 (95% CI 0.87 to 0.97), a 2012 population-based nested case–control HR 0.78 (95% CI 0.65 to 0.94), and a 2015 cohort HR 0.80 (95% CI 0.64 to 1.00). Near-null results included a 2013 prospective cohort analysis using WHI clinical trials and observational study data (HR 0.88, 95% CI 0.72 to 1.07) and a 2020 case–control study (HR 0.98, 95% CI 0.93 to 1.03). A subset suggested possible harm: a 2010 nested case–control of gastrointestinal sites reported an elevated risk for at least one GI cancer site (HR 1.30, 95% CI 1.02 to 1.66), and a 2012 population-based retrospective cohort reported HR 1.69 (95% CI 1.39 to 2.04).

Author interpretations, where inferable from the study context and effect estimates, were generally cautious. Large prospective data (e.g., the WHI-based analysis) interpreted non-significant results as not supporting a strong relationship between bisphosphonate use and overall cancer risk. Some case–control and cohort studies reporting inverse associations discussed the potential for residual confounding—particularly confounding by indication (osteoporosis/low bone mineral density correlating with lower lifetime estrogen exposure and potentially lower risks for hormone-related cancers), healthy-user bias, and differential screening patterns—as alternative explanations for observed protective signals. Studies that found increased risks for some gastrointestinal sites emphasized site-specific mechanisms and exposure duration (e.g., prolonged oral bisphosphonate exposure potentially increasing upper GI risk), while often noting null findings for colorectal cancer. Taken together, these author conclusions align with our pooled estimate in suggesting no strong signal of harm for overall cancer and at most a modest inverse association, but they also highlight that inverse findings may not be causal.

The high heterogeneity merits emphasis. Pooling across all cancers (all-cause plus multiple site-specific outcomes) likely conflates different etiologies. Differential adjustment (few studies had bone density, menopausal status, hormone therapy, smoking, BMI, or calcium/vitamin D use simultaneously), variations in exposure definitions (ever-use versus new-user, cumulative dose, recency), and time-related biases (immortal time and latency handling) are plausible contributors. The convergence of the random-effects (0.84) and fixed-effect (0.88) estimates suggests that, despite heterogeneity, the directionality is consistent; nevertheless, the presence of a few outlying harmful estimates underscores that site-specific risks cannot be excluded.

Given that all included studies were observational, causal inference is limited. Some studies attempted to mitigate bias with nested case–control designs or prospective cohorts, but few used active comparators, time-varying exposure models, or rigorous new-user designs. Confounding by factors linked to both osteoporosis and cancer risk (e.g., frailty, reproductive history, alcohol, and other medications) likely remains. Therefore, while the meta-analytic signal suggests a small protective association, it should be interpreted as hypothesis-generating rather than evidential of chemopreventive effects.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 16 observational studies with quantitative data, bisphosphonate use was associated with a modest reduction in overall cancer risk (pooled HR 0.84, 95% CI 0.77 to 0.91), but heterogeneity was considerable (I²=90%). Individual studies ranged from protective to harmful, and large prospective data (e.g., WHI-based analysis, HR 0.88, 95% CI 0.72 to 1.07) were compatible with no strong association. Given observational designs and potential residual confounding, the evidence does not support using bisphosphonates for cancer prevention, nor does it indicate a large increase in overall cancer risk.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include incomplete reporting of key study characteristics in the extracted records (e.g., participant counts, detailed exposure and outcome definitions), preventing aggregation of total sample size and limiting stratified analyses by cancer site, duration, route (oral vs intravenous), or sex. The included studies spanned all-cause and various site-specific cancers; pooling across diverse endpoints likely inflates heterogeneity and obscures site-specific effects. We lacked sufficient data to perform predefined subgroup or sensitivity analyses (e.g., by duration of use, timing, or specific bisphosphonate agents), and we could not formally assess publication bias.

Study-level limitations are intrinsic to the predominance of observational designs. Confounding by indication (osteoporosis/low estrogen states), healthy-user bias, protopathic bias (early symptoms of undiagnosed cancer leading to bisphosphonate discontinuation/initiations), and differential screening may influence associations with hormone-sensitive and colorectal cancers. Time-related biases (immortal time, inadequate lagging for latency) were infrequently addressed. Exposure misclassification is possible due to prescription fill versus ingestion, and outcome misclassification may occur when registry linkages are incomplete. Adjustment sets varied; few studies controlled for bone mineral density, menopausal status, hormone therapy, smoking, BMI, and alcohol concurrently—factors plausibly correlated with both bisphosphonate use and cancer risk.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current evidence does not justify prescribing bisphosphonates to prevent cancer. The pooled estimate provides some reassurance that, in general populations using bisphosphonates for osteoporosis, there is no large increase in overall cancer risk. Nonetheless, given heterogeneity and a small number of studies suggesting elevated risk for certain gastrointestinal sites, clinicians should continue to follow established indications and monitor patients per standard practice, rather than for cancer-related concerns linked to bisphosphonate use.

For research: Future studies should be site-specific and methodologically robust. Priorities include large new-user, active-comparator cohort studies with time-varying exposure, sufficient lag periods, and detailed control for bone density, reproductive/hormonal factors, lifestyle variables, and concomitant medications. Stratification by sex, menopausal status, route (oral vs intravenous), agent, dose, and duration is needed, as is careful attention to latency and detection bias. Linkage to high-quality cancer registries will improve outcome ascertainment. Where feasible, emulation of target trials and instrumental-variable approaches may help mitigate unmeasured confounding. An individual participant data meta-analysis could harmonize definitions and enable consistent confounder adjustment, clarifying whether any apparent inverse associations are causal or reflect residual bias.
