## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Three comparative studies met the eligibility criteria from 90 records screened (87 excluded at title/abstract; 3 assessed at full text; 0 excluded at full text). The included studies spanned 2015–2025 and comprised one cross-sectional comparison (2025) and two within-course quasi-experiments (2015 and 2017). Sample sizes and setting details were not reported in the extracted data (N=NR for all studies). ### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The main finding of this review is that the current comparative evidence base for TBL in undergraduate dental education is sparse and under-reported. Among the three included studies, only one provided a computable effect estimate (OR 0.78). Without the underlying outcome definition and confidence interval, this estimate is difficult to interpret. An OR below 1.0 implies reduced odds of the coded outcome in the TBL group relative to the comparator, but the direction of benefit depends on whether the outcome was desirable (e.g., achieving a high grade) or undesirable (e.g., dissatisfaction). In the absence of outcome coding details, we cannot ascertain whether OR=0.78 favors TBL or the comparator, nor can we assess statistical significance. The other two quasi-experimental studies (2015, 2017) reported comparative results that could not be converted to standardized effect sizes with the available data. Consequently, no quantitative synthesis was possible.

Heterogeneity is likely substantial. The studies differed in design (cross-sectional vs. within-course quasi-experiment), comparators (traditional lectures vs. other active-learning approaches such as PBL/CBL/EBL), and outcome constructs (student satisfaction versus academic performance). Within-course comparisons can mitigate some between-cohort confounding but are vulnerable to order effects, instructor variability, and concurrent curricular changes. Cross-sectional designs are particularly sensitive to unmeasured confounding (e.g., baseline student ability, motivation, prior exposure to team learning). Variation in TBL implementation fidelity (e.g., readiness assurance, team composition, application exercises) and in the assessment formats (e.g., multiple-choice exams, practicals, or global course grades for performance; Likert-scale items for satisfaction) would further contribute to heterogeneity and complicate cross-study interpretation.

Risk-of-bias considerations meaningfully temper confidence in any apparent direction of effect. None of the included studies were randomized; quasi-experimental and cross-sectional designs are subject to selection bias, confounding, and performance bias. The use of an odds ratio in the single study with extractable data suggests dichotomization of an outcome that may originally have been continuous (e.g., exam scores), which can reduce statistical power and obscure nuances. Without confidence intervals or p-values, the precision and statistical significance of the observed OR=0.78 cannot be evaluated. Moreover, the absence of reported sample sizes precludes judgment about study power and susceptibility to small-study effects.

Author conclusions from the included studies were not available in the extracted dataset, limiting our ability to triangulate our synthesis with the studies’ own interpretations. In many educational evaluations, authors conclude that TBL improves engagement and collaborative skills, but such claims cannot be assumed here for undergraduate dental students or for the specific outcomes of satisfaction and academic performance. Given the incomplete reporting, we cannot compare individual study conclusions with any pooled estimate, nor can we assess consistency of findings across studies.

In sum, the available evidence does not support a definitive statement about whether TBL improves student satisfaction or academic performance compared with lectures, PBL, EBL, or CBL in undergraduate dental education. The small number of studies, heterogeneous comparators and outcomes, nonrandomized designs, and incomplete reporting (N not reported; no CIs; two non-computable effects) constrain inference. No meta-analysis was conducted because only one study yielded an extractable effect estimate.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on three heterogeneous, nonrandomized comparative studies (2015–2025) with incomplete reporting and only one computable effect estimate (OR 0.78; outcome coding and precision not reported), the evidence is insufficient to determine whether team-based learning improves student satisfaction or academic performance relative to other pedagogies in undergraduate dental education. Any direction or magnitude of benefit (or harm) remains uncertain.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations:
- The synthesis relies on a local corpus of 90 records; although screening was comprehensive within this corpus, relevant studies outside it may have been missed. 
- Extracted data were incomplete: sample sizes were not reported for any included study; two studies lacked data amenable to effect size calculation; confidence intervals and p-values were unavailable for the single reported OR. 
- No formal risk-of-bias assessment could be performed due to insufficient methodological detail in the extracted records, precluding grading of evidence certainty.

Study-level limitations:
- All included studies were nonrandomized (cross-sectional or within-course quasi-experimental), increasing susceptibility to selection bias, confounding (e.g., baseline academic ability), and performance bias (e.g., instructor effects). 
- Heterogeneity in comparator pedagogies (lectures vs. PBL/CBL/EBL), TBL implementation fidelity, course content, and outcome measurement likely varied widely and was not consistently documented, limiting comparability.
- The single OR suggests outcome dichotomization, which can reduce sensitivity; the lack of reporting on measurement validity (for satisfaction scales) and objectivity (for performance metrics) further limits interpretability.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice:
- Given the limited and weakly reported comparative evidence, there is no robust basis to recommend for or against adopting TBL specifically to improve academic performance or student satisfaction in undergraduate dental curricula over lectures, PBL, EBL, or CBL. Programs considering TBL should base decisions on local priorities, resources, faculty expertise, and broader educational objectives (e.g., teamwork, accountability), while evaluating outcomes rigorously in their own context.

For research:
- There is a clear need for adequately powered, well-reported comparative studies—ideally randomized (e.g., cluster-randomized by tutorial group) or strong quasi-experiments (e.g., stepped-wedge designs)—that compare TBL with specific alternative pedagogies in undergraduate dental courses.
- Studies should prespecify primary outcomes, use standardized and validated measures (e.g., objective exam performance, OSCE scores; validated satisfaction instruments), and report complete data (Ns, effect sizes with 95% CIs, p-values).
- Implementation fidelity (readiness assurance testing, team composition, facilitation quality) and context (course topic, year of study) should be documented to enable subgroup and moderator analyses.
- Longer-term outcomes (knowledge retention, clinical reasoning, team competencies), cost/resource implications, and equity considerations (effects by baseline achievement, language background) warrant systematic evaluation.
- Prospective registration and adherence to reporting guidelines (e.g., CONSORT for cluster trials or TREND for nonrandomized studies) would improve transparency and reduce risk of selective reporting.
