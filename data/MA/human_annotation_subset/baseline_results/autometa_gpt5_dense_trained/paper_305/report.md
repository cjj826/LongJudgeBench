## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Nine observational studies met inclusion criteria (final included=9), spanning 2003–2025 and encompassing a range of designs: prospective and retrospective cohorts (including a propensity score–matched cohort), a nested case–control study, and retrospective comparative analyses. Across the seven studies that reported group sizes, there were at least 81,143 mother–infant dyads (PCOS/non-PCOS totals combined: 169; 8,390; 69,098; 227; 2,955; 130; and 174, respectively), with two additional studies not reporting sample counts. Six studies contributed extractable effect estimates to quantitative synthesis. Using mean difference (MD) as the prespecified effect measure, the random-effects pooled estimate was 0.3904 (95% CI -0.1185 to 0.8992; p=0.1327), with considerable heterogeneity (I²=88.1%, τ²=0.258959). A fixed-effects model yielded an MD of -0.1450 (95% CI -0.2491 to -0.0408).

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The principal quantitative result under a random-effects model suggests no statistically significant overall difference in the evaluated offspring birth outcome(s) between PCOS and non-PCOS groups (pooled MD 0.3904; 95% CI -0.1185 to 0.8992; p=0.133). In contrast, fixed-effects modeling indicated a modest adverse difference in offspring outcomes among PCOS pregnancies (MD -0.1450; 95% CI -0.2491 to -0.0408). While the MD scale reflects continuous outcomes (most plausibly mean birthweight or standardized birthweight), the absolute magnitude of the fixed-effect estimate is small. The divergent conclusions between random- and fixed-effects models, combined with high heterogeneity (I²=88%), indicate that any association varies substantially across studies and that the summary effect is not robust to model specification.

Individual study estimates mirror this inconsistency. Two contemporary cohorts reported lower mean outcomes among PCOS offspring: Unknown (2024) (N=390/68,708) found MD -0.2600 (95% CI -0.3800 to -0.1400), and Unknown (2025) (N=65/65) reported MD -0.4046 (95% CI -0.7519 to -0.0573). Conversely, several studies indicated higher risk of adverse binary outcomes in PCOS: Unknown (2005) (N=47/180) reported an effect of 1.6335 (95% CI 1.4903 to 17.6038), Unknown (2003) (N=38/136) reported 1.4258 (95% CI 1.3597 to 12.7359), and Unknown (2018) reported 2.3000 (95% CI 0.3000 to 4.3000). Unknown (2024) (N=985/1,970) presented an effect of 0.4121 with a confidence interval of 1.2600 to 1.8200; interpreted as a log odds ratio, this would be consistent with an odds ratio around 1.51 (95% CI ~1.26 to 1.82), indicating increased odds of an adverse outcome. Three additional studies (Unknown 2023, N=75/94; Unknown 2025 nested case–control, N=1,678/6,712; and Unknown 2024 observational, N=NR/NR) did not provide sufficient data for effect computation. Taken together, individual studies variously suggest modest reductions in mean birthweight or increased odds of adverse outcomes such as preterm birth, small for gestational age, or neonatal unit admission in PCOS pregnancies, but findings are not uniform.

Heterogeneity is substantial and likely driven by multiple factors. First, outcomes differ across studies (continuous mean birthweight versus binary outcomes including preterm birth, SGA, NICU admission, perinatal mortality), and the extracted effect metrics appear mixed (MDs vs odds ratios or possibly log odds ratios). Although the quantitative synthesis used MD as the effect measure, the available study-level data suggest that some effects represent relative risks/odds for binary outcomes, which would not be directly compatible with a MD model without transformation. Second, study design and size varied widely, from small retrospective series (e.g., N=38/136; 47/180) to a large cohort contrasting 390 PCOS pregnancies with 68,708 non-PCOS pregnancies. Under fixed-effects, very large studies can dominate the pooled estimate, whereas random-effects upweight smaller studies—potentially explaining the sign reversal between models if smaller studies trend toward increased risk but the largest cohort(s) suggest minimal or modestly negative differences. Third, confounding control likely varied (e.g., adjustment for maternal BMI, gestational diabetes mellitus, hypertensive disorders, smoking, mode of conception), and PCOS diagnostic criteria and phenotypes may not have been consistent across studies and time periods (2003–2025), all of which can materially affect observed associations.

Author conclusions, as inferred from the direction and precision of estimates, were generally consistent with each study’s point estimates: studies reporting negative MDs concluded that PCOS was associated with lower mean birthweight, while studies reporting elevated odds (or log odds) concluded that PCOS increased the risk of adverse outcomes. However, explicit author-claimed causal inferences and adjustment strategies were not available in the extracted data for most studies, limiting our ability to fully reconcile study-level interpretations with the pooled results. The discrepancy between model-based pooled estimates and the heterogeneous individual study findings underscores the importance of outcome harmonization and standardized reporting.

Given the lack of a coherent, low-heterogeneity signal and the internal inconsistency of the pooled models, the current evidence does not support a precise pooled estimate across all offspring outcomes. The most defensible interpretation is that offspring of women with PCOS may experience small reductions in mean birthweight in some settings, and may have higher odds of certain adverse outcomes in others, but the magnitude and even the direction of association are highly context-dependent.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across nine observational studies (≥81,143 dyads with reported sample sizes), evidence linking maternal PCOS to adverse offspring birth outcomes is inconsistent. The random-effects meta-analysis did not show a statistically significant overall difference (MD 0.3904; 95% CI -0.1185 to 0.8992; p=0.1327), while a fixed-effects model suggested a small adverse difference (MD -0.1450; 95% CI -0.2491 to -0.0408). High heterogeneity (I²=88.1%) and mixed effect metrics limit confidence in a unified summary estimate. The balance of evidence points to possible modest reductions in mean birthweight and potentially increased odds of some adverse outcomes in PCOS, but effect sizes vary and are sensitive to study design and adjustment.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include incomplete extraction of author conclusions and lack of a formal risk-of-bias assessment for included studies, precluding grading of certainty. The quantitative synthesis appears to pool studies that likely reported different effect metrics (mean differences for continuous outcomes and odds ratios/log odds for binary outcomes), which can bias or inflate heterogeneity if not carefully transformed and harmonized. Publication bias and small-study effects were not assessed.

Study-level limitations are substantial. Several studies were small and retrospective (e.g., N=38/136; 47/180), increasing susceptibility to selection bias and residual confounding. PCOS definitions, phenotyping, and ascertainment likely varied over the 2003–2025 period, as did clinical management (e.g., metformin use, assisted reproduction), potentially modifying risk profiles. Adjustment for key confounders (maternal BMI, gestational diabetes, hypertensive disorders, smoking, parity, and conception mode) was not available in the extracted data and may differ across studies. Outcome definitions (e.g., preterm birth thresholds, SGA standards, NICU admission criteria) and reporting formats likely varied, complicating comparability. Three studies did not provide computable effect estimates, limiting the usable evidence base.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: The current evidence does not support a definitive, generalized increase in adverse offspring birth outcomes solely attributable to maternal PCOS. Clinicians should continue individualized risk assessment, recognizing that women with PCOS often have coexisting risk factors (e.g., obesity, insulin resistance, gestational diabetes, hypertensive disorders) that are established drivers of preterm birth, fetal growth abnormalities, and NICU admission. Pragmatic implications include vigilant screening and management of modifiable mediators (preconception weight optimization, glycemic control, blood pressure management) and appropriate fetal growth and preterm birth surveillance aligned with existing obstetric guidelines, rather than changes based solely on PCOS status.

For research: Future studies should (1) standardize PCOS exposure definitions (e.g., Rotterdam vs NIH criteria, phenotype stratification), (2) prespecify and harmonize offspring outcomes (mean birthweight in grams, z-score, SGA by INTERGROWTH or WHO standards; standardized definitions for preterm birth, NICU admissions, perinatal mortality), and (3) adjust comprehensively for confounders and mediators (BMI, GDM, hypertensive disorders, smoking, ART use). Large prospective cohorts and registry-based studies with transparent, granular confounder adjustment and stratified analyses (by BMI category, ART vs spontaneous conception, GDM status, PCOS phenotype) are needed. Reporting both absolute and relative risks and providing sufficient data for meta-analysis (counts, means/SDs, adjusted and unadjusted estimates) will enable robust synthesis. An individual participant data meta-analysis would be particularly valuable to reconcile heterogeneity and explore effect modification.
