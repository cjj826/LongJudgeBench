## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Five cross-sectional surveys published between 2010 and 2021 met inclusion criteria. Across these, at least 460 participants were reported (N=232 in one 2021 study; N=161 in a 2020 study; N=67 in a 2019 study), with sample sizes not reported in two studies (2010 and 2018). All studies were observational and conducted in dental care settings; specific participant roles (e.g., practitioners vs. students vs. staff) were not consistently reported in the extracted data. No interventional studies were identified. Three studies provided odds ratios (ORs), two did not provide computable effect sizes. Author conclusions were not available in the extracted dataset.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Overall, the evidence base is sparse, heterogeneously reported, and insufficient to draw definitive conclusions about the prevalence of sexual misconduct in dental settings, underreporting rates, the emotional or professional sequelae, or the effectiveness of targeted interventions. Three studies reported ORs: one 2010 survey reported an OR of 1.0682 with a 95% CI of 1.1130 to 7.6110, which is internally inconsistent because the confidence interval does not include the point estimate (suggesting a data extraction/transcription error and precluding reliable interpretation). Two later cross-sectional surveys reported ORs of 0.7660 (2018) and 0.9600 (2019), without confidence intervals. In the absence of variance estimates or p-values, these latter two effects cannot be interpreted as statistically significant or clinically meaningful. Importantly, the underlying exposure and outcome definitions corresponding to these effect sizes were not available in the extracted record (e.g., whether the OR compares odds of reporting an incident, experiencing an incident, or another correlate), limiting interpretation.

Heterogeneity across the included studies is substantial and multifaceted. First, populations likely varied (e.g., dentists, hygienists, assistants, students, educators, and/or patients within dental settings), but role stratification and sampling frames were not consistently reported. Second, operationalization of “sexual misconduct” almost certainly differed across surveys (e.g., sexual harassment, unwanted touching, coercion, patient-on-staff vs. staff-on-staff behaviors), yet the extracted data contained no standardized definitions or instruments, making cross-study comparisons tentative at best. Third, the outcomes of interest to this review—prevalence, underreporting, emotional trauma, professional disengagement, and intervention effectiveness—were either not measured or not reported in a way that could be abstracted; none of the included studies evaluated an intervention targeting sexual misconduct.

Given these limitations, we did not pool effect estimates. Although at least two studies reported ORs, heterogeneity in exposures/outcomes and the absence of confidence intervals (2018, 2019) or the presence of an implausible CI (2010) meant that meta-analysis would be inappropriate and potentially misleading. We therefore undertook a narrative synthesis. In qualitative terms, the reported ORs cluster near the null (0.77–0.96) with one apparently larger effect in 2010 that cannot be trusted due to internal inconsistency in the reported figures. Without clarity on the constructs measured, even this qualitative pattern is not informative about direction or magnitude of association relevant to our PICO.

Risk of bias considerations further temper confidence. All five studies were cross-sectional, relying on self-report of sensitive experiences subject to underreporting, social desirability bias, and recall error. Selection bias is plausible (e.g., nonresponse by those most affected), and confounding was not documented in the extracted summaries. Without study-level risk-of-bias appraisals, we cannot judge internal validity. The lack of reported author conclusions prevents triangulation between our synthesis and original interpretations; we cannot assess whether study authors cautioned similarly about measurement or sampling limitations.

In summary, the current evidence does not permit estimation of sexual misconduct prevalence, underreporting rates, or associated emotional/professional outcomes in dental care settings, nor does it speak to the effectiveness of preventive or remedial interventions. The few available effect estimates are insufficiently specified, imprecise, or internally inconsistent, and cannot support quantitative synthesis or actionable inference.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on five cross-sectional surveys (2010–2021; at least 460 participants, with additional numbers not reported), the available evidence is insufficient—in quantity, quality, and reporting detail—to quantify sexual misconduct prevalence or underreporting in dental settings, to estimate associated emotional trauma or professional disengagement, or to evaluate intervention effectiveness. Reported ORs (0.7660; 0.9600; and 1.0682 with an implausible CI of 1.1130–7.6110) are either near-null without precision estimates or internally inconsistent, precluding confident interpretation.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on a local corpus (96 records screened) that may not capture the full international literature, potential language or indexing bias, and incomplete data extraction in the included records (e.g., missing sample sizes, missing or inconsistent effect size details, absence of author conclusions). Because outcome definitions and statistical details were not consistently available, we could not compute standardized effect measures or perform sensitivity analyses. No study-level risk-of-bias assessments were available, limiting our ability to evaluate internal validity across studies.

Study-level limitations likely include cross-sectional designs that cannot establish temporality; small or non-representative samples (e.g., N=67 in one study; two studies with N not reported); self-reported measures of sensitive experiences prone to underreporting and social desirability bias; unclear or heterogeneous definitions of sexual misconduct; and inadequate reporting of confounder adjustment, precision (CIs, p-values), and outcome stratification (e.g., by role, gender, or setting). The absence of interventional studies leaves a critical gap regarding effective prevention and response strategies.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice and policy: This review does not provide evidence to support or refute specific interventions within dental settings. Given known risks of underreporting in sexual misconduct research generally, dental organizations should not infer low prevalence from this evidence gap. In the interim—and drawing on broader workplace harassment prevention principles rather than this review’s findings—dental settings may consider adopting clear codes of conduct; confidential, accessible reporting pathways; trauma-informed support for complainants; training on bystander intervention and boundary setting; use of chaperones when appropriate; and routine monitoring of climate and reporting metrics. These actions should be evaluated rigorously.

For research: Priority needs include (1) standardized definitions and validated instruments for sexual misconduct in dental contexts; (2) representative, adequately powered prevalence studies stratified by role (practitioners, staff, students), gender, and patient/staff perpetrator categories; (3) mixed-methods work to understand underreporting and barriers to disclosure; (4) longitudinal studies to quantify emotional trauma, mental health sequelae, and professional disengagement/turnover; and (5) rigorous evaluations of interventions (e.g., policy changes, reporting systems, training, environmental and organizational modifications), using controlled designs where feasible and reporting effect sizes with precision estimates. Preregistration, transparent reporting, and ethical safeguards (confidentiality, safety protocols) are essential to improve evidentiary value and protect participants.
