## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Very low-certainty evidence from one RCT suggests that an internet- or mobile-based mental health intervention may improve at least one continuous outcome relevant to adults with overweight/obesity and comorbid depressive symptoms (MD −3.10; 95% CI −4.30 to −1.90). ### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include the reliance on a single included trial with incomplete reporting, which precluded meta-analysis, assessment of heterogeneity, and formal grading of certainty. The extracted data did not specify the outcome scale, comparator details, follow-up duration, or sample size, leaving critical contextual information unavailable. Although the screening process excluded many protocols and secondary analyses (consistent with our focus on RCTs), it also underscores that the accessible evidence base for the precise PICO remains extremely sparse. Without risk-of-bias data, our synthesis cannot adjust for potential methodological shortcomings.

Study-level limitations (as far as can be inferred) are substantial due to missing information. We do not know if randomization was adequately concealed, whether outcome assessment was blinded, how missing data were addressed (e.g., intention-to-treat), or whether selective reporting occurred. Engagement and adherence, central to digital interventions, were not reported. The absence of a clearly defined outcome and scale prevents interpretation of clinical relevance (e.g., minimal clinically important differences for depression scales or absolute/percent weight loss), and we cannot determine whether effects persisted beyond the immediate post-intervention period.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current evidence is insufficient to support or refute routine use of internet- or mobile-based mental health interventions specifically to improve depressive symptoms and reduce weight among adults with overweight/obesity and comorbid depression. Clinicians considering such tools should view them, at best, as adjuncts within comprehensive, evidence-based care pathways, with close monitoring of both mental health and weight outcomes. Decisions should be individualized, accounting for patient preferences, digital literacy, access, and the availability of established treatments.

For research: There is a clear need for adequately powered, preregistered RCTs explicitly enrolling adults with overweight/obesity and comorbid depressive symptoms, comparing IMIs to well-characterized control conditions (e.g., usual care, attention control). Trials should prespecify co-primary outcomes—validated depression measures (e.g., PHQ-9) and objective or standardized weight outcomes (kg and percent change)—with sufficient follow-up (≥6–12 months) to assess durability. Core design features should include concealed allocation, blinded outcome assessment where feasible, intention-to-treat analyses with appropriate handling of missing data, and transparent reporting aligned with CONSORT-eHEALTH. Investigators should report engagement metrics, intervention components (e.g., guidance level), and conduct mediation/moderation analyses (e.g., baseline severity, BMI class, comorbidity burden). Economic evaluations and equity considerations (access, digital divide) are also needed. Finally, consistent reporting of outcome scales and units will enable future meta-analyses and meaningful judgments about clinical significance.
