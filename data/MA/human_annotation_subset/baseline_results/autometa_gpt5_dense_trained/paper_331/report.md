## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
We included 24 comparative studies (out of 89 screened; none excluded at full text) evaluating interventions for patients with Persistent Spinal Pain Syndrome Type 2 (PSPS‑T2) across publication years 2005–2024. Designs spanned randomized controlled trials (parallel-group, cross-over, single- and double-blind), equivalence trials, and comparative cohorts. Sample sizes ranged from small (e.g., N=24/26 in Unknown 2005; N=26/30 in Unknown 2022) to larger (N=110/108 in Unknown 2019), with several reporting numbers as NR. Although our a priori outcome was pain intensity as standardized mean difference (SMD), reporting heterogeneity meant that only 10 studies provided comparable dichotomous “responder” data (e.g., ≥50% pain relief), pooled as risk ratios (RR). The random-effects meta-analysis yielded RR=1.5583 (95% CI 0.4853 to 5.0043; p=0.4561), with considerable heterogeneity (I²=91.4%; τ²=2.4197). A fixed-effects model suggested a modest benefit (RR=1.2531; 95% CI 1.0393 to 1.5109).

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The core quantitative finding is mixed: under random-effects, the pooled responder RR of 1.56 was not statistically significant, whereas fixed-effects suggested a statistically significant but modest improvement (RR 1.25). This divergence reflects substantial between-study variability and underscores limited confidence in a single summary estimate. Individually, several trials favored neuromodulation (predominantly spinal cord stimulation, SCS) over conservative care or reoperation. For example, Unknown (2005; RCT, N=24/26) reported RR=1.4123 (95% CI 1.2804 to 13.1621), Unknown (2016; RCT, N=28/24) RR=2.3308 (95% CI 1.4405 to 73.4456), and Unknown (2019; RCT) RR=3.0134 (95% CI 2.8174 to 147.0925). Conversely, other trials suggested minimal or no between-group differences on continuous pain measures: Unknown (2019; double-blind cross-over) SMD=-0.0100 (95% CI -0.2200 to 0.2000). One randomized study with an open-label follow-up reported a large mean difference favoring neuromodulation (Unknown 2019 MD=-30.0; 95% CI -37.7 to -22.4), and another (Unknown 2014) reported a large point estimate (39.2) whose metric was not clearly specified. These mixed signals likely reflect differences in interventions (e.g., low-frequency tonic SCS, high-frequency or burst paradigms), comparators (conservative care, reoperation, injections), outcome metrics (responder vs continuous VAS/NRS), and trial designs (parallel vs cross-over; blinding; open-label phases).

Heterogeneity (I²=91.4%) is considerable, and potential sources include:
- Clinical diversity: variable SCS technologies and programming, differing comparators (e.g., epidural injections vs reoperation vs multimodal conservative care), and patient selection (e.g., leg- vs back-pain predominance).
- Methodological diversity: cross-over vs parallel-arm RCTs, open-label follow-up phases, and differing responder thresholds.
- Reporting diversity: continuous outcomes not convertible to SMD due to missing standard deviations or unclear units, and dichotomous outcomes with unusual confidence intervals (e.g., several trials with very wide CIs indicating imprecision).

Risk of bias was not formally assessed in this review, limiting interpretability. Several studies had small sample sizes (e.g., Unknown 2022 N=26/30 with RR=0.1431, 95% CI 0.5045 to 2.6392) and potentially unstable estimates. The presence of cross-over designs (Unknown 2019; SMD ~0) and open-label phases (Unknown 2019; MD ~-30) complicates attribution of effects solely to the intervention and may introduce performance and detection biases. In addition, the mismatch between our planned SMD outcome and available RR-based responder data constrains synthesis and may bias toward studies that defined categorical thresholds rather than continuous pain scores.

Where reported, study authors generally concluded that SCS provides clinically meaningful pain relief compared with conventional medical management or reoperation in selected FBSS/PSPS‑T2 populations (e.g., the earlier RCTs with RR>1.4–3.0 concluded superiority of neuromodulation). However, at least one double-blind cross-over study (Unknown 2019; SMD=-0.01) concluded no significant difference between tested modalities/programming on pain intensity, tempering generalizability. The fixed-effects meta-analytic suggestion of benefit aligns with pro-SCS conclusions in several trials, but the non-significant random-effects estimate and high heterogeneity emphasize that the magnitude—and consistency—of benefit across contemporary PSPS‑T2 populations remains uncertain. Evidence for other minimally invasive treatments and reoperation is comparatively sparse within our dataset and heterogeneously reported, precluding firm comparative inferences beyond suggesting that neuromodulation often outperforms conservative care in trials optimized for SCS.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 24 comparative studies, evidence for neuromodulation (primarily SCS) in PSPS‑T2 suggests potential pain benefits in selected patients, but pooled responder analyses under random-effects were not statistically significant (RR=1.5583; 95% CI 0.4853 to 5.0043; p=0.4561) and heterogeneity was high (I²=91.4%). A fixed-effects model indicated a modest benefit (RR=1.2531; 95% CI 1.0393 to 1.5109). Continuous pain outcomes were variably reported and often not combinable. Overall, while several RCTs conclude SCS is superior to conservative management or reoperation for pain, the strength and consistency of evidence across modalities and comparators are limited by methodological and reporting diversity.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on heterogeneous reporting that forced deviation from the prespecified SMD outcome to a responder-based RR meta-analysis for only 10 studies, leaving many trials unpooled due to non-computable data (e.g., missing SDs, unclear units). The lack of a formal risk-of-bias assessment reduces confidence in the synthesized estimates, and unusual or extremely wide confidence intervals in several studies suggest imprecision and potential extraction or reporting issues.

Study-level limitations include small sample sizes, variable blinding, cross-over designs that may not reflect routine practice, open-label follow-up phases that can inflate perceived benefit, and diverse comparators (conservative care regimens, injections, and reoperation) that hinder direct comparability. Patient heterogeneity (pain distribution, prior surgical history, psychological comorbidities) and differences in SCS technology/programming further limit generalization. Adverse event reporting and long-term durability of effects were insufficiently captured in the data provided.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: The current evidence indicates that SCS may offer pain relief for some PSPS‑T2 patients compared with conservative care or reoperation, consistent with several trial-level conclusions (e.g., Unknown 2005, 2016, 2019 reporting RR≈1.4–3.0). However, given the non-significant random-effects pooled result and pronounced heterogeneity, clinicians should individualize decisions, consider trial stimulation to gauge response, and integrate patient preferences, psychological readiness, and risk–benefit profiles. Conservative multimodal management remains appropriate, and reoperation should be reserved for clearly defined structural indications rather than pain alone.

For research: Future trials should standardize pain outcome measurement (continuous VAS/NRS with SMDs), define consistent responder thresholds, and ensure adequate sample sizes and blinding (including sham controls where feasible). Head-to-head comparisons across SCS paradigms (tonic, burst, high-frequency), epidural injections, structured conservative programs (physiotherapy plus cognitive-behavioral therapy), and reoperation are needed, with longer follow-up and comprehensive adverse event and quality-of-life reporting. Pre-registration, transparent data sharing, and risk-of-bias mitigation are essential to produce robust estimates and reduce heterogeneity. Harmonized reporting will allow more meaningful meta-analyses and clearer guidance for PSPS‑T2 care.
