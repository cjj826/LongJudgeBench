## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Seven single-arm clinical studies published between 2012 and 2025 met inclusion criteria, enrolling small cohorts of hypertensive patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and treatment-resistant hypertension who underwent endovascular, catheter-based radiofrequency renal denervation (RDN). Designs included case series, pilot studies, and retrospective or prospective cohorts with pre–post comparisons (sample sizes per study ranged from 11 to 54; approximate total n ≈ 199). Across studies, extractable within-patient effect sizes were available for four reports and generally reflected changes from baseline in blood pressure; data for ambulatory blood pressure, kidney function (estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR], serum creatinine), and procedure-related complications were variably reported and largely not extractable in a consistent form.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
Across the four studies with extractable quantitative effects, RDN was associated with reductions in blood pressure from baseline, most likely in office systolic blood pressure (SBP), though the specific outcome label was not consistently reported. Reported mean differences (MD) in SBP ranged from −33.0 mmHg (2012 single-arm case series, N=15) to −6.0 mmHg (2020 cohort, N=12; 95% CI −18.9 to 6.9), with intermediate estimates of −20.0 mmHg (2015 observational study, N=27) and −13.9 mmHg (2021 cohort, N=40). One study provided uncertainty bounds and included the null effect (−6.0 mmHg; 95% CI −18.9 to 6.9), indicating imprecision and the possibility of no clinically meaningful change in that cohort. For the remaining three included studies (2017 pilot, N=11; 2022 post hoc subgroup, N=54; 2025 retrospective cohort, N=40), quantitative effect sizes could not be computed from the available information. Collectively, the direction of effect suggests that RDN may lower office BP in CKD patients with resistant hypertension, but the magnitude and certainty are highly variable.

Heterogeneity in observed effects is substantial. Likely sources include differences in patient selection (CKD stage distribution and baseline BP severity), procedural technique (extent of ablation in main vs branch renal arteries, device iteration), operator experience, and follow-up duration. Medication regimens and adherence may have changed between baseline and follow-up; because all studies used pre–post single-arm designs, such co-interventions can confound the attribution of BP changes to RDN. Outcome definitions were also not uniform, with most extractable data appearing to reflect office SBP change; ambulatory BP, when reported qualitatively in the source corpus, was not provided in a way that allowed extraction of consistent mean differences and variance. Given that ambulatory BP effects in sham-controlled RDN trials are typically smaller than office BP effects, reliance on office measurements alone may overestimate the true antihypertensive effect.

Internal validity concerns inherent to pre–post single-arm studies limit causal inference. Regression to the mean, placebo effects, white-coat effect attenuation, and enhanced follow-up after an invasive procedure can all lower office BP independently of the neuroablative intervention. Without sham controls or parallel comparators, it is difficult to disentangle these factors. Only one included study reported a confidence interval; the lack of variance estimates in other reports precluded any meaningful pooling or sensitivity analyses. Although in principle a meta-analysis could be attempted for office SBP, we did not pool effects because outcomes and their measurement (office vs ambulatory, SBP vs DBP, follow-up timepoints) were not consistently specified, few studies reported uncertainty, and clinical heterogeneity was pronounced. Consequently, we cannot comment on robustness across fixed- and random-effects models.

Author conclusions could not be systematically synthesized from the dataset because explicit conclusion statements were not available for the included records. Based on the numerical findings and the inclusion of these reports as CKD, treatment-resistant cohorts, it is likely that most study authors interpreted RDN as feasible and potentially effective for lowering BP in this high-risk population, while calling for controlled, CKD-focused trials; however, we cannot verify or quote study-specific conclusions from the available information. Importantly, kidney safety outcomes (eGFR, serum creatinine) and procedural complications were not extractable in a standardized way across studies. Given the centrality of renal function and vascular safety in CKD, this represents a major evidence gap: neither short- nor longer-term renal function trajectories nor adverse event rates (e.g., renal artery stenosis, vascular access complications) could be quantified from the included reports.

Overall, the available evidence suggests potential BP reductions after RDN in CKD patients with resistant hypertension, but inconsistency in effect sizes (−33 to −6 mmHg), imprecision, and the absence of controlled comparisons or standardized renal outcomes substantially limit the strength of inference.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
In CKD patients with treatment-resistant hypertension, single-arm studies of catheter-based radiofrequency RDN report within-patient reductions in blood pressure from baseline (office SBP mean differences approximately −6 to −33 mmHg across four studies; one 95% CI included 0), but outcome definitions were inconsistent and precision was limited. Ambulatory BP, renal function (eGFR, creatinine), and procedural complication rates were not consistently extractable. The evidence is insufficient—both in quantity and quality—to draw confident conclusions about the magnitude of BP reduction, durability, renal safety, or net clinical benefit in this CKD population.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on a local corpus with incomplete reporting of key fields (e.g., explicit outcome labels, variance estimates, author conclusions). We were unable to compute effect sizes for three of seven studies and could not perform a formal risk-of-bias assessment due to lack of extractable domain-level information. Heterogeneity in reported outcomes (office vs ambulatory measurements, unclear timepoints) and the paucity of uncertainty measures precluded meta-analysis.

Study-level limitations are substantial. All included studies used single-arm pre–post designs without sham or control comparators, leaving results vulnerable to regression to the mean, placebo effects, and confounding by medication/intensity changes and adherence. Sample sizes were small (N=11–54), and CKD staging, baseline BP severity, and follow-up durations were not consistently detailed. Importantly, renal function outcomes and safety endpoints were variably reported and not amenable to synthesis, hindering assessment of whether RDN stabilizes, preserves, or harms kidney function in CKD. These factors collectively limit internal validity and generalizability.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current CKD-specific evidence does not support routine use of radiofrequency RDN outside of research or specialized centers for patients with resistant hypertension. While individual cohorts show sizeable office BP reductions (e.g., −20 to −33 mmHg in some reports), the absence of controlled data, inconsistent ambulatory BP outcomes, and unclear renal safety mean that clinicians should exercise caution. If considered, RDN should be offered within clinical trials or prospective registries with standardized follow-up, explicit medication stabilization protocols, and rigorous renal and vascular safety monitoring, accompanied by shared decision-making.

For research: High-priority needs include adequately powered, multicenter, sham-controlled randomized trials dedicated to CKD populations (e.g., stages 3–4) with resistant hypertension. Trials should prespecify 24-hour ambulatory SBP as the primary endpoint; collect serial office/ambulatory BP, eGFR, serum creatinine, albuminuria, and renal artery imaging; and adjudicate procedural and vascular complications with blinded core laboratories. Protocols should standardize ablation technique, report the number and location of ablations, and stabilize antihypertensive regimens to minimize confounding. Stratified analyses by CKD stage, baseline BP, and medication burden, with follow-up of at least 12–36 months, are essential to evaluate durability and renal outcomes (including progression to kidney failure and cardiovascular events). Harmonized reporting (means, SDs, CIs) will enable future meta-analyses and clearer guidance for practice.
