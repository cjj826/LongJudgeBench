The pattern of exclusions helps explain why no effect sizes could be calculated. First, many sodium-related publications evaluate intermediate outcomes (notably blood pressure) or use designs (cross-sectional, modeling) that cannot support causal inference on clinical endpoints and therefore did not match our outcome and design criteria. Second, several studies centered on disease-specific populations—particularly CKD cohorts—where baseline risk, clinical management, and dietary counseling differ substantially from the general population, limiting external validity for our question. Third, a frequent shortcoming was the lack of a clearly defined comparator that met guideline-relevant thresholds: studies either did not quantify sodium intake with sufficient precision or did not include a group at or below 2 g/day sodium (<5 g/day salt). Accurate quantification at low intake levels is technically demanding and typically requires repeated 24-hour urinary sodium collections; reliance on spot urines or dietary questionnaires often leads to misclassification, especially near the lower end of intake distribution. This likely contributed to the scarcity of eligible studies.

Because no studies met inclusion, we could not examine heterogeneity across populations, settings, or exposure definitions; compare fixed- versus random-effects models; or perform subgroup and sensitivity analyses. We also could not test for small-study effects or publication bias. The planned meta-analytic framework (RR as the effect measure) could not be implemented because pooling requires ≥2 studies with extractable effect sizes. Importantly, we did not synthesize or weigh author conclusions from excluded records. Although some excluded studies—such as modeling analyses or CKD-specific cohorts—often conclude that sodium reduction is beneficial, their populations, exposures, or designs were not congruent with our PICO and therefore cannot be used to infer effects in the general adult population at the specified intake threshold.

The absence of eligible evidence should not be misinterpreted as evidence of no effect. Rather, it reflects a mismatch between our clinically relevant question—focused on hard outcomes in general populations at explicitly low sodium intakes—and the available primary research. Long-term randomized trials that achieve and maintain <2 g/day sodium are logistically challenging, and observational cohorts with repeated high-quality sodium assessment are uncommon. This feasibility barrier may explain why policy recommendations often rely on blood pressure effects and pathophysiological plausibility rather than direct general-population outcome trials at the guideline threshold. ### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations: The search and retrieval were conducted within a predefined local corpus. Although we screened all 107 records at the title/abstract level, no records progressed to full-text assessment, which increases the risk that potentially eligible studies with insufficiently informative abstracts were missed. We did not supplement the corpus with additional databases, trial registries, or grey literature, and we could not perform forward/backward citation chasing from included studies because none qualified. These factors may limit comprehensiveness.

Study-level limitations in the field: Because no studies were included, we cannot comment on internal validity of eligible evidence. However, the exclusion patterns highlight pervasive methodological challenges in sodium research that future studies must address: precise exposure measurement (preferably multiple non-consecutive 24-hour urinary sodium assessments), ensuring the presence of a comparator at the guideline-relevant threshold (<2 g/day sodium), adequate follow-up for clinical outcomes, and rigorous control for confounding (e.g., overall diet quality, potassium intake, energy intake, comorbidity, socioeconomic factors). Reverse causation (e.g., ill individuals reducing sodium) and measurement error are particularly concerning and can bias associations if not preemptively managed.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice and policy: This review does not provide new, outcome-based evidence to support, refine, or contradict current sodium intake recommendations for the general adult population. Clinicians and policymakers should recognize that guideline targets for sodium are largely derived from intermediate outcomes (notably blood pressure) and mechanistic rationale rather than direct evidence of clinical endpoints at <2 g/day sodium in general populations. Until higher-quality direct evidence becomes available, practice should continue to be informed by the broader body of evidence and public health considerations, acknowledging that this review neither strengthens nor weakens existing recommendations.

For research: There is a pressing need for primary studies that align with guideline thresholds and our PICO. Priorities include:
- Large, well-characterized prospective cohorts with repeated 24-hour urinary sodium measurements that explicitly compare participants achieving <2 g/day sodium against higher intake categories, with prespecified analyses of CVD, CKD progression, osteoporosis, physical performance, and mortality.
- Pragmatic randomized or cluster-randomized interventions (e.g., reformulation or procurement policies) with robust exposure assessment, fidelity monitoring, and long-term follow-up, enabling intention-to-treat inference.
- Quasi-experimental evaluations of population-level sodium reduction policies using difference-in-differences designs, linking changes in exposure to clinically meaningful endpoints.
- Standardized reporting of effect measures (RR/HR with 95% CIs), clear exposure definitions anchored to <2 g/day sodium, and sensitivity analyses addressing reverse causation and measurement error.
- Exploration of effect modification by age, sex, baseline blood pressure, comorbidities, and concurrent dietary factors (e.g., potassium), to inform targeted guidance.

Addressing these gaps would move the evidence base from intermediate measures toward clinically decisive endpoints in the populations to whom guidelines are applied.
