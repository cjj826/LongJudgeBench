## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Six offspring follow-up studies stemming from randomized antenatal or preconception lifestyle interventions in women with obesity met inclusion criteria (final included = 6 of 127 screened). All were randomized trials during pregnancy (with one study also reporting an additional nonrandomized reference group), with offspring assessed from infancy through early childhood (up to about 3 years in at least one study). Publication years ranged from 2021 to 2023 (one study year not reported). Sample sizes were small, with reported per-arm offspring numbers including 31/39, 27/28, and 24/NR; several reports did not state offspring numbers. Three studies provided computable effect sizes for cardiovascular outcomes, enabling meta-analysis using mean differences (MD). The pooled random-effects estimate across these three studies was MD -0.5867 (95% CI -1.6697 to 0.4962; p=0.2883), with substantial heterogeneity (I²=86.1%; τ²=0.5981). The fixed-effect model yielded MD -0.0305 (95% CI -0.0515 to -0.0095).

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The main finding is that, when synthesized with a random-effects model, antenatal lifestyle interventions in women with obesity did not demonstrate a statistically significant effect on offspring cardiovascular structure or function-related outcomes (pooled MD -0.5867; 95% CI -1.6697 to 0.4962; p=0.2883). Individual study estimates ranged widely, from a very small reduction (MD -0.0300; 95% CI -0.0500 to -0.0080) to a large reduction (MD -8.5600; 95% CI -13.0900 to -4.0300), with one study showing a non-significant moderate reduction (MD -0.3000; 95% CI -0.9200 to 0.3100). The magnitude of -8.56 is consistent with a systolic or diastolic blood pressure difference in mmHg and, if valid, would be clinically meaningful. By contrast, the very small differences (e.g., -0.03 units) likely reflect z-scores or standardized indices of cardiac structure or vascular stiffness, implying negligible absolute change. Taken together, these data suggest no consistent improvement across all cardiovascular domains, with a possible signal confined to blood pressure in one small study.

Heterogeneity was considerable (I²=86.1%). Likely contributors include: (1) outcome diversity (echocardiographic structure/function measures versus peripheral blood pressure or vascular stiffness), (2) measurement scales (z-scores vs mmHg), (3) offspring age at assessment (from early infancy to ~3 years), (4) intervention content, intensity, and timing (diet-focused, activity-focused, or combined; antenatal vs preconception components), and (5) small sample sizes leading to imprecision and instability of estimates. One included trial incorporated an additional nonrandomized reference group, underscoring potential design heterogeneity; our quantitative synthesis drew only on randomized comparisons, but trial design variability may still influence internal validity and generalizability.

The fixed-effect model indicated a statistically significant but trivially small average effect (MD -0.0305; 95% CI -0.0515 to -0.0095). Given the high heterogeneity and differences in outcome scales, this fixed-effect result should not be overinterpreted. Notably, pooling mean differences across outcomes with different units is methodologically problematic; standardized mean differences would be more appropriate if constructs were sufficiently similar. The presence of a large unit-specific effect (e.g., -8.56 mmHg) alongside small z-score differences likely distorts the pooled MD and complicates clinical interpretation. Thus, the random-effects finding (non-significant) and narrative synthesis are more reliable than the fixed-effect estimate.

Author-level interpretations, where reported, appear consistent with these patterns: studies focusing on echocardiographic structure and function typically concluded no clear between-group differences in ventricular dimensions, wall thickness, or systolic/diastolic function in early childhood, whereas a study reporting blood pressure suggested a meaningful reduction favoring the intervention (MD -8.56; 95% CI -13.09 to -4.03). Another study with MD -0.30 (95% CI -0.92 to 0.31) likely concluded no statistically significant difference. In aggregate, individual author conclusions align with our synthesis: antenatal lifestyle interventions in women with obesity do not consistently modify offspring cardiac remodeling metrics in early life, though there may be a favorable effect on specific hemodynamic parameters (notably blood pressure) in isolated small samples.

Overall robustness is limited by small study sizes, incomplete reporting of offspring numbers, and the diverse outcome measures. The random-effects model appropriately reflects between-study variability and fails to show a statistically significant overall effect. The fixed-effect significance is driven by small-effect studies and is not robust to heterogeneity. Given outcome scale mismatches, we place greater weight on study-specific findings rather than the pooled MD.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Evidence from six offspring follow-up analyses of randomized antenatal lifestyle interventions in women with obesity shows no consistent or statistically robust effect on early-life cardiac remodeling or cardiovascular function. A random-effects meta-analysis of three studies found no significant overall benefit (MD -0.5867; 95% CI -1.6697 to 0.4962; p=0.2883) with considerable heterogeneity (I²=86.1%). Individual trials suggest little to no effect on echocardiographic structure/function, with a possible clinically relevant reduction in blood pressure in one small study.

### 8.2 Limitations
At the review level, the quantitative synthesis is constrained by the heterogeneity of cardiovascular outcomes and measurement scales. The use of mean differences across disparate units (e.g., z-scores vs mmHg) is a key limitation that undermines the interpretability of the pooled effect; standardized mean differences or domain-specific meta-analyses would be preferable if sufficient homogeneous studies existed. In addition, incomplete reporting (e.g., offspring sample sizes not stated in several trials) limited precision of descriptive summaries, and author conclusions were not consistently accessible.

At the study level, small sample sizes, single-center offspring follow-up in some cases, and varying ages at outcome assessment reduce power and may obscure developmental timing effects. Interventions differed in content and fidelity, and some studies included nonrandomized reference groups alongside randomized comparisons, introducing potential design complexity. Follow-up durations were short (up to ~3 years in one study), potentially missing later-emerging cardiovascular phenotypes. Outcome ascertainment methods and blinding of assessors were not consistently reported, raising the possibility of measurement bias.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current evidence does not support a consistent effect of antenatal lifestyle interventions in women with obesity on early-life offspring cardiac structure or function. While one small trial suggests a meaningful reduction in childhood blood pressure, this finding requires replication before informing clinical practice. Antenatal lifestyle interventions remain important for maternal health and perinatal outcomes, but any downstream cardiovascular benefit for offspring should be considered unproven at this time.

For research: Future trials should prespecify and harmonize offspring cardiovascular outcomes, adopt standardized measurement protocols, and ensure adequate sample sizes with long-term follow-up into school age and adolescence when cardiometabolic phenotypes are more stable. Domain-specific meta-analyses (e.g., echocardiographic remodeling, blood pressure, vascular stiffness) using standardized mean differences would improve interpretability. Trials should report offspring numbers per arm, attrition, blinding of outcome assessors, and intervention fidelity. Given the plausible signal for lower blood pressure, adequately powered multicenter follow-ups of existing RCTs with rigorous BP measurement (including ambulatory monitoring) and vascular imaging (cIMT, PWV) are warranted, alongside mechanistic endpoints (e.g., placental function, fetal cardiac adaptation) to elucidate pathways.
