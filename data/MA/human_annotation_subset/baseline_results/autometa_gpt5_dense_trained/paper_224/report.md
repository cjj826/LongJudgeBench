## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
We included 11 controlled studies (publication years 1979–2017) evaluating biofeedback for blood pressure reduction in adults with hypertension. Designs ranged from randomized controlled trials (including several double‑blind, sham/contingency–controlled trials) to controlled nonrandomized studies and multi‑arm parallel trials. Sample sizes were generally small; for studies reporting group counts, examples include 20/18 (2007), 28/28 (1997), and 16/NR (1998). Only three trials provided sufficient quantitative data for meta‑analysis. Across these three, the pooled mean difference (MD) in blood pressure was 3.43 mmHg (95% CI −2.97 to 9.83; p=0.293), with considerable heterogeneity (I²=84.3%). A fixed‑effects model yielded an essentially null estimate (MD 0.31 mmHg; 95% CI −0.72 to 1.34). The remaining eight trials were included qualitatively but did not contribute extractable effect estimates.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The main quantitative finding is that, when pooling the three trials with usable data, biofeedback did not demonstrate a statistically significant reduction in blood pressure compared with controls (random‑effects MD 3.43 mmHg, 95% CI −2.97 to 9.83). The direction of effect varied across studies: a small randomized trial from 2007 suggested a clinically relevant benefit (MD 8.50 mmHg; 95% CI 3.83 to 13.17), whereas a double‑blind, sham‑controlled trial from 2013 reported a non‑significant difference (MD 2.35 mmHg; 95% CI −6.50 to 11.20), and a 1997 RCT was essentially null (MD −0.15 mmHg with a confidence interval reported as 0.30 to 2.50, indicating internal inconsistency in the source data but overall a negligible effect). Taken together, these results are more consistent with no clear, reproducible antihypertensive effect of biofeedback beyond control conditions, though a benefit in selected contexts cannot be excluded.

Heterogeneity was considerable (I²=84.3%, τ²=25.12), indicating important between‑study differences. Plausible sources include: biofeedback modality (e.g., heart rate variability [HRV] biofeedback vs electromyographic [EMG] muscle tension biofeedback), differences in control conditions (wait‑list or standard care vs active relaxation vs sham/contingency feedback), baseline hypertension severity and medication use, and variable intervention intensity and duration. Notably, the 2013 double‑blind, sham‑controlled design—methodologically stronger for isolating specific biofeedback effects—showed no significant advantage over sham, whereas the 2007 positive result may have been more vulnerable to nonspecific effects (e.g., attention, expectancy, relaxation). The fixed‑effects model (MD 0.31 mmHg; 95% CI −0.72 to 1.34) further suggests that when weighting more precise studies, the average effect approximates zero, with the random‑effects signal driven by between‑study variability.

Formal study‑level risk of bias was not extracted in this review, but study reports indicate several limitations common in behavioral intervention trials. Older trials (late 1970s–1990s) frequently had unclear randomization and allocation concealment, limited blinding, and small sample sizes. Only a subset used sham or “modified contingency placebo” controls to address expectancy and placebo effects. Several multi‑arm trials combined biofeedback with relaxation or medication, complicating attribution of effects. These features likely inflate heterogeneity and reduce confidence in pooled estimates. Small trials with promising results are at greater risk of overestimation due to bias and imprecision; conversely, better‑controlled trials (e.g., double‑blind sham) tended to report null findings, which is informative for distinguishing specific biofeedback effects from nonspecific influences.

Where author interpretations were reported, they were broadly aligned with study‑level findings. The 2007 RCT authors concluded that biofeedback (likely HRV‑based) reduced blood pressure meaningfully relative to control, consistent with the MD 8.50 mmHg (95% CI 3.83 to 13.17). In contrast, the 2013 double‑blind, sham‑controlled trial authors interpreted their results as showing no significant blood pressure reduction beyond sham (MD 2.35 mmHg; 95% CI −6.50 to 11.20). The 1997 RCT reported no meaningful difference relative to control, consistent with the near‑zero effect. This pattern—positive findings in smaller, less stringently controlled contexts and attenuation toward null with rigorous sham controls—suggests that any true antihypertensive effect of biofeedback is likely small and/or highly context‑dependent.

Overall, the evidence base is limited by incomplete reporting and variability in interventions and comparators. While one or two small trials suggest potentially clinically important reductions for some patients, the balance of controlled evidence, including sham‑controlled data, does not demonstrate a consistent, statistically significant effect on clinic blood pressure.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 11 controlled trials of biofeedback for hypertension, only three provided extractable data, yielding a non‑significant pooled effect on blood pressure (random‑effects MD 3.43 mmHg; 95% CI −2.97 to 9.83; p=0.293; I²=84.3%). A fixed‑effects model was essentially null (MD 0.31 mmHg; 95% CI −0.72 to 1.34). Individual trials were mixed: one small RCT (2007) suggested a clinically relevant reduction (MD 8.50 mmHg), while a double‑blind, sham‑controlled trial (2013) and a 1997 RCT showed no meaningful benefit. Given heterogeneity, small samples, and limited use of sham controls, the current evidence does not support a reliable, specific antihypertensive effect of biofeedback beyond control conditions.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review‑level limitations include incomplete outcome extraction from older, variably reported trials; absence of a formal, study‑by‑study risk‑of‑bias assessment; and insufficient data to stratify effects by modality (HRV vs EMG), by outcome (systolic vs diastolic), or by setting (clinic vs ambulatory/home). Only three studies contributed to meta‑analysis, precluding assessment of publication bias and limiting moderator analyses. The available effect coding suggests potential inconsistencies (e.g., the 1997 CI not centered on the point estimate), warranting cautious interpretation.

Study‑level limitations were common: small sample sizes, short follow‑up, variable comparators (standard care, relaxation, or sham), and multi‑component interventions that made isolating the biofeedback effect challenging. Blinding was rare except in the 2013 sham‑controlled trial. Several studies were conducted decades ago with unclear randomization, allocation concealment, and outcome measurement protocols. Many relied on clinic blood pressure without standardized ambulatory or home monitoring, raising concerns about measurement variability and regression to the mean.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: On current evidence, biofeedback cannot be recommended as a standalone antihypertensive treatment to lower blood pressure beyond standard care or well‑matched controls. Clinicians may consider biofeedback as an adjunctive stress‑management tool for motivated patients, with the understanding that any blood pressure benefit is uncertain and likely modest. Biofeedback should not replace guideline‑directed lifestyle modification and pharmacotherapy. If used, set realistic expectations, monitor blood pressure with validated home or ambulatory devices, and integrate with comprehensive cardiovascular risk management.

For research: Future trials should be adequately powered, preregistered RCTs using standardized, well‑described biofeedback protocols (e.g., HRV biofeedback parameters), with rigorous controls (attention‑matched relaxation and sham/contingency feedback) to isolate specific effects. Outcomes should include 24‑hour ambulatory and home blood pressure, with prespecified primary endpoints for both systolic and diastolic pressures, and follow‑up extending beyond 3–6 months to assess durability. Trials should report allocation methods, blinding procedures, adherence, intervention “dose,” and concomitant medication management. Mechanistic measures (e.g., HRV indices, baroreflex sensitivity) should be collected to link physiological targets to clinical outcomes. Subgroup analyses (e.g., baseline stress, autonomic function, medication status) and economic evaluations would clarify who, if anyone, benefits meaningfully and at what cost.
