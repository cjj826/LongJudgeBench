### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The principal finding of this review is the paucity of empirical evidence on barriers and facilitators to engagement with synchronous (real-time) remote weight management interventions among adults with BMI ≥27.5 kg/m². Heterogeneity could not be assessed. With a single, qualitatively designed study and no reported sample size, we cannot examine variability across intervention modalities (e.g., videoconference vs telephone), provider types, session frequency, or participant characteristics (e.g., baseline BMI, comorbidities, digital literacy). We also cannot evaluate whether engagement barriers/facilitators differ by context (primary care, community, or commercial programs), or by equity-relevant factors (age, sex, socioeconomic status, race/ethnicity). These potential sources of heterogeneity remain important hypotheses for future work but are not addressable with the available data.

The limited reporting in the included study also impedes risk-of-bias interpretation. Qualitative evaluations can provide rich, contextualized insights into engagement; however, their credibility depends on transparent sampling strategies (e.g., purposive vs convenience), reflexivity of the research team, analytic procedures (coding reliability, audit trails), and evidence of thematic saturation. None of these details were extractable from the corpus record. The unknown participant number further undermines our ability to judge the density and transferability of the evidence. Without these methodological anchors, any inferences about barriers or facilitators would be speculative.

Author conclusions from the included study were not available in the retrieved record; thus, we cannot present the study team’s own synthesis of barriers and facilitators or their mapping to COM-B. Based on the described method (focus groups with thematic network analysis), it is likely the authors generated thematic clusters regarding engagement experiences in synchronous remote care, but the specific content of those themes (e.g., digital literacy as capability, scheduling flexibility as opportunity, perceived efficacy as motivation) was not provided. As a result, we cannot compare our planned synthesis to the authors’ interpretations or assess convergence/divergence with our review questions.

Robustness checks were not feasible. With a single qualitative study and no extractable numeric outcomes, there is no basis for fixed- versus random-effects comparisons, sensitivity analyses, or publication bias assessments. The most defensible interpretation is that the current, accessible evidence is insufficient to characterize which factors most strongly facilitate or hinder engagement with synchronous remote weight management among adults with excess weight or obesity. This evidence gap appears to stem from two features of the literature we screened: many studies focused on effectiveness without reporting empirical barriers/facilitators; others examined asynchronous or pediatric populations; and several were reviews rather than primary studies. Together, these exclusions highlight a narrow and weak primary evidence base on our specific question rather than a negative finding about engagement determinants per se.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
There is insufficient empirical evidence to determine which factors facilitate or hinder engagement with synchronous, remote weight management interventions among adults with BMI ≥27.5 kg/m². Only one qualitative study (2021) met inclusion criteria, with no reported sample size and no extractable data to map barriers/facilitators to COM-B or to estimate effect sizes. As such, no confident conclusions can be drawn regarding specific determinants of engagement in this context.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include the extremely small evidence base after applying eligibility criteria tailored to synchronous, real-time interventions and explicit engagement determinants. Of 86 records, most were excluded due to wrong outcomes (effectiveness only; feasibility/acceptability without empirical barriers/facilitators), wrong study design (systematic/scoping reviews), wrong intervention (asynchronous modalities), or wrong population (adolescents/pediatric). It is possible that relevant primary studies exist outside the local corpus or use terminology that obscured eligibility at screening. In addition, the absence of full reporting in the included study limited data extraction.

Study-level limitations are substantial. The included qualitative evaluation did not report the number or characteristics of participants in the accessible record and provided no extractable thematic results or author conclusions. Without details on sampling, context, analytic procedures, or saturation, we cannot appraise credibility, dependability, or transferability. The lack of quantitative engagement metrics (e.g., session attendance rates, dropout, interaction counts) further precludes triangulation between qualitative themes and behavioral data, and it prevents any estimation of effect size.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice, current evidence does not identify specific, empirically supported strategies to enhance engagement with synchronous remote weight management for adults with excess weight or obesity. Clinicians and program designers should continue to use established behavior change frameworks (e.g., COM-B, motivational interviewing) and apply individualized approaches that consider digital access, scheduling constraints, and patient preferences. Routine collection of engagement metrics (attendance, retention, interaction duration) and brief, structured feedback on barriers/facilitators in real-world programs could generate practice-based evidence until stronger research emerges.

For research, there is a clear need for well-reported primary studies focused specifically on engagement determinants in synchronous remote interventions. Priority studies include mixed-methods designs that: (1) define and operationalize engagement consistently; (2) prospectively measure engagement behaviors; (3) collect qualitative data to map barriers/facilitators onto COM-B; and (4) report participant characteristics to enable equity analyses. Comparative studies should examine modality (video vs telephone), provider type, scheduling models, and supportive components (reminders, digital literacy assistance, social support). Future reports should follow TIDieR for intervention description and COREQ for qualitative methods, preregister protocols, and provide sufficient detail for risk-of-bias appraisal and synthesis.
