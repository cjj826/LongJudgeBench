## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Nineteen before–after LVSG studies met inclusion criteria (publication years 2012–2025), spanning prospective and retrospective cohorts, one nonrandomized comparative trial, and one RCT with noncomputable data. Usable quantitative data for meta-analysis were available from 9 studies; the remaining 10 reported relevant outcomes but without extractable pre–post statistics. Across studies, at least 651 participants contributed to the evidence base (sum of samples with reported N: 73, 69, 26, 45, 21, 18, 60, 100, 153, 56, 30; several cohorts did not report N). Outcomes included lower esophageal sphincter pressure (LESP), lower esophageal sphincter length (LESL), DeMeester Score (DMS), and BMI. The pooled standardized mean difference (SMD) across studies with computable data was -0.4564 (95% CI -1.7975 to 0.8846; p=0.5047) under a random-effects model, with considerable heterogeneity (I²=97.3%, τ²=3.825). A fixed-effect model yielded a small-to-moderate mean change (SMD -0.3959, 95% CI -0.6007 to -0.1912), but the between-study heterogeneity limits interpretability.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The primary quantitative finding is that, when pre- to post-LVSG differences in LES metrics, DMS, and BMI were pooled as SMDs, the random-effects estimate did not demonstrate a statistically significant overall change (SMD -0.4564, 95% CI -1.7975 to 0.8846; p=0.505). The very high heterogeneity (I²=97.3%) indicates that study results varied in both magnitude and direction. This is also evident in individual estimates: several studies reported large negative SMDs consistent with marked postoperative decreases in measured outcomes (e.g., -2.6434 [95% CI -3.2089 to -2.0778], -2.9165 [-3.4305 to -2.4025], -1.8857 [-2.6127 to -1.1588], and -4.0000 [-8.3000 to 0.2000]), while others showed postoperative increases (e.g., 1.0133 [95% CI 0.4757 to 1.5508] and 1.0649 [0.6826 to 1.4473]). Taken together, these patterns likely reflect true clinical divergence across outcomes: BMI is expected to decline substantially after LVSG, whereas LESP/LESL may decrease and DMS may increase in some patients, yielding opposing directions when pooled as standardized differences.

The heterogeneity has plausible methodological and clinical sources. First, studies measured different outcomes (LESP, LESL, DMS, BMI) on different scales and at varying follow-up times; even with standardization, aggregating outcomes with different clinical desirability (e.g., lower BMI favorable; lower LESP unfavorable; lower DMS favorable) risks obscuring directionally coherent effects within each outcome. Second, surgical technique (bougie size, antral preservation, hiatal hernia repair, sleeve geometry), patient selection (baseline GERD, hiatal hernia, motility disorders), and testing protocols (upright/supine manometry, catheter systems, pH thresholds) likely contributed to variability. Third, data quality varied; in a few reports, confidence intervals appeared incongruent with point estimates (e.g., effects 1.7566 and 2.3567 with much larger CI bounds than expected), suggesting reporting or extraction errors that may inflate between-study variance.

Because risk of bias was not formally assessed, we interpret findings with caution. Most included studies were observational before–after cohorts, prone to confounding, regression to the mean, selection bias (who returns for postoperative HRM/pH), and protocol drift over time. The nonrandomized comparative trial and RCT did not contribute computable pre–post data. The fixed-effect model suggested a small-to-moderate mean change (SMD -0.3959, 95% CI -0.6007 to -0.1912), but given I²=97.3%, this estimate likely over-weights large, precise studies and underrepresents genuine between-study differences; as such, the random-effects result—no clear overall effect—is a more appropriate summary of the mixed evidence.

Authors’ conclusions, as reported in the included studies, were not uniform. Several prospective before–after cohorts interpreting HRM and pH-metry concluded that LVSG may reduce LESP/LESL and increase esophageal acid exposure (higher DMS), potentially predisposing to de novo or worsened GERD despite weight loss. Conversely, other studies reported no significant deterioration or even improvements in objective reflux metrics and/or symptoms, attributing favorable findings to careful technique or concurrent hiatal hernia repair. Studies with pronounced negative SMDs likely captured substantial postoperative BMI reductions, whereas studies with positive SMDs likely reflected increased DMS or other reflux-related measures post-LVSG. The divergence between individual study interpretations and the overall null random-effects estimate underscores that conclusions are sensitive to which outcome is emphasized and to study-specific contexts.

In practice, these results mean that while LVSG reliably induces weight loss, its effects on esophagogastric junction physiology and acid exposure are inconsistent across studies. The current meta-analytic signal does not support a single, universal effect direction for LES function or acid exposure after LVSG when aggregated with BMI, and the high heterogeneity suggests that patient- and technique-level factors critically shape outcomes.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across 19 before–after LVSG studies (≥651 participants), the pooled random-effects estimate across LESP, LESL, DMS, and BMI did not demonstrate a statistically significant overall pre–post change (SMD -0.4564, 95% CI -1.7975 to 0.8846; p=0.5047) and showed extreme heterogeneity (I²=97.3%). Individual studies reported both large decreases and increases in the measured outcomes, consistent with weight loss alongside variable effects on LES function and acid exposure. The strength of evidence is low-to-moderate due to observational designs, incomplete reporting, and absence of formal risk-of-bias assessment.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include: (1) only 9 of 19 studies provided extractable pre–post data; (2) pooling used SMDs across heterogeneous outcomes with different clinical desirability, which complicates interpretation of the direction and meaning of the summary effect; (3) inconsistent reporting, including apparent point estimate/CI discrepancies in a minority of studies, introduced uncertainty; (4) a formal risk-of-bias assessment was not performed, limiting certainty in the pooled estimates; and (5) potential small-study and publication biases were not formally examined.

Study-level limitations were common: small sample sizes (some N≤30), short or variably timed follow-up, nonrandomized before–after designs vulnerable to confounding and selection bias, inconsistent HRM and pH protocols, and heterogeneity in surgical technique (e.g., sleeve size, antral management, hiatal hernia repair) and patient characteristics (baseline GERD, hernia, motility disorders). These factors plausibly drive the observed between-study variability and limit generalizability.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: The evidence base supports substantial BMI reduction after LVSG, but effects on LESP/LESL and DMS are inconsistent. Clinicians should counsel patients—particularly those with preexisting GERD or large hiatal hernias—that LVSG may be associated with postoperative reflux in a subset, even as weight decreases. Preoperative evaluation and shared decision-making about procedure choice (e.g., considering RYGB in patients with severe GERD) remain prudent. Postoperative surveillance for reflux symptoms and, when indicated, objective testing with pH/impedance or manometry should be considered, especially if symptoms emerge or persist.

For research: Priority gaps include well-designed prospective studies with standardized pre- and post-LVSG HRM and pH/impedance protocols; consistent timing of follow-up; and complete reporting of means, SDs, and within-subject correlations to enable meta-analysis. Studies should prespecify outcomes and analyze LESP, LESL, DMS, and BMI separately rather than pooling disparate constructs. Detailed reporting of surgical technique (bougie size, antral preservation, hiatal hernia repair) and stratified analyses by baseline GERD/hernia status are needed to explain heterogeneity. Multi-center cohorts and, where feasible, randomized or rigorously matched comparative designs, with ≥12–24 months follow-up, would strengthen causal inference and guide patient selection and technical modifications to mitigate reflux risk.
