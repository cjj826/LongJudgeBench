## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
From 70 records screened, five randomized clinical trials met the inclusion criteria for children aged 10–13 years with palatally displaced maxillary canines (PDC) in the mixed dentition, comparing extraction of the primary canine to no interceptive/delayed treatment. The studies spanned 2004–2018 and included randomized prospective longitudinal/clinical trial designs, including one split‑mouth randomized controlled trial (RCT) conducted in 2014 (n=24 pairs) and one parallel‑group RCT in 2018 (n=35 participants; comparison group size not reported). Across the five trials, reporting was sparse: four studies did not provide extractable outcome data for our prespecified outcomes, and one (the 2014 split‑mouth RCT) provided an odds ratio (OR) for successful normalization/eruption of 1.0296 (95% CI 0.8654 to 9.0598). Sample sizes were incompletely reported in three trials, precluding calculation of a total participant count. Because fewer than two studies provided extractable effect sizes, meta-analysis was not possible.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The single study with extractable data (Unknown, 2014; split‑mouth, n=24/24) yielded an OR of 1.03 (95% CI 0.87–9.06) for normalization/successful eruption when the primary canine was extracted versus not extracted on the contralateral side. Although the point estimate is close to the null, the confidence interval is extremely wide and includes both a modest reduction and a large increase in the odds of successful eruption. This imprecision indicates that the trial was underpowered to detect clinically meaningful differences and that the true effect may range from negligible to substantial benefit (or even harm) relative to observation. In practical terms, the data from this trial neither confirm nor refute a benefit of interceptive extraction on the targeted outcomes.

The remaining four trials were randomized and prospective (2004, NR year randomized prospective clinical trial, 2015 RCT, and 2018 RCT with n=35), but none provided extractable 2×2 data or directly comparable effect estimates for our outcomes. Consequently, we could not quantify between‑study heterogeneity, nor could we compare fixed‑ versus random‑effects models. Nonetheless, heterogeneity is plausible given differences in design (split‑mouth vs parallel‑group), reporting of sample sizes, and likely variation in outcome definitions (e.g., “normalization of eruptive position” vs “successful eruption”), imaging modalities, follow‑up duration, and baseline severity of displacement. Split‑mouth designs reduce between‑person confounding but can be susceptible to carry‑across effects and may not generalize to unilateral cases, whereas parallel‑group trials are sensitive to baseline imbalances unless allocation concealment and stratification are robust.

Risk of bias could not be formally assessed because trial‑level details (random sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of outcome assessors, attrition, selective reporting) were not extractable from the included records. This limits confidence in internal validity. For example, unblinded outcome assessment of eruption on serial radiographs could introduce detection bias, and inadequate concealment could exaggerate treatment effects. The non‑computable data from four trials also raises concern about selective reporting (i.e., reporting conclusions without numerical data), though we cannot verify this without full texts and protocols.

Author conclusions from the included trials were not available in sufficient detail in the extracted records to quote or synthesize. Therefore, we cannot compare our quantitative synthesis to the investigators’ interpretations. Given that only one trial provided a computable effect size and that its result was highly imprecise, any alignment or discordance with author conclusions from the other trials remains unknowable. This is a key evidentiary gap: without clear reporting of event counts, denominators, and prespecified outcome definitions and time points, both effect estimation and cross‑study interpretation are severely constrained.

Robustness checks (e.g., influence analyses, subgroup analyses by age within 10–13 years, degree of palatal displacement, or root development stage) were not possible. Likewise, we could not examine adverse events or time‑to‑eruption outcomes, which are clinically important in decisions about interceptive extraction. In sum, while five randomized studies address the PICO question, the extractable evidence is insufficient to conclude whether extraction of the primary canine improves normalization of eruptive position or successful eruption compared with no interceptive/delayed treatment.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
The current randomized evidence base (five trials, 2004–2018) provides insufficient and imprecise data to determine whether extraction of the primary canine improves normalization and successful eruption of palatally displaced maxillary canines in 10–13‑year‑old children compared with no interceptive/delayed treatment. The only extractable estimate (Unknown, 2014; OR 1.03, 95% CI 0.87–9.06; n=24 pairs) is compatible with no effect and with substantial benefit or harm. No pooled analysis was possible. Accordingly, the strength of evidence is very low.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review‑level limitations include incomplete reporting in the included trials, which prevented calculation of effect sizes for four studies and precluded meta‑analysis, heterogeneity assessment, and planned sensitivity analyses. We were unable to extract or verify author conclusions in sufficient detail, and we could not perform a risk‑of‑bias assessment due to missing information on randomization, allocation concealment, blinding, attrition, and selective reporting. These reporting gaps limit our ability to make confident inferences.

Study‑level limitations likely include small sample sizes (e.g., split‑mouth n=24 pairs; one RCT with n=35 participants), possible variability in outcome definitions (normalization vs successful eruption), unclear follow‑up durations, and potential differences in diagnostic criteria and imaging protocols. Without explicit details, we cannot assess consistency across trials or explore effect modification by baseline canine angulation, sector position, root development, or patient age within the 10–13‑year range. The net effect is substantial imprecision and uncertainty.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Based on the currently extractable randomized evidence, routine extraction of the primary canine solely to improve normalization/successful eruption of palatally displaced maxillary canines cannot be recommended or discouraged with confidence relative to observation/delayed treatment. Clinical decisions should be individualized, integrating radiographic assessment of displacement severity, risks to adjacent teeth (e.g., incisor root resorption), patient age and growth status, potential harms of extraction, patient and caregiver preferences, and feasibility of close monitoring. Where interception is considered, clinicians should discuss the uncertainty of benefit with families.

For research: There is a pressing need for adequately powered, parallel‑group RCTs (and carefully designed split‑mouth trials where appropriate) that compare primary canine extraction versus no interceptive/delayed treatment using standardized, prespecified outcomes: (a) successful eruption by a fixed time point, (b) normalization of position/angulation, (c) time‑to‑eruption, and (d) adverse events (pain, infection, adjacent tooth resorption). Trials should report event counts, denominators, allocation methods, concealment, blinding of outcome assessment, attrition, and protocol‑specified analyses to enable risk‑of‑bias assessment and meta‑analysis. Stratified randomization or prespecified subgroup analyses by initial displacement severity, root development stage, and age (10, 11, 12, 13 years) are important. Longer follow‑up to capture delayed eruption, plus economic evaluations and patient‑reported outcomes, would enhance clinical relevance. Data sharing and adherence to CONSORT guidelines would materially improve the evidence base.
