## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Seven original intervention studies met inclusion criteria and reported peripheral blood brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) in the context of psychoplastogen administration (publication years 2013–2025). Designs spanned randomized controlled trials (including within-subject/crossover placebo-controlled designs), cohort pre–post studies, and single-arm before–after studies. Across studies with reported sample sizes, at least 175 participants contributed data (2013 open-label pre–post N=25; 2013 pre–post N=30; 2021 single-arm before–after N=94; 2023 randomized controlled trial N=27 randomized/26 analyzed), with three additional trials not reporting analyzable sample numbers. Despite seven included studies, only one provided extractable quantitative data for effect size computation (standardized mean difference [SMD] = −2.0110). The remaining six lacked sufficient statistics (e.g., means/SDs, change scores, or crossover-compatible data) to compute SMDs. Given a single computable effect size, pooling was not possible.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The principal quantitative signal from the available extractable data is a large within-subject decrease in peripheral BDNF following psychoplastogen administration in a single-arm before–after study (2021; N=94; SMD = −2.0110). By conventional benchmarks, an SMD of approximately −2 indicates a very large reduction relative to baseline. However, interpretability is constrained: the study lacked a concurrent control, and we did not have access to confidence intervals or p-values. Without a comparator, the observed change could reflect non-specific influences (e.g., diurnal variation, peri-procedural stress, pre-analytical factors such as clotting time or platelet release in serum, regression to the mean, or assay drift) rather than a pharmacologic effect. Consequently, while numerically striking, this single estimate does not establish a causal effect of psychoplastogens on peripheral BDNF.

The broader body of included evidence—three randomized, placebo-controlled trials (including at least two crossover/within-subject designs) and three additional pre–post studies—was insufficiently reported for quantitative synthesis. This paucity of extractable statistics suggests extensive heterogeneity in reporting practices and methodological details. Heterogeneity is expected across compounds (e.g., ketamine/esketamine vs classic psychedelics vs MDMA, scopolamine, or rapastinel), dosing regimens, participant populations (healthy volunteers vs various psychiatric diagnoses), biological matrices (serum vs plasma), assay platforms, and, critically, sampling timepoints. BDNF exhibits circadian patterns and is highly sensitive to pre-analytical handling; serum measurements are dominated by platelet degranulation during clotting, whereas platelet-poor plasma yields far lower and more labile values. Variations in fasting status, exercise, and sample processing (e.g., clotting time, centrifugation, freeze–thaw cycles) can yield changes of similar magnitude to drug effects. Without consistent control and reporting of these factors, between-study differences in BDNF trajectories may reflect methodological rather than biological variation.

The randomized and crossover designs among the included trials would, in principle, allow stronger inference regarding causal effects compared with single-arm before–after studies. However, the absence of extractable data (means/SDs, change scores, or model-based contrasts with standard errors) precluded effect size computation. The brief study descriptors did not provide author-reported estimates (e.g., mean differences, CIs, or p-values) for incorporation into a narrative meta-analysis. Accordingly, we could not contrast controlled vs uncontrolled estimates or assess robustness across analytic choices (e.g., fixed vs random effects). Nor could we explore dose-response, timing (acute vs subacute vs delayed), or subgroup differences by diagnosis.

We planned to juxtapose study authors’ conclusions with our synthesized estimates, but author conclusions were not captured in analyzable form for the included records. Where trials are randomized and placebo-controlled, alignment of authors’ conclusions with null or directional effects would typically be anchored by reported between-condition contrasts; the lack of such contrasts in the extractable dataset prevents verification. Thus, our synthesis is limited to noting that one sizeable before–after study suggests a large peripheral BDNF decrease post-intervention (SMD = −2.0110; 2021; N=94), while the remaining six studies provide insufficient quantitative detail to support or refute that signal.

In sum, the available evidence is insufficient to determine whether psychoplastogens reliably increase, decrease, or do not change peripheral BDNF. The single computable estimate suggests a decrease, but the absence of concurrent control, combined with extensive methodological heterogeneity and incomplete reporting across the wider set of trials, means that any overarching conclusion would be premature.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
The evidence base linking psychoplastogen administration to changes in peripheral blood BDNF in humans is sparse, heterogeneous, and largely underreported in a form suitable for meta-analysis. Only one of seven included studies yielded an extractable effect size, a large within-subject decrease (SMD = −2.0110; 2021; N=94) without a control group. Given the lack of comparable data from randomized placebo-controlled trials and the strong susceptibility of peripheral BDNF to methodological factors, there is currently insufficient evidence to conclude that psychoplastogens produce consistent, directionally reliable changes in peripheral BDNF.

### 8.2 Limitations
At the review level, several constraints limit inference. First, although we systematically screened 93 records and included 7, only one study provided data amenable to effect size computation. We lacked access to raw data or complete summary statistics (means, SDs/SEs, change scores, or model-based estimates with CIs) from the six remaining studies, precluding meta-analysis and sensitivity analyses. Second, the included records were drawn from a local corpus; while our screening was comprehensive within that frame, we cannot exclude the possibility of relevant studies outside the corpus or unpublished negative results. Third, we were unable to extract study-level risk of bias assessments due to reporting insufficiency in the dataset.

At the study level, heterogeneity and methodological issues complicate interpretation. Designs ranged from single-arm pre–post to randomized crossover trials, with variable participant populations (healthy and psychiatric), compounds, doses, and sampling schedules. Pre-analytical handling and assay variability, especially the use of serum (dominated by platelet BDNF) versus plasma, can cause large fluctuations independent of intervention. Time-of-day, fasting, recent exercise, and clotting/processing parameters were not consistently reported. Small-to-moderate sample sizes in several studies, open-label designs, and potential selective outcome reporting further reduce confidence. Finally, the translatability of peripheral BDNF to central neuroplasticity remains uncertain.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice and policy: Current evidence does not support using peripheral BDNF as a clinical biomarker of psychoplastogen exposure or treatment response in routine care. Clinicians and policymakers should not interpret single-timepoint serum/plasma BDNF changes as indicative of therapeutic efficacy, mechanism engagement, or safety in the context of ketamine/esketamine, classic psychedelics, MDMA, scopolamine, or rapastinel. Any exploratory BDNF measurements in clinical programs should be treated as research data and not used for decision-making.

For research: High-priority needs include well-powered, preregistered randomized trials with standardized biospecimen protocols and transparent statistical reporting. Specifically:
- Use concurrent controls (placebo or active comparator) and, where feasible, crossover designs with adequate washout.
- Predefine and harmonize sampling windows (e.g., acute 1–6 h; subacute 24–72 h; delayed 1–4 weeks), with serial sampling to map trajectories.
- Standardize pre-analytical variables: fasting status, time-of-day, activity restrictions, clotting time, centrifugation, platelet-poor plasma vs serum, freeze–thaw cycles, and assay platform/lot; report these in detail.
- Report absolute concentrations and changes with means and SDs/SEs (or medians and IQRs with distribution checks), along with between-condition contrasts, CIs, and exact p-values; share deidentified individual participant data where possible.
- Adjust for platelet count in serum studies and consider prioritizing platelet-poor plasma for reduced confounding.
- Stratify by diagnosis, sex, age, and baseline BDNF; examine dose-response; and link BDNF trajectories to clinical outcomes and central biomarkers (e.g., imaging, CSF, EEG).
- Plan analyses that accommodate between-study heterogeneity (e.g., Bayesian hierarchical models) and correct for multiplicity.

Such improvements would enable definitive meta-analytic evaluation of whether psychoplastogens exert consistent effects on peripheral BDNF and whether any observed changes are clinically meaningful or mechanistically informative.
