### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The central question was whether, among adults with class II–III obesity (BMI 35.5–42.6 kg/m²), weight-loss magnitude (kg) or rate (kg/week) under TDR differs between those with T2DM and those without diabetes. The body of evidence identified for this direct comparison is extremely limited. Only one cohort study (2012) was eligible, but it did not provide extractable quantitative weight outcomes for the two subgroups; effect sizes were not computable. Accordingly, we cannot estimate or compare mean weight loss (kg) or weight-loss rate between T2DM and non-diabetic participants for either very low calorie or low-energy TDRs.

The lack of computable data precludes assessment of between-study or within-study heterogeneity. Nonetheless, the exclusions during screening point to likely sources of heterogeneity that future research must anticipate and control: (a) population definitions—most reports included broader BMI ranges (e.g., BMI ≥30 kg/m²) or T2DM-only samples, which would confound comparisons by diabetes status; (b) intervention differences—many studies used partial meal replacements rather than complete TDR, or mixed calorie targets, limiting comparability; and (c) outcome reporting—studies often reported percent weight change, remission outcomes, or non-kg metrics without subgroup-specific means and variances, impeding computation of the pre-specified outcomes.

Risk of bias could not be formally assessed because the included study did not provide sufficient design or outcome details in the accessible record. This limitation materially affects the confidence in any qualitative inference. For example, in cohorts comparing T2DM and non-diabetic patients under TDR, weight loss may be influenced by medication adjustments (e.g., reduction or withdrawal of insulin or sulfonylureas), early diuresis related to glycogen depletion, and differences in adherence stemming from glycemic symptoms or medication side effects. Without explicit reporting of medication protocols, attrition, adherence, and blinding of outcome assessment (where applicable), confounding and measurement biases cannot be excluded.

The statistical framework outlined for this review planned to synthesize continuous outcomes (kg, kg/week). Although the analysis log lists an odds ratio (OR) as the effect measure and notes “studies with data: 1,” an OR is not an appropriate summary for continuous weight outcomes, underscoring that no valid quantitative synthesis could be conducted. We therefore relied on narrative synthesis. Critically, author conclusions from the 2012 cohort could not be extracted from the corpus record; as a result, we cannot compare our synthesis with the study’s stated interpretation. This further limits interpretability.

The broader screening record offers insight into the evidence gap. Most excluded studies failed on one or more of the following: lack of direct T2DM versus non-diabetic comparison under the same TDR (numerous T2DM-only studies); use of partial rather than total diet replacement; inclusion of overweight or lower-class obesity beyond the pre-specified BMI range; and absence of subgroup-specific weight outcomes in kg. Collectively, these patterns suggest that, while TDRs are studied in both T2DM and non-diabetic populations, head-to-head comparisons at class II–III obesity with extractable kg outcomes are rarely reported. In sum, there is insufficient evidence to determine whether diabetes status modifies the magnitude or rate of weight loss under TDR in adults with BMI 35.5–42.6 kg/m².

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Based on one eligible cohort (2012) without extractable quantitative outcomes, there is insufficient evidence to conclude whether adults with T2DM versus those without diabetes experience different final weight loss (kg) or weight-loss rates (kg/week) when undergoing total diet replacement with very low calorie or low-energy liquid formula diets in the BMI range 35.5–42.6 kg/m². No pooled estimates or study-level effect sizes could be computed.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include the narrow, pre-specified eligibility: adults with class II–III obesity (BMI 35.5–42.6 kg/m²), receiving TDR (not partial replacement), and direct within-study comparisons by diabetes status with outcomes reported in kg. These stringent criteria likely reduced the number of eligible studies but were necessary to address the focused PICO and avoid confounding by BMI category or intervention intensity. Although 123 records were screened, only 1 met criteria, and even that study lacked extractable quantitative data. The statistical plan to prioritize continuous outcomes and compute pooled effects could not be implemented, and the analysis log’s default OR framework was not applicable to the intended outcomes.

Study-level limitations are pronounced. The included cohort lacked reported sample size, subgroup counts, and outcome statistics necessary for effect estimation. Without information on recruitment, baseline comparability, diabetes duration, medication regimens and adjustments, adherence to the diet, attrition, and follow-up duration, key risks of bias (selection, performance, detection, attrition, and confounding) cannot be appraised. The absence of author-reported conclusions in the extracted record further constrains interpretation and triangulation.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: There is no reliable, quantitative evidence from eligible studies to support or refute a difference in weight-loss magnitude or rate between T2DM and non-diabetic adults with BMI 35.5–42.6 kg/m² undergoing TDR. Clinicians should not assume a systematic difference based solely on diabetes status. Management should prioritize individualized care, especially proactive adjustment of glucose-lowering medications to mitigate hypoglycemia risk during rapid caloric restriction, and close monitoring of adherence and safety. Expected weight-loss targets in kg or kg/week cannot be specified from this review.

For research: There is a clear need for adequately powered prospective studies—preferably randomized or well-designed cohorts—that enroll both T2DM and non-diabetic adults within the 35.5–42.6 kg/m² BMI range and apply the same TDR protocol (with prespecified caloric targets, including VLCD <800 kcal/day and low-energy >800 kcal/day arms). Trials should pre-register and report final weight loss in kg and rate (kg/week) for each subgroup, provide measures of variance, and detail medication algorithms for diabetes therapy adjustments. Analyses should adjust for baseline BMI, diabetes duration, insulin use, and other potential confounders, use intention-to-treat principles, and transparently report adherence and attrition. Reporting subgroup data in tabular form would enable future meta-analysis. Safety outcomes (e.g., hypoglycemia) should be co-reported, as medication changes may influence early weight dynamics and clinical risks under TDR.
