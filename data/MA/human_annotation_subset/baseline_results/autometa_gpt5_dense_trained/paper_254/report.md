## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Our search identified 120 records; 120 were screened at title/abstract, 6 proceeded to full text, and all 6 were included. The six included studies were cross-sectional case-control MRI investigations comparing medication-free patients with major depressive disorder (MDD) to healthy controls, published between 2014 and 2025. Four studies reported group sizes in our extraction: 27/27, 16/16, 86/81, and 93/77 (MDD/healthy control), yielding at least 222 medication-free MDD participants and 201 controls; two studies did not have sample sizes available in the extracted dataset (NR/NR). All studies used surface-based morphometry to estimate cortical thickness. No study provided extractable group-level means and standard deviations or comparable effect estimates for the prespecified outcome, and we therefore did not conduct a meta-analysis.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The primary objective was to quantify differences in cortical thickness between medication-free MDD patients and healthy controls. Although six cross-sectional case-control studies were eligible, none yielded extractable standardized mean differences (SMDs) for ROI- or global-thickness outcomes. This appears to reflect a common reporting pattern in cortical thickness studies whereby results are presented as vertex-wise statistical maps and cluster-level statistics (typically with family-wise error or false discovery rate correction), without accompanying group means and standard deviations for harmonized regions of interest. Consequently, despite an evidence base spanning at least 423 participants across four studies with reported sample sizes (222 MDD; 201 controls) and two additional studies of unspecified size, we could not derive quantitative summary estimates or assess direction or magnitude of differences in a comparable metric.

Heterogeneity likely further limits synthesize-ability. The included studies span more than a decade (2014–2025), a period during which MRI field strengths, surface reconstruction pipelines (e.g., FreeSurfer versions), parcellation schemes, and smoothing kernels evolved. Analytic choices vary across cortical thickness studies: whole-brain vertex-wise versus ROI-based analyses; global mean thickness versus regional measures; and differing multiple-comparison corrections. Clinical heterogeneity is also probable: medication-free status can encompass drug-naïve participants or those scanned after various washout intervals; samples may include first-episode and recurrent MDD; and severity, illness duration, age range, and comorbidity exclusions vary. Two studies in 2025 were larger (86/81 and 93/77), while earlier studies were smaller (16/16 and 27/27), introducing small-study effects and variable statistical power. These sources of heterogeneity, along with incomplete reporting, precluded meaningful pooling and complicate qualitative cross-study comparisons.

Author conclusions could not be reliably synthesized from the extracted dataset. The included records did not provide, in our abstraction, standardized summaries of whether cortical thickness was greater, reduced, or unchanged in specific regions for medication-free MDD versus controls. Without consistent ROI definitions and extractable statistics, we cannot adjudicate whether individual studies converged on thinning in canonical prefrontal or cingulate regions or whether findings varied by sample characteristics. We therefore refrain from inferring study-level directions of effect.

Given the absence of extractable effect sizes, we were unable to evaluate robustness via fixed- versus random-effects models, conduct sensitivity analyses, assess small-study bias, or quantify between-study heterogeneity (I²). Nevertheless, typical methodological concerns in surface-based morphometry remain salient: risk of inflated type I error when multiple comparisons are not adequately controlled, potential residual confounding by age, sex, intracranial volume, and head motion, and variability in site/scanner characteristics. Although all six studies enrolled medication-free patients, the operational definition (drug-naïve versus washout) and the handling of prior treatment exposure were not available in our extraction and may influence cortical thickness.

In sum, the current body of evidence includes several case-control MRI studies in medication-free MDD, including two relatively larger and recent datasets. However, due to heterogeneous analytic choices and insufficient reporting of group-level summary statistics, we cannot quantify differences in cortical thickness between medication-free MDD and healthy controls or determine whether any apparent regional differences are consistent, robust, or clinically meaningful.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Six cross-sectional MRI studies (2014–2025) compared cortical thickness in medication-free MDD and healthy controls, with at least 222 medication-free patients and 201 controls among studies that reported sample sizes. None provided extractable, harmonized statistics to compute standardized effect sizes. As a result, no quantitative synthesis was possible, and no confident conclusion can be drawn regarding the presence, absence, or regional pattern of cortical thickness differences in medication-free MDD relative to healthy controls.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on a single local corpus, which may omit relevant studies outside the repository; the small number of included studies; and lack of access to complete numerical results. Despite prespecifying SMD as the effect measure, the necessary group means and standard deviations (or equivalent) were not reported in the extracted records. We could not perform a formal risk-of-bias assessment due to insufficient methodological detail in the extraction, and thus we cannot quantify the credibility of individual findings.

Study-level limitations inferred from available information likely include modest sample sizes in several studies (16–27 per arm), variability in MRI acquisition parameters and processing pipelines, inconsistent ROI definitions, and potential inconsistencies in the operational definition of “medication-free” (drug-naïve versus varying washout periods). Covariate handling (age, sex, intracranial volume), multiple-comparison correction strategies, and reporting practices (e.g., vertex-wise statistics without ROI-level descriptive data) likely differed across studies, increasing the risk of both false positives and false negatives and precluding aggregation.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice and policy: Current evidence does not support using cortical thickness measures to inform clinical decision-making in medication-free MDD. The available studies are not sufficiently consistent or transparent to establish reliable case-control differences, and no quantitative summary is possible. MRI cortical thickness should not be used for diagnostic or prognostic purposes in medication-free MDD outside research settings.

For research: Future studies should prioritize harmonization and transparent reporting. Specifically:
- Pre-register hypotheses and analysis plans, adopting common ROI parcellations alongside whole-brain analyses.
- Report, at minimum, ROI-level group means and standard deviations (or standardized effect sizes with confidence intervals) for primary regions, along with vertex-wise maps in open repositories.
- Standardize key covariates (age, sex, intracranial volume) and document motion handling, scanner characteristics, and multiple-comparison corrections.
- Define “medication-free” explicitly (drug-naïve versus washout duration), report prior treatment exposure, and characterize episode status (first-episode vs recurrent), illness duration, and severity.
- Target adequately powered samples (e.g., ≥100 per group) and consider multi-site consortia with harmonized acquisition and processing to improve precision and generalizability.
- Facilitate reproducibility by sharing code and statistical maps, enabling downstream meta-analyses and image-based synthesis.

Until such standards are met and comparable effect estimates are available, conclusions about cortical thickness differences in medication-free MDD relative to healthy controls will remain tentative.
