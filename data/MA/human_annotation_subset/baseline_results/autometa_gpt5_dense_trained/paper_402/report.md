## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Eight studies met inclusion criteria (final included = 8), published between 2022 and 2025, predominantly cross-sectional secondary analyses of nationally representative household surveys (e.g., DHS, MICS), with one secondary analysis of a randomized trial endline survey and one multi-country observational study. Extracted records lacked sample sizes (N=NR/NR for all studies), and effect metrics were variably reported as proportions, regression coefficients, or relative measures. Two studies contributed numeric data that were meta-analyzable under the “prevalence” effect designation. The remaining six reported incompatible or incomplete statistics (e.g., effect without variance, incongruent confidence intervals, or non-comparable modeling scales), rendering them not directly combinable.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The random-effects meta-analysis yielded a pooled “prevalence” estimate of 16.5152 with a 95% confidence interval spanning −16.9631 to 49.9934 (p=0.3336), alongside extreme heterogeneity (I²=100.0%, τ²=583.393729). The negative lower bound, which is not meaningful for a prevalence parameter, and the very wide interval indicate model instability driven by incompatible scales and substantial between-study variation. A fixed-effect model produced an estimate of −0.2239 (95% CI: −0.3234 to −0.1244), which is also not interpretable as a prevalence proportion. The divergence between models and the impossible bounds strongly suggest that at least one study contributed an effect on a different scale (e.g., a regression coefficient or log-transformed measure), and that the meta-analytic label “prevalence” was not consistently applicable to the included inputs.

At the individual study level, the range of reported effects underscores this lack of commensurability. A multilevel logistic regression analysis of the Lesotho MICS 2018 reported an effect of 33.6000 (95% CI: 31.6000 to 33.6000), which is plausibly a stunting prevalence percentage around one-third. A 2022 cross-sectional study reported 0.3310, which may represent 33.1% on a 0–1 scale. By contrast, a multi-country observational study reported 0.9936 (likely a relative measure close to null), and a 2024 cross-sectional analysis reported 1.3700 (suggesting a 37% higher odds or prevalence ratio). Two additional analyses reported negative effects (−0.2000; −0.5621) including one with a discordant confidence interval (−0.5621 with 95% CI: 0.4800 to 0.6800), confirming that not all extracted estimates were on a prevalence scale and at least one record contained internal inconsistencies. Two studies had “not computable” effects.

Potential sources of the considerable heterogeneity include: mixed effect metrics (prevalence proportions, odds ratios or prevalence ratios, regression coefficients), different statistical frameworks (instrumental variables, multilevel logistic regression, generalized additive mixed models), diverse geographies and timepoints, and unreported or varying covariate adjustment sets. With missing sample sizes, inverse-variance weighting could be distorted or default to assumptions that are inappropriate for prevalence synthesis. These factors, combined with the small number of studies contributing to the quantitative synthesis (n=2), render any pooled estimate unreliable and likely misleading.

Because author conclusions were not extractable from the records, we infer cautiously from the reported point estimates. The Lesotho analysis (effect 33.6000; 95% CI: 31.6000 to 33.6000) is consistent with a high stunting burden in that setting. The multi-country near-null (0.9936) suggests no strong association for the modeled predictor(s) with stunting odds on average, while the 2024 estimate of 1.3700 implies elevated risk associated with the modeled exposure(s). However, without standardized definitions of the effect measure and fully specified models, these interpretations remain speculative. Overall, individual studies appear to corroborate that stunting prevalence can be substantial in certain LMIC contexts (around one-third in Lesotho), but the direction and magnitude of relative associations vary and cannot be synthesized credibly with the available data.

Given the absence of a coherent, standardized outcome metric across studies and incomplete reporting (N, standard errors, clear effect labels), the current quantitative results should not be used to estimate a pooled prevalence or to compare stunted versus non-stunted children across LMICs. The evidence base retrieved here highlights the need for harmonized reporting rather than providing a definitive summary measure.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across eight included studies (2022–2025), reporting heterogeneity and missing core data precluded a valid pooled estimate of WHO-defined stunting among children under five in LMICs. The random-effects “prevalence” estimate of 16.5152 (95% CI: −16.9631 to 49.9934; p=0.3336) and fixed-effect estimate of −0.2239 (95% CI: −0.3234 to −0.1244) are not interpretable as prevalence, reflecting incompatible metrics and extreme heterogeneity (I²=100%). Individual estimates suggest substantial burden in some settings (e.g., Lesotho ~33.6%), but there is insufficient, standardized evidence here to support a generalizable summary across LMICs.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations:
- Incomplete reporting in the included records (N=NR/NR; missing standard errors; unlabeled effect scales) impeded weighting and precluded consistent metric transformation. Only two studies contributed to the quantitative synthesis, undermining precision and robustness.
- The extracted “effect measure: PREVALENCE” did not match several studies’ reported metrics (e.g., odds ratios, regression coefficients), producing uninterpretable pooled estimates and very high heterogeneity. Author conclusions were not available, limiting triangulation with qualitative interpretations.
- Risk-of-bias assessment could not be performed due to insufficient methodological detail in the extracted fields, reducing our ability to grade confidence in study findings.

Study-level limitations:
- Predominantly cross-sectional designs, often secondary analyses of DHS/MICS, limit causal inference and are vulnerable to confounding (socioeconomic status, maternal characteristics, infection burden) and residual selection biases (nonresponse, sampling frame differences).
- Heterogeneous modeling strategies (multilevel logistic regression, generalized additive mixed models, instrumental variables) and inconsistent covariate adjustment sets likely contributed to disparate effect estimates. One record contained an effect with an internally inconsistent confidence interval (−0.5621 with 95% CI: 0.4800 to 0.6800), raising data integrity concerns.
- Geographic and temporal diversity without stratified presentation (urban/rural, age subgroups within <5 years, country-specific contexts) reduces comparability.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice and policy:
- This review does not provide a reliable pooled stunting prevalence for LMICs. Practitioners and policy-makers should rely on country-specific, primary DHS/MICS and national nutrition survey estimates for planning and monitoring, ensuring they use WHO height-for-age z-score definitions.
- The Lesotho-specific estimate (≈33.6%) illustrates that local burdens can be high; interventions should remain context-specific and grounded in locally validated data rather than generalized from heterogeneous cross-country syntheses.

For research:
- Standardize outcome reporting: clearly label effect types (prevalence proportion, prevalence ratio, odds ratio), provide sample sizes, standard errors/95% CIs, and specify adjustment sets. When reporting prevalence, present both raw proportions and model-based estimates.
- Enable synthesis: adopt common metrics (e.g., prevalence proportions with binomial or logit transformations) and provide stratum-specific data (age-in-months bands, sex, urban/rural, wealth quintiles) to support meta-analysis and meta-regression.
- Improve study designs: prospective cohorts and repeated cross-sections with harmonized measures can clarify temporal trends and determinants. For analytic studies, preregister protocols, justify modeling choices (e.g., IV assumptions), and report diagnostics.
- Transparency: share analytic code and de-identified summary data to reduce extraction errors and allow independent verification. Provide explicit author conclusions regarding stunting burden and comparative risk to facilitate qualitative synthesis.
