## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Twelve randomized trials met inclusion criteria (2016–2025), encompassing parallel-group, crossover, and reduced factorial designs in exercising adults assigned to daily time-restricted eating (TRE; 4–12 h feeding windows) versus an exercise-matched comparator with an unrestricted eating window. Reporting of sample sizes was incomplete in the extraction (examples include N=26/26 in one parallel RCT and N=10/10 and N=16/16 in two crossover trials), precluding a reliable total participant count. Only two trials provided sufficient data to meta-analyze fat mass (FM, kg); no pooled estimates were possible for body fat percentage (BF%) or fat-free mass (FFM). For FM, the pooled mean difference favored TRE by -2.53 kg (95% CI: -3.47 to -1.59; p<0.001) with low heterogeneity (I²=0%).

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The primary quantitative finding is that, when combined with structured exercise, TRE produces a statistically significant and potentially clinically relevant additional reduction in fat mass versus an unrestricted eating window with the same exercise prescription. The fixed-effects meta-analysis across two trials estimated a -2.53 kg (95% CI: -3.47 to -1.59) advantage for TRE, with no observed heterogeneity (I²=0%). Individually, one RCT (Unknown, 2021) reported -1.92 kg (95% CI: -3.52 to -0.32), and another RCT with N=26/26 (Unknown, 2025) reported -2.85 kg (95% CI: -4.01 to -1.69). A third RCT reported a similar point estimate (-1.867 kg) but lacked variance data and was not pooled. The direction and magnitude of effects across these trials are consistent, suggesting that TRE adds approximately 2–3 kg of fat loss over the short term, beyond the effect of exercise alone.

Heterogeneity statistics (I²=0%, τ²≈0) indicate little between-study variability among the two pooled trials, but this should be interpreted cautiously given the very small number of contributing studies. Potential sources of clinical and methodological heterogeneity include differences in TRE window length (most commonly 8 h, but the range specified allowed 4–12 h), exercise modality (aerobic, resistance, or concurrent), intervention duration, and body composition assessment methods (e.g., DXA vs. BIA), none of which could be explored in subgroup or sensitivity analyses. A fixed-effects model was used; given I²=0%, random-effects results would be expected to be similar, but we did not perform a random-effects re-estimation. The consistent directionality across the two pooled estimates and the additional non-pooled estimate (-1.867 kg) lends some robustness to the inference that TRE facilitates added fat loss with training.

Risk of bias was not formally assessed in this review, which limits the confidence we can place in the pooled estimate. Typical challenges in dietary trials likely apply: limited blinding (impractical for eating-window interventions), potential selection and attrition biases in small samples, and reliance on self-reported dietary adherence. Crossover designs were included among the 12 trials; while useful for acute outcomes, they can be vulnerable to carryover effects for body composition unless washouts are long relative to the time needed for weight and composition changes. These issues underscore the need for interpretive caution.

Author conclusions were not available in the extraction for most included studies. Where numerical data were provided, individual RCTs reported significant reductions in fat mass with TRE plus exercise compared with matched-exercise controls, broadly aligning with the pooled estimate reported here. However, we could not synthesize authors’ statements regarding BF% or FFM due to absent or non-computable data. Accordingly, no pooled conclusions can be drawn about FFM preservation or changes in BF%. The lack of analyzable BF% and FFM outcomes from the 12 included trials represents a meaningful evidence gap: in practice, the desirability of TRE in athletes or resistance trainers hinges not only on fat loss but also on maintenance of lean mass and performance.

In sum, short-term TRE, when layered onto a structured exercise program, appears to yield additional fat mass reduction compared with an unrestricted eating window with the same exercise. The strength of this inference is moderated by small numbers, incomplete reporting, and the absence of a formal risk-of-bias assessment, alongside a lack of pooled evidence for BF% and FFM.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across two randomized trials with analyzable data, time-restricted eating (4–12 h feeding window) combined with exercise produced a greater reduction in fat mass than an unrestricted eating window with matched exercise (pooled MD -2.53 kg; 95% CI: -3.47 to -1.59; p<0.001; I²=0%). Evidence for effects on body fat percentage and fat-free mass is insufficient for meta-analytic conclusions. Overall confidence is limited by small numbers of contributing studies and the absence of a formal risk-of-bias assessment.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include incomplete extraction of study-level details (e.g., participant totals, intervention durations, exact TRE windows), the inability to compute pooled effects for BF% and FFM, and the absence of a risk-of-bias appraisal and publication bias assessment. Only two studies contributed to the quantitative synthesis, precluding exploration of heterogeneity sources, subgroup effects (e.g., resistance vs. aerobic training), or sensitivity analyses.

Study-level limitations likely include small sample sizes, short intervention durations, variable TRE windows, and heterogeneous body composition measurement methods. Adherence to TRE windows and dietary intake are rarely blinded and often self-reported, leaving open the possibility that observed effects reflect spontaneous energy restriction rather than timing per se. Crossover designs included among the 12 trials may be ill-suited to detect body composition changes without long washouts, and carryover cannot be excluded. The generalizability to women, older adults, competitive athletes, or individuals with obesity or metabolic disease is uncertain based on the available extraction.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Preliminary evidence suggests TRE can be paired with exercise to achieve an additional ~2–3 kg fat mass reduction over the short term compared with unrestricted eating windows, without clear evidence (from pooled data) on concomitant changes in FFM. Given uncertainties, clinicians and coaches should individualize use of TRE, monitor body composition (preferably by DXA where feasible), and be attentive to energy availability, performance, and adherence—especially in populations where lean mass preservation is critical or where disordered eating risk is a concern.

For research: Adequately powered, parallel-group RCTs are needed that: (1) standardize TRE windows (e.g., 8–10 h) and intervention duration (≥12–24 weeks); (2) employ isoenergetic comparators or tightly measure and adjust for energy intake to disentangle timing from caloric restriction; (3) prespecify and rigorously assess body composition (DXA) and performance outcomes; (4) include diverse populations (sex balance, age, BMI, training status); (5) report adherence, chrono-alignment with exercise (early vs. late TRE), and adverse events; and (6) transparently report allocation, attrition, and analysis details to permit risk-of-bias assessment and comprehensive meta-analysis of FM, BF%, and FFM.
