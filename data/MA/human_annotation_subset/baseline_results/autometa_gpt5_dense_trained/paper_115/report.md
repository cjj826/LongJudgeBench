## 7. Results and Evidence Synthesis

### 7.1 Summary of Evidence
Seven retrospective cohort studies met inclusion criteria, all comparing overall survival (OS) between patients with primary stage IV endometrial cancer who underwent complete or optimal cytoreductive surgery (CRS) versus incomplete CRS. Publication years spanned 2000–2012 for five studies (2000, 2001, 2004, 2011, 2012), with two studies undated. Two analyses were described as multi-institutional/multicenter; the remainder appeared single-center. Sample sizes, where reported, were small to moderate (for example, 24/21; 101/95; 9/43; 36/29; 16/15), but the exact meaning of these paired counts (e.g., total vs stage IV subset, or complete vs incomplete CRS) could not be verified from the extracted records. None of the studies provided extractable, internally consistent hazard ratios (HRs) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for the prespecified comparison; consequently, quantitative pooling was not feasible. One multicenter retrospective analysis reported a point estimate recorded as −1.5606 with a 95% CI recorded as 13.7 to 26.4, a mismatch of scales that precluded interpretation.

### 7.2 Discussion of Findings
The consistent question across all seven cohorts was whether achieving complete/optimal cytoreduction improves OS compared with incomplete CRS in primary stage IV disease. The available data did not permit calculation or verification of HRs or adjusted effect sizes. As such, our synthesis is necessarily qualitative. Across cohorts, the direction of association described in study reports generally favored complete/optimal CRS over incomplete CRS with respect to OS, but the magnitude and statistical precision of these differences were not extractable in our dataset. The single extract that paired a negative point estimate (−1.5606) with a CI expressed in months (13.7 to 26.4) illustrates a broader problem: survival differences were often presented as median OS by residual disease category rather than as comparably defined HRs with CIs or adjusted models, and in at least one case, the numerical reporting was internally inconsistent.

Heterogeneity in clinical context likely contributed to variability in observed effects and limits comparability. Stage IV endometrial cancer encompasses patients with peritoneal carcinomatosis (IVA) and extra-abdominal metastases (IVB), whose biology and resectability differ. The included studies spanned an era (2000–2012) preceding widespread adoption of current systemic therapies and molecular classification, and they likely varied in adjuvant chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and use of neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NACT) with interval debulking. Definitions of “complete” or “optimal” cytoreduction also vary across gynecologic oncology series (e.g., no gross residual disease vs residual nodules <1 cm), and the extracted records did not standardize these definitions. Histologic composition (endometrioid, serous, clear cell, carcinosarcoma) and the distribution of metastatic burden were not uniformly reported, yet are key determinants of both resectability and survival.

The risk of bias inherent to retrospective surgical series is high and likely biased in favor of surgery achieving lesser residual disease. Patients who undergo complete/optimal CRS are typically fitter, with lower tumor burden, more favorable metastatic distribution, and treated in higher-volume centers; these factors also predict better survival independent of surgery. Without adjustment for key confounders—such as age, performance status, comorbidity, histology, disease distribution (peritoneal vs hematogenous), tumor biology, and adjuvant therapy—the observed survival differences may overestimate the effect of cytoreductive completeness. Our dataset did not include the underlying covariate adjustments or model specifications, preventing an assessment of whether reported associations persisted after appropriate adjustment.

Because effect sizes were not extractable in a consistent metric, we could not test robustness using fixed- versus random-effects meta-analytic models, nor could we examine small-study effects or conduct subgroup analyses (e.g., IVA vs IVB, serous vs endometrioid histology). Nonetheless, narrative signals across the included cohorts and the two multi-institutional analyses suggested that authors generally concluded improved OS with complete/optimal debulking compared with incomplete debulking, and advocated maximal safe cytoreduction when feasible. These author conclusions are consistent with surgical oncology principles and analogous findings in ovarian cancer. However, without verifiable HRs, CIs, or adjusted analyses, we cannot determine whether the apparent benefit is large, modest, or confounded.

In sum, the evidence base consists of small, retrospective cohorts from the early 2000s through 2012, with non-uniform definitions and incomplete reporting. The direction of effect appears to favor complete/optimal cytoreduction, but the certainty of evidence is very low due to imprecision, inconsistency in reporting, and high risk of confounding. No pooled quantitative estimate of OS benefit could be generated from the available data.

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

## 8. Conclusions and Implications

### 8.1 Main Conclusions
Across seven retrospective cohorts of primary stage IV endometrial cancer, authors generally described longer overall survival among patients who achieved complete or optimal cytoreduction compared with those with incomplete cytoreduction. However, due to absent or internally inconsistent effect estimates (no extractable HRs with CIs) and likely confounding by indication and disease burden, the certainty of this apparent association is very low. A quantitative summary effect could not be derived.

### 8.2 Limitations
Review-level limitations include reliance on incomplete extraction from older retrospective reports, absence of study identifiers and verbatim author conclusions in several records, and lack of extractable HRs with CIs. One study provided a point estimate and CI on incompatible scales (−1.5606 with a 95% CI reported as 13.7 to 26.4), precluding interpretation. Because quantitative data were sparse and inconsistent, we could not perform meta-analysis, assess between-study heterogeneity, or explore subgroup effects. A formal risk-of-bias assessment could not be completed given the limited detail available in the extracted dataset.

Study-level limitations are substantial. All included cohorts were retrospective and spanned 2000–2012, predating contemporary systemic therapy regimens and molecular classification. Definitions of “optimal” cytoreduction likely varied (no gross residual vs <1 cm residual), and staging within stage IV (IVA vs IVB) and histologic composition were not consistently reported. Small sample sizes (reported counts ranging from approximately 16–101 in various cohorts) raise concerns about imprecision and underpowered adjusted analyses. Selection bias (operability, center experience) and confounding by tumor burden, performance status, and adjuvant therapy are probable and could inflate the apparent survival benefit of complete/optimal CRS.

### 8.3 Implications
For practice: Current evidence is insufficient to provide a precise, evidence-based estimate of OS benefit from complete/optimal cytoreduction in primary stage IV endometrial cancer. Nonetheless, the direction of effect reported by included cohorts aligns with surgical principles: when technically feasible and safe, achieving minimal or no residual disease may be associated with longer survival. Decisions should be individualized within multidisciplinary teams, considering disease distribution (IVA vs IVB), resectability, patient fitness and comorbidity, histology, and available systemic therapies. Given the high likelihood of selection bias in the existing evidence, clinicians should avoid extrapolating these retrospective findings to mandate aggressive surgery in patients unlikely to benefit or at high operative risk. Referral to high-volume centers and consideration of enrollment in clinical trials or prospective registries are advisable.

For research: There is a clear need for contemporary, adequately powered comparative effectiveness studies. Priority features include: prospective multicenter cohorts (or pragmatic trials where feasible) with standardized definitions of residual disease (CC0, CC1, >1 cm), clear delineation of stage IVA vs IVB, detailed histology and molecular subtype, and prespecified systemic and radiation therapy protocols. Analyses should provide adjusted HRs with 95% CIs for OS and progression-free survival, using multivariable models and propensity methods to address confounding; time-dependent biases should be mitigated. Comparative studies of primary CRS versus neoadjuvant chemotherapy with interval debulking, stratified by disease distribution and histology, are particularly needed. Reporting should include perioperative morbidity, quality of life, and patient-centered outcomes to balance survival gains against surgical risks. An individual patient data meta-analysis of existing cohorts could provide interim insights if raw data on residual disease, covariates, and outcomes can be harmonized.
