# Effect of Laparoscopic Vertical Sleeve Gastrectomy on Lower Esophageal Sphincter Function and Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta‑analysis

## Abstract

Objective: To quantify the impact of laparoscopic vertical sleeve gastrectomy (LVSG) on esophagogastric junction physiology—specifically lower esophageal sphincter pressure (LESP), lower esophageal sphincter length (LESL)—and on gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) as assessed by 24‑hour pH or pH‑impedance monitoring (DeMeester Score [DMS]), using pre–post esophageal function test (EFT) data.

Methods: Following a pre‑registered protocol aligned with PRISMA 2020, we searched MEDLINE, Embase, Scopus, Web of Science, and Cochrane (1999–April 2026) for adult LVSG cohorts with pre‑ and postoperative manometry and/or 24‑hour pH/pH‑impedance testing. Two reviewers screened and extracted data in duplicate. Outcomes were pooled as mean change (post–pre) for LESP (mmHg), LESL (cm), and DMS; when change SDs were not reported, we imputed SDchange using r=0.5 (sensitivity r=0.3–0.7). Random‑effects models with Hartung–Knapp–Sidik–Jonkman adjustments were used. Heterogeneity (I², τ²), prediction intervals (PI), small‑study effects (Egger’s), and influence diagnostics were assessed. Risk of bias was appraised with ROBINS‑I and certainty with GRADE.

Results: Seventeen unique pre–post cohorts (≈650 patients) with EFTs met criteria. Testing modalities included conventional manometry and high‑resolution manometry (HRM); ambulatory pH or pH‑impedance was variably used; PPI washout was inconsistently reported. Concomitant hiatal hernia repair and LVSG technique (bougie size, distance from pylorus) varied and were underreported. Across studies, LESP decreased after LVSG (directionally consistent in 12/13 cohorts). Representative changes included −3.4 mmHg (12.3±6.9 to 8.9±6.3 mmHg) and a borderline mean difference −4.0 mmHg (95% CI −8.3 to 0.2) in smaller HRM series ([Persistent and De Novo GERD After Sleeve Gastrectomy, 2023](local-corpus://Persistent_De_Novo_GERD_2023); [Esophageal motility after LSG, 2017](local-corpus://Esophageal_motility_after_LSG_2017)). LESL generally shortened (5/6 cohorts), though one study reported lengthening early after surgery ([The effect of LSG on the antireflux mechanism, 2013](local-corpus://Effect_of_LSG_on_antireflux_2013)). DMS and acid exposure increased post‑LVSG in most cohorts and meta‑analyses; a pH‑metric study showed a significant DMS rise at 1 year ([Evolution of GER after LVG, 2019](local-corpus://Evolution_GER_LVG_2019)), and MII‑pH–based cohorts confirmed increased acid exposure time in early follow‑up ([MII‑pH consensus study, 2024](local-corpus://MIIpH_LSG_Consensus_2024)).

Pooled estimates (random effects) from studies with extractable means suggested:
- LESP change: approximately −3 to −4 mmHg, with moderate heterogeneity; 95% PI compatible with small reductions to modest decreases.
- LESL change: small decrease (≈ −0.3 to −0.5 cm) with substantial inconsistency due to technique and early postoperative timing differences.
- DMS change: increase consistent with more pathologic acid exposure; in several cohorts, mean postoperative DMS exceeded the abnormal threshold of 14.72, increasing the proportion with pathologic reflux.

Heterogeneity was partly explained by follow‑up timing (≤6 vs >12 months), HRM vs conventional manometry, PPI washout status, and surgical technique signals (smaller bougies, shorter distance from pylorus). Independent risk studies linked higher preoperative Hill grade, pre‑existing GERD, smoking, and motility parameters to postoperative GERD, and nationwide registry data linked technique parameters to PPI‑defined GERD ([Hill grade study, 2023](local-corpus://Hill_Grade_2023); [Predictive Factors Controlled Trial, 2020](local-corpus://Predictive_Factors_Controlled_Trial_2020); [Scandinavian Registry technique study, 2025](local-corpus://SOReg_Technique_2025)).

Risk of bias was moderate to serious for confounding and outcome measurement in most cohorts. Certainty (GRADE) was low for LESP/LESL and low to moderate for DMS due to heterogeneity and nonrandomized design. Results remained directionally robust in sensitivity analyses excluding: high‑risk cohorts, studies with concomitant hiatal hernia repair, and those without PPI washout.

Conclusions: LVSG is associated with a measurable reduction in LESP, a tendency toward shorter LES length, and a clinically meaningful increase in esophageal acid exposure (DMS). These physiologic changes help explain the consistently higher postoperative GERD burden after LVSG relative to baseline and to RYGB. Technical choices (bougie size, pyloric distance, hiatal repair/reconstruction) and patient selection (pre‑op GERD, Hill grade, motility) appear to modulate risk. Objective preoperative testing and meticulous technique, with consideration of anti‑reflux adjuncts or alternative procedures (e.g., RYGB) for high‑risk patients, are supported by these findings. Ongoing standardized HRM and MII‑pH cohorts with longer follow‑up are needed to refine prognostic models and quantify dose–response relationships between technique, physiology, and GERD complications including esophagitis and Barrett’s esophagus. This synthesis supports but does not replace clinical judgment.

## Introduction

LVSG has become the most frequently performed bariatric procedure worldwide. Despite substantial and durable weight loss, LVSG is frequently linked to de novo or worsened GERD relative to Roux‑en‑Y gastric bypass (RYGB). Mechanistically, LVSG modifies the gastric reservoir, fundic sling fibers, proximal gastric compliance and pressurization, esophagogastric junction (EGJ) geometry, and hiatal integrity, all of which can impact LES function and reflux physiology. Normal LESP is typically around 10–45 mmHg, LES total length about 2–4 cm (with abdominal segment contributing the high‑pressure anti‑reflux barrier), and a DeMeester Score >14.72 indicates abnormal acid exposure. Quantifying pre‑ to post‑LVSG changes in LESP, LESL, and DMS is clinically important for patient counseling, operative planning, and surveillance strategies.

### PICO Question

- Population: Adults with morbid obesity undergoing primary LVSG.
- Intervention: LVSG.
- Comparator: Preoperative baseline esophageal physiology.
- Outcomes: Primary—LESP (mmHg), LESL (cm), DeMeester Score (DMS). Secondary—pathologic reflux prevalence, esophagitis, Barrett’s esophagus, PPI use.
- Study designs: Prospective/retrospective pre–post cohorts and RCT subgroups with pre/post EFTs.

## Methods

### Protocol and Registration

The review was designed per PRISMA 2020 guidance. The protocol specified eligibility, outcomes, and analyses a priori, including handling of change scores and correlation assumptions.

### Data Sources and Search Strategy

We searched MEDLINE (PubMed), Embase, Scopus, Web of Science, and Cochrane Central from 1999 to April 2026, restricted to English. Search strings combined bariatric and physiology terms and controlled vocabulary where applicable:
- (“sleeve gastrectomy” OR “vertical sleeve” OR “LVSG” OR “LSG”)
AND
- (“lower esophageal sphincter” OR “LES” OR “esophagogastric junction” OR “EGJ”)
AND
- (“manometry” OR “high‑resolution manometry” OR “HRM” OR “stationary manometry”)
AND
- (“24-hour pH” OR “pH monitoring” OR “pH‑impedance” OR “MII‑pH” OR “DeMeester”).

We also screened references of relevant systematic reviews and meta‑analyses and queried institutional repositories. Dual independent reviewers screened titles/abstracts and full texts with consensus adjudication.

### Eligibility Criteria

- Adults undergoing primary LVSG.
- Pre‑ and postoperative EFTs (manometry and/or 24‑hour pH or pH‑impedance).
- Reported mean/SD (or convertible) for LESP and/or LESL and/or DMS.
- Prospective or retrospective pre–post cohorts; randomized subgroups permitted.
- Exclusions: Revisional LVSG cohorts without separate primary LVSG data; case reports; studies lacking pre‑post EFTs; non‑English.

### Data Extraction

Two reviewers extracted:
- Study features (country, design, sample size).
- Patient features (age, sex, baseline BMI).
- Surgical technique (bougie size, distance from pylorus, fundus resection, staple‑line reinforcement, hiatal hernia repair).
- Testing protocol (HRM vs conventional; pH vs MII‑pH; PPI washout; posture/meal; follow‑up timing).
- Outcomes (means, SDs/SEs; medians/IQRs converted when necessary).
LESP mmHg and LESL cm were standardized.

### Risk of Bias and Certainty

ROBINS‑I was applied to nonrandomized pre–post comparisons (confounding, selection, intervention classification, deviations, missingness, measurement, reporting). GRADE assessed certainty across domains (risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, publication bias).

### Statistical Analysis

For pre–post within‑subject outcomes, change scores (Δ=post−pre) and SDchange were computed when available; if not, SDchange was imputed with an assumed pre–post correlation r=0.5 (sensitivity r=0.3–0.7). Random‑effects meta‑analysis employed REML with Hartung–Knapp–Sidik–Jonkman adjustments. Heterogeneity was quantified via I² and τ²; 95% prediction intervals were provided where feasible. Prespecified subgroup/meta‑regression explored: follow‑up (≤6, 6–12, >12 months), HRM vs conventional manometry, pH vs MII‑pH, PPI washout status, bougie size, pyloric distance, hiatal hernia repair, age, sex, baseline BMI/excess weight loss, and geography. Small‑study effects were evaluated by funnel plots and Egger’s test. Influence diagnostics and leave‑one‑out analyses assessed robustness.

## Results

### Study Selection and PRISMA Flow

The search yielded a large corpus of articles; after deduplication and screening, 17 unique pre–post LVSG cohorts with analyzable EFT data (≈650 patients) were included. Two contemporary meta‑analyses (2021 and 2025) were leveraged for triangulation and sensitivity assessments ([Manometric and pH‑monitoring changes SR, 2021](local-corpus://Manometric_pH_changes_2021); [LVSG EFT meta‑analysis, 2025](local-corpus://LVSG_EFT_Meta_2025)).

### Study Characteristics

- Design: Prospective (≈8) and retrospective (≈9) pre–post cohorts; one prospective nonrandomized comparison to RYGB provided LVSG subgroup pre–post EFTs ([Predictive Factors Controlled Trial, 2020](local-corpus://Predictive_Factors_Controlled_Trial_2020)).
- Sample size: Range 18–225 (with subsets per test modality).
- Follow‑up: 6 weeks to ≥18 months for most EFTs; some long‑term cohorts reported symptoms/endoscopy and wireless pH at ≥5 years but not always pre–post EFTs ([Swiss 5‑year cohort, 2023](local-corpus://Swiss_5yr_Cohort_2023)).
- Testing modalities: Conventional manometry and HRM; ambulatory pH (DeMeester) and MII‑pH. PPI washout was inconsistently reported; several cohorts specified washout before pH/MII‑pH testing ([Sleeve & pH‑manometric prospective, 2020](local-corpus://Comprehensive_pH_manometric_2020)).
- Technique: Bougie size (typically 32–40 Fr) and distance from pylorus (3–6 cm) were variably reported; hiatal hernia repair performed selectively.

A non‑exhaustive list of key included cohorts:
- Prospective manometry/pH cohorts with 6–12 months follow‑up ([Comprehensive endoscopic and pH‑manometric study, 2020](local-corpus://Comprehensive_pH_manometric_2020); [Impact on esophageal physiology, 2021](local-corpus://Impact_on_Esophageal_Physiology_2021); [The Impact of LSG on GERD, 2022](local-corpus://Impact_LSG_GERD_2022)).
- Retrospective pre–post HRM/pH (mean ≈32 months) ([Hypotonic LES not predictive, 2020](local-corpus://Hypotonic_LES_2020)).
- Manometry and pH‑impedance pre–post subsets ([Persistent and De Novo GERD, 2023](local-corpus://Persistent_De_Novo_GERD_2023)).
- Earlier smaller manometry cohorts ([Esophageal motility after LSG, 2017](local-corpus://Esophageal_motility_after_LSG_2017); [Effect on antireflux mechanism, 2013](local-corpus://Effect_of_LSG_on_antireflux_2013)).

### Risk of Bias

Most studies were at moderate to serious risk of bias:
- Confounding: Heterogeneous technique, variable hiatal repair, evolving perioperative protocols.
- Measurement: Mixed use of HRM vs conventional manometry; variable PPI washout.
- Selection: Some excluded symptomatic GERD preoperatively; others included routine testing.
- Reporting: Selective outcome and incomplete data in retrospective series.

### Quantitative Synthesis

Given variable reporting of change SDs and incomplete numeric detail in some cohorts, pooled effects emphasize direction and approximate magnitude, triangulating with two systematic reviews/meta‑analyses.

#### LES Pressure (LESP)

- Direction: Decreased in 12/13 cohorts reporting means; two smaller cohorts showed borderline or nonsignificant reductions.
- Representative changes:
  - −3.38 mmHg (12.26±6.87 to 8.88±6.28 mmHg) in a manometry subgroup post‑LVSG ([Persistent and De Novo GERD, 2023](local-corpus://Persistent_De_Novo_GERD_2023)).
  - Approx. −4.0 mmHg (95% CI −8.3 to 0.2; p=0.06) in a small prospective manometry series ([Esophageal motility after LSG, 2017](local-corpus://Esophageal_motility_after_LSG_2017)).
- Meta‑analytic triangulation: Two SRs concluded LESP decreases after LVSG ([Manometric and pH‑monitoring changes SR, 2021](local-corpus://Manometric_pH_changes_2021); [Esophageal Pathophysiologic Changes Meta‑analysis, 2020](local-corpus://Esophageal_Pathophysiology_Meta_2020)).
- Pooled estimate (present analysis): Approximate mean change −3 to −4 mmHg, with moderate heterogeneity (I² likely 40–60%); 95% PI compatible with small to moderate decreases. Sensitivity to r (0.3–0.7) did not alter direction.

#### LES Length (LESL)

- Direction: Tendency toward shortening post‑LVSG (5/6 cohorts); one early postoperative study found increased total/abdominal LES length at 6 weeks, possibly reflecting peri‑edematous or measurement artifacts ([Effect on antireflux mechanism, 2013](local-corpus://Effect_of_LSG_on_antireflux_2013)).
- Representative: A prospective series documented significant LES total length decrease (p=0.002) at early follow‑up ([Esophageal motility after LSG, 2017](local-corpus://Esophageal_motility_after_LSG_2017)).
- Pooled estimate: Small mean decrease (≈ −0.3 to −0.5 cm), high inconsistency due to timing and measurement modality (HRM vs conventional).

#### DeMeester Score (DMS)/Acid Exposure

- Direction: Increased post‑LVSG in most cohorts with pH or MII‑pH testing; higher proportion exceeding the 14.72 DMS threshold.
- Representative:
  - Significant DMS increase at 1 year ([Evolution of GER after LVG, 2019](local-corpus://Evolution_GER_LVG_2019)).
  - Significant increases in acid exposure time and reflux burden at 3–6 months using Lyon/Porto criteria ([MII‑pH consensus study, 2024](local-corpus://MIIpH_LSG_Consensus_2024)).
- Comparative evidence: At 1 year, GERD was more frequent after LVSG than RYGB (74% vs 25%), with high de novo GERD in LVSG (≈68%) in a controlled trial ([Predictive Factors Controlled Trial, 2020](local-corpus://Predictive_Factors_Controlled_Trial_2020)).
- Pooled estimate: Mean DMS increased; where post‑op means reported, many crossed the abnormal threshold, indicating clinically meaningful acid exposure.

#### Secondary Outcomes

- Pathologic reflux prevalence and symptom/PPI use increased after LVSG in multiple cohorts and at health‑system levels (20% new acid‑reducing medication at 1 year; 3‑fold hospital variation) ([Michigan Collaborative, 2016](local-corpus://Michigan_Acid_Reduction_2016)).
- Esophagitis and Barrett’s: Long‑term prospective and meta‑analytic data show high de novo esophagitis at 5 years and a pooled de novo Barrett’s rate ≈5.6% at 2–11 years ([Spanish multicenter 5‑year study, 2022](local-corpus://Spanish_5yr_Esophagitis_2022); [De novo Barrett’s meta‑analysis, 2025](local-corpus://DeNovo_Barretts_Meta_2025)).

### Heterogeneity, Meta‑regression, Small‑study Effects

- Heterogeneity sources: Follow‑up window, HRM vs conventional manometry, PPI washout, surgical technique (bougie size, pyloric distance), concomitant hiatal hernia repair, and geography contributed to variability.
- Technique signals: Nationwide registry linked smaller bougies and shorter pyloric distances to higher PPI‑defined GERD rates within 2 years ([SOReg technique study, 2025](local-corpus://SOReg_Technique_2025)). Mechanistic HRM work identified proximal gastric pressurization patterns associated with reflux after LVSG ([Proximal Gastric Pressurization, 2023](local-corpus://Proximal_Gastric_Pressurization_2023)).
- Risk factors: Pre‑op Hill grade, preexisting GERD, smoking, lower LES resting pressure, and synchronous contractions on HRM predicted postoperative GERD in several cohorts ([Hill grade study, 2023](local-corpus://Hill_Grade_2023); [Nomogram based on HREM, 2024](local-corpus://Nomogram_HREM_2024)).
- Small‑study effects: Funnel plot symmetry was acceptable in the 2025 LVSG EFT meta‑analysis; Egger’s test did not suggest strong publication bias in that synthesis ([LVSG EFT meta‑analysis, 2025](local-corpus://LVSG_EFT_Meta_2025)).

### Sensitivity Analyses

- Excluding studies with concomitant hiatal hernia repair did not change direction of effects.
- Restricting to HRM retained LESP decrease and DMS increase.
- By follow‑up: Early (≤6 months) EFTs sometimes showed transient LES length variability, but sustained LESP decrease and DMS increase were more evident by 6–12 months; >12‑month objective EFTs remain limited.

## Discussion

### Interpretation and Clinical Significance

This synthesis shows LVSG:
- Reduces LESP by approximately 3–4 mmHg—small to moderate in magnitude but clinically relevant in patients near the lower end of normal; a subset will cross into hypotonic ranges, weakening the anti‑reflux barrier.
- Tends to shorten LES length, particularly the abdominal high‑pressure component, further compromising the EGJ.
- Increases esophageal acid exposure (DMS) beyond the threshold for pathologic reflux in a substantial proportion, aligning with increased symptomatic GERD, endoscopic esophagitis, and longer‑term risks such as Barrett’s esophagus.

Even modest LESP reductions combined with increased gastroesophageal pressure gradients after LVSG can meaningfully raise reflux burden. The physiologic pattern—reduced LES competence with increased intragastric and EGJ pressure gradients and proximal sleeve pressurization—provides a cohesive explanation for postoperative GERD ([Esophageal Pathophysiology Meta‑analysis, 2020](local-corpus://Esophageal_Pathophysiology_Meta_2020); [Proximal Gastric Pressurization, 2023](local-corpus://Proximal_Gastric_Pressurization_2023)). Clinically, this translates into higher medication use, symptom burden, and mucosal injury after LVSG compared with RYGB.

### Mechanistic Considerations

- Fundic resection removes sling fibers and the gastric “flap valve,” reducing the obliquely oriented cardia’s barrier function.
- Sleeve morphology (narrow proximal segment, incisura angulation) increases proximal gastric pressurization and reflux events.
- EGJ/hiatal anatomy: Disruption of the phreno‑esophageal ligament and unrecognized/untreated hiatal hernia can worsen EGJ competence.
- Pyloric function and gastric emptying changes may alter intragastric pressure dynamics and reflux patterns.

Objective HRM has demonstrated elevated intragastric pressure and altered EGJ contractile integral, while MII‑pH confirms increased acid exposure post‑LVSG ([Esophageal HRM mechanisms study, 2020](local-corpus://Esophageal_HRM_Mechanisms_2020)).

### Practice Implications

- Preoperative evaluation: Comprehensive assessment (symptoms, endoscopy, HRM, pH‑impedance) identifies patients at higher risk (e.g., high Hill grade, abnormal pH, IEM, low LES pressure). Patients with normal pre‑op anatomy/physiology have low de novo GERD risk after LVSG in experienced hands, whereas those with abnormalities may fare better with RYGB or LVSG modifications ([De Novo GERD infrequent with comprehensive assessment, 2024](local-corpus://Comprehensive_Assessment_2024)).
- Technique optimization:
  - Avoid excessively tight proximal sleeves and extreme narrowing at the incisura; consider larger bougie and longer pyloric distance where safe.
  - Identify and repair hiatal hernias; consider reconstructing the phreno‑esophageal ligament to reduce intrathoracic sleeve migration and reflux ([R‑PEL study, 2023](local-corpus://R_PEL_2023)).
  - In select GERD‑prone patients, LVSG with fundoplication or cardiopexy may mitigate reflux, albeit with evolving evidence and trade‑offs ([Sleeve‑fundoplication meta‑analysis, 2025](local-corpus://FSG_Meta_2025); [LTC study, 2025](local-corpus://LTC_2025)).
- Postoperative management:
  - Objective testing for refractory symptoms; consider magnetic sphincter augmentation in carefully selected patients with preserved motility and anatomy, acknowledging limited data ([MSA prospective study, 2025](local-corpus://MSA_Prospective_2025)).
  - Conversion to RYGB remains the most validated surgical remedy for refractory GERD, improving DMS and symptoms in most patients ([Sleeve‑to‑bypass conversion, 2025](local-corpus://Sleeve_to_Bypass_2025); [Conversion cohort with pH/GERDq, 2025](local-corpus://Conversion_GERDq_pH_2025)).

### Strengths and Limitations

- Strengths: PRISMA‑aligned methodology; focus on objective pre–post EFTs; triangulation with recent meta‑analyses; exploration of technique and patient factors.
- Limitations:
  - Predominantly nonrandomized pre–post designs vulnerable to confounding.
  - Measurement variability (HRM vs conventional; pH vs MII‑pH) and incomplete reporting (PPI washout, technique details).
  - Heterogeneity in follow‑up timing; paucity of long‑term pre–post EFTs.
  - Some contradictory LES length findings likely reflect early postoperative artifacts or modality differences.

### Certainty of Evidence (GRADE)

- LESP: Low certainty—consistent direction but imprecision and risk of bias.
- LESL: Low certainty—inconsistency and measurement variability.
- DMS/acid exposure: Low to moderate certainty—consistent increases across cohorts and corroborating meta‑analyses, but nonrandomized design.

## Conclusions

LVSG produces reproducible physiologic changes at the esophagogastric junction—lower LES pressure, shorter LES length—and increases in esophageal acid exposure that collectively raise the risk of postoperative GERD and its complications. While the magnitude of LESP reduction is modest on average, it is clinically meaningful in the context of increased intragastric pressurization and EGJ gradient after LVSG. Technical strategy and patient selection modulate this risk; preoperative objective testing should guide procedure choice, with RYGB favored in high‑risk GERD phenotypes. For patients undergoing LVSG, meticulous technique (prudent bougie size and pyloric distance, systematic hiatal assessment/repair) and selective anti‑reflux adjuncts may mitigate risk. Future research should standardize HRM/MII‑pH protocols, report surgical technique in detail, and extend follow‑up to clarify long‑term physiology and mucosal outcomes.
