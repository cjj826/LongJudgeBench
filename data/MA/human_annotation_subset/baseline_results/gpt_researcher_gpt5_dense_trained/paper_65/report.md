Data items and harmonization
- PCOS diagnosis: accepted NIH, Rotterdam, AE-PCOS Society, or clinical guideline-based diagnoses; recorded criteria used where reported.
- NC measurement: extracted anatomical landmark, posture, and device when available; otherwise recorded as “not reported.”
- Metabolic syndrome: extracted definition (NCEP ATP III or IDF) and component thresholds; where unspecified, noted as “unspecified MetS criteria.”
- Insulin resistance: extracted HOMA-IR or QUICKI; if HOMA-IR, noted assay methods and any IR cutoff used. When only correlations were provided, we planned Fisher’s z transformation for pooling.
- Effect measures:
  - PCOS vs non-PCOS NC difference: planned extraction of means/SDs and conversion to standardized mean difference (Hedges g).
  - Associations with MetS/IR: planned extraction of adjusted odds ratios per cm or per SD of NC, regression coefficients (beta) with standard errors, or correlation coefficients (r). Where studies reported ROC results, we extracted AUC, sensitivity, specificity, and proposed cutoffs.
- Confounders: recorded whether models adjusted for age, BMI, WC, smoking, and reproductive hormones.

Synthesis, heterogeneity, and additional analyses
- Meta-analysis: Not feasible due to absent extractable group means/SDs for NC and lack of comparable adjusted effect sizes (ORs per cm/SD or consistent correlation coefficients). Consequently, we did not calculate pooled effects, I2, or tau2.
- Subgroups/sensitivity: Pre-specified subgroups by PCOS diagnostic criteria, BMI/WC adjustment, and NC protocol were not analyzable given data constraints.
- Meta-regression: Not performed due to insufficient number of studies and heterogeneity in effect measures.
- Publication bias: Not assessed (funnel plots/Egger’s) owing to <10 studies per outcome and absence of directly comparable effect sizes.

Certainty of evidence (GRADE, outcome-specific)
- NC vs non-PCOS difference (mean NC): Very low. Downgraded for risk of bias (serious), inconsistency/indirectness (insufficient direct comparative data), and imprecision (no pooled estimate).
- NC associated with insulin resistance in PCOS: Low. Downgraded for risk of bias (moderate–serious), imprecision (no pooled effect), and indirectness (single-country cohorts), but not for inconsistency (directionally consistent).
- NC associated with metabolic syndrome in PCOS: Low to moderate. Downgraded for risk of bias (serious in key study), imprecision (no pooled effect), and indirectness (criteria not consistently specified). Some upgrade consideration for large sample size and reported independence from adiposity in at least one study, but observational design limits certainty.

Practice points
- Measure NC in PCOS clinics as an adjunct to BMI and waist circumference:
  - Position: standing, head in Frankfort plane; measure horizontally at or just below the thyroid cartilage; use non-stretch tape; record to 0.1 cm.
  - Re-measure if NC ≥33 cm in young South Asian women or visibly disproportionate to BMI; thresholds likely population-specific and require local validation.
  - Elevated NC should prompt assessment of IR (fasting glucose/insulin, HOMA-IR) and MetS components (BP, triglycerides, HDL-C, waist).
- Communicate that NC is a risk marker, not a diagnosis; integrate with lifestyle counseling and, where appropriate, metformin or other guideline-based interventions.

Research priorities
- Prospective, multi-ethnic PCOS cohorts with standardized NC protocols; report adjusted ORs per cm/SD for MetS and IR, and incremental value beyond BMI/WC using discrimination and reclassification metrics.
- Harmonize MetS definitions (NCEP ATP III, IDF) and IR metrics (HOMA-IR assay standardization; prespecified cutoffs).
- Establish and validate NC cutoffs by age, ethnicity, and PCOS phenotype; examine links to incident T2D, MASLD, and CVD.
- Explore imaging correlates (MRI/CT) to elucidate NC’s relation to ectopic fat depots and mechanistic pathways.

Overall conclusion
NC is a simple, reproducible anthropometric marker that aligns with metabolically adverse phenotypes in PCOS. Current observational evidence—though limited by cross-sectional design, incomplete reporting, and heterogeneity—indicates that greater NC is independently associated with insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome in women with PCOS, and NC-based thresholds may offer practical screening utility in some populations. Evidence is insufficient to quantify whether NC is systematically larger in PCOS versus non-PCOS controls after accounting for adiposity. Incorporating standardized NC measurement into routine PCOS care is reasonable as part of comprehensive cardiometabolic risk stratification, while stronger, harmonized studies are needed to define population-specific cutoffs and determine NC’s incremental predictive value beyond BMI and waist circumference.
