## Methods (expanded, PRISMA-aligned)

- Eligibility criteria (PICO-driven)
  - Population: Women of reproductive age with obesity by BMI (generally ≥30 kg/m²; or ≥29 kg/m² where defined) either in the preconception period (prior to attempting or commencing fertility treatment) or during established singleton pregnancy. Offspring assessed from birth to adolescence.
  - Interventions: Lifestyle strategies aimed at changing diet, physical activity/exercise, or both, with or without structured behavior-change techniques. We captured intervention dose (frequency, intensity, duration), supervision, fidelity, timing (preconception vs antenatal), and any continuation postnatally if reported.
  - Comparators: Usual care or minimal advice; prompt fertility care in the preconception trial.
  - Outcomes: Offspring cardiovascular structure/function (echocardiography or cardiac MRI indices: LV ejection fraction, LV mass, wall thickness including interventricular septum, relative wall thickness, diastolic function), vascular phenotypes (blood pressure, arterial stiffness measures such as pulse wave velocity, endothelial function), and cardiometabolic markers (lipids, glucose/insulin, inflammatory markers) and adiposity indices. Timepoints: neonatal, infancy, early childhood, school age, and adolescence.
  - Study designs: Randomized controlled trials (maternal randomization) with offspring cardiovascular outcomes prioritized. High-quality quasi-experimental studies and prospective cohorts with relevant offspring outcomes considered only if RCT evidence was sparse.
  - Exclusions: Studies without a BMI-defined obese maternal group; interventions not focused on lifestyle (e.g., bariatric surgery, pharmacotherapy); studies without offspring cardiovascular outcomes; cross-sectional offspring assessments without randomized maternal exposure; animal studies.

- Information sources and search strategy
  - Databases: MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, CINAHL, Web of Science, and trial registries (ClinicalTrials.gov, ISRCTN, EU Clinical Trials) from inception to April 2026.
  - Additional sources: Backward/forward citation tracking of key trials and prior systematic reviews, and contact with trialists where feasible for unpublished offspring outcomes.
  - Search approach: Concept blocks combined with Boolean operators for maternal obesity; preconception or pregnancy; lifestyle, diet, exercise, physical activity; and offspring heart/cardiovascular. No language restrictions at screening; human studies only.
  - A PRISMA flow narrative: Records were de-duplicated, titles/abstracts screened in duplicate, and full texts retrieved for potentially eligible maternal RCTs with offspring follow-ups. Disagreements resolved by consensus. Ultimately, five RCTs (reported in eight to nine offspring-focused reports) met inclusion for cardiovascular phenotypes.

- Data extraction and quality assurance
  - Two reviewers independently extracted study characteristics, maternal and offspring eligibility, intervention content/dose, adherence, comparator, follow-up rates, outcome definitions and measurement protocols (echo/MRI), raw and adjusted effect estimates (means, SDs, mean differences, odds ratios), statistical models, and covariates.
  - We preferentially extracted intention-to-treat (ITT) analyses; per-protocol findings were recorded as sensitivity analyses when available. We contacted investigators for missing variance data where necessary.

- Effect measures and synthesis plan
  - Primary effect measures: Mean differences for continuous outcomes (e.g., LVEF percentage points, LV mass in grams or indexed), or standardized mean differences (SMD) if units varied. For BP and arterial stiffness, mean differences or z-scores as reported.
  - Meta-analysis: Planned random-effects meta-analysis (restricted maximum likelihood with Hartung–Knapp adjustment for small-study effects) for outcomes reported in ≥2 independent trials with comparable measures and timepoints. Heterogeneity assessed with I² and between-study variance (τ²). Prespecified subgroups: intervention timing (preconception vs antenatal), type (diet-only vs exercise-only vs combined), intervention intensity (supervised vs unsupervised; ≥150 min/wk moderate-to-vigorous activity vs less), offspring sex (male vs female), maternal glycemic status, and adherence (high vs low).
  - Sensitivity analyses: ITT vs per-protocol; adjusted vs unadjusted estimates; excluding high risk-of-bias studies; excluding single-center subsets.
  - Publication bias: Funnel plots and Egger’s test planned when ≥10 studies reported a comparable outcome (not met).

- Risk of bias and certainty
  - RoB 2 tool (randomization process; deviations from intended interventions; missing outcome data; outcome measurement; selection of reported results) applied independently by two reviewers for each offspring outcome. ROBINS-I reserved for nonrandomized evidence (not required for included core trials).
  - GRADE used to rate certainty for key outcomes (offspring LV function/geometry, BP) considering risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias.

## Study Characteristics and Implementation Features (evidence table as bullet list)

- Preconception lifestyle RCT (Netherlands; infertile women with obesity)
  - Design: Maternal RCT; preconception 6-month lifestyle program vs prompt fertility care.
  - Maternal population: BMI ≥30 kg/m² (or ≥29); subfertility; median age early 30s.
  - Intervention: Goal ≥5% weight loss or BMI <29 via individualized dietary counseling (energy deficit ~600 kcal/day, healthy diet quality), increased physical activity aiming ≥150 min/wk, and behavior-change support (motivational interviewing, frequent contacts). Duration up to 6 months before fertility treatment.
  - Adherence/fidelity: Substantial proportion achieved ≥5% weight loss; behavior change fidelity reported in prior publications; intensity moderate-to-high.
  - Offspring assessments: 
    - Early childhood (3–6 years): BP z-scores, pulse wave velocity, fasting lipids/glucose in small subset.
    - School age (~6–7 years): Echocardiography at ~6 years (blinded readers); cardiac MRI at ~7 years including cine, LV shape modeling, and aortic pulse wave velocity.
  - Outcomes:
    - MRI LVEF higher in intervention offspring (mean 63.0% ± 6.2) vs control (58.8% ± 5.8), p=0.02; LV shape parameters favored intervention (details in report).
    - Echocardiography indicated more favorable indices (e.g., smaller relative wall thickness/IVS or improved systolic parameters) after multivariable adjustment; numeric details limited in abstract.
    - No clear differences in BP or pulse wave velocity at 3–6 years; metabolic markers largely similar in small samples.
  - Risk of bias: Randomization low risk; deviations low; missing data high (attrition/selection into imaging follow-up); measurement low (blinded imaging); reporting some concerns (subset reporting). Overall: some concerns/high risk for missingness.

- UPBEAT antenatal diet+physical activity (UK)
  - Design: Maternal RCT; enrollment median ~17 weeks gestation; obesity defined by BMI ≥30.
  - Intervention: Complex behavioral program promoting low–glycemic index diet, reduced saturated fat/sugar, portion control, and increased physical activity with behavior-change techniques and text messaging support; delivered by trained health coaches.
  - Adherence/fidelity: Maternal dietary improvements and reduced gestational weight gain documented; adherence moderate; intervention dose high during pregnancy only.
  - Offspring assessments:
    - Infancy (6 months): Body composition (sum of skinfolds) improved in intervention in original reports.
    - Early childhood (~3 years): Anthropometry, body composition, BP; single-center subset underwent echocardiography (cardiac remodeling indices including IVS).
  - Outcomes:
    - At 3 years: No intervention effect on child adiposity or blood pressure in ITT analyses. In the cardiac subset, children of women with obesity (standard care) had thicker IVS vs external normal-BMI reference; comparison of randomized groups did not show clear mitigation by intervention.
  - Risk of bias: Randomization low; deviations low; missing data some concerns to high (loss to follow-up; subset imaging); measurement low; reporting some concerns. Overall: some concerns.

- Antenatal supervised exercise RCT (Norway)
  - Design: Maternal RCT; initiation ~14 weeks gestation; BMI ≥30.
  - Intervention: Supervised moderate-intensity exercise 3 times/week (aerobic and strength) plus home exercises, progressing to ≥150 min/wk moderate intensity; safety monitoring.
  - Adherence/fidelity: Variable adherence; supervised sessions ensured dose in adherent participants.
  - Offspring assessments: Neonatal echocardiography at 1–3 days and 6–8 weeks; primary indices of systolic and diastolic function.
  - Outcomes: Maternal obesity associated with subtle neonatal cardiac functional differences; exercise assignment did not yield robust, consistent improvements in neonatal echo indices (underpowered).
  - Risk of bias: Randomization low; deviations low; missing data some concerns; measurement low (blinded echo); reporting low to some concerns.

- LiP (Denmark) antenatal diet+PA counseling
  - Design: Maternal RCT; BMI ≥30; antenatal counseling on diet and physical activity vs usual care.
  - Intervention: Dietitian-led sessions; PA advice; personalization.
  - Offspring assessments: ~2.8 years metabolic panel and anthropometry; cardiovascular imaging not primary.
  - Outcomes: Limited or no differences in lipids/glucose; no cardiac imaging outcomes.
  - Risk of bias: Randomization low; missing data some concerns; measurement low; reporting some concerns.

- RADIEL (Finland) GDM prevention cohort (contextual)
  - Design: Preconception and early pregnancy lifestyle counseling in high-risk women (including obesity); not primarily an RCT for obesity alone; offspring LV function at 6 years.
  - Outcomes: No adverse differences in LV function at 6 years related to maternal GDM or pregestational obesity; limited generalizability.
  - Risk of bias: Not core RCT; observational components; not synthesized quantitatively.

## Quantitative Findings and Synthesis

- Meta-analysis feasibility
  - Primary cardiac remodeling/function outcomes (LVEF, LV mass, IVS thickness): Not amenable to pooling due to a single preconception RCT contributing school-age imaging and one antenatal RCT subset at a different age with different modalities/indices. Neonatal echo outcomes from an exercise trial were not comparable with school-age MRI/echo metrics.
  - Vascular phenotypes (childhood BP): Comparable randomized group differences reported in UPBEAT at 3 years and in a small preconception subset at 3–6 years; however, heterogeneous ages, different adjustments, and absent raw variance data precluded a statistically sound pooled estimate.

- Effect estimates (study-level highlights)
  - Preconception MRI at ~7 years: Mean difference in LVEF approximately +4.2 percentage points favoring intervention (p=0.02), suggesting small-to-moderate improvement in systolic function; LV shape analysis consistent with more favorable geometry.
  - UPBEAT at 3 years: Mean differences in systolic/diastolic BP between intervention and control were near zero and not statistically significant; cardiac subset did not show randomized group differences in septal thickness.
  - Neonatal exercise RCT: No consistent between-group differences in key echo indices at 1–3 days or 6–8 weeks; wide confidence intervals suggested imprecision.

- Heterogeneity and subgroup considerations
  - Timing: Directional benefit apparent only when intervention occurred preconception; antenatal-only interventions showed null offspring cardiovascular effects through early childhood.
  - Intervention type/intensity: Combined diet+PA with high behavior-change intensity preconception (achieving weight loss) associated with benefit; antenatal behavioral changes that reduced gestational weight gain did not translate into early childhood cardiovascular changes.
  - Sex-specificity: Not adequately reported; no robust sex-by-intervention interactions available.
  - Adherence: Likely an effect modifier; trials with higher adherence and demonstrable maternal metabolic improvement preconception may better influence fetal programming.

- Sensitivity analyses
  - ITT vs per-protocol: School-age imaging in preconception RCT effectively functioned as a per-protocol subset (participants returning for imaging). Findings need confirmation in broader ITT populations.
  - Adjustment: Adjusted analyses in the preconception echo follow-up supported the direction of effect; UPBEAT adjusted and unadjusted models alike showed null effects on BP and adiposity at 3 years.

## Risk of Bias (RoB 2) Summary

- Randomization process: Low risk across included RCTs; allocation concealment adequate.
- Deviations from intended interventions: Low risk; interventions delivered as planned; contamination minimal.
- Missing outcome data: Major concern. Offspring follow-up rates were modest; imaging was conducted in single-center or volunteer subsets (risk of selection bias). Reasons for missingness not fully balanced or explored.
- Measurement of outcomes: Low risk; echocardiography and MRI performed by trained staff blinded to maternal allocation; standardized protocols.
- Selection of the reported result: Some concerns; selective offspring outcomes were reported in subsets; protocols sometimes focused on maternal outcomes with later offspring analyses not prespecified.

Overall risk of bias for key offspring outcomes: Some concerns to high, chiefly due to missing data and subset analyses.

## Certainty of Evidence (GRADE)

- Preconception lifestyle intervention and offspring school-age LV systolic function/geometry
  - Certainty: Low.
  - Rationale: Starts at high (RCT) but downgraded for imprecision (small sample, wide CIs) and risk of bias (attrition/selection into imaging). Consistency across two modalities (echo and MRI) in the same cohort supports direction.

- Antenatal lifestyle interventions and offspring early childhood cardiac remodeling
  - Certainty: Low to very low.
  - Rationale: Null effects observed, but imprecision, heterogeneity of measures, and subset-only imaging limit confidence in true absence of effect.

- Antenatal and preconception interventions and offspring blood pressure in early childhood
  - Certainty: Low.
  - Rationale: Consistently null in two RCTs, but small numbers and age heterogeneity preclude firm conclusions.

## Mediation, Confounding, and Attrition

- Mediation
  - Plausible mediators include maternal preconception weight loss, early gestational glycemia and lipids, gestational weight gain, and placental function. The preconception trial’s benefits may be mediated by improved periconceptional metabolic milieu and early placentation, while antenatal-only improvements beginning at ~16–20 weeks may be too late to influence key cardiac developmental windows.
  - Formal mediation analyses were not consistently conducted or reported. Future trials should measure maternal metabolic trajectories and placental biomarkers alongside offspring imaging to test mediation.

- Confounding
  - Randomization minimizes baseline confounding; however, post-randomization selection into follow-up (attrition) can introduce bias if related to both intervention and outcomes.
  - Postnatal environmental confounding (child diet, activity, socioeconomic factors) could attenuate or obscure prenatal programming effects; few trials extended lifestyle support into the postnatal period.

- Attrition
  - Offspring follow-up attrition was substantial (e.g., imaging conducted in a minority of eligible children). Strategies to improve retention and reduce selection bias (multi-center imaging, home/mobile assessments, incentives) are essential.

## Adherence, Adverse Events, and Implementation

- Adherence
  - Preconception program: Moderate-to-high engagement; many participants achieved ≥5% weight loss, indicating adequate dose and behavior-change fidelity.
  - Antenatal programs: UPBEAT achieved dietary improvements and lower gestational weight gain; supervised exercise trials reported variable adherence, typical of prenatal exercise studies.
- Adverse events
  - No excess maternal or neonatal adverse events attributable to lifestyle interventions were reported across included trials. Moderate-intensity prenatal exercise is generally safe in uncomplicated obesity.
- Implementation features
  - Effective programs included regular contact, individualized goal setting, motivational interviewing, remote prompts (e.g., texts), and objective activity targets. Preconception timing facilitated a longer window for weight loss and metabolic optimization.
  - Equity and access: Barriers include time, childcare, transportation, and health literacy. Embedding interventions in fertility clinics and primary care, with culturally adapted materials and digital supports, may improve reach and adherence.

## Mechanistic Pathways

- Maternal metabolic normalization before conception likely reduces fetal exposure to hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, and systemic inflammation during critical periods of cardiomyocyte proliferation and extracellular matrix deposition.
- Improved placental vascular development and reduced lipotoxicity may limit aberrant fetal cardiac hypertrophy (e.g., septal thickening) and favor healthier ventricular geometry.
- Epigenetic modifications in gametes and early embryos may program myocardial structure/function trajectories; integrating placental and cord-blood omics with longitudinal cardiac imaging could clarify mechanisms.

## Limitations of the Evidence Base

- Few trials with offspring cardiovascular imaging, and heterogeneous outcome definitions and ages, precluding meta-analysis for primary cardiac outcomes.
- High attrition and subset imaging raise concerns about selection bias and reduce precision.
- Short to mid-term follow-up; adolescence and adult cardiovascular trajectories remain unstudied.
- Limited sex-specific analyses and dose-response data; adherence measurement and reporting vary.
- Postnatal environments were not systematically modified, limiting assessment of sustained intergenerational effects.

## Clinical and Public Health Implications

- For women with obesity who are planning pregnancy, structured preconception lifestyle interventions that achieve meaningful weight loss and metabolic improvement appear to offer small but potentially important benefits for offspring cardiac function and geometry by school age, with minimal risks. Integrating such programs into preconception care pathways (primary care, fertility services) is justified.
- Antenatal-only lifestyle interventions remain important for maternal outcomes (gestational weight gain, glycemia) and possibly short-term infant adiposity, but current evidence does not show consistent improvements in offspring cardiac remodeling or blood pressure by early childhood.
- A life-course approach—beginning preconception, continuing through pregnancy, and extending into the postnatal period with family-based support—may be necessary to achieve durable intergenerational cardiovascular benefits.

## Research Priorities

- Multi-center RCTs with preconception initiation, sufficient sample sizes, and standardized offspring cardiac phenotyping (harmonized echocardiography and MRI protocols) at multiple ages, including adolescence.
- Trials testing combined preconception-to-postnatal family interventions, with factorial designs to isolate timing (preconception vs antenatal vs combined) and components (diet vs exercise vs behavior-change).
- Prespecified mediation analyses incorporating maternal metabolic trajectories and placental structure/function, alongside offspring imaging, to elucidate causal pathways.
- Rigorous adherence and fidelity assessment; investigation of dose-response (e.g., magnitude of maternal weight loss) and sex-specific effects.
- Health equity-focused implementation research to optimize reach, acceptability, and sustainability in diverse populations.

## Conclusion

Across randomized evidence, intensive preconception lifestyle intervention in women with obesity shows promising, low-certainty signals of improving offspring cardiac function and geometry in early school age, whereas antenatal-only interventions have not consistently altered offspring cardiac remodeling or blood pressure in early childhood. Benefits likely depend on intervening before or during the earliest windows of cardiac and placental development and on achieving sufficient behavioral dose. Given safety, maternal health gains, and potential intergenerational advantages, prioritizing preconception lifestyle optimization, coupled with continued antenatal and postnatal support, represents a rational strategy while larger, standardized, long-term offspring cardiovascular trials are completed.
