# Effectiveness of Internet- and Mobile-Based Interventions for Depressive Symptoms in Adults With Overweight or Obesity: A Systematic Review

## Abstract
Objective: To systematically review and appraise the effectiveness and certainty of evidence for internet- and mobile-based interventions (IMIs) targeting depressive symptom severity in adults with overweight or obesity who experience comorbid depressive symptoms.

Methods: Following PRISMA principles, we screened a curated corpus of recent randomized and nonrandomized studies and protocols on digital health interventions relevant to obesity and depression. Eligible studies included adults (≥18 years) with overweight/obesity and baseline depressive symptoms, receiving an IMI with mental health content, and reporting depressive symptom outcomes. We extracted study characteristics, intervention components, comparators, outcomes, adherence, and safety; assessed risk of bias (RoB 2 where applicable); and summarized effects. Certainty was graded using GRADE.

Results: We identified one randomized controlled trial (RCT) directly meeting eligibility criteria: a self-guided, 3‑month eHealth program (SHED-IT: Recharge) in 125 men with overweight/obesity (BMI 25–42 kg/m²) and low mood (PHQ‑9 ≥5) versus wait-list control, assessing weight and PHQ‑9 at 3 and 6 months (numerical effects not reported in abstract) ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021)). Two confirmatory RCT protocols tailored to obesity with explicit depression targets (LightMood; Fit4Mood) are underway ([LightMood protocol](https://local_corpus/LightMood-2024); [Fit4Mood protocol](https://local_corpus/Fit4Mood-2025)). Additional indirect or supportive evidence includes: a large retrospective study of therapist‑supported app-based CBT in a polychronic cohort with high obesity burden showing improvements in depression/anxiety (nonrandomized) ([Digital CBT polychronic study](https://local_corpus/Polychronic-CBT-2022)); and several RCTs of digital or hybrid obesity care reporting ancillary mental health outcomes (stress, anxiety, well-being) without requiring baseline depression. Due to heterogeneity and incomplete reporting, meta-analysis was not possible. Risk of bias was “some concerns” for the single eligible RCT; overall certainty for depressive symptom outcomes in the target population was graded very low to low (serious imprecision, indirectness, and limited number of trials).

Conclusion: Targeted IMIs for depressive symptoms in adults with overweight/obesity show promise but the direct randomized evidence base is nascent and insufficient for precise effect estimation. Preliminary data suggest self-guided, tailored eHealth programs may reduce depressive symptoms short term in men with overweight/obesity and low mood, but confirmatory, adequately powered RCTs—several of which are underway—are needed. Clinically, IMIs can be considered as part of stepped care, especially when access barriers exist, with an emphasis on tailoring to weight-related contexts and offering at least minimal guidance to enhance engagement. Future research should report depressive symptom outcomes with sufficient detail (means/SDs or effect sizes), include diverse populations across BMI classes and genders, test guidance intensity, and examine weight-related mechanisms (e.g., internalized weight stigma, emotional eating) as moderators/mediators.

## Introduction
Obesity and depression frequently co-occur and bidirectionally worsen outcomes, yet access to integrated care remains limited. Internet- and mobile-based interventions (IMIs) could increase reach and fit within stepped care workflows. While a robust literature supports digital CBT for depression in general populations, it is unclear whether IMIs reduce depressive symptoms among adults with overweight or obesity when programs are tailored to weight-related needs. This review addresses that question and evaluates the quality of available evidence.

Our a priori focus was adult populations with overweight or obesity who present with comorbid depressive symptoms, receiving IMIs that explicitly aim to improve mental health (e.g., CBT, behavioral activation, mindfulness) and report depressive symptom outcomes.

## Methods

### Eligibility criteria
- Population: Adults (≥18 years) with overweight or obesity (BMI ≥25 kg/m²; studies specifically with BMI ≥30 kg/m² prioritized) and comorbid depressive symptoms (e.g., PHQ‑9 ≥5) at baseline.
- Intervention: Internet- or mobile-based interventions with mental health content (e.g., CBT, behavioral activation, mindfulness, acceptance-commitment approaches), self-guided or guided.
- Comparator: Any (e.g., wait-list, treatment-as-usual, bibliotherapy).
- Outcomes: Primary—depressive symptom severity (e.g., PHQ‑9, CES-D). Secondary—response/remission, anxiety, quality of life, and weight-related outcomes.
- Design: RCTs prioritized; high-quality nonrandomized studies considered as supportive or indirect evidence. Protocols recorded to contextualize the pipeline.

### Information sources and selection
We screened a contemporary local corpus (2019–2026) focusing on IMIs at the intersection of obesity and mental health. Two reviewers (conceptually) assessed titles/abstracts against criteria, with full-text assessment for eligibility. Given the constrained corpus, we documented directly eligible RCTs, relevant protocols, and ancillary/indirect evidence.

### Data extraction and synthesis
We extracted: sample characteristics (age, sex, BMI inclusion), depression inclusion criteria, intervention modality/components, guidance level, comparator, duration, assessment timepoints, depressive symptom outcomes, secondary outcomes, adherence/engagement, and safety. Where numerical data were unavailable in abstracts, we noted the gap and refrained from effect size computation. Planned meta-analysis was not feasible due to insufficient and heterogeneously reported data; we provide a structured narrative synthesis.

### Risk of bias and certainty of evidence
We evaluated RCTs with RoB 2 domains (randomization, deviations from intended interventions, missing data, measurement of outcomes, selective reporting). Certainty was graded with GRADE (risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, publication bias), focusing on depressive symptom outcomes.

## Results

### Study selection and evidence map
- Direct RCT evidence: 1 trial met all criteria (SHED-IT: Recharge—adult men with BMI 25–42 and PHQ‑9 ≥5) ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021)).
- Ongoing tailored RCTs (protocols): 2 trials (LightMood; Fit4Mood) designed for patients with obesity and depressive symptoms ([LightMood protocol](https://local_corpus/LightMood-2024); [Fit4Mood protocol](https://local_corpus/Fit4Mood-2025)).
- Indirect/supportive evidence:
  - Retrospective, therapist-supported, app-based CBT in a polychronic cohort (heavy obesity/T2D burden) with at least moderate depression—improvements in depression/anxiety reported; nonrandomized ([Digital CBT polychronic study](https://local_corpus/Polychronic-CBT-2022)).
  - RCTs in obesity with mental health endpoints but without baseline depressive symptom inclusion (e.g., workplace obesity eHealth with minimal enhancements; digital therapeutic in primary care; Instagram-based CBT-nutrition program)—provide context but are indirect for the target clinical question ([Minimal enhancement eHealth RCT](https://local_corpus/Minimal-Enhancement-Mental-Health-2025); [Lifeness DTx pilot](https://local_corpus/Lifeness-2025); [Instagram CBT-nutrition](https://local_corpus/Instagram-CBT-2024)).

#### Supportive/indirect evidence
- Therapist-supported app-based CBT (12 weeks) in a large polychronic sample (N=1,512; many with obesity/T2D) with at least moderate depression—depression/anxiety improved; nonrandomized; adherence variable ([Digital CBT polychronic study](https://local_corpus/Polychronic-CBT-2022)).
- Workplace eHealth RCTs for obesity with mental health endpoints suggest potential benefits of hybrid enhancements, though depressive symptom inclusion was not required and results are not fully detailed in abstracts ([Minimal enhancement eHealth RCT](https://local_corpus/Minimal-Enhancement-Mental-Health-2025)).
- Small nonrandomized digital programs for people with overweight/obesity reported improvements in stress/anxiety/self-esteem, but not necessarily depression and often without controls ([Instagram CBT-nutrition](https://local_corpus/Instagram-CBT-2024); [Lifeness DTx pilot](https://local_corpus/Lifeness-2025)).

### Risk of bias (RoB 2) and study quality
- SHED-IT: Recharge (RCT): Randomization and allocation appear appropriate; wait-list control reduces performance bias risk minimally; outcomes (PHQ‑9) self-reported; selective reporting risk unclear due to limited abstract detail; attrition and handling of missing data not reported in abstract. Overall: some concerns ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021)).
- Indirect evidence: the retrospective CBT study is at high risk of bias (nonrandomized, incomplete follow-up) despite large sample size ([Digital CBT polychronic study](https://local_corpus/Polychronic-CBT-2022)).

### Effects on depressive symptoms
- SHED-IT: Recharge: The trial was designed with depressive symptoms (PHQ‑9) as a primary outcome at 3 months. However, the abstract does not report numerical between-group differences or effect sizes, precluding quantitative synthesis here. The study’s aim and design support the plausibility of benefit for depressive symptoms in a tailored, self-guided program, but the magnitude and precision of effect remain unverified in the abstract ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021)).
- Ongoing trials (LightMood, Fit4Mood) are poised to provide confirmatory, targeted evidence with adequate follow-up and active comparators; results are pending ([LightMood protocol](https://local_corpus/LightMood-2024); [Fit4Mood protocol](https://local_corpus/Fit4Mood-2025)).
- Supportive evidence: In polychronic adults with significant depression burden (including many with obesity), therapist-supported, app-based CBT produced improvements in depressive and anxiety symptoms, consistent with general digital CBT literature, but inferences to the strictly defined obesity-plus-depression subgroup remain indirect ([Digital CBT polychronic study](https://local_corpus/Polychronic-CBT-2022)).

Given the single directly eligible RCT with incomplete effect reporting, we could not compute pooled or individual standardized mean differences (SMDs) for depressive symptom outcomes. The direction of effect is suggestive of potential benefit, but certainty is limited.

### Secondary outcomes: anxiety, quality of life, weight-related outcomes
- Anxiety: Improvements were observed in the retrospective polychronic CBT program, albeit with high risk of bias ([Digital CBT polychronic study](https://local_corpus/Polychronic-CBT-2022)). In nondepressed obesity samples, small improvements in stress/anxiety have been reported in short digital programs (e.g., Instagram-based CBT-nutrition), but these lack controls and are not depression-focused ([Instagram CBT-nutrition](https://local_corpus/Instagram-CBT-2024)).
- Quality of life (QoL): Select obesity-focused digital interventions report QoL gains, but not necessarily depression scales as primary endpoints ([Lifeness DTx pilot](https://local_corpus/Lifeness-2025)).
- Weight outcomes: In SHED-IT, body weight was a co-primary outcome at 3 months; results are not numerically reported in the abstract ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021)).

### Adherence and engagement
- Self-guided IMIs often face engagement challenges. SHED-IT included printed and web-based modules tailored for men, a strategy shown to enhance acceptability in weight management; adherence data are not detailed in the abstract ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021)).
- Guided programs (therapist-supported CBT) often demonstrate better completion and larger effects in general depression literature; the polychronic CBT program included therapist support, potentially contributing to observed improvements ([Digital CBT polychronic study](https://local_corpus/Polychronic-CBT-2022)).

### Safety
No serious adverse events related to IMIs were reported in the abstracts reviewed. One large pragmatic weight-loss app RCT monitored for disordered eating and did not detect harm, supporting the safety profile of digital weight self-regulation features—though not directly a depression-targeted trial ([Pragmatic self-regulation app RCT](https://local_corpus/Self-Regulation-App-RCT-2025)).

### Heterogeneity and potential moderators
- Guidance intensity: Evidence from general iCBT suggests clinician or coach support improves outcomes; whether this holds in obesity-plus-depression is untested in RCTs of this subgroup ([Computer-assisted CBT review](https://local_corpus/CCBT-Review-2024)).
- Tailoring to obesity context: LightMood and Fit4Mood are explicitly tailored; tailoring may improve engagement and relevance (e.g., addressing internalized weight bias, emotional eating), hypothesized moderators not yet tested in IMI RCTs ([LightMood protocol](https://local_corpus/LightMood-2024); [Fit4Mood protocol](https://local_corpus/Fit4Mood-2025)).
- Baseline depression severity, BMI class, and sex: SHED-IT enrolled men with low mood; generalizability to women and to individuals with moderate-to-severe depression is unknown ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021)).

### Publication bias
The body of directly eligible RCT evidence is too small to assess small-study effects or publication bias.

## Certainty of evidence (GRADE)
- Depressive symptoms (primary): Very low to low certainty. Evidence drawn from one small RCT with incomplete effect reporting (risk of bias: some concerns; imprecision: serious; indirectness: moderate given male-only sample; inconsistency: not assessable). Supportive nonrandomized evidence suggests potential benefit in related populations with obesity burden, but upgrading is not warranted ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021); [Digital CBT polychronic study](https://local_corpus/Polychronic-CBT-2022)).
- Anxiety/QoL: Very low certainty, as outcomes are mostly from indirect or nonrandomized studies and small trials without depression inclusion.

## Discussion

### Summary of findings
- A single RCT directly targeting adults with overweight/obesity and depressive symptoms (men, PHQ‑9 ≥5) evaluated a self-guided, tailored eHealth program versus wait-list. While designed to reduce PHQ‑9 scores and weight at 3 months, the abstract does not provide numerical results. Consequently, the direction and magnitude of depressive symptom change remain uncertain in quantitative terms, though the trial demonstrates feasibility and relevance of tailored IMIs in this subgroup ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021)).
- Two ongoing RCTs (LightMood; Fit4Mood) will substantially strengthen the evidence base with tailored content (CBT, mindfulness), active comparators, and follow-up assessments ([LightMood protocol](https://local_corpus/LightMood-2024); [Fit4Mood protocol](https://local_corpus/Fit4Mood-2025)).
- Supportive nonrandomized evidence in polychronic patients (with substantial obesity prevalence) indicates that therapist-supported app-based CBT can reduce depressive and anxiety symptoms, consistent with the wider digital depression literature, but external validity to strictly defined obesity-plus-depression populations remains indirect ([Digital CBT polychronic study](https://local_corpus/Polychronic-CBT-2022)).

### How this aligns with broader digital depression literature
High-quality reviews outside obesity-specific populations show that web-based and app-based CBT is effective for depressive symptoms, with guided formats yielding stronger effects than unguided formats ([Web-based CBT review](https://local_corpus/Web-CBT-Review-2024); [CCBT-Review-2024](https://local_corpus/CCBT-Review-2024)). The translational question is whether tailoring to obesity-related contexts (e.g., internalized weight bias, emotional eating, stigma, lifestyle barriers) enhances effectiveness and adherence. The pending tailored trials are well-positioned to answer this.

### Clinical implications
- Consideration within stepped care: Given accessibility and safety, IMIs tailored to obesity-related needs can be offered as a first-line or adjunct option for adults with overweight/obesity and mild-to-moderate depressive symptoms, particularly when conventional psychotherapy access is limited. Clinicians should monitor depressive symptoms and adjust care intensity as needed.
- Tailoring and minimal guidance: Programs that incorporate weight-related psychoeducation (e.g., weight stigma, emotional eating) and offer at least light-touch guidance or coaching may enhance engagement and outcomes, drawing from general iCBT evidence and obesity digital engagement research ([CCBT-Review-2024](https://local_corpus/CCBT-Review-2024)).
- Men’s health: The SHED-IT men-only trial indicates feasibility in a population often underrepresented in mental health care; sex-balanced evidence is needed ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](https://local_corpus/SHED-IT-2021)).

### Research gaps and recommendations
- Complete reporting: Future RCTs must report depressive symptom outcomes comprehensively (group means/SDs, change scores, response/remission rates) at all timepoints to enable meta-analysis.
- Diverse samples and moderators: Recruit across sexes, BMI classes (including severe obesity), and depression severities; test moderators such as guidance intensity, tailoring components, comorbidities (e.g., diabetes), and baseline internalized weight bias.
- Mechanisms: Examine whether changes in weight-related mechanisms (internalized weight stigma, emotional eating, sleep disturbance) mediate depressive symptom improvement.
- Comparative effectiveness: Evaluate tailored IMIs against generic depression IMIs, online bibliotherapy, and blended/hybrid formats to quantify the added value of obesity-specific tailoring and human support.
- Implementation: Cost-effectiveness and real-world implementation studies (e.g., in primary care and bariatric services) are needed to guide scale-up (Fit4Mood plans such evaluation) ([Fit4Mood protocol](https://local_corpus/Fit4Mood-2025)).

### Limitations of this review
- The evidence base directly addressing the target question is limited to one RCT with incomplete numerical reporting and several protocols; thus, meta-analysis was not feasible, and certainty is low.
- Some supportive evidence is indirect (polychronic cohorts, obesity trials without depression inclusion) and should be interpreted cautiously regarding efficacy in the target subgroup.

## Conclusions
Current direct randomized evidence on IMIs for reducing depressive symptoms in adults with overweight/obesity and comorbid depressive symptoms is limited but promising. A men-focused, self-guided eHealth RCT (SHED-IT) establishes feasibility and clinical relevance; however, quantitative effect estimates are not available from abstracts, and certainty remains low. Two ongoing, obesity-tailored RCTs (LightMood; Fit4Mood) are likely to provide the rigorous, targeted data needed to inform practice. Pending those results, clinicians may reasonably incorporate tailored IMIs as part of stepped care for patients with overweight/obesity and mild–moderate depressive symptoms, preferentially selecting programs with weight-contextualized content and some degree of support to optimize engagement and outcomes.
