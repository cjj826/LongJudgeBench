## Additional Methodological Notes: Imaging Protocols, Reliability, and Segmentation

- Spectral‑ vs swept‑source OCT/OCTA
  - SD‑OCT with enhanced depth imaging (EDI) allows reliable manual subfoveal CT measurement and has strong longitudinal repeatability when scan location is stabilized with follow‑up mode. However, intergrader variability for manual CT and CVI increases with poor choroid–scleral interface visibility.
  - SS‑OCT/OCTA penetrates deeper and often measures thicker CT than SD‑OCT, partly due to longer wavelength and segmentation differences. These platform differences plausibly contribute to conflicting postmenopausal CT findings.

- OCTA metrics and quality control
  - Vessel density and perfusion density are sensitive to segmentation boundaries, projection artifact removal, scan size (e.g., 3×3 vs 6×6 mm), motion correction, and signal strength. Studies variably reported signal strength indices; some used fixed midday imaging to reduce diurnal effects.
  - FAZ delineation can be automated or manual; manual methods with masked, dual graders reduce bias but add variability unless intraclass correlation coefficients are reported.
  - Peripapillary radial peripapillary capillary (RPC) slab definitions differ across vendors; cross‑study comparisons should be cautious.

- Choroidal vascularity index (CVI)
  - CVI requires binarization of EDI/SS image stacks; thresholding algorithms and region of interest (ROI) selection vary and can shift absolute CVI values. Reporting of intergrader agreement and sensitivity analyses across binarization methods was uncommon.

- Instrument test–retest
  - The smallest detectable change for CT and OCTA VD in healthy eyes can approximate the magnitude of reported menstrual phase differences. Without repeated baseline scans to establish within‑subject variability, interpreting small between‑phase differences remains challenging.

- Timing, diurnal control, and physiological standardization
  - Diurnal variation in CT and OCTA metrics is well‑recognized; only a subset standardized imaging windows (e.g., 11:00–12:00). Hydration status, recent caffeine or nicotine, and exercise were rarely standardized despite potential effects on ocular perfusion pressure and vascular tone.
  - Blood pressure and ocular perfusion pressure (OPP) were recorded in a minority of studies; where measured, luteal BP elevations co‑occurred with choroidal thinning.

## Heterogeneity and Certainty of Evidence (Narrative Mapping)

- Menstrual cycle, choroidal thickness: Low‑to‑moderate certainty that mid‑luteal CT is thinner than in follicular/ovulatory phases. Supported by multiple small prospective EDI‑OCT studies with internal consistency and some control of MAP/IOP/SE.
- Menstrual cycle, CVI: Low certainty. One study shows significant phase‑dependent CVI changes; methods vary and replication is limited.
- Menstrual cycle, retinal perfusion (SCP/DCP) and FAZ: Very low certainty. Multiple OCTA studies with careful timing report minimal or inconsistent changes; likely small effects within device variability.
- Menstrual cycle, macular thickness/volume: Very low certainty for any effect; largely null across contemporary data.
- Postmenopause, macular thickness: Low‑to‑moderate certainty for thinner inner and select outer ring sectors on SD‑OCT after accounting for age; cross‑sectional designs limit causal inference.
- Postmenopause, choroidal thickness: Low certainty due to platform discordance (SD‑OCT thinner vs one SS‑OCTA thicker) and heterogeneous adjustment for age and axial length.
- Postmenopause, peripapillary perfusion (VD): Moderate certainty for lower peripapillary VD, with correlations to IOP; consistent direction across OCTA studies.
- Postmenopause, RNFL/GCC: Low certainty; mixed findings with sectoral effects and potential confounding by age, BMI, and IOP.
- Postmenopause, FAZ and choriocapillaris: Low‑to‑moderate certainty for larger FAZ and lower CC VD on SS‑OCTA in one well‑described study.

## Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria Applied in This Scoping Synthesis

- Included: Healthy adult women with natural menstrual cycles or postmenopausal women not on HRT; studies that separated analyses from eyes without ocular pathology and without systemic disease known to affect the retina/choroid.
- Excluded from primary synthesis: Pregnancy/postpartum studies; cohorts using exogenous hormonal contraception or HRT; participants with diabetes, hypertension, migraine, PCOS, or other systemic/ocular comorbidities when not analyzed separately. Such studies are cited only to contextualize mechanisms.
- Imaging modalities considered: SD‑/EDI‑OCT, SS‑OCT/OCTA, scanning laser ophthalmoscopy (HRT II), and color Doppler ultrasound, provided outcomes pertained to macula, choroid, optic disc/ONH, RNFL/GCC, or retinal perfusion.

## Limitations of the Evidence Base and of This Review

- Small sample sizes and limited repeated measures per participant constrain precision and the ability to detect subtle cyclical changes.
- Menstrual phase misclassification is likely where serum estradiol/progesterone/LH were not measured; calendar‑day definitions assume regular ovulatory cycles.
- Cross‑sectional postmenopausal vs reproductive‑age comparisons are confounded by age, axial length, IOP, and cardiovascular risk; not all studies adjusted adequately.
- Device and software heterogeneity (SD vs SS, segmentation boundaries, OCTA algorithms) impede quantitative synthesis and may underlie discordant choroidal findings.
- Diurnal timing, hydration, caffeine/nicotine abstinence, and recent physical activity were inconsistently controlled; ocular perfusion pressure was rarely computed.
- As a scoping review, we mapped and critically appraised patterns across modalities rather than performing a meta‑analysis; where only abstracts were available, effect magnitudes were sometimes not extractable.
- The synthesis emphasizes healthy, non‑pregnant women not using exogenous hormones; findings do not generalize to women with systemic diseases or on hormonal therapies, though those contexts may illuminate mechanisms.

## Practical Recommendations for Investigators and Clinicians

- For research protocols in premenopausal women:
  - Fix imaging sessions to a consistent time of day and the same menstrual phase across visits; if feasible, biochemically verify ovulation (LH surge) and luteal progesterone.
  - Record and adjust for axial length, refractive error, IOP, blood pressure/OPP, signal strength, and scan size; standardize caffeine/nicotine and hydration.
  - Use device follow‑up modes to reduce relocation error; report intergrader reliability for manual measures and quality metrics for OCTA.
- For clinical interpretation:
  - Anticipate small luteal‑phase choroidal thinning that may be within test–retest limits; avoid over‑interpreting single‑visit CT changes in premenopausal women without considering phase and diurnal timing.
  - In postmenopausal women, recognize that reduced peripapillary VD and larger FAZ may reflect combined effects of aging and hormone milieu; interpret alongside IOP, axial length, and vascular risk factors.

## Priorities for Future Research (Expanded)

- Longitudinal, hormone‑verified studies that sample ≥5 cycle time points per participant, enabling mixed‑effects modeling of within‑subject variance across CT, CVI, OCTA VD, FAZ, RNFL/GCC, and ONH perfusion.
- Harmonized multicenter protocols comparing SD‑EDI and SS platforms with standardized segmentation, projection‑artifact correction, and vendor‑agnostic analysis pipelines.
- Simultaneous assessment of ocular perfusion pressure and autonomic markers (e.g., heart rate variability) to probe vascular regulatory mechanisms across phases.
- Deeper choroidal phenotyping beyond subfoveal CT, including regional choroidal volume maps and stromal versus luminal compartment analysis with validated CVI pipelines.
- Inclusion of diverse populations varying in age, ethnicity, axial length, and cardiovascular profiles to test for effect modification.
- Mechanistic bridging studies leveraging interventional paradigms (e.g., posture, CO2 challenge) and animal models to link sex steroid signaling to choroidal and retinal microvascular dynamics.

## Final Conclusions

- The choroid shows the clearest and most reproducible association with endogenous menstrual cycle physiology: multiple EDI‑OCT studies indicate mid‑luteal choroidal thinning across subfoveal and parafoveal loci, accompanied by higher systemic blood pressure and, in at least one study, CVI shifts. These observations support hormonally modulated changes in choroidal vascular tone and volume, likely modest in magnitude.
- In contrast, modern OCTA studies have not demonstrated consistent menstrual cycle effects on macular capillary plexus perfusion (SCP/DCP) or FAZ in healthy women, and macular thickness/volume appears largely stable across phases.
- Postmenopausal status, absent HRT, is cross‑sectionally associated with reduced peripapillary vessel density and, in some studies, thinner macula and choroid. FAZ enlargement and lower choriocapillaris density on SS‑OCTA further suggest microvascular remodeling. However, device/segmentation differences and confounding by age, axial length, and IOP limit causal attribution to hormonal depletion alone.
- Overall certainty is low‑to‑moderate: strongest for luteal‑phase choroidal thinning and postmenopausal peripapillary perfusion reduction; weakest for menstrual cycle effects on retinal perfusion and for postmenopausal choroidal thickness due to platform discordance.
- For both research and clinical practice, careful attention to menstrual phase, diurnal timing, and key covariates (axial length, IOP, BP/OPP, signal strength) will improve measurement reliability and interpretation. Rigorous, hormone‑verified, and standardized imaging studies are required to definitively establish the magnitude and clinical relevance of endogenous hormonal influences on posterior pole structures.
