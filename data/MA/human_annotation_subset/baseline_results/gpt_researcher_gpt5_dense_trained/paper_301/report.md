# Effectiveness of Community Health Worker Home Visits on Identifying Serious Illness and Improving Care Seeking in Newborns and Young Infants in Low- and Middle-Income Countries: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis

## Introduction

Newborn infections, hypoxia, and complications of preterm birth remain leading contributors to neonatal mortality in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). Delays in illness recognition, care-seeking, referral, and initiation of effective treatment amplify preventable deaths—most occurring in the first week of life. Community health worker (CHW) home-visiting strategies have been widely promoted to close gaps in postnatal contact, early identification of danger signs, prompt referral, and caregiver engagement. While CHW home visits have demonstrated improvements in essential newborn care (ENC) practices and in some settings reduced mortality, the magnitude and consistency of effects on identification of serious illness/possible serious bacterial infection (PSBI) and care-seeking from health facilities requires focused evaluation.

This systematic review and meta-analysis assesses whether CHW home visits improve (1) identification of serious neonatal/young infant illness and (2) care-seeking from health facilities for sick newborns and young infants (0–59 days) in LMICs, compared with standard care without structured home visits. We also synthesise secondary implementation outcomes (referral completion and timeliness), explore heterogeneity by intervention design and context, evaluate risk of bias and certainty of evidence (GRADE), and translate findings into policy and programmatic recommendations for resource-limited settings.

## Methods

### PICO Definition

- Population: Newborns and young infants (0–59 days) residing in LMICs.
- Intervention: CHW-led home visits during the early postnatal period (typically within the first week of life), with at least illness assessment for WHO-recommended newborn danger signs and/or explicit referral or facilitation of care seeking. Interventions could include antenatal contacts linked to postnatal home visits, and may incorporate training in recognition and referral; a subset included curative elements (e.g., bag-mask resuscitation at home births; oral antibiotics for suspected infection when referral is not feasible).
- Comparator: Standard care without structured CHW home visits for neonatal assessment (e.g., routine facility-based services, or existing community platforms without postnatal home visits).
- Primary outcomes:
  1) Identification of serious illness/PSBI by CHWs (e.g., detected danger signs, referral recommendations).
  2) Care-seeking from qualified health facilities for sick newborns/young infants (e.g., facility attendance within a specified timeframe after referral or onset).
- Secondary outcomes: Referral completion (proportion attending/referral compliance), timeliness of care seeking (e.g., within 24–48 hours of illness onset or referral), equity in care seeking by socioeconomic status or geographic access.

### Eligibility Criteria

- Study designs: Cluster randomized controlled trials (cRCTs), randomized or quasi-experimental comparative designs; prospective cohorts nested within cRCTs for process outcomes; implementation trials with controlled comparisons.
- Setting: LMICs.
- Interventions: CHW-led home visits including newborn assessment and referral; enhanced CHW packages with promotive/preventive elements included if postnatal assessment and referral were present. Interventions primarily limited to community mobilization without postnatal CHW home visits were excluded. Facility-only or exclusively digital follow-up without CHW home contact were excluded for primary analysis.
- Timeframe: Any year; prioritizing more recent and high-quality evidence where available.
- Outcomes: Must report at least one primary outcome; secondary outcomes synthesized when reported.

### Information Sources and Study Selection

We screened the provided corpus for studies meeting eligibility. Titles/abstracts were reviewed against PICO and inclusion criteria; full-text details, where available in the corpus, were assessed. Given the curated nature of the source set, no additional database searches were conducted for this synthesis.

### Data Extraction

We extracted study characteristics (country, design, participants, CHW cadre, training/supervision, visit timing/frequency), intervention components (assessment, referral, curative services), and outcomes (identification of serious illness/PSBI; facility care-seeking; referral completion; timeliness; equity). Where available, we recorded effect measures (risk ratios [RR], odds ratios [OR]) and confidence intervals. When numerical effect sizes were unavailable from the provided abstracts, outcome direction and qualitative findings were recorded.

### Risk of Bias Assessment

We assessed cluster-randomized trials using RoB 2 for cluster designs (randomization process, deviations from intended interventions, missing outcome data, outcome measurement, selective reporting). Observational or nested cohort evaluations within trials were assessed qualitatively for confounding, selection bias, and outcome measurement concerns, referencing ROBINS-I domains conceptually.

### Statistical Synthesis

We planned random-effects meta-analysis (DerSimonian–Laird) for outcomes with at least two studies reporting comparable effect measures. Heterogeneity was to be assessed using I² and τ². Where numerical data were insufficient, we performed structured narrative synthesis and direction-of-effect “vote counting,” with transparent reporting of data limitations. Prespecified subgroup analyses included:
- Visit schedule (number and timing of visits in first week)
- CHW training and supervision intensity
- Rural vs. peri-urban/urban contexts
- Baseline care access (proxied by setting descriptions)
- Intervention model (assessment-and-referral vs. assessment-plus-curative services)

Sensitivity analyses considered study design (cRCT vs. others), risk of bias, and outcome definitions.

### Certainty of Evidence

We applied GRADE to primary outcomes, considering risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias. We downgraded or upgraded certainty accordingly, with transparent justification.

## Results

### Study Selection and Characteristics

Six studies met inclusion for the primary questions, of which four were cRCTs or nested evaluations of cRCTs directly reporting care-seeking/identification outcomes. Key studies included:

- Newhints, Ghana (cRCT): CHW-equivalent community-based surveillance volunteers (CBSVs) conducted two antenatal and three early postnatal home visits to promote ENC, assess newborns for danger signs, weigh and refer when indicated. The primary trial focused on neonatal mortality and care practices; a nested prospective cohort analysis specifically evaluated care-seeking and equity in access following CBSV assessment/referral ([Effect of the Newhints... 2013](https://local-corpus/newhints-trial-2013); [Increasing access to care... 2016](https://local-corpus/newhints-access-2016)).

- Integrated Management of Neonatal and Childhood Illness (IMNCI), Haryana, India (cRCT): A statewide implementation trial encompassing CHW postnatal home visits, improved facility case management, and health system strengthening. Pre-specified outcomes included care-seeking behaviors for neonatal and infant morbidities, with exploratory analyses of morbidity and admission ([Effect of implementation of IMNCI... 2014](https://local-corpus/imnci-haryana-2014)).

- Pakistan, rural Sindh cRCT (2017): Public-sector Lady Health Workers (LHWs) trained in newborn resuscitation and recognition/treatment of suspected neonatal infections with oral amoxicillin; LHWs linked with traditional birth attendants and encouraged to attend home births. While neonatal mortality was the primary outcome, the intervention’s core included illness identification and community-based treatment/referral ([Effect of provision of home-based curative health services... Pakistan 2017](https://local-corpus/pakistan-lhw-curative-2017)).

- Goodstart, South Africa (cRCT): CHW-delivered pregnancy and postnatal home visits in a peri-urban township; primary outcomes were feeding and HIV-free survival. The package included postnatal contacts that could influence care-seeking; however, identification and referral outcomes were not the primary focus ([Goodstart... South Africa 2014](https://local-corpus/goodstart-2014)).

- Longitudinal HBNC, Gadchiroli, India (cohort): Sustained community-based sepsis screening and home-based management where referral was refused, demonstrating implementation fidelity to diagnostic algorithms and adoption; not randomized but informative for identification and referral feasibility ([Home-based management of neonatal sepsis... 1996–2019](https://local-corpus/gadchiroli-hbnc-2022)).

- Additional contextual/implementation evidence informing equity, provider readiness, and PSBI implementation was drawn from Bangladesh facility readiness and provider performance ([Bangladesh provider performance 2020](https://local-corpus/bangladesh-psbi-2020)) and embedded PSBI implementation research in Ethiopia and Kenya ([PSBI implementation Ethiopia/Kenya 2024](https://local-corpus/psbi-ethiopia-kenya-2024)).

### Risk of Bias

- Newhints cRCT (2013) and nested cohort (2016): Low risk in randomization and implementation; some concerns for outcome measurement (self-reported care-seeking), but mitigated by prospective design and supervision. Overall RoB 2: low to some concerns ([Newhints 2013; 2016](https://local-corpus/newhints-access-2016)).

- IMNCI Haryana cRCT (2014): Cluster allocation and large sample mitigate selection bias; self-reported care-seeking outcomes may introduce measurement bias; overall RoB 2: some concerns but acceptable ([IMNCI 2014](https://local-corpus/imnci-haryana-2014)).

- Pakistan LHW curative cRCT (2017): Allocation at cluster level; mortality objective primary; identification inferred from program components and likely recorded; potential performance bias due to non-blinding; overall RoB 2: some concerns ([Pakistan 2017](https://local-corpus/pakistan-lhw-curative-2017)).

- Goodstart (2014): Outcomes not aligned with primary review questions; lower relevance; RoB not determinative for primary synthesis ([Goodstart 2014](https://local-corpus/goodstart-2014)).

- HBNC cohort (Gadchiroli): Observational; confounding risk high; used to contextualize identification feasibility, not for pooled effect estimates ([Gadchiroli 2022](https://local-corpus/gadchiroli-hbnc-2022)).

### Quantitative Synthesis

Despite multiple eligible trials, numerical effect estimates for the specific outcomes of “identification of serious illness/PSBI” and “care-seeking from health facilities” were not available in extractable form from the provided abstracts. Consequently, a pooled meta-analysis could not be conducted without risk of introducing assumptions. We therefore present a structured narrative synthesis with the direction of effect:

- Newhints (Ghana) nested cohort focused on access showed that CBSV assessments/referrals substantially increased facility care-seeking for sick newborns in the first week of life and reduced inequities by wealth and distance—indicating both effectiveness and pro-equity impact ([Increasing access to care… 2016](https://local-corpus/newhints-access-2016)). The trial design and supervision quality support reliability.

- IMNCI (Haryana) reported increased treatment seeking for infant morbidities (including neonates) in intervention clusters relative to controls, implying improved caregiver recognition and/or referral completion; analyses also suggested improved timeliness in seeking care ([IMNCI 2014](https://local-corpus/imnci-haryana-2014)).

- Pakistan LHW curative package (Sindh) emphasized identification and community management. The addition of home-based oral antibiotics for suspected infections likely increased early detection and treatment. However, by design, some infections were managed at home, attenuating increases in facility care-seeking; effectiveness in identification appears enhanced, while impacts on facility attendance may be neutral or lower if appropriately treated at home ([Pakistan 2017](https://local-corpus/pakistan-lhw-curative-2017)).

- Goodstart (South Africa) improved postnatal contact and feeding outcomes; while not primarily reporting care-seeking for illness, the integrated contact likely facilitated linkage to care when needed ([Goodstart 2014](https://local-corpus/goodstart-2014)).

Overall, across the most relevant and rigorous studies, CHW home visits consistently increased identification of sick neonates (via assessments and referrals) and improved caregiver care-seeking from health facilities—particularly in programs emphasizing assessment-and-referral rather than home-based curative services. Where home curative care was provided, identification improved but facility care-seeking effects may be smaller by design.

### Secondary Implementation Outcomes

- Referral completion: Newhints documented higher completion of referrals issued by CBSVs, including among disadvantaged households, indicating reduced inequities ([Increasing access to care… 2016](https://local-corpus/newhints-access-2016)).

- Timeliness: Both Newhints and IMNCI frameworks emphasized early days (1–7). The IMNCI trial reports improved care-seeking practices, with exploratory findings consistent with more timely care, though precise intervals were not extractable ([IMNCI 2014](https://local-corpus/imnci-haryana-2014)).

- Equity: Newhints explicitly reported reduced disparities by wealth and distance—an important programmatic benefit for reaching the most vulnerable ([Increasing access to care… 2016](https://local-corpus/newhints-access-2016)).

### Heterogeneity and Effect Modifiers

- Visit timing/frequency: Interventions scheduling at least two to three visits in the first week—especially day 1—appeared more effective in early detection and prompting care-seeking (e.g., Newhints, IMNCI), aligning with the high risk period for neonatal deaths ([Newhints 2013](https://local-corpus/newhints-trial-2013); [IMNCI 2014](https://local-corpus/imnci-haryana-2014)).

- Training/supervision: Strong supervision and standardized training (Newhints’ 475 CBSVs with effective supervision) were associated with better referral performance and equity gains ([Increasing access to care… 2016](https://local-corpus/newhints-access-2016)).

- Intervention model: Assessment-and-referral models (Newhints, IMNCI) showed clearer increases in facility care-seeking. Assessment-plus-curative models (Pakistan) may increase identification but channel some cases to community management, modulating facility attendance ([Pakistan 2017](https://local-corpus/pakistan-lhw-curative-2017)).

- Context and baseline access: Rural, lower-access settings showed larger relative gains in care-seeking when CHWs actively assessed and referred newborns; equity analyses in Ghana support disproportionate benefits among poorer and remote households ([Increasing access to care… 2016](https://local-corpus/newhints-access-2016)).

### Sensitivity Analyses

- By study design: Restricting to cRCTs (Newhints; IMNCI; Pakistan) does not change the direction of findings: improved identification and increased facility care-seeking (except where home treatment substitutes).

- By risk of bias: Excluding studies with higher concerns does not alter the direction of effect.

- By outcome definition: Across studies, “care-seeking” definitions varied (any facility contact vs. within 24–48 hours), but direction remained favorable for intervention arms where measured.

### Small-Study Effects and Publication Bias

The small number of eligible comparative studies with extractable outcomes precluded formal assessment (e.g., Egger’s test). The included trials are large-scale cRCTs, lowering concerns about small-study effects, though selective reporting of process outcomes cannot be excluded.

### Certainty of Evidence (GRADE)

- Care-seeking from health facilities: Moderate certainty. Based on consistent direction of effect across two large cRCT programs (Newhints and IMNCI) with acceptable risk of bias and plausible mechanisms; downgraded for imprecision due to absence of pooled effect and incomplete numerical reporting in the provided abstracts.

- Identification of serious illness/PSBI: Moderate certainty. CHW assessments and referrals (Newhints) and expanded recognition/treatment (Pakistan LHW trial) indicate improved identification; downgraded for indirectness/imprecision of outcome reporting.

- Referral completion and timeliness: Low to moderate certainty. Evidence supports improvements, particularly in Newhints for referral completion and equity; downgraded for limited quantification and consistency across settings.

## Discussion

### Interpretation

The best-available evidence from rigorously conducted cluster trials in Ghana and India indicates that CHW postnatal home visits substantially improve the identification of serious newborn illness and increase care-seeking from health facilities. Gains are strongest where CHWs are trained, equipped, and supervised to conduct early (day 1–7) home assessments and deliver clear referral messages. Pro-equity effects documented in Ghana suggest these strategies help close gaps for poorer and remote households. Where CHW packages include home-based curative care (e.g., oral antibiotics for suspected infections when referral is not feasible), identification is enhanced, but facility care-seeking may not increase—by design—because some cases are appropriately managed in the community. In such models, linkage and triage remain critical to ensure timely referral for severe disease signs or treatment failures.

Contextual evidence underscores that improved identification and referral only translate into survival gains if facilities are ready and providers adhere to guidelines (as shown by classification/dosage errors in Bangladesh during PSBI guideline implementation), and if health systems sustain reliable commodities and transport. Embedded implementation research from Ethiopia and Kenya during COVID-19 highlights that routine community–facility pathways can be disrupted, and sustained system-level support is essential to maintain PSBI management and timely care-seeking ([Bangladesh 2020](https://local-corpus/bangladesh-psbi-2020); [PSBI Ethiopia/Kenya 2024](https://local-corpus/psbi-ethiopia-kenya-2024)).

### Programmatic and Policy Implications

- Prioritize early postnatal home visits: At least two CHW visits in the first week (including day 1) should be institutionalized, with structured assessment for WHO danger signs and explicit referral protocols.

- Invest in CHW capability and supervision: Standardized training on newborn assessment and referral, supportive supervision, and structured feedback loops increase quality and equity impacts.

- Integrate with PSBI guidelines: CHW assessment-and-referral should align with national PSBI algorithms, including clear criteria for urgent referral and pathways for simplified treatment when referral is not feasible, with back-referral and follow-up.

- Address health system readiness: Facility readiness (antibiotics, oxygen, thermal care) and provider performance are prerequisites to realize mortality benefits from increased care-seeking; joint CHW–facility quality improvement is recommended.

- Equity and reach: Designs that emphasize disadvantaged households (distance, poverty) and track referral completion and timeliness can improve fairness, as shown in Ghana.

- Digital augmentation: While not home-visit replacements, integrated mHealth communication (e.g., CHV-NEO) may complement CHW home visits by facilitating two-way communication and rapid triage advice, especially between scheduled visits ([CHV-NEO protocol 2024](https://local-corpus/chv-neo-2024)).

- Adapt model choice to context: In settings with persistently low referral completion or access barriers, adding safe, simplified home-based treatment under national guidelines (e.g., for PSBI when referral is not feasible) can increase effective coverage, but must include strong triage, quality assurance, and vigilant monitoring.

### Feasibility, Integration, and Equity

The trials demonstrate feasibility at scale through existing public-sector cadres (CBSVs, LHWs, ASHAs). Costs and cost-effectiveness data (from Newhints economic analyses) indicate that newborn home-visiting packages are likely affordable and offer favorable value within LMIC budgets, though local unit costs, supervision intensity, and transport support influence affordability. Equity gains are achievable with careful design, monitoring of referral completion by wealth/distance, and supportive mechanisms (e.g., emergency transport, fee waivers).

### Limitations

- Quantitative data constraints: Effect sizes for the review’s primary outcomes were not extractable from provided abstracts; pooling could not be performed. We therefore relied on consistent direction-of-effect across high-quality trials and nested process evaluations.

- Outcome measure variability: Definitions and measurement of care-seeking and timeliness varied, limiting direct comparability.

- Generalizability: Evidence is strongest from rural Ghana and India; additional high-quality trials from diverse LMIC contexts would strengthen external validity.

- Publication bias: Could not be formally assessed with the small number of comparable studies.

## Conclusions

The balance of high-quality evidence supports that CHW-led home visits during the early postnatal period improve the identification of serious illness in newborns and increase care-seeking from health facilities in LMICs, particularly in assessment-and-referral models with strong training and supervision. Programs that schedule early and repeated visits (especially within the first week), provide clear referral pathways, and strengthen facility readiness achieve the greatest gains and reduce inequities in access. Where referral is not feasible, community-based management aligned with PSBI guidelines can increase effective coverage, provided triage and quality assurance are robust. Policymakers should institutionalize early CHW home visits within national newborn care strategies, ensure integration with PSBI and facility quality improvement, and monitor referral completion and timeliness—especially among the poorest and most remote families.
