## Methods (Expanded)

Eligibility criteria
- Population: Adults (≥18 years) with overweight or obesity; primary focus on BMI ≥27.5 kg/m² to enhance applicability across diverse populations (including groups where cardiometabolic risk rises at lower BMI thresholds).
- Intervention: Behavioral weight management programs delivered via synchronous, remote modalities (e.g., videoconferencing, telephone, live chat). Studies where synchronous contact comprised a substantial component (group sessions or 1:1 counseling/check-ins) were eligible. Programs could include adjunct asynchronous elements (apps, texts) if real-time human contact was central.
- Comparator: In-person or alternative remote formats; single-arm feasibility studies and qualitative/mixed-methods evaluations without comparators were included for engagement insights.
- Outcomes: Engagement-related processes (attendance, session completion, retention, adherence to self-monitoring, interaction frequency), barriers/facilitators to participation, and qualitative experiences. Weight outcomes were extracted when linked to engagement.
- Study designs: Randomized controlled trials (pilot or full), quasi-experimental and pragmatic cohorts, mixed-methods program evaluations, and qualitative studies.
- Timeframe and setting: Emphasis on contemporary telehealth-capable periods (approximately 2014 onward) across healthcare, community, workplace, and Veterans Health Administration settings.
- Language: English.

Information sources and search approach
- Databases: MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, PsycINFO, CINAHL, Web of Science, and trial registries were targeted in the source systematic review. Reference lists of included studies and relevant reviews were screened.
- Search concepts combined: (weight management OR obesity OR overweight) AND (telehealth OR telemedicine OR videoconference OR telephone OR “live chat” OR synchronous) AND (engagement OR attendance OR adherence OR retention OR acceptability OR barriers OR facilitators) AND (adult).
- Grey literature: Program evaluations and implementation reports (e.g., VHA TeleMOVE) were considered.

Study selection and screening
- Two reviewers independently screened titles/abstracts, then full texts, resolving disagreements by discussion. Priority was given to studies with synchronous core components and adult samples meeting the BMI threshold. Studies focused exclusively on asynchronous apps without real-time contact were excluded.

Data extraction and coding
- Extracted items: Study design, setting, population characteristics, modality and dose of synchronous contact, definitions and measures of engagement, quantitative engagement-outcome associations, and qualitative themes.
- COM-B/TDF mapping: A codebook aligned to COM-B and TDF domains was applied deductively to engagement determinants; inductive subthemes were then synthesized within domains. Disagreements were resolved through iterative consensus.

Quality appraisal and certainty
- Randomized trials: Risk of bias assessed with RoB 2 (randomization, deviations from intended interventions, missing data, outcome measurement, reporting).
- Nonrandomized studies: ROBINS-I domains considered (confounding, selection, classification, deviations, missing data, measurement, reporting).
- Qualitative studies: CASP appraisal (aims, methodology, design, recruitment, data collection, reflexivity, ethics, analysis, findings, value).
- Mixed methods: MMAT domains applied.
- Certainty judgments: For qualitative syntheses, CERQual principles guided confidence in each thematic finding (methodological limitations, coherence, adequacy, relevance). For quantitative engagement-outcome associations, an adapted GRADE lens informed interpretive strength (study design, risk of bias, consistency, directness, precision).

## Study Characteristics (Narrative Summary)

- Modalities and format:
  - Group videoconference behavioral weight-loss sessions delivered via clinical or workplace platforms.
  - Group telephone conference calls as a lower-bandwidth, accessible synchronous option.
  - One-to-one telephone coaching or MI sessions embedded within larger programs.
  - Real-time chat features with health professionals adjunctive to scheduled calls or sessions.
- Settings and populations:
  - Veterans Health Administration programs (TeleMOVE; videoconference MOVE! clinics) across multiple sites with documented implementation variability.
  - Rural programs offering equipment and onboarding support; older adults included in several feasibility studies.
  - Workplace-based coach-assisted eHealth with group or individual synchronous support.
  - Studies enrolling diverse participants, with focused analyses on equity (e.g., Black women) indicating comparable outcomes between remote and in-person formats when designed and implemented well.
- Engagement measures:
  - Attendance rates for group sessions (video/phone), session completion, retention at program milestones.
  - Self-monitoring adherence (e.g., weigh-ins) and volume of real-time contact (chat messages, call counts).
  - Qualitative acceptability and perceived barriers/facilitators gathered via interviews/focus groups.

## Additional Results: Patterns in Engagement

- Attendance and retention:
  - Videoconference and telephone group sessions achieved attendance and retention comparable to in-person in several program evaluations, with some settings noting improved access reducing missed visits.
  - Implementation supports (standardized workflows, facilitator training, helpdesk availability) were associated with better session fidelity and participant participation.
- Modality preferences and fit:
  - Many participants valued telephone for privacy and lower technological burden; others preferred video for richer social presence. Offering choice improved perceived fit.
  - Group-based formats fostered social norms and accountability; individuals uncomfortable with cameras benefited from telephone alternatives or camera-optional norms.
- Engagement behaviors and outcomes:
  - Higher frequency of self-weighing and greater volume of chat/real-time contacts with health professionals correlated with greater weight loss, highlighting engagement behaviors as proximal indicators and potential causal mechanisms.
- Contextual constraints:
  - Competing priorities (work, caregiving) and limited private space hindered engagement, particularly for video sessions.
  - Broadband challenges in rural areas were mitigated by device provisioning and connectivity support, facilitating participation among older adults and those with limited digital skills.

## Limitations of the Evidence and This Review

- Evidence base heterogeneity:
  - Variability in intervention content, dose of synchronous contact, and engagement definitions limited quantitative pooling and direct comparisons.
  - Several studies were feasibility pilots or observational cohorts vulnerable to selection bias and confounding; randomized evidence exists but is often small-scale or pilot in nature.
- Measurement variability:
  - Engagement metrics (e.g., attendance thresholds, self-monitoring definitions) lacked standardization, complicating synthesis.
  - Qualitative studies varied in reflexivity reporting and depth, though findings were generally coherent across contexts.
- Implementation variability:
  - Site-level differences (staffing, workflows, leadership support) were influential yet inconsistently measured, limiting attribution of engagement effects.
- Generalizability:
  - Strong representation of Veterans and rural/older adults; fewer studies in low-income urban populations or non-English-speaking groups.
  - Limited long-term follow-up on engagement trajectories beyond initial program phases.
- Review constraints:
  - As a targeted synthesis emphasizing higher-quality and synchronous-dominant studies, this review may underrepresent smaller or unpublished programs and grey literature beyond the included sources.

## Research Gaps and Priorities

- Standardized engagement metrics:
  - Consensus on core engagement measures (attendance, retention, self-monitoring frequency, interaction counts) and reporting standards to enable cross-study comparison.
- Mechanistic trials:
  - Factorial or MOST designs to isolate the additive value of synchronous group sessions, 1:1 MI calls, and real-time feedback around self-monitoring.
- Equity-oriented intervention testing:
  - Trials explicitly designed for and co-produced with priority populations (e.g., Black and Indigenous communities, non-English speakers), assessing culturally tailored synchronous formats and digital access supports.
- Privacy- and camera-use strategies:
  - Comparative evaluations of camera-optional norms, telephone-only pathways, and their effects on engagement and outcomes among individuals with weight stigma concerns.
- Implementation science:
  - Pragmatic cluster trials evaluating standardized onboarding, device provisioning, facilitator training, and EHR integration on engagement and equity outcomes at scale.
- Maintenance and relapse prevention:
  - Long-term studies examining post-intervention synchronous touchpoints (brief boosters) to sustain engagement and weight control.

## Practical Implementation Checklist (COM-B/TDF–Aligned)

- Capability
  - Provide simple onboarding: one-click links, test calls, quick-start guides, and live tech support.
  - Scaffold behavior change: calendar invites, SMS/email reminders, pre-session checklists, and habit-formation prompts.
- Opportunity
  - Offer modality options (video/phone/live chat), flexible scheduling (evenings/weekends), and camera-optional policies.
  - Address access: device/data provisioning, low-bandwidth pathways, and private-space guidance for home participation.
  - Integrate with care: EHR-linked weights, primary care messaging, clear pathways for comorbidity management.
- Motivation
  - Pair self-monitoring with timely, tailored feedback; establish regular brief check-ins between sessions.
  - Train facilitators in MI, stigma-aware communication, and group facilitation to enhance autonomy, confidence, and belonging.
  - Personalize goals and content culturally and linguistically; involve peers or family when desired by participants.
- Fidelity and monitoring
  - Standardize session templates and facilitation protocols; track attendance, weigh-ins, and contact volume.
  - Use dashboards and just-in-time outreach for early signs of disengagement; apply rapid-cycle quality improvement.

## Conclusion

Synchronous, remote weight management interventions can achieve robust engagement and outcomes when designed and implemented to address capability, opportunity, and motivation together. Key facilitators include streamlined technology with strong onboarding; flexible modality and scheduling options; high-quality, stigma-aware interpersonal contact in group and/or 1:1 formats; and timely, individualized feedback tied to self-monitoring. Common barriers—digital literacy gaps, bandwidth and privacy constraints, competing role demands, insufficient tailoring, and variable implementation support—are modifiable with deliberate design and organizational investment.

Mapping to COM-B and the TDF highlights practical levers for action: build capability through skills support and behavioral scaffolding; expand physical and social opportunity via resource provision, flexible access, and group connectedness; and strengthen motivation with accountability, MI-consistent counseling, and reinforcement around early wins. When these elements are operationalized—particularly within integrated care pathways and with equity-focused strategies—synchronous remote programs can match or surpass in-person engagement across diverse adult populations, including Veterans, rural and older adults, and Black women. Future work should standardize engagement metrics, test mechanistic components, and evaluate scalable, culturally responsive implementation models to close gaps and sustain long-term participation.
