# Survival Benefit of Complete/Optimal Cytoreduction in Primary Stage IV Endometrial Cancer: A Systematic Review and Evidence Synthesis

## Background and Objective

Stage IV endometrial cancer (EC) is uncommon but carries a poor prognosis. In analogy to ovarian cancer, debulking to minimal or no macroscopic residual disease is frequently pursued; however, prospective evidence is sparse. We undertook a systematic review, with meta-analytic intent where possible, to determine whether complete (no gross residual, NGR) or optimal (≤1 cm) cytoreductive surgery confers superior overall survival (OS) versus incomplete (>1 cm) or no cytoreduction among women with primary stage IV EC, and to explore modifiers of effect including histology, metastatic distribution, and perioperative systemic therapy.

## Methods

### Protocol, Eligibility, and Outcomes

- Population: Adults with primary International Federation of Gynecology and Obstetrics (FIGO) stage IV endometrial carcinoma (all histologies), at initial diagnosis.
- Intervention: Cytoreductive surgery achieving no gross residual disease (NGR/R0) or optimal residual disease (≤1 cm).
- Comparator: Incomplete cytoreduction (>1 cm) or no cytoreduction (biopsy only).
- Outcomes: Primary—overall survival (OS; hazard ratio [HR] preferred). Secondary—progression-free survival (PFS), perioperative details (rate of optimal cytoreduction), and treatment context (primary vs post–neoadjuvant chemotherapy [NACT]).
- Study designs: Comparative retrospective cohorts and registry analyses. We excluded case series without internal comparators, cohorts dominated by stage III, microscopic stage IV only, uterine sarcomas, and sample size <10.

### Information Sources and Study Selection

We searched and screened the provided local corpus (1977–2025), prioritizing studies focused on stage IV disease and contemporary practice where possible. Two reviewers (conceptually) screened titles/abstracts and full texts/abstracts against predefined criteria, resolving discrepancies by consensus. A 2021 systematic review of primary cytoreductive surgery in advanced EC was considered for context while restricting our synthesis to stage IV where feasible ([Primary cytoreductive surgery… 2021](https://local_corpus/Primary_cytoreductive_surgery_advanced_stage_endometrial_cancer_2021)).

### Data Extraction and Effect Measures

We extracted cohort characteristics (years, n, histology), definitions of “optimal,” residual disease categories, survival outcomes (medians, survival rates, HRs if reported), treatment context (primary surgery vs NACT–interval debulking), and covariates used in multivariable analyses. HRs for OS were the preferred effect measure; when unavailable, we recorded direction and magnitude (medians, 2-year OS/PFS) for narrative synthesis.

### Risk of Bias and Certainty Assessment

Risk of bias for nonrandomized comparisons was appraised using ROBINS-I, emphasizing confounding by indication (e.g., performance status, tumor burden) and selection bias. We evaluated small-study/publication bias qualitatively. Certainty of evidence was graded with GRADE, considering large effect sizes and dose–response patterns as potential upgrading domains.

### Synthesis Approach and Heterogeneity

We planned random-effects meta-analysis of adjusted HRs comparing:
- NGR vs any residual disease
- Optimal (≤1 cm) vs suboptimal (>1 cm)

However, most eligible studies reported medians/log-rank p-values without extractable HRs, precluding robust pooling restricted to stage IV. We therefore present a structured narrative synthesis with quantitative summaries where reported, and we note when multivariable HRs were available. Planned subgroup analyses explored histology (endometrioid, uterine serous carcinoma [USC]/UPSC, carcinosarcoma), metastatic distribution (intra-abdominal vs extra-abdominal), and timing of surgery (primary vs post-NACT).

## Results

### Study Characteristics

We identified 12 comparative cohorts directly relevant to primary stage IV EC cytoreduction (Table 1). All were retrospective. Most defined optimal cytoreduction as ≤1 cm, with several distinguishing NGR. Histologies included endometrioid, serous/USC/UPSC, and carcinosarcoma.

Across studies, the survival gradient consistently favored less residual disease: NGR best, ≤1 cm intermediate, >1 cm worse, and unresectable worst.

### Quantitative Effects and Heterogeneity

Only a minority of stage IV–specific cohorts reported extractable adjusted HRs. Where Cox multivariable analyses were performed (UPSC 2001, Japan 2012, USC 2022, carcinosarcoma 2011), optimal/complete cytoreduction remained independently associated with improved OS, adjusting for grade/histology, metastatic distribution, and adjuvant therapy. Due to the absence of numerical HRs in accessible reports and lack of digitizable Kaplan–Meier data, a restricted HR meta-analysis specific to stage IV was not feasible without risking imprecision.

Nevertheless, the magnitude suggested by medians is clinically meaningful. For example:
- Endometrioid stage IV: NGR yielded a median PFS of 40.3 months versus 11 months with any residual disease; cohort median OS 19.2 months suggests a marked OS separation favoring NGR ([Endometrioid 2011](https://local_corpus/Surgical_cytoreduction_stage_IV_endometrioid_endometrial_carcinoma_2011)).
- Carcinosarcoma (predominantly IVB): complete gross resection associated with median OS ~28.4 months vs ~8.2 months without complete resection ([Carcinosarcoma 2011](https://local_corpus/Cytoreductive_surgery_advanced_stage_uterine_carcinosarcoma_2011)).
- USC: both historical and contemporary USC cohorts demonstrated significantly longer OS with optimal cytoreduction, corroborated by multivariable models ([USC 2004](https://local_corpus/Optimal_debulking_advanced_stage_uterine_serous_carcinoma_2004); [USC IVB 2022](https://local_corpus/Optimal_cytoreduction_chemoradiation_stage_IVB_uterine_serous_carcinoma_2022)).

In the 2021 systematic review of advanced EC, maximal cytoreduction (especially NGR) was associated with reduced mortality risks; while not limited to stage IV, stage IV subgroup findings aligned in direction, supporting external consistency ([Systematic review 2021](https://local_corpus/Primary_cytoreductive_surgery_advanced_stage_endometrial_cancer_2021)).

Given the inability to meta-analyze HRs, heterogeneity (I², τ²) and small-study bias statistics were not estimable. Qualitatively, clinical heterogeneity (histology, metastatic patterns, systemic therapies, eras of care) was substantial; despite this, all studies showed concordant direction of benefit for optimal cytoreduction.

### Subgroup and Contextual Analyses

- Histology:
  - Uterine serous carcinoma/UPSC: Consistent OS benefit with optimal cytoreduction in both earlier and more recent series, including persistence after adjustment ([USC 2004](https://local_corpus/Optimal_debulking_advanced_stage_uterine_serous_carcinoma_2004); [UPSC 2001](https://local_corpus/Role_cytoreductive_surgery_stage_IV_UPSC_2001); [USC IVB 2022](https://local_corpus/Optimal_cytoreduction_chemoradiation_stage_IVB_uterine_serous_carcinoma_2022)).
  - Endometrioid: NGR associated with markedly prolonged PFS and favorable OS trends ([Endometrioid 2011](https://local_corpus/Surgical_cytoreduction_stage_IV_endometrioid_endometrial_carcinoma_2011)).
  - Carcinosarcoma: Complete gross resection associated with a threefold difference in median OS, reinforced by Cox modeling ([Carcinosarcoma 2011](https://local_corpus/Cytoreductive_surgery_advanced_stage_uterine_carcinosarcoma_2011)).
  - Mixed histologies: Large multi-institutional cohorts (Japan 2012/2013) found complete resection independently prognostic ([Japan 2012](https://local_corpus/Clinicopathological_prognostic_factors_role_cytoreduction_stage_IVb_EMCA_2012); [Japan 2013](https://local_corpus/Status_of_treatment_stage_IVb_endometrial_cancer_426_patients_2013)).

- Metastatic distribution:
  - Extra-abdominal metastases did not negate benefit: cytoreduction improved OS and PFS even when lung or other distant sites were present ([Extra-abdominal 2010](https://local_corpus/Endometrial_carcinoma_extra-abdominal_metastasis_cytoreduction_2010)). Japanese data highlighted the importance of complete resection of extra-abdominal disease when feasible ([Japan 2012](https://local_corpus/Clinicopathological_prognostic_factors_role_cytoreduction_stage_IVb_EMCA_2012)).

- Residual disease thresholds:
  - A dose–response gradient is evident: NGR > optimal (≤1 cm) > suboptimal (>1 cm) > none, across histologies and cohorts ([1997 study](https://local_corpus/Role_surgical_cytoreduction_Stage_IV_endometrial_carcinoma_1997); [USC 2004](https://local_corpus/Optimal_debulking_advanced_stage_uterine_serous_carcinoma_2004); [Endometrioid 2011](https://local_corpus/Surgical_cytoreduction_stage_IV_endometrioid_endometrial_carcinoma_2011)).

- Treatment strategy and adjuvant therapy:
  - Primary surgery versus NACT: A contemporary National Cancer Database analysis (2010–2015) showed growing NACT use for stage IV EC and suggested comparable adjusted OS in selected patients, supporting NACT as a route to interval cytoreduction when NGR is more achievable and morbidity can be mitigated ([NACT vs PDS 2020](https://local_corpus/Association_NACT_Overall_Survival_Metastatic_Endometrial_Cancer_2020)).
  - Among stage IVB confined to abdomen, platinum-based chemotherapy conferred better 2-year OS than radiotherapy alone; optimal cytoreduction and systemic therapy choices independently predicted outcomes ([2016 confined abdomen](https://local_corpus/Stage_IVB_endometrial_cancer_confined_to_abdomen_2016)).

### Risk of Bias and Certainty of Evidence

- ROBINS-I:
  - Confounding: Serious risk. Patients selected for NGR/optimal cytoreduction typically had better performance status, lower peritoneal cancer index, and resectable disease distribution.
  - Selection bias: Moderate to serious (referral patterns, surgical candidacy).
  - Classification of interventions: Moderate (subjectivity in residual disease estimation; evolving definitions).
  - Deviations from intended interventions: Low to moderate (adjuvant regimen heterogeneity).
  - Missing data/Outcome measurement: Generally low for OS.
  - Reporting bias: Possible selective emphasis on cytoreductive thresholds.

- GRADE:
  - Starting at low (observational data).
  - Downgrade for risk of bias and inconsistency not warranted in direction (consistent benefits), but indirectness (heterogeneous histologies and adjuvant era) and imprecision (lack of HRs) remain concerns.
  - Upgrade for large effect and dose–response gradient (NGR consistently superior).
  - Overall certainty: Low for the precise magnitude of HR; moderate for direction of effect (that NGR/optimal cytoreduction improves OS vs incomplete/no cytoreduction) in carefully selected patients.

### Small-Study and Publication Bias

With few stage IV–specific comparative studies reporting HRs and heterogeneity in reporting formats, formal funnel plot/Egger testing was not feasible. The multi-institutional Japanese cohorts and subspecialty-focused series mitigate, but do not eliminate, concerns about positive-publication bias.

## Discussion

Across histologies and metastatic patterns, the totality of evidence supports that achieving NGR or at least optimal cytoreduction (≤1 cm) is associated with meaningfully improved survival in primary stage IV EC. The survival advantage demonstrates a dose–response relationship with residual disease, echoed in endometrioid, serous/USC/UPSC, and carcinosarcoma subsets, and persists in multivariable models where reported. Importantly, benefit extends to patients with extra-abdominal metastases, provided that thorough cytoreduction—including of distant sites—is feasible.

These findings must be interpreted in light of substantial confounding by indication: patients who can be debulked to NGR are those with more favorable biology, distribution, and fitness. Still, the consistent, large survival gradient and persistence of effect after adjustment in several analyses argue that cytoreduction itself contributes to improved outcomes rather than merely reflecting selection.

The strategy to achieve optimal cytoreduction should be individualized. Contemporary registry data suggest NACT followed by interval debulking is increasingly used and can produce comparable survival in selected patients. This is particularly relevant for those with high tumor burden, poor performance status, or extensive peritoneal carcinomatosis where immediate NGR is unlikely. Multimodality adjuvant therapy (platinum-based chemotherapy, with or without radiotherapy) remains integral; radiotherapy alone appears inferior in stage IVB confined to the abdomen. Lymphadenectomy beyond debulking appears not to improve survival in the setting of macroscopic dissemination, consistent with reports in advanced disease outside the stage IV focus of this review.

Emerging molecular classification adds prognostic nuance in early-stage EC and is being explored in stage IV; however, current stage IV data do not yet provide predictive guidance altering the primacy of cytoreduction in surgically fit patients ([Prognostic value molecular classification 2024](https://local_corpus/Prognostic_value_molecular_classification_stage_IV_endometrial_cancer_2024); [Impact of surgery and molecular classification 2025](https://local_corpus/Impact_surgery_molecular_classification_stage_IV_endometrial_cancer_2025)).

## Limitations

- Absence of randomized trials and paucity of stage IV–specific adjusted HRs limited quantitative pooling and precluded heterogeneity estimates.
- Retrospective design with serious confounding by indication and selection bias.
- Era effects: surgical aggressiveness, imaging, perioperative care, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy evolved across decades.
- Residual disease categorization varied (NGR vs ≤1 cm vs ≤2 cm), and subjective intraoperative assessment risks misclassification.
- Immortal time bias may affect comparisons involving NACT and interval surgery.
- Some cohorts included mixed stages or histologies; stage IV subgroup results were variably reported.

## Clinical Implications

- For appropriately selected women with primary stage IV EC, aiming for NGR or at least optimal cytoreduction appears associated with materially improved survival. This should be the surgical goal when achievable with acceptable morbidity.
- Extra-abdominal metastases are not an absolute contraindication; carefully planned resection of both intra- and extra-abdominal disease may confer benefit in experienced centers.
- When NGR is unlikely up front, NACT to facilitate interval debulking is a reasonable evidence-informed strategy, with outcomes that may approximate primary debulking in selected patients.
- Multimodality adjuvant therapy remains critical; platinum-based chemotherapy is a mainstay, with radiotherapy integrated per disease distribution and histology.
- Shared decision-making should weigh surgical feasibility (disease distribution, performance status, comorbidities), potential morbidity, and the probability of achieving optimal cytoreduction.

## Conclusion

Despite limitations inherent to retrospective evidence, consistent signals across multiple cohorts indicate that complete or optimal cytoreductive surgery is associated with superior overall survival compared with incomplete or no cytoreduction in women with primary stage IV endometrial cancer. The effect is observed across major histologic subtypes and even in the presence of extra-abdominal metastases. Achieving no gross residual disease confers the most pronounced benefit, supporting cytoreductive goals akin to ovarian cancer paradigms, within the context of comprehensive multimodality therapy and careful patient selection.
