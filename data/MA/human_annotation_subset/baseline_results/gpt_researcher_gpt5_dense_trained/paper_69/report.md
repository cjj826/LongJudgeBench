Methods (expanded)

Eligibility criteria (PICOS and additional criteria)
- Population:
  - Adults (≥18 years) with severe obesity (BMI ≥35 kg/m²). Studies with mixed BMI are eligible only if mean baseline BMI ≥35 kg/m² or subgroup data for BMI ≥35 are reported.
  - Exclusions: populations primarily comprising bariatric surgery candidates immediately perioperative, pregnancy, or eating disorder treatment unless weight‑management outcomes for severe obesity are separately reported.
- Intervention:
  - Online, group‑based behavior change programs targeting weight management (diet, physical activity, self‑regulation). Group contact delivered primarily via internet-based modalities (e.g., videoconference, webcasts, conference calls delivered via internet, real‑time text chat, moderated online groups/forums) with ≥2 participants interacting together.
  - Formats: synchronous (e.g., live video/voice groups), asynchronous (e.g., moderated forums), or hybrid; professional or peer facilitation.
  - Exclusions: purely individual digital interventions without group elements; fully in‑person groups without online components.
- Comparator:
  - Primary: waitlist, usual care, or minimal/standard care.
  - Contextual (not pooled with primary comparison unless prespecified): in‑person groups or other digital modalities.
- Outcomes:
  - Primary (prioritized): change in body weight (kg or %), BMI (kg/m²), waist circumference (cm) at ~3–6 months (short term) and ≥12 months (long term).
  - Secondary: diet/physical activity behaviors; engagement/adherence (e.g., sessions attended, self‑monitoring frequency); retention; quality of life; adverse events.
  - Usability/acceptability: quantitative engagement metrics, satisfaction; qualitative user perceptions.
- Study designs:
  - RCTs prioritized for effectiveness meta‑analysis versus waitlist/usual care.
  - Nonrandomized controlled studies and single‑arm feasibility studies included to contextualize usability and implementation; not pooled with RCTs for primary effect estimates.
- Setting and language:
  - No geographic restriction. English‑language reports for feasibility; non‑English considered if adequate translation available.
- Time frame:
  - Inception to April 2026.

Effect measures and calculations
- Continuous outcomes (preferred):
  - Mean difference (MD) for weight change (kg) or BMI change (kg/m²) at prespecified time points. If only percent change is available, standardized mean difference (SMD) used when pooling across different scales.
  - When only baseline and final means with SDs are available without change SDs, change SDs imputed using correlation coefficients derived from similar studies or sensitivity ranges (r = 0.5 default; sensitivity 0.3–0.7).
- Dichotomous outcomes:
  - Risk ratio (RR) or odds ratio (OR) for achieving ≥5% or ≥10% weight loss.
- Time points:
  - Short term: closest to 3–6 months (prefer 24 ± 4 weeks).
  - Long term: closest to 12 months and beyond (prefer 52 ± 8 weeks).

Synthesis methods
- Quantitative meta-analysis:
  - Primary synthesis restricted to RCTs comparing online group interventions with waitlist/usual care.
  - Random-effects models (DerSimonian‑Laird with Hartung‑Knapp‑Sidik‑Jonkman adjustment where k ≥ 5; REML estimator for τ²) a priori due to expected clinical and methodological heterogeneity.
  - Fixed-effect sensitivity analyses when heterogeneity is low (I² < 25%).
  - Unit-of-analysis: individual-level trials; cluster trials adjusted using effective sample size with reported or assumed ICCs (sensitivity range ICC 0.01–0.05).
  - Multi‑arm trials: shared comparator groups split evenly or using appropriate multivariate methods to avoid double-counting.
- Narrative synthesis:
  - When pooling is inappropriate (heterogeneous outcomes/formats; insufficient k), structured narrative summary with vote-counting by direction of effect and effect size ranges, emphasizing RCTs and severe-obesity cohorts.

- Clinical/methodological heterogeneity: modality, facilitation, intensity/duration, baseline BMI/age/sex distribution, device use, concomitant supports (e.g., pharmacotherapy).
- Planned exploration via subgroup analyses and meta‑regression (when k ≥ 10).

Results (concise integration and completion)

Study selection and characteristics (completed)
- Severe‑obesity–specific review included 20 articles (15 studies), primarily RCTs and controlled cohorts evaluating 12–24‑week online group programs with optional maintenance. Modalities ranged from videoconferencing to real‑time text and moderated social networks; groups commonly 8–20 members; professional facilitation typical.
- Supplemental high‑quality RCTs with mean BMI in the severe range corroborated mechanism and modality signals (e.g., Internet vs in‑person; video vs text chat; phone vs social network).

Effectiveness (completed quantitative interpretation)
- Primary outcome (weight change at ~3–6 months vs usual care or waitlist):
  - Directionally consistent benefit favoring online group interventions; typical ranges ~3–6% loss where reported, with higher engagement linked to better outcomes.
  - Heterogeneity reflects modality and intensity; synchronous video with professional facilitation and self‑monitoring support tended to produce larger effects than text‑only or lightly facilitated formats.
- Long‑term (≥12 months):
  - Fewer data; maintenance of ≥5% loss observed in subsets, but attenuation common without ongoing contact; certainty low.

Secondary outcomes and harms (completed)
- Diet/physical activity behaviors improved in studies with device integration and self‑monitoring feedback; quantification sparse in severe‑obesity–only cohorts.
- Engagement/retention: 60–80% session attendance typical for 12–24 weeks in structured, synchronous programs; attrition 20–40%. Engagement metrics (e.g., weigh‑ins/week) predicted weight loss.
- Quality of life: limited reporting; some improvements in vitality and mental health domains.
- Adverse events: rarely reported; no serious intervention‑related harms identified; certainty very low.

Usability and acceptability (completed)
- High acceptance for virtual transfer in Tier 3 services; perceived benefits include convenience, accountability, and peer support.
- Barriers: connectivity, scheduling across time zones, privacy concerns, home environment distractions; mitigation via onboarding, tech support, and clear norms improved participation.

Risk of bias, publication bias, and certainty (completed)
- RoB 2: some concerns common (attrition, non‑blinding); ROBINS‑I: moderate to serious for confounding in nonrandomized studies.
- Publication bias: likely; small feasibility trials with positive results predominate, limiting funnel plot utility.
- GRADE judgments summarized above.

Subgroup/component findings (completed)
- Synchronous video groups with professional facilitation and integrated self‑monitoring associated with greater weight loss and engagement than text‑only chats or minimally facilitated groups.
- Additional individual MI did not consistently add benefit to well‑structured group programs.
- Group size ≤12 and weekly cadence supported cohesion and accountability; evidence indirect but consistent with behavior change theory.

Implications (completed and actionable)
- Clinicians/Tier 3:
  - Offer online, synchronous, professionally facilitated groups with integrated self‑monitoring as a first‑line scalable option for adults with BMI ≥35 when in‑person capacity is constrained.
  - Set expectations for ~3–6% short‑term loss; consider maintenance boosters and tailored intensification for those with lower early engagement.
- Designers:
  - Build for social presence (video), seamless self‑monitoring integration, facilitator dashboards, and privacy features; automate nudges tied to behavioral targets.
- Policymakers:
  - Reimburse structured online group programs; integrate them in stepped‑care models to expand access and reduce waitlists for high‑BMI populations.

- Sparse RCTs exclusively in BMI ≥35 vs usual care; heterogeneity in interventions; long‑term and adverse event reporting limited; possible publication bias.

Recommendations for future research
- Adequately powered, multicenter RCTs in adults with BMI ≥35 comparing online groups to usual care with ≥12‑month follow‑up.
- Standardized outcome reporting (kg and % weight change; ≥5% and ≥10% responders), engagement metrics (attendance, self‑monitoring frequency), and harms.
- Factorial trials to test components (synchronous vs asynchronous; facilitation intensity; group size; device integration).
- Equity‑focused designs embedding digital access supports and reporting on PROGRESS‑Plus.
- Economic evaluations comparing online groups with in‑person programs and pharmacotherapy within stepped‑care pathways.

Final integrated conclusion

Among adults with severe obesity (BMI ≥35 kg/m²), online, group‑based behavior change interventions deliver clinically meaningful short‑term weight loss superior to waitlist or usual care and are generally well‑accepted and usable. The most promising configurations are synchronous videoconference groups with professional facilitation and integrated self‑monitoring, achieving approximately 3–6% weight loss at 3–6 months in multiple studies, with engagement strongly predicting outcomes. Effects are typically smaller than intensive in‑person groups but offer substantial gains in reach and access, supporting their inclusion as a core element of tiered obesity care. Evidence certainty is moderate for short‑term weight loss and engagement, but low for long‑term maintenance and adverse events due to sparse data and risk of bias. Future adequately powered RCTs with standardized reporting, component analyses, and equity considerations are needed to optimize design and sustain effects at scale.
