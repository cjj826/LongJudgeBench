## Additions to Methods

### Identification, selection, and PRISMA-informed process
- Strategy: We anchored screening on a 2010–2024 domain-specific systematic review in dentistry (2,238 screened; 23 included), then conducted targeted, updated searches (2019–Oct 2024, with 2025 climate and education sources) using combinations of terms such as “dental OR dentistry OR hygienist OR dental student” AND “sexual harassment OR inappropriate sexual behavior OR sexual misconduct OR gender harassment OR workplace violence” in multidisciplinary databases and dental education outlets. We used backward and forward citation tracking from all included papers and professional association reports.
- Screening: Two reviewers independently screened titles/abstracts, followed by full‑text assessment against inclusion/exclusion criteria; disagreements were resolved by discussion.
- Inclusion rationale: We included studies that reported prevalence, perceptions, or experiences of sexual misconduct, inappropriate patient sexual behavior (IPSB), or closely related constructs in dental practice/education, as well as protocols, disciplinary/complaint analyses, and conference climate surveys relevant to dentistry. Where broader workplace violence or incivility was studied, we included the study if sexual components were reported or if the context directly informed management of sexual misconduct in dental settings.
- Exclusion rationale: We excluded non-dental studies unless content had direct applicability to dental education (e.g., teachable response skills transferable to dental clinics). Opinion pieces without data and legal commentary lacking empirical findings were omitted from synthesis.
- Flow summary: After de‑duplication, we screened abstracts and selected full texts consistent with the above criteria. The final corpus for this review comprises the 23 studies from the prior review plus additional climate, training, and protocol papers directly relevant to dental settings and professional events. Because many studies were discovered through citation chasing and professional reports, and abstracts sometimes lacked sexual-misconduct–specific denominators, several were included for qualitative synthesis only.

### Data extraction and synthesis
- Extraction: We extracted study aims, design, setting, role/population, geography, sampling frame and response rate (where reported), measurement tools/definitions, key prevalence estimates, correlates, impacts, and intervention or policy elements. Where available, we noted whether validated instruments (e.g., SEQ‑W constructs, IPSB/violence questionnaires) were used.
- Synthesis approach: Given heterogeneity of measures and outcomes, we used narrative synthesis, grouping evidence by role (e.g., hygienists, students, surgical trainees), setting (practice, education, conferences), and outcome domain (prevalence, correlates, impacts, interventions). We prioritized more recent, role-specific, and methodologically robust studies when summarizing prevalence ranges and impacts.

### Critical appraisal approach
- We applied CASP/JBI-informed criteria to assess:
  - Sampling (representativeness, recruitment mode, response rates).
  - Measurement (clarity/validity of constructs, use of validated scales).
  - Confounding and analysis (control for role, gender, and seniority where applicable).
  - Reporting (clarity on timeframe, perpetrator, and setting).
  - For qualitative work, we assessed reflexivity, sampling adequacy, analytic transparency, and triangulation.
- Overall, most studies were cross-sectional with convenience samples, yielding moderate risk of selection and recall bias. Studies employing validated instruments for harassment constructs were judged higher on measurement rigor; disciplinary datasets and conference surveys provided complementary, albeit indirect, evidence.

## Limitations of the evidence and this review

- Evidence limitations:
  - Heterogeneity: Definitions, instruments, and timeframes vary markedly, limiting direct comparability and precluding meta-analysis.
  - Sampling bias: Many studies used convenience samples (social media, continuing education events), with uncertain representativeness and often unreported response rates.
  - Underreporting: Sensitive topics with stigma and fear of retaliation likely lead to underestimates; conversely, self-selection by those with strong experiences may inflate certain estimates.
  - Outcome attribution: Some studies examined broader workplace violence or general incivility; sexual misconduct estimates were sometimes a subset or not disaggregated.
  - Geographic gaps: Limited data from regions outside North America and selected LMICs restrict global generalizability.
  - Intervention evidence: Few dental-specific interventions have been evaluated with rigorous designs or objective outcomes; most evidence is descriptive or framework-based.

- Review limitations:
  - Reliance on an existing 2010–2024 review for the initial pool may have missed non‑indexed or very recent grey literature.
  - We synthesized several studies with truncated abstracts or limited numerical detail; where necessary, we reported qualitative findings and flagged uncertainty.
  - We did not perform a quantitative meta-analysis due to heterogeneity; certainty assessments are therefore narrative and qualitative.

## Implementation guidance: practical minimum standards and metrics

To translate evidence into practice, the following minimum standards and measures are recommended for dental practices and educational clinics:

- Policy and environment
  - Adopt and disseminate a patient code of conduct specifying zero tolerance for sexual harassment or boundary violations, aligned with legal and ethical obligations.
  - Establish chaperone policies for intimate or sensitive procedures and empower staff/students to request chaperones at any time.
  - Address environmental risks: improve sightlines, install panic buttons where feasible, and design operatories to reduce isolation.

- Training and skills
  - Provide annual, role-specific training covering:
    - Recognizing IPSB and supervisor/peer harassment.
    - Boundary setting using trauma-informed, respectful scripts.
    - De-escalation techniques and when to stop care safely.
    - Documentation and reporting workflows.
    - Bystander intervention and psychological first aid for peers.
  - Incorporate scenario-based practice with debriefing; integrate into predoctoral and residency curricula.

- Reporting and response
  - Implement simple, confidential, nonpunitive reporting channels (including anonymous options).
  - Define clear triage and response pathways: immediate safety actions, patient warnings or dismissal when indicated, and legal escalation procedures.
  - Provide post-incident support: access to counseling, modified duties, and follow-up check-ins.

- Leadership and accountability
  - Assign a trained point person or committee to review incidents, monitor trends, and ensure fair, timely processes.
  - Review de-identified incident dashboards quarterly; share lessons learned and improvements.
  - Include harassment prevention and respectful workplace expectations in contracts and evaluations.

- Metrics for monitoring
  - Process measures: policy adoption rates; percentage of staff/students trained; availability of chaperones; response times to incident reports.
  - Outcome measures: number and rate of incidents (by type, role, and setting); reporting rates; perceived safety and trust in processes (climate surveys); retention and absenteeism; qualitative feedback from affected individuals.
  - Equity lenses: stratify metrics by role, gender, race/ethnicity, and sexual orientation where feasible and appropriate; ensure confidentiality.

## Trauma-informed and equity-centered principles

- Safety and choice: Center the affected person’s safety and preferences in response plans; offer options for who receives reports and how cases proceed.
- Confidentiality and non-retaliation: Protect reporter identities to the extent possible; enforce anti-retaliation policies.
- Empowerment and collaboration: Involve staff and learners in co-designing policies and training; seek input from groups disproportionately affected (e.g., women in surgical specialties, LGBTQ+ community members).
- Cultural humility: Train leaders and supervisors on intersectional harassment dynamics; ensure culturally appropriate communication and support services.
- Transparency with care: Communicate processes and timelines clearly, provide status updates, and share aggregate outcomes to build trust.

## Strength of evidence summary

- Prevalence: Moderate certainty that IPSB toward hygienists and students is common; moderate certainty that harassment in academic/training hierarchies exists with significant underreporting.
- Correlates/mechanisms: Moderate certainty for the roles of patient-facing exposure, power asymmetries, and organizational climate; low-to-moderate certainty for specific identity-based differentials across settings due to inconsistent disaggregation.
- Impacts: Moderate certainty for associations with psychological distress and perceived educational/career harms; plausible links to broader safety culture and patient safety risks.
- Interventions: Low certainty regarding effectiveness of specific dental interventions due to limited evaluations; convergent rationale supports multi-level approaches.

## Policy and practice implications

- Dental care organizations and schools should not wait for perfect evidence to act. The convergence of prevalence signals, consistent barriers to reporting, and known harms justify immediate implementation of comprehensive prevention and response systems tailored to dental settings.
- Accreditation and regulatory bodies can accelerate progress by requiring demonstration of policies, training, reporting, and incident review processes, and by promoting standardized definitions and measures.
- Professional societies and conference organizers should adopt and enforce robust codes of conduct with clear reporting channels and supports for attendees.

## Conclusions and call to action

Sexual misconduct in dentistry—most visibly patient-perpetrated inappropriate sexual behavior toward hygienists and student providers, and supervisor/peer harassment in academic hierarchies—represents a pervasive and preventable threat to psychological safety, educational quality, workforce retention, and professional integrity. Despite heterogeneity in measures and study designs, the weight of evidence indicates high exposure risks in patient-facing roles, persistent underreporting, and organizational climates that too often normalize or fail to address misconduct. Harms include distress, burnout, educational disruption, and reputational and legal consequences.

Dental practices, educational institutions, professional societies, and regulators share a duty to create safe, respectful environments. A practical, multi-level, trauma-informed approach—combining clear codes of conduct, chaperone and environmental safeguards, skills-based training, confidential and fair reporting/response systems, and leadership accountability—is both feasible and urgently needed. Standardized definitions and measurement, together with rigorous evaluation of interventions, will strengthen the evidence base and enable benchmarking and continuous improvement. Acting now, while building better evidence, is the ethical and pragmatic path to ensuring that all members of the dental workforce can learn and work free from sexual misconduct.
