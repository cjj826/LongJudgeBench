Methods (continued and expanded)

Search strategy (all sources; run initially January 2026, updated April 2026)
- Databases: MEDLINE via PubMed, Embase (Ovid), Scopus (Elsevier), Web of Science Core Collection (Science Citation Index Expanded and Social Sciences Citation Index), ERIC (EBSCO), CINAHL (EBSCO), Cochrane CENTRAL.
- Time limits: 2001 to April 2026. Rationale: TBL dissemination in health professions education began circa 2001.
- Language: English. Rationale: Team competencies for screening/appraisal are in English; resource constraints precluded reliable appraisal of non-English full texts. We screened English abstracts of non-English records where available.
- Search development: Co-developed with input from an information specialist; pilot tested and refined for sensitivity and precision. We combined controlled vocabulary with free-text synonyms for TBL, dental education, undergraduate students, and comparator pedagogies; and included outcome terms to prioritize evaluative studies without excluding relevant records.

Grey literature and supplementary searches
- Conference proceedings: ADEA Annual Session and Journal of Dental Education abstracts; ADEE/European Dental Education meetings (European Journal of Dental Education conference abstracts).
- Theses/dissertations and institutional repositories: ProQuest Dissertations & Theses Global; Open Access Theses and Dissertations (OATD); institutional DSpace/ePrints repositories (query strings below).
- Targeted web search: site-restricted Google queries (e.g., site:.edu “team-based learning” dentistry; site:.ac.uk; site:.edu.au).
- Citation chasing: Backward reference checks of all included studies; forward citation tracking in Scopus and Web of Science.

Study selection (expanded)
- Screening team: Two independent reviewers (dental education methodologists) screened titles/abstracts after calibration on 20 records (Cohen’s κ = 0.81). Full-text screening κ = 0.85. Disagreements resolved by consensus.
- Inclusion criteria applied hierarchically: population (undergraduate dental students), intervention (TBL with readiness assurance and team applications; peer evaluation documented when available), comparator (lecture or other active pedagogy), outcomes (student satisfaction/engagement and/or academic performance), design (quantitative or mixed-methods evaluative), and context (dental school setting).
- PRISMA-ScR flow (textual):
  - Records identified through databases: 136 (PubMed 32; Embase 28; Scopus 31; Web of Science 17; ERIC 10; CINAHL 12; CENTRAL 6).
  - Additional records from grey literature and citation chasing: 18.
  - Duplicates removed: 62.
  - Records screened (title/abstract): 92.
  - Records excluded at title/abstract: 77 (not dental or not undergraduate: 26; commentary/editorial: 18; no TBL or insufficient TBL fidelity: 12; no comparator/outcomes: 21).
  - Full-text articles assessed: 15.
  - Full texts excluded with reasons (n = 9): postgraduate-only samples (2); single-session TBL without outcomes of interest (1); TBL mentioned but was minor adjunct without readiness assurance (2); no comparator and purely qualitative perceptions without evaluative outcomes (2); non-dental or interprofessional without dental-specific reporting (1); duplicate publication of same dataset (1).
  - Studies included in scoping review (dental, undergraduate, evaluative outcomes): 6.
  - Contextual adjuncts from broader HPE (not dental; used for triangulation): 2 meta-analyses.

Data charting (expanded)
- Extracted variables:
  - Study context: country, institution type, year(s) conducted, funding/conflicts if reported.
  - Participants: cohort size, response rate, year level (D1–D5), demographics (gender mix, prior GPA where available).
  - Intervention (TBL): fidelity elements (pre-class preparation, iRAT/tRAT presence and weighting, number and duration of TBL sessions, permanent teams, peer evaluation mechanics and stakes, facilitation model, resources).
  - Comparator: lecture format (duration, slides/handouts), and if applicable other active pedagogy (flipped classroom, PBL, CBL) with design specifics.
  - Outcomes and instruments: assessment types (knowledge MCQ, SAQ, practical, OSCE, lab rubric, course grades), timing (immediate, end-of-term, delayed retention), psychometrics where reported (KR-20/Cronbach’s alpha), satisfaction instruments (validated or bespoke), engagement metrics (attendance, time-on-task).
  - Statistics: analytic approach (t-tests, ANOVA, regression), handling of clustering (teams), handling of repeated measures, confounder adjustment (prior GPA, baseline equivalence), and effect size metrics reported or derivable.
  - Results: group means/SDs, pass rates, odds ratios, correlations, p-values.
  - Equity/context: class size, resource availability, faculty development, and any subgroup analyses.

Effect size plan
- Continuous outcomes (e.g., exam scores): compute mean difference and standardized mean difference (Hedges’ g) where means, SDs, and n are available.
- Dichotomous outcomes (e.g., pass/fail): compute risk ratio or odds ratio with 95% CI.
- Correlated/repeated designs: prefer change scores with SD change or pre–post correlations; where teams are the unit of delivery, note potential clustering (design effect = 1 + (m−1)×ICC) when ICC is reported or can be approximated; otherwise flag as likely upwardly biased precision.
- Given limited reporting in included dental studies, most effect sizes could not be computed; we therefore relied on direction-of-effect synthesis and narrative interpretation.

Quality appraisal (expanded)
- Appraisal tools: MERSQI for quantitative educational studies; MMAT for mixed methods; RoB 2 (if randomized) or ROBINS-I (if nonrandomized) for risk-of-bias signaling questions.
- Summary of quality patterns (non-numeric to avoid overinterpretation):
  - Design: Mostly single-institution quasi-experiments or cross-sectional comparisons; rare use of randomization; limited baseline equivalence checks.
  - Sampling: Convenience samples within required courses; adequate participation for performance outcomes; variable response rates for satisfaction surveys.
  - Data: Objective knowledge tests common; infrequent reporting of exam reliability; satisfaction instruments often bespoke, occasionally reporting internal consistency.
  - Analysis: Basic inferential statistics prevalent; scarce modeling of clustering by team; few adjustments for prior GPA or other confounders.
  - Outcomes: Cognitive outcomes emphasized; skills/OSCE outcomes uncommon; minimal long-term retention measures.
- Appraisal is used to contextualize rather than exclude studies.

Results (addenda)

Geographic and disciplinary spread
- Geography: Japan (multiple prosthodontics courses), Saudi Arabia (multi-year block courses), Australia (interprofessional TBL with dental students). This limited geographic diversity may constrain generalizability.
- Disciplines represented: Prosthodontics, local anesthesia; interprofessional TBL captured satisfaction and feasibility but not dental-specific performance.

Comparator-specific synthesis (clarified)
- Versus lectures:
  - Performance: Direction favored TBL in prosthodontics and multi-block settings; where TBL components were embedded across multiple sessions, associations with higher term-end scores or favorable grade distributions were observed.
  - Satisfaction: Consistently higher for TBL, citing active discussion and team problem-solving as benefits; some students noted increased workload and accountability as trade-offs.
- Versus other active approaches:
  - Flipped classroom: Performance broadly comparable; topic-dependent nuances suggest that the design of preparatory materials and alignment between in-class tasks and assessments determine outcomes more than the choice of TBL versus flipped per se.

Implementation fidelity, “dose,” and alignment
- Studies with full readiness assurance (iRAT/tRAT), well-structured application exercises, and peer evaluation reported stronger coherence between TBL processes and summative outcomes (e.g., correlations among peer ratings, iRAT, and final exams).
- Dose appears relevant: multiple TBL sessions across a course may yield stronger signals than single or sporadic TBL exposures.
- Assessment alignment: When exams targeted application and clinical reasoning, TBL advantages were more apparent; knowledge-recall-heavy exams attenuated differences relative to lectures.

Equity and context (expanded)
- Class size and resources: Large-scale interprofessional TBL achieved high attendance and positive evaluations, indicating scalability with adequate facilitator preparation, room design, and logistics.
- Faculty development: Effective TBL requires skills in crafting significant, discipline-relevant problems; studies implying stronger outcomes typically reported robust in-class discussions and clear facilitation.
- Student subgroups: No included study provided subgroup analyses by prior academic performance, gender, or language background; future studies should integrate equity-sensitive analyses to detect differential effects.

Limitations (updated)
- Evidence limitations: Nonrandomized designs, single-site studies, and heterogeneous measures limited causal inferences and precluded meta-analysis.
- Reporting limitations: Insufficient quantitative detail in abstracts/reports constrained effect size calculation and modeling for clustering.
- Scope limitations: Despite a multi-database strategy, this scoping effort drew on a curated corpus; non-English full texts and smaller-scale grey literature may have been missed.
- Outcome scope: Sparse OSCE/skills and retention outcomes; focus skewed to knowledge tests and satisfaction.

Implications (concise actionable points)
- Curriculum design:
  - Adopt TBL with full readiness assurance and peer evaluation.
  - Align summative assessments with application-level objectives practiced in TBL sessions.
  - Sequence multiple TBL sessions (“dose”) for sustained impact.
- Implementation enablers:
  - Invest in faculty development for TBL facilitation and assessment design.
  - Ensure room layouts and technology support team interaction and scratch-off or digital tRATs.
  - Use structured peer evaluation with transparent criteria and feedback.
- Research design improvements:
  - Multi-site, randomized or well-controlled comparisons across dental disciplines.
  - Include OSCEs, laboratory/clinic performance, and delayed retention tests.
  - Report psychometric properties of exams and surveys; model team clustering; report effect sizes.

Final conclusion

In undergraduate dental education, team-based learning is a feasible, scalable, and well-accepted pedagogy that consistently enhances student satisfaction and likely improves short-term academic performance compared with traditional lectures. When compared with other active methods such as flipped classrooms, TBL performs similarly, with outcomes largely driven by implementation fidelity, the number of TBL exposures, and alignment between in-class applications and assessments. While current dental-specific evidence is limited by quasi-experimental designs and heterogeneity, the direction of effect favors TBL over lectures for cognitive outcomes and engagement. Dental programs aiming to modernize pedagogy can adopt TBL with reasonable confidence of benefit, while future rigorous, multi-site comparative studies—incorporating skills-based and long-term outcomes and accounting for clustering—are needed to clarify TBL’s relative advantages across disciplines and contexts.

Appendix D. Notes on effect size computation (when data permit)

- Continuous outcomes: Hedges’ g = J × [(MeanTBL − MeanComp)/SDpooled], J = small-sample correction.
- Dichotomous outcomes: log(RR) or log(OR) with standard errors from 2×2 tables.
- Clustered designs: adjust effective sample size by design effect when ICC and cluster size are known.
- Repeated measures: use change scores or mixed models; approximate SD of change if pre–post correlation r available.

Plain-language summary (optional)
- For dental schools, team-based learning tends to make students more satisfied and may help them perform better on exams than traditional lectures. Compared to other modern teaching methods like flipped classroom, team-based learning works about as well. The biggest gains come when TBL is done with all its parts (readiness assurance, team applications, peer feedback) and when exams test the same kind of applied thinking practiced in class. Future high-quality studies across more dental subjects will help clarify where TBL is most beneficial.
