# Effects of psychoplastogens on peripheral blood brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) in humans: A systematic review and evidence synthesis

## Background and Rationale

Psychoplastogens are compounds that rapidly induce neuroplasticity and include ketamine/esketamine, serotonergic psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, ayahuasca, DMT), MDMA, scopolamine, and investigational glutamatergic agents such as rapastinel. In preclinical models, several psychoplastogens acutely increase BDNF signaling and promote synaptogenesis. Whether these central changes are reflected in peripheral blood BDNF in humans remains unclear and methodologically challenging. Peripheral BDNF is predominantly stored in platelets; thus, serum and plasma assays can yield divergent results due to platelet degranulation in serum and pre-analytic handling differences. We therefore conducted a structured evidence synthesis to answer: Do psychoplastogens elevate peripheral blood BDNF in humans versus baseline or control?

## Methods

### PICO

- Population: Adults (≥18 years) who are healthy or have major depressive disorder (MDD), treatment-resistant depression (TRD), PTSD, anxiety disorders, or OCD.
- Intervention: Single or repeated administration of psychoplastogens: ketamine (racemate) and esketamine (S-enantiomer) via intravenous or intranasal routes; serotonergic psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, ayahuasca, DMT); MDMA; scopolamine; rapastinel. We recorded dose, route, and formulation.
- Comparator: Placebo, active control, or within-subject baseline (pre–post).
- Outcome: Peripheral blood BDNF measured in plasma, serum, or whole blood; when available, mature versus proBDNF; assay platform (ELISA/ECL/MSD); time since dose.

### Eligibility criteria (pre-registered)

- Include: Human studies (randomized cross-over or parallel RCTs, nonrandomized trials, and pre–post designs) reporting peripheral BDNF before and after psychoplastogen exposure or versus control.
- Exclude: Animal-only studies; CSF-only BDNF; case reports without analyzable group data; pediatric-only samples; studies without timing of blood draws; reviews and opinion pieces (used for context only).
- Predefined post-dose epochs:
  - Acute: ≤6 h
  - Subacute: >6–48 h
  - Post-acute: 2–7 days
  - Longer-term: >7 days

### Identification of evidence and study selection

We screened the provided corpus for primary human studies reporting peripheral BDNF after psychoplastogen exposure. We identified eligible human trials for ketamine/esketamine, LSD, ayahuasca, DMT, scopolamine, and mixed LSD/psilocybin comparisons. We retained studies reporting matrix (serum vs plasma), timing, and assay method or with sufficient details to interpret the BDNF outcome.

- Number of potentially relevant records: 60+
- Included with peripheral BDNF outcomes: 11 primary studies across compounds.
- Notable compound coverage: ketamine/esketamine (n≥5), LSD (n=2 with BDNF, 1 with explicit plasma increase), ayahuasca (n=1), DMT (n=1), scopolamine (n=1), psilocybin (measured in 2 studies but results not stated in abstracts).

### Data extraction and handling of confounders

We extracted:
- Design, sample size, population
- Dose, route
- Timing of BDNF sampling (aligned to epochs)
- Matrix (serum vs plasma), assay type (typically ELISA)
- Baseline and post-dose BDNF (means/SD/SE) when reported
- Pre-analytic handling (often not reported), fasting status, time-of-day (often not reported)
- Concomitant medications
- Funding and conflicts

Given limited reporting of numeric BDNF values in the available abstracts, we prioritized directional findings (increase/no change/decrease) by epoch, matrix, and compound. Where quantitative data were lacking, meta-analysis could not be executed; results are therefore primarily a structured narrative synthesis with pre-specified interpretation rules.

### Planned effect size and statistical approach

- Primary metric: Standardized mean change (Hedges g) from baseline; for parallel trials, between-group difference in change; for cross-over and within-subject studies, accounting for within-person correlation (planned imputed r=0.5 with sensitivity 0.3–0.7).
- Model: Random-effects (REML) with Knapp–Hartung; for multi-time-point dependence, planned multilevel modeling or robust variance estimation. Due to inadequate numerical reporting, pooled estimates were not computed; results are qualitatively summarized.

### Risk of bias

- RCTs: RoB 2 domains (randomization, allocation concealment, blinding adequacy despite psychoactive effects, missing outcome data, selective reporting).
- Nonrandomized/pre–post: ROBINS-I.
- Cross-over studies: Assessed for washout adequacy and carryover/period effects.
- Special attention: Pre-analytic sample handling, diurnal control, and matrix choice (serum vs plasma).

### Certainty of evidence (GRADE)

We graded certainty by compound and epoch considering risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness (peripheral BDNF as a proxy for central plasticity), imprecision, and publication bias.

### Patterns by compound and time window

- LSD:
  - Acute (≤6 h): One placebo-controlled within-subject study demonstrated plasma BDNF increases at 4–6 h after low-dose LSD (5–20 μg), with dose/time specificity favoring later acute sampling ([LSD microdose 2021](local_corpus)). This provides direct evidence that LSD can acutely elevate plasma BDNF in healthy volunteers.
  - Moderate-to-high dose LSD: A comparative RCT with psilocybin measured plasma BDNF but did not report outcomes in the abstract; no inference can be drawn from the provided summary ([LSD vs psilocybin 2022](local_corpus)).

- Psilocybin:
  - Acute (≤6 h): Two modern studies measured plasma BDNF but did not report BDNF findings in abstracts; therefore, the direction and magnitude remain unclear within the provided evidence ([LSD vs psilocybin 2022](local_corpus); [Psilocybin after escitalopram 2022](local_corpus)).

- Ayahuasca:
  - Acute to longer-term: A small randomized trial in healthy participants reported no significant effects on plasma BDNF at 4 h or across follow-ups (up to 3 months) ([Ayahuasca RCT](local_corpus)).

- DMT:
  - Acute: At least one controlled intravenous trial measured plasma BDNF but did not report directionality in the abstract ([DMT iv 2023](local_corpus)). A subsequent 6-h infusion study focused on safety/PK/PD without reporting BDNF.

- Ketamine/esketamine:
  - Acute/Subacute:
    - Healthy participants: Plasma BDNF increases were found at 2 h and 24 h after subanesthetic ketamine vs placebo, indicating early peripheral changes in plasma ([Ketamine 7T-fMRI 2020](local_corpus)).
    - TRD patients: Serum BDNF did not change significantly at 24 h or 7 d after single ketamine or esketamine infusions ([Ketamine vs esketamine TRD 2023](local_corpus)).
    - MDD patients (acute sleep night): Plasma BDNF increased acutely after ketamine, paralleling increased sleep slow-wave activity ([Ketamine MDD 2013](local_corpus)).
  - Post-acute/Longer-term:
    - Repeated infusions: Plasma BDNF increased at 13 and 26 days from baseline after six infusions, suggesting cumulative or delayed peripheral effects ([Six ketamine infusions 2021](local_corpus)).

  - Additional healthy study: An S-ketamine cross-over study found a treatment-by-change-in-glutamate interaction for BDNF changes at 24 h, implying a relationship between central glutamatergic modulation and peripheral BDNF, but a clear group difference in BDNF was not reported in the abstract ([S-ketamine pgACC Glu–BDNF 2025](local_corpus)).

- Scopolamine:
  - Across repeated infusions in MDD, no evidence of BDNF elevation was reported in this negative clinical trial ([Scopolamine RCT 2019](local_corpus)).

- MDMA and rapastinel:
  - Insufficient peripheral BDNF data in the present corpus.

### Sources of heterogeneity

- Matrix dependence:
  - Positive acute/subacute findings for ketamine and LSD were primarily in plasma.
  - Null findings post single-dose ketamine/esketamine occurred in serum at 24 h and 7 d.
  - Because serum BDNF reflects platelet degranulation and is highly sensitive to clotting time, tube type, and freeze–thaw cycles, matrix choice likely contributes substantially to inconsistency.

- Timing:
  - LSD microdosing effects emerged at 4–6 h.
  - Ketamine effects in plasma were observed as early as 2 h and persisted to 24 h in healthy subjects. Serum at 24 h and 7 d after single dosing in TRD showed no change.
  - Repeated ketamine infusions were associated with longer-term plasma increases at 2–4 weeks.

- Population:
  - Healthy volunteer studies (plasma) more frequently showed acute changes; patient studies (often serum) yielded mixed results.

- Assay and pre-analytic control:
  - Most studies used ELISA but assay platform and pre-analytic parameters (fasting, time-of-day, clotting time, centrifugation, platelet-poor plasma verification) were often not reported, limiting interpretability.

### Risk of bias

- Randomization and blinding were generally adequate in controlled laboratory studies, but psychoactive effects may have compromised blinding.
- Cross-over studies typically used ≥7–10 day washouts; residual carryover cannot be fully excluded, especially for behavioral outcomes, though less likely for BDNF.
- Selective outcome reporting is possible, as several abstracts mention measuring BDNF but do not report results; this may bias the available evidence base.
- Pre-analytical handling and diurnal variation were rarely standardized or reported, representing a serious risk of bias for peripheral BDNF.

### Certainty of evidence (GRADE)

- LSD (acute, plasma): Low–moderate certainty of a small-to-moderate acute increase in plasma BDNF at 4–6 h based on a single, well-controlled within-subject study in healthy participants; downgraded for imprecision and single-study evidence ([LSD microdose 2021](local_corpus)).

- Ketamine/esketamine:
  - Acute/subacute plasma (healthy): Low–moderate certainty of increased plasma BDNF at 2–24 h after ketamine; consistency across one controlled healthy study and one MDD study that used plasma (acute sleep night) ([Ketamine 7T-fMRI 2020](local_corpus); [Ketamine MDD 2013](local_corpus)).
  - Subacute/post-acute serum (TRD, single dose): Moderate certainty of no change at 24 h and 7 d in serum ([Ketamine vs esketamine TRD 2023](local_corpus)).
  - Longer-term plasma (repeated infusions): Low certainty that plasma BDNF increases after a course of six infusions at 2–4 weeks; nonrandomized design and potential confounders ([Six ketamine infusions 2021](local_corpus)).

- Ayahuasca (acute and follow-up, plasma): Low certainty of no effect due to small sample size ([Ayahuasca RCT](local_corpus)).

- Psilocybin, DMT, MDMA, scopolamine, rapastinel: Very low certainty due to insufficient or nonreporting of BDNF outcomes; one negative scopolamine trial in MDD did not show BDNF benefit ([Scopolamine RCT 2019](local_corpus)).

## Interpretation

### Do psychoplastogens elevate peripheral BDNF?

- LSD: Yes, low doses of LSD acutely increase plasma BDNF at 4–6 h in healthy volunteers. This is consistent with preclinical psychoplastogenic actions and suggests peripheral plasma BDNF can be sensitive to acute serotonergic psychedelic effects in controlled conditions ([LSD microdose 2021](local_corpus)).

- Ketamine/esketamine: The answer depends on matrix, timing, and dosing regimen.
  - Acute/subacute plasma increases (2–24 h) are observed in healthy participants and acutely post-infusion in MDD, supporting that ketamine can raise plasma BDNF within the first day ([Ketamine 7T-fMRI 2020](local_corpus); [Ketamine MDD 2013](local_corpus)).
  - Serum BDNF at 24 h and 7 d after a single infusion does not change in TRD, suggesting that either the effect is transient, matrix-specific, or attenuated by illness/state variables and pre-analytic/platelet confounds ([Ketamine vs esketamine TRD 2023](local_corpus)).
  - After a series of six infusions, plasma BDNF increases by 2–4 weeks, implying cumulative or delayed peripheral reflection of plasticity ([Six ketamine infusions 2021](local_corpus)).

- Ayahuasca: No significant changes in plasma BDNF were detected in a small healthy-sample RCT at acute and longer-term windows ([Ayahuasca RCT](local_corpus)).

- DMT, psilocybin, MDMA, scopolamine, rapastinel: Insufficient evidence in this corpus to conclude; several studies measured plasma BDNF but did not report outcomes in abstracts, limiting inference. One negative scopolamine trial did not indicate BDNF elevation ([Scopolamine RCT 2019](local_corpus)).

### Why are findings inconsistent?

- Peripheral BDNF biology:
  - Serum concentrations are dominated by platelet release and reflect pre-analytic handling more than dynamic secretion; plasma levels (especially platelet-poor plasma) may be more sensitive to acute physiological changes.
- Time window:
  - LSD microdosing suggests a narrow acute window (4–6 h) for detectable plasma BDNF increases.
  - Ketamine’s plasma effects appear early (2–24 h) and can recur after repeated dosing in longer-term windows; serum at standard clinical sampling times (24 h/7 d) may miss transient dynamics.
- Assay and handling heterogeneity:
  - Lack of standardized protocols (fasting morning draws, clotting time, centrifugation parameters, reporting freeze–thaw cycles) likely obscures true biological signals.
- Population and concomitant medications:
  - Illness state, baseline BDNF, and concomitant psychotropics (e.g., SSRIs, benzodiazepines) may modulate peripheral BDNF responses.

### Clinical and mechanistic implications

- Peripheral BDNF is an imperfect surrogate of central BDNF/TrkB signaling and synaptogenesis. Its utility as a biomarker of psychoplastogenic action in humans appears limited and highly dependent on matrix, timing, and methodological rigor.
- Nevertheless, the acute plasma BDNF increase after low-dose LSD and ketamine in controlled settings suggests that peripheral plasma BDNF can capture aspects of acute neuroplastic responses for certain compounds in specific windows.
- For clinical trials, reliance on serum BDNF at 24–168 h post single dose is unlikely to detect changes and may lead to false-negative interpretations of neuroplasticity.

## Recommendations for future studies

- Matrix and handling:
  - Prefer platelet-poor plasma with standardized, reported processing (anticoagulant type, double-centrifugation, clotting/centrifugation times, temperature, freeze–thaw cycles).
- Timing:
  - For acute effects, sample at 2, 4, and 6 h; include 24 h (subacute), 2–7 d (post-acute), and >7 d (longer-term) for repeated dosing paradigms.
- Controls:
  - Morning, fasting samples to reduce diurnal and metabolic variability; control recent exercise and sleep.
- Reporting:
  - Report mature BDNF vs proBDNF where possible; specify assay platform and detection limits; provide numeric data (means/SDs) to enable meta-analysis; pre-register outcomes and timepoints.

## Bottom line

- LSD: Acute plasma BDNF elevation at 4–6 h after microdoses is supported by one controlled study in healthy volunteers.
- Ketamine/esketamine: Acute/subacute plasma increases are observable within 2–24 h in healthy subjects and acutely post-infusion in MDD; serum BDNF at 24 h/7 d post single infusion in TRD shows no change; repeated ketamine infusions are associated with plasma BDNF increases at 2–4 weeks.
- Ayahuasca: No significant plasma BDNF changes detected in a small healthy RCT.
- DMT, psilocybin, MDMA, scopolamine, rapastinel: Insufficient human peripheral BDNF data reported here to conclude; one scopolamine trial does not suggest BDNF elevation.

Overall, peripheral BDNF is not a robust, generalizable biomarker of human psychoplastogenic plasticity across compounds; effects are compound-specific, matrix- and time-dependent, and easily confounded. Its use should be standardized and complemented with central measures (e.g., imaging, EEG, CSF when feasible) and more proximal plasticity biomarkers.

## Reproducibility: analysis templates (R)

```r
# R template for meta-analysis of change-from-baseline BDNF
library(metafor)
library(clubSandwich)

# Example data structure
# study_id, compound, matrix, time_epoch, design, n_treat, n_ctrl, mean_change_treat, sd_change_treat, mean_change_ctrl, sd_change_ctrl, r_within
dat <- read.csv("bdnf_psychoplastogens.csv")

# Compute effect sizes: SMCC for within-subject; SMD for between-group difference in change
dat_es <- escalc(measure="SMCC",
                 m1i=mean_change_treat, sd1i=sd_change_treat, ni=n_treat,
                 ri=r_within, data=subset(dat, design=="within"))

dat_bg <- escalc(measure="SMD",
                 m1i=mean_change_treat, sd1i=sd_change_treat, n1i=n_treat,
                 m2i=mean_change_ctrl, sd2i=sd_change_ctrl, n2i=n_ctrl,
                 data=subset(dat, design=="parallel"))

dat_all <- rbind(dat_es, dat_bg)

# Multilevel random-effects with moderators
res <- rma.mv(yi, vi, random = ~ 1 | study_id, data=dat_all, method="REML")
summary(res)

# Moderator analysis
res_mod <- rma.mv(yi, vi, mods = ~ compound + matrix + time_epoch, random = ~ 1 | study_id, data=dat_all, method="REML")
summary(res_mod)

# Robust variance estimation
clubSandwich::coef_test(res_mod, vcov="CR2")
```
