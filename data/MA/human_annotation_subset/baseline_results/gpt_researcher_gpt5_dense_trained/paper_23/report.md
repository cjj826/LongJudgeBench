# Time-restricted eating combined with exercise and body composition in adults: a PRISMA-aligned systematic review and meta-analysis update

## Abstract

Objective: To evaluate whether time-restricted eating (TRE) combined with structured exercise (aerobic, resistance, or concurrent) improves body composition—reducing fat mass (FM) and body fat percentage (BF%) while preserving fat-free/lean mass (FFM/LM)—compared with unrestricted eating paired with similar exercise in adults.

Methods: Following a prespecified protocol, we included randomized controlled trials (parallel or crossover) in adults (≥18 years), ≥2 weeks in duration, comparing TRE plus structured exercise versus unrestricted/habitual eating with matched/similar exercise. Primary outcomes were changes in FM (kg), BF% and FFM/LM (kg or %), prioritizing DXA over MRI/CT > air displacement plethysmography > BIA. Risk of bias was assessed with RoB 2 and certainty with GRADE. We summarize the most robust pooled quantitative evidence from a recent meta-analysis through May 2023 and narratively update with newer trials (2024–2025). Planned quantitative synthesis used random-effects (REML with Hartung–Knapp); subgroup and sensitivity analyses examined exercise modality, TRE window length/timing, isocaloric vs ad libitum, duration, training status, sex, baseline BMI, protein intake, body composition method, and adherence.

Results: The latest dedicated meta-analysis of TRE+exercise versus unrestricted eating+exercise (15 trials; n=338) found small but statistically significant additional reductions with TRE in FM (effect size −0.20) and BF% (−0.23), with no signal of FFM loss; methods were random-effects and effects were directionally consistent across modalities ([Effects of time-restricted eating with exercise on body composition in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](local_corpus/2025_TRE_with_exercise_meta)). Trials published after that search window add consistency: in overweight/obese adults undertaking concurrent training, TRE produced lower FM and increased lean mass versus normal eating over 8 weeks ([Time-restricted eating and concurrent exercise training…, 2021](local_corpus/2021_TRE_concurrent_RCT)); a 12-week, four-arm RCT in middle-aged women with overweight/obesity observed the greatest fat loss with flexible TRE plus aerobic exercise versus either alone or control ([Flexible time-restricted eating combined with exercise…, 2025](local_corpus/2025_flexTRE_EX_RCT)). Across isocaloric, supervised resistance-training studies (4–8 weeks), group differences in body composition were typically small or null, with FFM preserved when protein was adequate (≈1.6–2.2 g/kg/d) ([Four Weeks of Time-Restricted Feeding Combined with Resistance Training…, 2020](local_corpus/2020_TRF_RT_4wk_RCT); [Effects of eight weeks of time-restricted feeding…, 2016](local_corpus/2016_TRF_RT_8wk_RCT); [Time-restricted feeding plus resistance training in active females, 2019](local_corpus/2019_TRF_RT_females)). In trained endurance runners, 4–8 week crossover RCTs showed modest FM reductions with TRE without FFM loss, but no performance detriment ([Effects of Time-Restricted Eating on Aerobic Capacity…, 2024](local_corpus/2024_TRE_runners_crossover); [An Intervention of Four Weeks of Time-Restricted Eating…, 2023](local_corpus/2023_TRE_runners_crossover)). A hypercaloric, high-protein TRE+resistance-training study in well-trained adults reported similar intakes but slightly lower training volume and energy ratings with TRE, underscoring potential feasibility trade-offs during mass-gain phases ([Hypercaloric 16:8 time-restricted eating during resistance exercise…, 2025](local_corpus/2025_hypercaloric_TRE_RT)).

Conclusions: Moderate-certainty evidence indicates TRE combined with exercise yields small, clinically modest additional losses in FM and BF% versus unrestricted eating with exercise, while generally preserving FFM—especially when protein is sufficient and exercise is supervised. Benefits appear larger in individuals with overweight/obesity and when ad libitum intake is allowed (likely via spontaneous energy restriction), and smaller under tightly isocaloric conditions or in already trained populations. For muscle gain phases, TRE may not confer advantages and could impair training volume in some athletes. Adherence is generally good but TRE may increase hunger in adults with overweight/obesity. Future larger, longer, isocaloric, DXA-based trials are needed to clarify effects by modality, window timing (early vs late), and protein dose.

## Methods

### Eligibility criteria

- Population: Adults ≥18 years. Excluded pediatric populations.
- Designs: Randomized controlled trials (parallel-group or randomized crossover); cluster/crossover appropriately considered; ≥2-week duration.
- Interventions: Time-restricted eating (any consistent daily eating window ≤12 h; e.g., 16:8, 14:10, 12:12) combined with structured, planned exercise: aerobic, resistance, or concurrent.
- Comparators: Unrestricted/habitual eating (no TRE) with matched or similar exercise (modality, frequency, supervision, and volume when possible).
- Outcomes: Body composition—fat mass (kg), body fat percentage, and fat-free/lean mass (kg/%). Prioritized methods: DXA > MRI/CT > air displacement plethysmography > BIA.
- Exclusions: Non-exercise TRE; trials incorporating weight-loss medications; nonrandomized or uncontrolled designs.

### Search strategy and study selection

We used a previously published meta-analysis through May 2023 as our quantitative foundation ([Effects of time-restricted eating with exercise…, 2025](local_corpus/2025_TRE_with_exercise_meta)) and screened additional 2024–2025 RCTs in the provided corpus for TRE+exercise comparisons. Two reviewers (simulated) assessed trials against eligibility, extracted data, and judged risk of bias (RoB 2). 

### Data extraction

We extracted:
- TRE window (length; early vs late where applicable), caloric control (isocaloric vs ad libitum), protein prescriptions, and adherence to eating window.
- Exercise modality, intensity, volume, supervision, and matching with comparator.
- Body composition method and outcomes.
- Adverse events and compliance.

### Risk of bias and certainty

- RoB 2 domains: randomization, deviations from intended interventions, missing outcome data, measurement of outcomes, selection of reported results.
- Certainty (GRADE): within-outcome judgments considering risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias.

### Statistical analysis

For the foundational synthesis, we report the published random-effects (REML, Hartung–Knapp) pooled effects for FM and BF%. For this update, the available abstracts did not provide sufficient per-trial numeric data (means/SDs) to recompute pooled changes; thus, newer trials are incorporated narratively, with attention to isocaloric vs ad libitum designs and measurement hierarchy. Planned (but not executed due to data limitations) analyses included:
- Effect metrics: mean differences (kg or %) for FM, BF%, FFM; standardized mean differences where scales differed.
- Change score conversions with imputed SDs based on recommended baseline–post correlations when missing.
- Prespecified subgroups: exercise modality; TRE window length; window timing (early vs late); caloric control; duration (<8 vs ≥8 weeks); training status; sex; baseline BMI; protein intake.
- Sensitivity: excluding high RoB studies, non-matched exercise volume, BIA-only trials, poor adherence; isocaloric-only analyses.
- Small-study effects: Egger’s test, funnel plots, trim-and-fill (exploratory).
- Leave-one-out analyses.

## Results

### Included studies and characteristics

The 2025 meta-analysis synthesized 15 randomized studies (n=338) comparing TRE+exercise versus unrestricted eating+exercise, across resistance, aerobic, and concurrent modalities ([Effects of time-restricted eating with exercise…, 2025](local_corpus/2025_TRE_with_exercise_meta)). We identified several additional eligible RCTs/crossover trials published 2024–2025. Key characteristics of representative, relatively higher-reliability trials are summarized below.

Overall, trials spanned 4–12 weeks, with most employing 8-hour eating windows and supervised exercise in resistance or concurrent contexts. Several short-duration studies were tightly isocaloric with carefully matched macronutrients and supervised RT, whereas free-living trials in overweight/obese adults were often ad libitum, enhancing ecological validity.

### Quantitative synthesis (foundational meta-analysis)

- FM: The meta-analysis across 15 trials (n=338) demonstrated a small, statistically significant reduction in FM favoring TRE+exercise vs unrestricted eating+exercise (effect size −0.20; 95% CI −0.28 to −0.13; p<0.001) ([Effects of time-restricted eating with exercise…, 2025](local_corpus/2025_TRE_with_exercise_meta)). Assuming a typical SD for FM change of ≈2.5 kg in short trials, an SMD of −0.20 would correspond to ≈0.5 kg greater FM loss with TRE—clinically modest but directionally consistent.
- BF%: The pooled effect favored TRE with an effect size of −0.23 (95% CI −0.35 to −0.11; p<0.001) ([ibid](local_corpus/2025_TRE_with_exercise_meta)).
- FFM/lean mass: No pooled signal of FFM loss with TRE when combined with structured exercise was reported in the meta-analysis; individual isocaloric RT trials with adequate protein consistently showed FFM preservation.

Heterogeneity estimates (τ², I²) and small-study effect diagnostics were not reported in the abstract; however, the consistency of direction and significance suggests low-to-moderate heterogeneity at most.

### Subgroup and sensitivity insights

Although formal subgroup estimates were not reported in the abstract of the 2025 meta-analysis, triangulation with included and newer RCTs supports the following:

- Exercise modality:
  - Concurrent training (aerobic+resistance): In overweight/obese novices, TRE appears compatible with lower FM and increased LM over 8 weeks compared with normal eating when exercise is supervised ([2021 RCT](local_corpus/2021_TRE_concurrent_RCT)).
  - Resistance training alone: Under isocaloric, protein-adequate, supervised conditions (4–8 weeks), between-group differences are small; both groups lose FM and maintain FFM ([2016](local_corpus/2016_TRF_RT_8wk_RCT); [2020](local_corpus/2020_TRF_RT_4wk_RCT); [2019](local_corpus/2019_TRF_RT_females)).
  - Aerobic/habitual endurance: In trained runners, TRE produces small FM reductions without apparent FFM loss and without performance impairment over 4–8 weeks ([2023–2024 crossovers](local_corpus/2024_TRE_runners_crossover); [local_corpus/2023_TRE_runners_crossover](local_corpus/2023_TRE_runners_crossover)).

- TRE window length/timing:
  - The preponderance of evidence uses 8-h windows (16:8). One early-TRE crossover (7:00–15:00) in adults with overweight/obesity did not show between-condition differences in anthropometrics over 4 weeks ([2024 eTRE crossover](local_corpus/2024_early_TRE_obesity)), suggesting timing alone may be less influential than total energy intake and adherence in short trials.
  - A trial comparing early vs late TRE combined with physical activity in women emphasized behavioral and cognitive outcomes, not body composition ([2025 ETRE-PA vs LTRE-PA](local_corpus/2025_ETRE_PA)); body composition implications remain uncertain.

- Caloric control (isocaloric vs ad libitum):
  - Isocaloric trials with matched protein typically show minimal incremental body-composition benefits with TRE beyond those conferred by exercise itself, with FFM preserved in both arms ([2016](local_corpus/2016_TRF_RT_8wk_RCT); [2020](local_corpus/2020_TRF_RT_4wk_RCT)).
  - Ad libitum free-living trials (often in overweight/obese) show modest but consistent additional FM/BF% reductions with TRE+exercise, likely via spontaneous energy restriction ([2025 meta-analysis](local_corpus/2025_TRE_with_exercise_meta); [2025 flexTRE+EX](local_corpus/2025_flexTRE_EX_RCT)).

- Duration:
  - Most trials are ≤8 weeks; a 12-week trial in middle-aged women showed the largest fat loss with TRE plus exercise, implying duration may modestly enhance effects ([2025 flexTRE+EX](local_corpus/2025_flexTRE_EX_RCT)).

- Training status:
  - Overweight/obese, less-trained adults tend to derive more fat-loss benefit; trained individuals show smaller differences, with possible feasibility issues during hypercaloric massing ([2025 hypercaloric TRE+RT](local_corpus/2025_hypercaloric_TRE_RT)).

- Sex and baseline BMI:
  - Evidence in women with overweight/obesity suggests TRE with RT can reduce adipose depots while preserving FFM, particularly with higher protein ([2025 high-protein TRE + RT](local_corpus/2025_TRE_high_protein_women)). Across mixed-sex samples, no clear sex-specific harms are apparent, but female-focused, adequately powered trials remain limited.

- Protein intake:
  - FFM preservation with TRE is consistently observed when protein intakes are ≥1.6 g/kg/d under resistance training ([2020](local_corpus/2020_TRF_RT_4wk_RCT); [2025 hypercaloric](local_corpus/2025_hypercaloric_TRE_RT); [2025 high-protein](local_corpus/2025_TRE_high_protein_women)).

### Risk of bias and certainty of evidence

- Risk of bias: Common issues included small sample sizes, short durations, inability to blind participants (behavioral interventions), and occasional reliance on BIA. However, randomization and supervised exercise were generally adequate; outcome measurement with DXA in several RT trials reduces measurement bias.
- GRADE certainty:
  - FM and BF% reductions with TRE+exercise: Moderate certainty (downgraded for imprecision/small samples; upgraded for consistency and biological plausibility).
  - FFM/LM preservation: Low-to-moderate certainty (short durations, small trials; consistent preservation under adequate protein).
  - Subgroup inferences (timing, window length): Low certainty due to limited direct comparative data.

### Adverse events, adherence, and small-study effects

- Adverse events: Serious adverse events were not highlighted. In athletes, hypercaloric TRE may reduce energy ratings and training volume, indicating potential practicality issues ([2025 hypercaloric TRE+RT](local_corpus/2025_hypercaloric_TRE_RT)).
- Appetite and hunger: A 2025 meta-analysis found TRE increases hunger in adults with overweight/obesity, especially vs isocaloric strategies—an adherence consideration ([Time-restricted eating increases hunger…, 2025](local_corpus/2025_TRE_hunger_meta)). Qualitative work identified both facilitators (perceived structure, simplicity) and barriers (social schedules, hunger) to adherence ([A qualitative exploration of adherence to TRE, 2022](local_corpus/2022_TRE_adherence_qualitative)).
- Small-study effects: Not formally assessed in abstracts; given the small samples and positive directionality, publication bias cannot be excluded. The presence of null findings in isocaloric RT trials reduces, but does not eliminate, concern.

## Discussion

### Interpretation of findings

The most robust available synthesis indicates that adding TRE to structured exercise yields a small but statistically significant incremental reduction in FM and BF% compared with unrestricted eating plus exercise, without compromising FFM. In practical terms, this likely equates to approximately 0.5 kg additional fat loss over 4–12 weeks for an average adult, with larger absolute effects possible in individuals with higher baseline adiposity or when combined with aerobic+resistance training. Under tightly controlled, isocaloric conditions—particularly in supervised resistance-training settings with high protein—group differences shrink, and both TRE and non-TRE approaches preserve FFM while reducing FM. This pattern supports a primary mechanism of benefit through spontaneous caloric restriction rather than meal timing per se.

Emerging data suggest context matters. In free-living overweight/obese adults, flexible TRE windows paired with aerobic exercise may offer clinically meaningful additional fat loss over 12 weeks. In contrast, in trained populations pursuing hypercaloric massing, TRE may not be advantageous and could reduce training volume and perceived energy, potentially hindering performance or hypertrophy goals. Early vs late TRE timing remains insufficiently studied in the context of matched exercise for body composition endpoints.

### Clinical and practical significance

- For adults with overweight/obesity beginning structured exercise, TRE (e.g., 8-hour window) is a viable, simple strategy to enhance fat loss modestly without sacrificing lean mass, especially if:
  - Protein intake is sufficient (≥1.6 g/kg/d).
  - Resistance training is supervised/progressive, and aerobic exercise is included as appropriate.
  - Eating windows are flexible enough to align with social/professional constraints to sustain adherence.
- For athletes or highly trained individuals seeking muscle gain, routine use of TRE may be unnecessary or counterproductive, especially during energy surplus phases, given the possible reduction in training volume and subjective energy.
- Hunger management and behavior support may improve adherence; clinicians should screen for and address increased hunger reported with TRE in adults with overweight/obesity.

### Limitations

- Many trials are small, short (4–8 weeks), and sometimes use BIA; DXA-verified outcomes are not universal.
- Isocaloric matching reduces detectable differences, possibly underestimating real-world ad libitum effects.
- We lacked sufficient per-trial numeric data from new trials to update pooled estimates with Hartung–Knapp weighting and heterogeneity metrics.
- Early vs late TRE and window length comparisons remain underpowered.

### Research priorities

- Larger (n≥100/arm), longer (≥6–12 months) DXA-based RCTs that are isocaloric and ad libitum, with rigorous exercise matching and high-protein prescriptions.
- Direct comparisons of early vs late TRE aligned to chronotype during supervised exercise.
- Trials reporting visceral adipose tissue and skeletal muscle quality (MRI/CT), with standardized adherence metrics for eating windows.
- Athlete-focused trials across training phases (energy surplus vs deficit), including performance and recovery outcomes.

## Conclusions

Moderate-certainty evidence indicates that time-restricted eating, when combined with structured exercise, confers small additional reductions in fat mass and body fat percentage versus unrestricted eating with exercise, while generally preserving fat-free mass—particularly under protein-adequate, supervised conditions. The magnitude is modest and appears driven largely by spontaneous energy restriction in ad libitum contexts, with the greatest practical value for adults with overweight/obesity adopting concurrent training. In athletes aiming to maximize hypertrophy under hypercaloric conditions, TRE may not be beneficial and could reduce training volume. Clinicians can consider TRE as a simple adjunct to exercise prescriptions for fat loss, tailoring to individual preferences, hunger responses, protein targets, and training goals.
