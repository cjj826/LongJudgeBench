Additional outcomes: remission and acceptability

- Remission: Patterns mirrored response. ECT and IV ketamine achieved the highest remission proportions in acute TRD, with intranasal esketamine next, followed by effective pharmacologic augmentations (aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, quetiapine, lithium, liothyronine). rTMS/iTBS achieved lower but clinically meaningful remission in a subset. Head‑to‑head data showed higher remission with esketamine versus quetiapine XR augmentation when both combined with an SSRI/SNRI. Remission advantages of IV ketamine over active comparators were consistent in placebo/sham‑controlled trials; the ketamine versus ECT trial, powered for response, also showed broadly comparable remission, consistent with response findings.
- Acceptability (all‑cause discontinuation): rTMS/iTBS had among the lowest discontinuation rates. Esketamine discontinuation was generally comparable to active augmentation and lower than ECT in many contexts, with transient dissociation and blood pressure elevations the main reasons for in‑clinic monitoring. Antipsychotic augmentations had higher discontinuation than placebo due to akathisia, sedation, and metabolic effects; lithium discontinuation was primarily related to tolerability and monitoring burden. ECT discontinuation reflected logistical and cognitive adverse effects. Minocycline discontinuation did not differ meaningfully from placebo but the absence of efficacy negates its use.

Small‑study effects and publication bias

- Visual assessment of comparison‑adjusted funnel plots from source NMAs suggested limited small‑study effects in large, industry‑sponsored augmentation and esketamine programs. Some asymmetry was noted in device trials (rTMS/TBS), consistent with early small, positive studies; effects persisted but attenuated when restricted to larger, multicenter or preregistered TRD trials.
- No pervasive evidence of publication bias was detected in the 2025 TRD NMA, but selective reporting and outcome switching cannot be excluded. We downgraded certainty where small, single‑center studies dominated a node or where imprecision widened confidence intervals across plausible benefit and no effect.

PRISMA flow and study selection summary

- We anchored on a prior TRD‑specific NMA that screened 8,234 records and included 69 RCTs meeting strict TRD criteria (≥2 adequate failures). We cross‑checked and integrated:
  - Augmentation trials for specified agents (aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, quetiapine, lithium, liothyronine, modafinil) from a comprehensive 2022 NMA with TRD‑relevant subsets.
  - Neuromodulation trials (ECT, rTMS/iTBS) from a rigorous NMA, applying TRD filters where available and conducting sensitivity analyses for stricter TRD.
  - Pivotal TRD head‑to‑heads (ketamine vs ECT; esketamine vs quetiapine XR).
  - A negative multicenter TRD RCT of minocycline.
- Discrepancies in TRD definition were resolved by including only arms meeting ≥2‑failure criteria or conducting sensitivity analyses excluding broader MDD samples. Trials with mixed unipolar/bipolar or psychotic features were excluded unless TRD‑unipolar subgroups could be isolated.

Intervention parameterization (for clinical translatability)

- IV ketamine: Most efficacious schedules used 0.5 mg/kg over ~40 minutes, 2–3 times per week for 2–4 weeks (6–8 infusions). Some trials allowed concurrent antidepressants; maintenance strategies vary and were not the focus of this acute‑phase NMA.
- Intranasal esketamine: Dosed 56–84 mg twice weekly for 4 weeks with concomitant SSRI/SNRI, then weekly; acute outcomes assessed at 4 weeks in pivotal trials.
- ECT: Right unilateral ultrabrief and bitemporal placements were used; bitemporal tends to maximize acute response at the expense of greater cognitive side effects; ultrabrief right unilateral trades some efficacy for improved tolerability.
- rTMS: High‑frequency left DLPFC (10 Hz, ~3,000 pulses/session; 5 sessions/week for 4–6 weeks) and low‑frequency right protocols both effective; bilateral protocols may modestly increase response at the cost of session time. iTBS (3‑minute sessions) is generally noninferior to standard 10‑Hz protocols; accelerated schedules are promising but need TRD‑specific RCT confirmation.

Network diagnostics recap

- Transitivity: Generally acceptable within pharmacologic nodes and within device nodes; potential strain across nodes with different blinding and concomitant medication requirements was acknowledged and explored in sensitivity analyses.
- Inconsistency: No global inconsistency signal across the primary network in source NMAs; loop‑specific differences were small and mostly explained by protocol heterogeneity. The ketamine versus ECT head‑to‑head RCT supported the top‑tier parity inferred from indirect comparisons.
- Heterogeneity: Modest within augmentation nodes; higher within device and ketamine nodes due to protocol variability. Treatment‑specific prediction intervals from source NMAs suggest that ketamine and ECT maintain superiority over placebo/sham across plausible settings, while rTMS effects vary more by protocol and patient selection.

Guidance for stepped‑care implementation in adult TRD (≥2 failures)

- When rapid and robust response is prioritized (suicidality, severe functional impairment, multiple prior failures):
  - Offer ECT or IV ketamine as first interventional steps, with shared decision‑making around cognitive side effects (ECT) versus dissociation and monitoring (ketamine).
- For patients preferring pharmacologic strategies or without access to interventional services:
  - Consider aripiprazole augmentation first, then brexpiprazole, quetiapine, lithium, or liothyronine based on comorbidities and tolerability. Reassess at 4–8 weeks and move to interventional options if nonresponse persists.
- For patients prioritizing nonpharmacologic approaches or with medical contraindications to anesthesia/ketamine:
  - Offer rTMS (10‑Hz left DLPFC or iTBS) with expectation of moderate response and high acceptability; consider protocol optimization (targeting, dosing) and escalate to ECT/ketamine if inadequate.
- When intranasal esketamine is available and clinic monitoring is feasible:
  - Offer esketamine plus an SSRI/SNRI, particularly after failure of one or more augmentation trials; evidence shows superiority to quetiapine XR augmentation and meaningful remission benefits.
- Avoid minocycline augmentation for acute TRD response given lack of efficacy.

Research priorities

- Direct, blinded head‑to‑head RCTs across top‑tier modalities:
  - IV ketamine versus intranasal esketamine; esketamine versus rTMS/iTBS in strict TRD.
- Standardized TRD staging and harmonized outcomes:
  - Use of Maudsley staging, consistent definition of adequate trials, and core outcome sets (response, remission, functioning, quality of life).
- Protocol optimization studies:
  - Comparative RCTs of ECT placements/pulse width in TRD; accelerated TBS and high‑dose/intensified rTMS regimens; maintenance strategies for ketamine/esketamine and ECT to sustain remission.
- Special populations and moderators:
  - Late‑life TRD, comorbid anxiety/substance use, medical comorbidities; biomarker‑guided or symptom‑profile‑guided selection.
- Long‑term safety and durability:
  - Cognitive longitudinal outcomes post‑ECT; cognitive and urinary outcomes with esketamine; ketamine misuse risk mitigation; real‑world effectiveness and relapse prevention strategies.

Strengths and limitations of this NMA

- Strengths:
  - Strict TRD inclusion (≥2 adequate failures), comprehensive inclusion of both pharmacologic and neuromodulatory modalities, incorporation of recent pivotal head‑to‑head RCTs, rigorous attention to transitivity and inconsistency, and GRADE/CINeMA‑based certainty assessments.
- Limitations:
  - Cross‑modality indirectness due to blinding and concomitant medication differences; variable protocol parameters in device and ketamine nodes; limited direct comparisons between IV ketamine and intranasal esketamine; some rTMS/iTBS evidence derived from broader MDD populations mitigated through sensitivity analyses.

Conclusions and implications

- In adult nonpsychotic TRD with at least two prior adequate antidepressant failures, ECT and IV ketamine yield the highest acute response and remission probabilities, with a large multicenter RCT demonstrating noninferiority of IV ketamine to ECT for response.
- Intranasal esketamine, delivered with an oral antidepressant and appropriate monitoring, is a high‑efficacy option superior to quetiapine XR augmentation in head‑to‑head testing.
- Among pharmacologic augmentations, aripiprazole has the most consistent and favorable risk‑benefit profile, with additional support for brexpiprazole, quetiapine, lithium, and liothyronine. These remain important steps, especially where interventional treatments are inaccessible or declined.
- rTMS/iTBS offers moderate acute efficacy with excellent acceptability and should be part of stepped care, particularly when pharmacologic and anesthetic options are unsuitable. Optimized and accelerated protocols warrant further TRD‑focused trials.
- Minocycline add‑on lacks efficacy in TRD and should not be prioritized for acute treatment.
- Clinicians and systems should consider earlier use of higher‑efficacy interventional treatments (ketamine/esketamine, ECT) in TRD algorithms to reduce prolonged morbidity, while tailoring choices to patient preferences, safety, comorbidities, and access. Health policy should support equitable access to these modalities and the infrastructure required for safe delivery and maintenance.
