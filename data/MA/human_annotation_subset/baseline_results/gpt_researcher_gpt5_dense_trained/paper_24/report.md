- Body composition:
  - 2012 VLCD comparative cohort: T2D participants exhibited less fat mass reduction per unit BMI decrease versus non-diabetic participants, implying relatively higher lean mass loss risk with VLCD in T2D.
  - Exercise adjunct: A systematic review/meta-analysis (2025) indicates adding structured exercise to VLEDs preserves fat-free mass by approximately 0.83 kg (95% CI 0.17 to 1.49) in general overweight/obese adults, supporting incorporation into VLCD/LED programs for T2D and non-diabetic patients alike.
- Glycemic outcomes and medication deintensification:
  - 1998 VLCD program: Substantial improvements in glycemia; 14 participants with T2D discontinued insulin over 1 year, with lipid improvements.
  - Partial meal replacement in T2D (2013 RCT): Greater HbA1c improvement and weight loss versus conventional subcaloric advice at 24 weeks.
  - Additional short-term VLCD data (4 weeks) in mixed cohorts suggest improved fasting glucose and HbA1c with VLCDs.
- Acceptability and adherence:
  - T2D LED acceptability (2024): Both continuous and intermittent ~800 kcal/day LEDs were acceptable and feasible with remote support within diabetes services.

Clinical practice considerations

- Candidate selection: Adults with obesity, with or without T2D, can be offered VLCDs with expectation of similar total weight loss over 24–52 weeks when programs are well supervised.
- Medication management: Implement a deprescribing algorithm at diet initiation for T2D patients on insulin or sulfonylureas to mitigate hypoglycemia; monitor glucose frequently, especially during the first 2–4 weeks.
- Lean mass preservation:
  - Incorporate progressive resistance training (2–3 sessions/week) and daily walking/activity.
  - Ensure adequate protein intake (often 1.2–1.6 g/kg reference body weight/day, individualized for renal function).
  - Consider higher-protein formula options where appropriate.
- Micronutrient adequacy: Use nutritionally complete formula products and provide vitamin/mineral supplementation per product guidance; monitor electrolytes and renal function in T2D.
- Program delivery: Remote or hybrid support for LEDs/VLCDs is feasible and acceptable in T2D populations; schedule regular check-ins for adherence, safety, and medication review.
- Transition/maintenance: Plan structured food reintroduction and weight-maintenance strategies (e.g., partial meal replacement, intermittent total diet replacement, continued resistance training) to sustain weight loss and metabolic benefits.

Evidence gaps and priorities (expanded)

- Randomized comparative trials administering identical VLCD and LED protocols to adults with and without T2D, with:
  - Standardized medication deintensification protocols
  - Prespecified body composition outcomes (DXA), physical function, and safety endpoints
  - Longer follow-up (≥12 months) including maintenance phases
- Stratified analyses by baseline HbA1c, insulin use, beta-cell reserve, and renal function to identify moderators of response and safety.
- Direct comparisons of continuous vs intermittent LEDs in T2D vs non-diabetic cohorts, including adherence and patient-centered outcomes.
- Mechanistic studies on differences in substrate utilization and proteolysis under severe energy restriction in T2D to optimize protein/exercise prescriptions.
- Standardized adverse event reporting (e.g., gallstone incidence, symptomatic hypoglycemia, orthostatic symptoms) across VLCD/LED trials.

Final conclusion

Across limited and predominantly nonrandomized comparative evidence, VLCDs (<800 kcal/day) produce broadly similar weight-loss magnitudes in adults with obesity with and without T2D over 24–52 weeks. In the single analyzable 24-week cohort, T2D participants lost a nonsignificantly smaller amount of weight (MD +0.90 kg; 95% CI -2.57 to +4.37), indicating no meaningful attenuation of total weight loss attributable to diabetes status. For LEDs (≥800 kcal/day) using liquid-formula approaches, direct between-status comparisons were not identified; however, T2D-only and general-population trials consistently demonstrate clinically important weight loss and metabolic improvements, suggesting effectiveness in both groups though comparative magnitude remains uncertain. Signals from body composition data indicate that, during aggressive energy restriction, people with T2D may lose a higher proportion of lean mass per unit of body weight lost than non-diabetic counterparts, reinforcing the value of progressive resistance training and adequate protein intake within VLCD/LED programs. Proactive medication deintensification in T2D is essential for safety. Given the very low certainty of the comparative evidence, high-quality randomized trials directly comparing VLCD and LED responses between T2D and non-diabetic adults are needed to refine clinical guidance, optimize lean mass preservation, and inform long-term maintenance strategies.
