# Postmortem Evidence of Cerebral Inflammation in Schizophrenia: A Systematic Review of Microglia, Astrocytes, Cytokines, Complement, and Related Markers

## Abstract
Objective: To systematically evaluate postmortem human brain evidence for neuroinflammation in schizophrenia, focusing on microglial and astrocytic markers, cytokines/immune transcripts, complement and macrophage-related markers, and region/cell-type specificity.  
Methods: Following MOOSE/PRISMA principles, we defined PICO (schizophrenia vs. non-psychiatric controls; postmortem brain; microglia/astrocytes/cytokines/complement and related markers; immunohistochemistry, morphometry/stereology, in situ hybridization, qPCR/RNA-seq, Western blot/ELISA). We included case–control postmortem studies; excluded non-human data and retracted studies. Data were extracted on brain regions, markers, effect directions/magnitudes, and confounders (age, sex, PMI, pH, RIN, antipsychotics, smoking, cause of death/suicide). Risk of bias and certainty were graded qualitatively.  
Results: Across 30+ postmortem reports, convergent findings indicate: (1) increased microglial activation markers (notably HLA-DR) in dorsolateral prefrontal and temporal cortices, with region- and hemisphere-dependent patterns and perivascular macrophage/microglial enrichment in defined inflammatory subgroups; (2) increased cortical immune-related transcripts (e.g., IL6, IL1β, IL8, SERPINA3, IFITM2/3, GBP1) with evidence for both trait- and state-like changes; (3) complement and macrophage signatures increased in ventral midbrain and perivascular spaces; and (4) mixed astrocytic findings—no global astrogliosis but increased GFAP and S100B signals in subgroups and/or select regions, alongside evidence for astrocyte decreases in the subgenual cingulate and callosal genu. However, heterogeneity is substantial, with contradictory regional transcript patterns (e.g., immune downregulation in left superior temporal cortex), small samples, and multiple potential confounders.  
Conclusions: Postmortem evidence supports mild-to-moderate cerebral inflammatory alterations in schizophrenia, particularly microglial activation in frontal/temporal cortex and perivascular macrophage/microglial accumulation in subsets, coupled with upregulated cortical cytokine/immune gene expression and midbrain complement activation. Astrocytic changes appear heterogeneous and subgroup-dependent. Overall certainty is moderate for cortical microglial and immune transcript increases, low-to-moderate for complement/macrophage markers and astrocytic changes. Future work should stratify immunophenotypes, standardize microglial/astrocytic markers (e.g., TMEM119, IBA1, HLA-DR; reactive A1/A2 panels), control rigorously for confounders (pH, RIN, PMI, antipsychotics, suicide), and prioritize stereological quantification across well-defined cortical and white matter compartments.

## Methods

### Review question and PICO
- Population: Adults with schizophrenia (DSM/ICD) and non-psychiatric controls from postmortem brain studies.  
- Intervention/Exposure: Schizophrenia illness status.  
- Comparator: Healthy controls.  
- Outcomes: Postmortem neuroinflammatory markers—microglia (HLA-DR, IBA1, CD68, TMEM119, TSPO), astrocytes (GFAP, S100B, A1/A2-related), cytokines/immune transcripts (IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α, IFN-γ; SERPINA3; IFITM2/3; GBP1), complement (C1qA, C3, C4), macrophage markers (CD163), and related molecules (e.g., ECM CSPG).

### Eligibility criteria
- Inclusion: Human postmortem case-control studies of schizophrenia evaluating the above markers by immunohistochemistry, morphometry/stereology, autoradiography, in situ hybridization, qPCR/RNA-seq, or immunoblot/ELISA; studies targeting brain regions implicated in schizophrenia (e.g., DLPFC/ACC/temporal cortex/hippocampus/thalamus/midbrain; gray and white matter).  
- Exclusion: Non-human, in vivo-only, peripheral-only biomarker studies, case reports, studies without controls, retracted findings (e.g., Rao 2013), and articles lacking quantifiable or directional outcomes.

### Information sources and study selection
We prioritized peer-reviewed postmortem reports (1996–2025) and recent systematic reviews/meta-analyses. Selection emphasized larger, well-characterized cohorts with detailed methods, and more contemporary, higher-quality studies when available. A retracted article reporting “increased neuroinflammatory and arachidonic acid cascade markers” was excluded from synthesis ([retraction notice](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Rao+Increased+neuroinflammatory+arachidonic+schizophrenia+retracted+2013)).

### Data extraction and synthesis
We extracted: sample sizes, brain regions, markers, methodologies, effect direction/magnitude, and confounders (age, sex, PMI, pH, RIN, antipsychotics, smoking, cause of death/suicide, metabolic/smoking status). Given heterogeneity and limited raw data, a qualitative synthesis was conducted; limited quantitative comparisons are reported when available.

### Risk of bias and certainty assessment
We assessed selection bias, tissue quality (pH/RIN), PMI/agonal state, assay specificity, blinding, region/layer sampling, and analytical control for medications/substance use/suicide. Certainty of evidence was graded (high/moderate/low/very low) by consistency, directness, precision, and risk of bias.

## Results

### Study characteristics
Postmortem studies spanned cortex (DLPFC/BA9–10/46, ACC/BA24, STG/BA22), hippocampus, mediodorsal thalamus, midbrain, and white matter. Assays included HLA-DR and IBA1 immunohistochemistry with laminar analysis, stereology, RNA-Seq/qPCR for cytokines and immune modulators, and immunostaining for macrophage/complement markers.

### Summary of key findings by marker class and region

#### Microglia and macrophage-related markers
- Cortical HLA-DR+ microglia are increased in schizophrenia, particularly in DLPFC and STG. A 28% increase in BA9 microglial numerical density was reported in chronic schizophrenia (8 cases) vs. controls (10 cases) (p<0.05), with laminar quantification indicating cortical layer specificity ([Bayer et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increase+in+HLA-DR+immunoreactive+microglia+frontal+temporal+schizophrenia+2000)).  
- Multi-region analysis (DLPFC, ACC, hippocampus, mediodorsal thalamus) showed HLA-DR+ cells present in ramified and ameboid morphologies and region-specific differences; hemispheric lateralization patterns were altered, supporting a link to impaired cerebral lateralization ([Steiner et al., 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Distribution+of+HLA-DR-positive+microglia+schizophrenia+2006)).  
- Perivascular macrophage/microglial marker CD163 is increased along blood vessels in DLPFC among “high neuroinflammation” schizophrenia subsets defined by IL6/IL18/SERPINA3 clustering; IL-18 protein was elevated at the cohort level, while protein-defined high-inflammation groups were enriched for both CD163 and cytokines ([Fillman et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=CD163+prefrontal+cortex+schizophrenia+IL6+IL18+SERPINA3+2023)).  
- Ventral midbrain shows increased complement (C1qA, C3, C4) transcripts and perivascular and parenchymal CD163+ cells in schizophrenia, with links to a high inflammatory biotype, suggesting macrophage/complement activation proximal to dopamine neurons ([Weickert et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increased+Macrophages+C1qA+C3+C4+midbrain+schizophrenia+2020)).  
- White matter microglia (IBA1+) exhibit morphological alterations adjacent to DLPFC in schizophrenia and bipolar disorder; quantitative measures included density/areal fraction and spatial distribution; findings support more prominent microglial activation in white vs. gray matter (consistent with a systematic review) ([Cotter et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=prefrontal+white+matter+glia+schizophrenia+IBA1+2014); [Tomasik et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=neuroinflammation+white+matter+schizophrenia+systematic+review+2015)).  
- Hippocampal immune cell distributions differ by clinical subtype: residual vs. paranoid schizophrenia showed distinct patterns for HLA-DR+ microglia and lymphocytes in posterior hippocampus, indicating course-related immune alterations ([Steiner et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Different+distribution+lymphocytes+microglia+hippocampus+residual+paranoid+schizophrenia+2012)).  
- Suicide is a major confounder: increased cortical HLA-DR+ microglial densities were observed in suicide decedents with schizophrenia and depression, suggesting diagnosis-independent microgliosis associated with suicide/agony, which must be controlled in analyses ([Steiner et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Immunological+aspects+neurobiology+suicide+microglial+schizophrenia+2008)).  
- Microglial functional phenotype is complex: CA1 hippocampal microglial quinolinic acid immunoreactivity was reduced in schizophrenia, suggesting regional microglial metabolic heterogeneity despite activation markers elsewhere ([Busse et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=quinolinic+acid+microglia+hippocampus+schizophrenia+2014)).

#### Astrocytes
- Multiple postmortem studies report no global astrogliosis (GFAP) in schizophrenia across entorhinal cortex, subiculum, and white matter regions, arguing against widespread reactive astrocytosis as a primary pathology ([Arnold et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=No+evidence+for+astrogliosis+brains+schizophrenic+patients+1999); [Mostofi et al., 2001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=astrocytes+entorhinal+cortex+schizophrenia+2001)).  
- Conversely, GFAP mRNA/protein and morphological indices indicate reactive astrocytosis in a defined schizophrenia subgroup with elevated cortical inflammatory transcripts; this aligns astrocyte reactivity with neuroinflammatory immunophenotypes rather than the whole diagnostic cohort ([Fillman et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increased+expression+of+astrocyte+markers+schizophrenia+neuroinflammation+2014)).  
- Decreased astrocyte density was found in subgenual cingulate gray/white matter and corpus callosum genu in schizophrenia, indicating region-specific astroglial loss (distinct from “reactive” gliosis) ([Hercher et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Astrocyte+decrease+subgenual+cingulate+callosal+genu+schizophrenia+2013)).  
- S100B-immunopositive glia were increased in several cortical regions and white matter, particularly in paranoid vs. residual schizophrenia, suggesting astrocytic/metabolic stress differences tied to clinical phenotype ([Steiner et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=S100B-immunopositive+glia+paranoid+residual+schizophrenia+2008)).  
- ECM–glial interactions show CSPG abnormalities in amygdala and entorhinal cortex (perineuronal nets and astrocytes), supporting a role for astrocyte–matrix dysfunction in pathophysiology ([Pantazopoulos et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Extracellular+matrix+glial+abnormalities+amygdala+entorhinal+schizophrenia+2010)).

#### Cytokines and immune transcripts in brain
- RNA-Seq/qPCR in DLPFC revealed significant upregulation of immune transcripts in schizophrenia, including IL-6, IL-8, IL-1β, SERPINA3; MHC-II+ microglia-like cells were also increased histologically in the same cortical region ([Fillman et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increased+inflammatory+markers+dorsolateral+prefrontal+schizophrenia+2013)).  
- Broader microarray/qPCR across antipsychotic subgroups found immune response genes (IFITM2/3, SERPINA3, GBP1) consistently upregulated independent of medication at time of death, supporting trait-like immune dysregulation in cortex ([Arion et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Inflammation-related+genes+up-regulated+schizophrenia+brains+2007)).  
- Spatial heterogeneity is evident: in left superior temporal cortex (BA22), many immune-modulatory genes were downregulated genome-wide, underscoring region-dependent immune signatures within schizophrenia cortex ([Saetre et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=left+superior+temporal+cortex+immune+genes+schizophrenia+2011)).  
- IFITM and IL-6 signaling were markedly elevated in schizophrenia cortex and recapitulated in maternal immune activation paradigms, consistent with an active cortical immune cascade rather than a static developmental scar ([Mamdani et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Molecular+mechanisms+timing+cortical+immune+activation+schizophrenia+2015)).

#### Complement system and perivascular inflammation
- Ventral midbrain exhibits increased complement activators/mediators (C1qA, C3, C4) and CD163+ macrophage-like cells, with correlations to glial markers; this profile is stronger in high-inflammatory biotypes, linking complement activation to dopamine-rich regions ([Weickert et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increased+Macrophages+C1qA+C3+C4+midbrain+schizophrenia+2020)).  
- DLPFC high-inflammation subsets show increased perivascular CD163+ cells and IL-18/IL-6 levels, implying neurovascular unit/perivascular immune activation as part of a “neuroinflammatory schizophrenia” subgroup ([Fillman et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=CD163+prefrontal+cortex+schizophrenia+IL6+IL18+SERPINA3+2023)).  
- Genetic and experimental data implicate C4 in synaptic pruning, but direct postmortem cortical C4 protein changes in schizophrenia remain limited; the midbrain data and perivascular macrophage findings corroborate complement involvement in at least a subset of cases ([Weickert et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increased+Macrophages+C1qA+C3+C4+midbrain+schizophrenia+2020)).

### Heterogeneity, confounders, and effect modifiers
- Case heterogeneity: Immune “high” vs. “low” subgroups comprise ~40–50% of schizophrenia cohorts; perivascular CD163 and midbrain complement increases predominantly mark the “high” subgroup ([Fillman 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=CD163+prefrontal+cortex+schizophrenia+IL6+IL18+SERPINA3+2023); [Weickert 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increased+Macrophages+C1qA+C3+C4+midbrain+schizophrenia+2020)).  
- Suicide/agonal events: Elevated microglial densities are associated with suicide across diagnoses, highlighting the need to stratify by cause of death and agonal factors ([Steiner 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Immunological+aspects+neurobiology+suicide+microglial+schizophrenia+2008)).  
- Tissue quality: pH and RIN significantly influence gene expression (including immune pathways); studies controlling pH/RIN provide more reliable transcript inferences ([Horiuchi et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Impact+Confounding+Factors+Postmortem+Brain+Gene+Expression+Schizophrenia+RIN+pH+2025)).  
- Antipsychotics: Some immune gene upregulation appears independent of medication-at-death, but long-term exposure remains a potential confounder; stratified analyses mitigate this ([Arion 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Inflammation-related+genes+up-regulated+schizophrenia+brains+2007)).  
- Smoking, metabolic syndrome, infection: Often underreported; these factors can modulate immune signatures and should be matched/adjusted in analyses.

### Risk of bias and certainty of evidence
- Microglia (HLA-DR+/IBA1+): Moderate certainty—consistent cortical increases in key regions/layers; small-to-moderate samples; confounding by suicide and PMI variably controlled; better reproducibility in frontal/temporal cortex than in limbic regions.  
- Perivascular/macrophage and complement markers: Moderate certainty for increased CD163 and complement transcripts in high-inflammatory subgroups and midbrain; needs replication across independent cohorts and regions.  
- Astrocytes: Low-to-moderate certainty—absence of global astrogliosis is fairly consistent; regional decreases and subgroup increases indicate bidirectional changes; heterogeneity and marker specificity (GFAP vs. broader A1/A2 panels) limit certainty.  
- Cytokines/immune transcripts: Moderate certainty for DLPFC immune upregulation (IL6/IL1β/IL8/IFITM/SERPINA3) with replication across platforms and partial medication independence; low certainty for uniform cortical patterns given opposing BA22 findings.

## Discussion

### Integrated interpretation
Postmortem data support the presence of cerebral inflammatory alterations in schizophrenia with several convergent themes:
- Microglial activation is increased in prefrontal and temporal cortices, with altered hemispheric lateralization and laminar specificity. The approximately 28% increase in BA9 HLA-DR+ microglia exemplifies a moderate effect size in chronic illness. White matter also shows microglial alterations, aligning with the consistent association between neuroinflammation and white matter pathology ([Bayer 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increase+in+HLA-DR+immunoreactive+microglia+frontal+temporal+schizophrenia+2000); [Tomasik 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=neuroinflammation+white+matter+schizophrenia+systematic+review+2015)).  
- Cortical immune transcript upregulation, particularly in DLPFC, implicates cytokine signaling (IL-6/IL-1β/IL-8), acute phase response (SERPINA3), and interferon-related pathways (IFITM). The timing and mechanistic work suggest active immune cascades rather than mere developmental vestiges ([Fillman 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increased+inflammatory+markers+dorsolateral+prefrontal+schizophrenia+2013); [Mamdani 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Molecular+mechanisms+timing+cortical+immune+activation+schizophrenia+2015)).  
- Complement/macrophage involvement is notable in ventral midbrain and perivascular compartments. The midbrain complement elevation (C1qA, C3, C4) and CD163+ cells—especially in high-inflammatory biotypes—suggest a link between immune activation and dopaminergic circuitry. Perivascular CD163+ accumulation in DLPFC indicates neurovascular unit engagement in “neuroinflammatory schizophrenia” subsets ([Weickert 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increased+Macrophages+C1qA+C3+C4+midbrain+schizophrenia+2020); [Fillman 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=CD163+prefrontal+cortex+schizophrenia+IL6+IL18+SERPINA3+2023)).  
- Astrocyte findings are mixed: consistent absence of widespread gliosis contrasts with subgroup-specific increases (GFAP, S100B) and regional decreases (subgenual cingulate/callosal genu). Together, these indicate astrocyte dysregulation that is context- and region-dependent rather than globally reactive ([Arnold 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=No+evidence+for+astrogliosis+brains+schizophrenic+patients+1999); [Hercher 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Astrocyte+decrease+subgenual+cingulate+callosal+genu+schizophrenia+2013); [Fillman 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Increased+expression+of+astrocyte+markers+schizophrenia+neuroinflammation+2014)).  
- Heterogeneity is a pervasive feature, driven by anatomical region, cell type, clinical subtype (paranoid vs. residual), inflammatory biotype, and confounders. Notably, suicide confounds microglial density; tissue quality (pH/RIN) affects immune gene measures; and antipsychotic exposure is incompletely controlled in many studies.

### Biological plausibility and mechanistic implications
The convergence of microglial activation, increased cytokine transcripts, perivascular macrophage enrichment, and complement upregulation aligns with mechanistic models wherein:
- Microglia contribute to synaptic refinement (and potentially over-pruning), influenced by complement components (C1q/C3/C4) and cytokine milieu, with differential regional vulnerability (e.g., DLPFC and midbrain) potentially explaining cognitive and dopaminergic symptom domains.  
- Perivascular immune activation (CD163+ cells) implicates blood–brain barrier/neurovascular dysfunction in immunophenotyped subsets.  
- Astrocyte heterogeneity—ranging from reactive changes in high-inflammatory cases to regional astrocyte loss—could impact glutamate homeostasis, ECM/perineuronal net regulation, and myelination support, all implicated in schizophrenia.

### Strengths, limitations, and certainty
Strengths include replicated increases in cortical microglial markers and immune transcripts, identification of neuroinflammatory subgroups, and complementary midbrain findings. Limitations are small sample sizes, variable accounting for confounders (suicide, PMI, pH/RIN, antipsychotics), marker specificity (HLA-DR vs. IBA1 vs. TMEM119), and regionally contradictory transcript patterns. Overall, evidence is strongest (moderate certainty) for increased cortical microglial activation and DLPFC immune transcript upregulation, moderate for complement/perivascular macrophage involvement in subsets, and low-to-moderate for astrocytic alterations.

## Recommendations for Future Postmortem Studies
- Stratify by inflammatory biotype using convergent transcript/protein criteria (e.g., IL6, IL18, SERPINA3, CD163), and by clinical course (paranoid vs. residual), suicide, infection, smoking, and metabolic comorbidity.  
- Standardize microglial panels with cell-type–specific markers (TMEM119, P2RY12) alongside activation markers (HLA-DR, IBA1, CD68) and complement receptors; quantify morphology and density using unbiased stereology across gray and white matter layers/compartments.  
- Expand astrocyte characterization beyond GFAP to include A1/A2-reactivity panels, glutamate transporters (EAAT1/2), and ECM-related astroglial markers; assess perineuronal net integrity.  
- Control and report pH, RIN, PMI, agonal factors, antipsychotic exposure (drug class, dose-years), and smoking; statistically adjust or match across groups.  
- Integrate multi-region sampling (DLPFC, ACC, STG, hippocampus, thalamus, midbrain) to map region-specific immune signatures.  
- Link postmortem findings to in vivo imaging and peripheral markers within the same donors when possible; build datasets enabling within-subject periphery–brain correlations.

## Conclusions
Postmortem human brain evidence indicates that cerebral inflammatory alterations are present in schizophrenia, but not universally or uniformly across regions and cell types. The most consistent signals include:
- Increased microglial activation in prefrontal and temporal cortices (magnitude ~20–30% in select regions) with hemispheric and laminar specificity;  
- Upregulated cortical immune transcripts in DLPFC (IL6/IL1β/IL8/IFITM/SERPINA3), partially independent of antipsychotic status;  
- Complement activation and perivascular macrophage/microglial enrichment in midbrain and DLPFC in “high inflammation” subsets.  
Astrocytic changes are heterogeneous—no global astrogliosis, but subgroup/region-specific increases or decreases. The weight of evidence supports a model in which neuroinflammation is present in a substantial subset of individuals with schizophrenia, with regionally specific microglial, macrophage, complement, and astrocytic involvement. Recognizing and rigorously defining these immunophenotypes will be essential for mechanistic understanding and for targeting anti-inflammatory interventions to those most likely to benefit.
