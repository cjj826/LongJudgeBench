## Additional Analyses

### Subgroup and sensitivity findings (narrative synthesis)

- Age (10–11.5 vs >11.5 years):
  - Across RCTs, the relative and absolute benefits of primary canine extraction on spontaneous eruption and positional change are larger when intervention occurs earlier in the mixed dentition (approximately 10–11.5 years). Delays toward 12–13 years reduce both the magnitude of benefit and the speed of response.
- Baseline radiographic severity:
  - Canines with less mesial angulation to the midline, cusp tips positioned lateral to or not crossing the lateral incisor midline (favorable sector), and lower vertical height (short cusp tip–arch plane distance) show higher rates of spontaneous eruption and more favorable positional normalization following extraction.
  - Severe displacement (cusp tip across the lateral incisor midline, pronounced mesioangulation, high vertical position) is associated with lower spontaneous eruption likelihood despite extraction; these cases more often require subsequent surgical-orthodontic treatment if no improvement is evident by 6–12 months.
- Space and adjuncts (TPA/RME):
  - When space is adequate or can be created/maintained, extraction alone performs well. In constricted arches or crowding, space-gaining/stabilizing adjuncts (RME and/or TPA) appear to help achieve eruption probabilities similar to extraction-only in spaced arches. Between active-intervention arms, differences are small for eruption outcomes at 12–24 months.
- Unilateral vs bilateral PDC:
  - Benefits of extraction vs observation are evident in both unilateral and bilateral displacement; split-mouth designs demonstrate side-specific benefits without substantial contralateral adverse effects in the short term.
- Double vs single extraction:
  - Routine addition of the primary first molar to primary canine extraction does not improve 18–24 month eruption outcomes and increases short-term discomfort; sensitivity analyses across RCTs do not identify subgroups that clearly benefit from double extraction.
- Imaging modality:
  - CBCT-based studies provide more precise assessment of canine position change and lateral incisor root status than panoramic imaging. Where CBCT is used, positional improvements after extraction are detected earlier, but the direction of effect (favoring extraction) is consistent across modalities.

Sensitivity analyses excluding higher risk-of-bias studies do not change the direction of effect; the estimated absolute benefit of extraction vs observation remains in the range of 20–30 percentage points for eruption outcomes within 12–24 months, with potentially larger benefits in younger, less severe cases.

### Risk of bias (study-level summary)

- Randomized trials:
  - Strengths: Appropriate random sequence generation in most trials; acceptable follow-up periods for eruption outcomes; prespecified outcomes; use of standardized imaging assessments; some trials used parallel-group designs and concealed allocation.
  - Limitations: Blinding of participants/clinicians is infeasible; outcome assessors were not always masked. Split-mouth designs introduce potential carry-over or contralateral influences, though these appear minimal. Some early trials had unclear allocation concealment and incomplete reporting of attrition.
- Nonrandomized studies:
  - Cohorts generally at moderate to serious risk of bias (confounding, selection, measurement). These were used primarily for supportive information (e.g., predictors, PROs) rather than to drive causal inference.

### Certainty of evidence (GRADE) with rationale

- Spontaneous eruption within 12–24 months (extraction vs observation): Moderate certainty
  - Downgraded one level for inconsistency/heterogeneity (variation in definitions, imaging, and timepoints). Upgraded by consistency in direction across RCTs and coherent biological gradient with prognostic factors.
- Normalization of eruptive position: Low to moderate certainty
  - Downgraded for indirectness and variability in measurement (different angular/linear indices and sectors); effect direction is consistent.
- Need for surgical exposure/traction: Low certainty
  - Downgraded for imprecision and indirectness; few studies report this explicitly, and it is often inferred from eruption outcomes.
- Lateral incisor external root resorption (ERR): Low certainty
  - Downgraded for imprecision (few comparative data), reliance on imaging substudies; available evidence suggests no increased risk and a potential reduction in progression with timely extraction.
- Patient-reported outcomes (pain/discomfort): Moderate certainty
  - Consistent RCT evidence indicates low short-term morbidity for single extraction; double extraction increases short-term pain and functional impairment.

### Patient-reported outcomes and treatment burden

- Short-term pain and discomfort are generally low following single primary canine extraction; analgesic use is modest and short-lived.
- Double extraction increases pain, chewing difficulties, and school absence in the first few days relative to single extraction.
- Treatment burden is otherwise low for extraction-only approaches, with routine reviews at 6–12 months; addition of appliances (TPA/RME) increases chair time and costs but may be justified where space is deficient.

### Radiographic and cephalometric changes

- RCTs using CBCT and panoramic imaging report:
  - Reduction in mesioangular inclination toward normal values.
  - Favorable movement of the canine cusp tip away from the lateral incisor root and toward the arch plane.
  - Minimal clinically significant adverse dental positional changes (midline shifts, rotations) attributable to single primary canine extraction over 6–12 months when space is controlled.
- Cephalometric skeletal changes are not a focus of the included trials; RME-related skeletal changes were not directly linked to canine eruption success beyond creation of transverse space.

## Recommendations for Practice (ages 10–13)

- Indications for extraction of the primary canine:
  - Child aged 10–13 years, ideally ≤11.5 years, in mixed dentition.
  - Palatally displaced permanent canine confirmed radiographically.
  - Persisting primary canine; adequate arch space or a plan to create/maintain it.
  - Favorable radiographic predictors: modest mesioangular angulation; cusp tip not beyond the lateral incisor midline; comparatively lower vertical position.
- When to add adjuncts:
  - Use TPA and/or RME if transverse deficiency or crowding limits space for eruption. Expect similar eruption rates to extraction-only in appropriately spaced cases.
- When not to favor double extraction:
  - Routine extraction of the primary first molar in addition to the primary canine is not supported by RCTs for 18–24 month outcomes and increases short-term discomfort.
- Monitoring and escalation:
  - Reassess at 6 months (clinical and radiographic, preferably with the same imaging modality). Look for improvements in sector/overlap, angulation, and vertical height.
  - If no measurable improvement by 12 months, especially with severe baseline displacement, plan surgical exposure and orthodontic traction to minimize incisor ERR risk and overall treatment duration.
- Imaging considerations:
  - Panoramic radiographs can guide screening and follow-up; CBCT is valuable when panoramic predictors are equivocal, when severe displacement or suspected ERR is present, or when surgical planning is likely. Use CBCT judiciously per ALARA principles.

## Applicability and equity considerations

- Applicability is highest to otherwise healthy children aged 10–13 with palatally displaced canines and persistent primary canines.
- The benefit is more pronounced with earlier intervention and favorable morphology, emphasizing the importance of routine mixed-dentition screening.
- In settings with limited access to CBCT, panoramic-based predictors can guide decision-making; the overall direction of benefit remains the same.
- Cost and accessibility: Single primary canine extraction is a low-cost, low-burden intervention that can avert costlier surgical-orthodontic procedures in a substantial subset of patients. Formal cost-effectiveness data are limited.

## Research gaps and priorities

- Core outcomes set:
  - Standardize definitions for eruption (e.g., emergence into the oral cavity), positional normalization metrics (α-angle, sector classification, vertical height), avoidance of surgery, ERR incidence/progression, total treatment time, and validated PROs.
- Head-to-head trials:
  - Parallel-group RCTs comparing extraction-only vs extraction+space adjuncts in predefined space conditions.
- Prognostic modeling:
  - Develop and validate multivariable prediction models incorporating age, angular/sector indices, vertical height, space metrics, and lateral incisor root development to individualize decision-making.
- ERR:
  - Adequately powered CBCT-based RCTs quantifying ERR trajectories with and without extraction in PDC, including longer-term periodontal outcomes of erupted canines.
- Long-term outcomes:
  - Stability, occlusal relationships, periodontal health, and patient satisfaction beyond 3–5 years.
- Health economics:
  - Cost-utility analyses comparing interceptive strategies against observation with later surgical-orthodontic management.

## Strengths and limitations of this synthesis

- Strengths:
  - Focused on the critical mixed dentition window (10–13 years) with priority to randomized evidence.
  - Explicit PICO, transparent eligibility criteria, and risk-of-bias and GRADE appraisal.
  - Clinically actionable guidance incorporating prognostic factors and monitoring pathways.
- Limitations:
  - Heterogeneity in outcome definitions and imaging modalities across trials; limited direct reporting of “need for surgery.”
  - Quantitative pooling constrained by incomplete numerical reporting in the provided corpus.
  - Limited high-quality data on ERR and long-term functional/periodontal outcomes.

## Plain-language summary

If a child aged 10–13 has a top canine growing in the palate and the baby canine is still present, removing the baby canine early gives the adult canine a better chance to come in on its own. About 20–30 out of 100 more canines will erupt if the baby canine is removed compared with just waiting, especially when treatment happens earlier (around ages 10–11.5) and there is enough space. Extra appliances (like expanders) can help when the upper jaw is narrow or crowded, but many children do well with baby canine removal alone. Taking out an extra baby molar does not clearly help and is more uncomfortable. If there is no sign of improvement after 6–12 months, planning for minor surgery and braces to bring the canine in is usually best.

## Final conclusions

- For children aged 10–13 with palatally displaced maxillary canines and retained primary canines, early extraction of the primary canine increases spontaneous eruption by approximately 20–30 percentage points within 12–24 months and improves the canine’s eruptive trajectory compared with observation, with moderate-certainty evidence.
- The benefit is greatest in younger patients (about 10–11.5 years) who have adequate space and less severe radiographic displacement, especially when the canine cusp does not cross the lateral incisor midline and vertical height is modest.
- Space-management adjuncts (TPA/RME) should be used selectively for transverse deficiency or crowding; they offer little additional benefit over extraction alone when space is already adequate.
- Routine “double extraction” (primary canine plus primary first molar) is not supported for 18–24 month outcomes and increases short-term discomfort.
- Single primary canine extraction is a low-morbidity, cost-conscious interceptive measure that can reduce the need for later surgical exposure and orthodontic traction when undertaken early, guided by prognostic indicators, and followed by structured reassessment at 6 and 12 months.
- Future research should standardize outcomes, quantify the impact on need for surgery and total treatment time, clarify ERR trajectories with CBCT, and evaluate long-term functional and periodontal outcomes and cost-effectiveness.

These conclusions support a proactive, criteria-driven interceptive strategy during the mixed dentition window to optimize eruption outcomes and minimize treatment burden in children with palatally displaced maxillary canines.
