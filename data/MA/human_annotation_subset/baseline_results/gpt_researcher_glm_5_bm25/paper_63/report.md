# Effect of Laparoscopic Vertical Sleeve Gastrectomy on Lower Esophageal Sphincter Function and Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease: A Meta-Analytic Report

## Abstract

This report provides a comprehensive evidence-based synthesis of clinical data regarding the effects of laparoscopic vertical sleeve gastrectomy (LVSG) on lower esophageal sphincter (LES) parameters and gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) in patients with morbid obesity. Drawing upon a systematic review of recent meta-analyses and prospective studies, the investigation focuses on objective esophageal function tests (EFTs), specifically lower esophageal sphincter pressure (LESP), lower esophageal sphincter length (LESL), and DeMeester Score (DMS). The findings indicate that LVSG is significantly associated with a decrease in LESP, a reduction in LESL, and a worsening of acid exposure parameters as measured by the DeMeester Score. Despite significant weight loss, the anatomical alterations induced by the sleeve gastrectomy compromise the anti-reflux barrier, leading to increased prevalence of de novo GERD and esophageal dysmotility.

## Introduction

Laparoscopic vertical sleeve gastrectomy (LVSG) has emerged as the most commonly performed bariatric procedure worldwide due to its technical simplicity and effectiveness in achieving sustained weight loss and metabolic improvement. However, the impact of LVSG on esophageal physiology remains a subject of significant clinical concern. Postoperative gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) is increasingly recognized as a major long-term complication, potentially leading to esophagitis, Barrett's esophagus, and a reduction in quality of life.

The pathophysiology of GERD following LVSG is multifactorial, involving alterations in the anatomy of the esophagogastric junction (EGJ), the creation of a high-pressure gastric tube, and potential disruption of the angle of His. To objectively evaluate these physiological changes, esophageal function tests (EFTs), including esophageal manometry and 24-hour pH-metry, are utilized. This report synthesizes data from a recent comprehensive meta-analysis and supporting literature to evaluate the impact of LVSG on lower esophageal sphincter pressure (LESP), lower esophageal sphincter length (LESL), and acid reflux severity (DeMeester Score).

## Methodology of the Synthesis

This synthesis prioritizes data from the high-quality systematic review and meta-analysis titled "Impact of laparoscopic vertical sleeve gastrectomy (LVSG) on lower esophageal sphincter pressure (LESP), lower esophageal sphincter length (LESL) and gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) using esophageal function tests (EFTs)" (2025), which analyzed 19 studies totaling 668 patients. This source is supplemented by earlier systematic reviews and prospective cohort studies to provide a longitudinal view of the evidence. The included studies utilized high-resolution manometry (HRM) or conventional manometry to assess LESP and LESL, and 24-hour pH monitoring to calculate DeMeester Scores. Statistical significance was determined using DerSimonian and Laird random effects models to account for heterogeneity across studies.

## Impact on Lower Esophageal Sphincter Pressure (LESP)

### Meta-Analytic Findings

The aggregate evidence demonstrates a statistically significant reduction in LESP following LVSG. The comprehensive meta-analysis from 2025, encompassing 668 patients, specifically highlighted a significant decrease in post-operative LESP compared to pre-operative baseline values. This finding is corroborated by earlier systematic reviews, including a 2021 review of 14 studies (402 patients) which concluded that manometry consistently shows a decrease in LESP after laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy ([Manometric and pH-monitoring changes after laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy: a systematic review](local_corpus), 2021).

### Quantitative Data and Trends

Specific cohort studies provide granular insight into these pressure changes. A prospective study by (2010) documented a decrease in mean LESP from 14.2 ± 5.8 mmHg preoperatively to 10.4 ± 4.8 mmHg postoperatively in 20 patients (p < 0.05) ([Manometric changes of the lower esophageal sphincter after sleeve gastrectomy in obese patients](local_corpus), 2010). Similarly, a retrospective analysis of 225 patients showed a decrease in LESP from 12.26 ± 6.87 mmHg to 8.88 ± 6.28 mmHg ([Persistent and De Novo GERD After Sleeve Gastrectomy: Manometric and pH-Impedance Study Findings](local_corpus), 2023). This reduction in sphincter pressure is a critical determinant of reflux, as a hypotensive LES (< 10 mmHg) is a primary risk factor for GERD.

While some earlier studies, such as one by (2012), initially suggested that LESP might not significantly decrease or could even increase due to post-operative edema or technical variations ([Functional importance of laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy for the lower esophageal sphincter in patients with morbid obesity](local_corpus), 2012), the weight of the most recent evidence with larger sample sizes confirms a definitive trend toward sphincter hypotonia. The 2025 meta-analysis provides the most robust confirmation that LESP reduction is a consistent outcome of the procedure.

## Impact on Lower Esophageal Sphincter Length (LESL)

### Anatomical Consequences

The impact of LVSG on LES length is a subject of distinct findings in the literature, varying by surgical technique and measurement method. The 2025 meta-analysis identified a significant alteration in LESL, contributing to the impairment of the anti-reflux barrier.

However, the direction of this change has been historically debated. Some prospective studies have reported a decrease in total LES length. For instance, a study of 18 patients found a significant decrease in LES total length postoperatively (p=0.002) ([Esophageal motility after laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy](local_corpus), 2017). Conversely, other studies have noted an increase in total and abdominal LES length, potentially due to the mobilization of the EGJ or the correction of hiatal hernias during surgery ([The effect of laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy on the antireflux mechanism: can it be minimized?](local_corpus), 2013).

### Synthesis of Evidence

The 2025 meta-analysis helps resolve these discrepancies by pooling data from 668 patients. The analysis suggests that while some surgical modifications may attempt to lengthen the sphincter, the overall functional outcome often points towards a reduction in effective length or competence. The structural integrity of the LES is vital for maintaining the flap valve mechanism at the EGJ. Disruption of the angle of His during sleeve creation inevitably alters the geometry of the EGJ. The systematic review by (2021) reinforces that anatomical changes at the EGJ are persistent, and even if length measurements vary, the *functional* length—defined by the effective pressure zone—often decreases or becomes less competent relative to the increased intragastric pressure generated by the sleeve ([Manometric and pH-monitoring changes after laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy: a systematic review](local_corpus), 2021).

## Impact on Gastroesophageal Reflux: DeMeester Score and pH-metry

### Worsening Acid Exposure

The most clinically significant finding is the deterioration of acid exposure parameters following LVSG. The 2025 meta-analysis confirmed a significant increase in the DeMeester Score (DMS), an objective composite score from 24-hour pH monitoring that quantifies the severity of acid reflux.

A study of 33 patients using multichannel intraluminal impedance-pH (MII-pH) monitoring revealed significant increases in acid reflux time, acid exposure time, and reflux index postoperatively ([Investigation of the Relationship Between Laparoscopic Sleeve Gastrectomy and Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease Using 24-hour Multichannel Intraluminal Impedance With pH Testing According to Current Consensus](local_corpus), 2024). The DeMeester Score specifically showed a significant increase in multiple cohorts. For example, a study of 26 patients found a significant increase in the DeMeester index one year after surgery (p = 0.028) ([Evolution of gastroesophageal reflux after laparoscopic vertical gastrectomy. A radiographic, manometric and pH-metric study](local_corpus), 2019).

### Prevalence of GERD

The increase in DMS correlates with a marked rise in the prevalence of GERD. A systematic review and meta-analysis by (2020) analyzing 27 nonrandomized studies found that total and recumbent acid exposure times were significantly increased after sleeve gastrectomy ([Esophageal Pathophysiologic Changes and Adenocarcinoma After Bariatric Surgery: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis](local_corpus), 2020). In a comparative trial, GERD was found to be significantly more frequent in LSG patients (74%) compared to LRYGB patients (25%) one year post-surgery ([Predictive Factors of Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease in Bariatric Surgery: a Controlled Trial Comparing Sleeve Gastrectomy with Gastric Bypass](local_corpus), 2020). Furthermore, de novo GERD occurred in a substantial proportion of patients, with one study citing a 67.9% incidence in LSG patients who were GERD-free preoperatively.

## Pathophysiological Mechanisms

The deterioration in LES parameters and the rise in DeMeester Scores can be attributed to several pathophysiological mechanisms:

1.  **Disruption of the Angle of His:** The sleeve gastrectomy involves stapling near the angle of His, which serves as a crucial anti-reflux valve. Resection in this area disrupts the flap valve mechanism, facilitating retrograde flow of gastric content.
2.  **Increased Intragastric Pressure:** The creation of a narrow, tubular gastric sleeve creates a high-pressure system. Studies using high-resolution manometry have documented a significant increase in intragastric pressure (IGP) and the gastroesophageal pressure gradient (GEPG) after LVSG. This increased pressure overwhelms a weakened LES, promoting reflux ([Esophageal High-Resolution Manometry Can Unravel the Mechanisms by Which Different Bariatric Techniques Produce Different Reflux Exposures](local_corpus), 2020).
3.  **Esophageal Dysmotility:** There is evidence of a rise in esophageal dysmotility and ineffective esophageal motility (IEM) post-sleeve. One study noted a rise in dysmotility and an increase in the inverted gastroesophageal gradient from 6.06% to 48.48% (p<0.05) ([Persistent and De Novo GERD After Sleeve Gastrectomy: Manometric and pH-Impedance Study Findings](local_corpus), 2023). Impaired peristalsis reduces esophageal clearance, prolonging acid exposure time.
4.  **Sleeve Migration:** Transhiatal sleeve migration (THSM) is an aggravating factor for postoperative reflux, as the sleeve moves into the thoracic cavity, further compromising the EGJ ([Effect of Gastric Sleeve Migration on Weight Loss and Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease After Laparoscopic Sleeve Gastrectomy](local_corpus), 2025).

## Clinical Implications and Management

### Predictive Value of Preoperative Testing

Preoperative esophageal testing is vital for risk stratification. A 2023 study found that abnormal pH studies in concert with ineffective esophageal motility lead to higher rates of postoperative reflux ([Preoperative esophageal testing predicts postoperative reflux status in sleeve gastrectomy patients](local_corpus), 2023). Furthermore, a predictive nomogram study indicated that synchronous contractions, low LES resting pressure, and smoking history are correlated with post-LVSG GERD development ([A Predictive Nomogram for the Occurrence of Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease After Sleeve Gastrectomy: A Study Based on Preoperative HERM](local_corpus), 2024). Patients with preoperative hypotonic LES may be at higher risk, though some data suggests the predictive power is complex ([Hypotonic Low Esophageal Sphincter Is Not Predictive of Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease After Sleeve Gastrectomy](local_corpus), 2020).

### Surgical Modifications and Remediation

To mitigate reflux, surgical modifications are being explored. Fundoplication sleeve gastrectomy (FSG) has been proposed, combining SG with an anti-reflux procedure. However, current evidence is limited, and more data is needed to confirm its efficacy ([Efficacy and Safety of Fundoplication Sleeve Gastrectomy in Obesity and GERD: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis](local_corpus), 2025). For refractory GERD post-SG, conversion to Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (RYGB) remains the gold standard, significantly reducing DeMeester scores and symptoms ([Evaluation of Clinical Score, Esophageal Manometry and pH Monitoring before and after Conversion from Sleeve Gastrectomy to Roux-en-Y Gastric Bypass for Refractory Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease](local_corpus), 2025). Magnetic sphincter augmentation (MSA) is also emerging as a viable remedial option for de novo reflux ([Magnetic sphincter augmentation for gastroesophageal reflux after sleeve gastrectomy: a prospective study](local_corpus), 2025).

## Conclusion

Based on a comprehensive synthesis of the available evidence, including a robust meta-analysis of 668 patients, it is concluded that laparoscopic vertical sleeve gastrectomy significantly impairs lower esophageal sphincter function. The procedure leads to a consistent decrease in lower esophageal sphincter pressure (LESP) and often reduces the functional lower esophageal sphincter length (LESL). These mechanical failures of the anti-reflux barrier, combined with increased intragastric pressure and esophageal dysmotility, result in a significant increase in the DeMeester Score and the prevalence of GERD. While weight loss is achieved, the physiological trade-off involves a compromised esophagogastric junction. Objective preoperative manometry and pH testing are essential for patient selection, and patients should be counseled on the significant risk of de novo or worsening GERD following LVSG.
