# Cellular Calcium Abnormalities in Bipolar Disorder: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Introduction

Bipolar disorder (BD) is a severe and chronic psychiatric condition characterized by dynamic, pathological mood fluctuations between mania, depression, and euthymia. The pathophysiology of BD remains incompletely understood, but compelling evidence accumulated over several decades implicates abnormalities in intracellular calcium (Ca²⁺) signaling. Calcium ions serve as ubiquitous second messengers critical for neurotransmitter release, neuronal excitability, gene expression, and synaptic plasticity. The "calcium hypothesis" of bipolar disorder suggests that dysregulation of intracellular calcium homeostasis may underlie the profound mood dysregulation seen in this condition ([Bipolar disorders, 2020](Source: local_corpus)).

This systematic review and meta-analysis synthesizes evidence from numerous studies examining intracellular calcium ion concentrations ([Ca²⁺]i) in peripheral cells—primarily platelets and lymphocytes—of individuals with bipolar disorder compared to healthy controls. The analysis draws upon a comprehensive 2021 meta-analysis by Agee et al., which critically appraised decades of research, alongside numerous original studies exploring the specificity of these findings to bipolar disorder, their presence across different mood states, and the effects of pharmacological interventions ([Cellular calcium in bipolar disorder: systematic review and meta-analysis, 2021](Source: local_corpus)). The report evaluates whether cellular calcium abnormalities represent a trait marker for the disorder, a state-dependent phenomenon, or a target for therapeutic intervention.

## Elevated Basal Intracellular Calcium in Bipolar Disorder

### Meta-Analytic Evidence

The most substantial evidence for calcium dysregulation in bipolar disorder comes from the 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis by Agee et al., which provided a rigorous, critical appraisal of the existing literature. The researchers identified 2,281 records, screened 117, and found 32 eligible studies, with 21 suitable for meta-analyses involving up to 642 patients and 404 control subjects. The primary finding was unequivocal: basal free intracellular [Ca²⁺] is significantly increased in bipolar disorder in both platelets and lymphocytes ([Cellular calcium in bipolar disorder: systematic review and meta-analysis, 2021](Source: local_corpus)).

The meta-analysis calculated a robust effect size of 0.55, with an estimated elevation of approximately 29% in basal intracellular calcium levels in BD patients compared to healthy controls. This finding is observed consistently in medication-free patients, suggesting it is not an artifact of treatment. The effect is present in both manic and depressive phases of the illness. However, the data for the euthymic state remained equivocal, meaning that while a trend may exist, the evidence for significantly elevated calcium during periods of symptom remission is less clear or consistent. The study concluded that cells from individuals with bipolar disorder also demonstrate an enhanced [Ca²⁺] response to stimulation with serotonin (5-HT) or thrombin, increasing by an estimated 25% with an effect size of 0.63 ([Cellular calcium in bipolar disorder: systematic review and meta-analysis, 2021](Source: local_corpus)).

### Historical Context and Replicated Findings

The findings of the 2021 meta-analysis confirm decades of research. As early as 1991, a study found that baseline and thrombin-stimulated free intracellular calcium concentrations in blood platelets were significantly higher in untreated depressed bipolar patients than in untreated unipolar depressed patients or controls. Notably, platelet intracellular calcium concentrations in euthymic-treated bipolar patients were equivalent to control values, suggesting a state-dependent change ([Elevated platelet intracellular calcium concentration in bipolar depression, 1991](Source: local_corpus)). Similarly, a 1992 study measuring intracellular Ca²⁺ concentrations in manic and bipolar depressed patients found that patients' mean levels were higher than comparison subjects and demonstrated more interindividual variation, suggesting a "diffuse abnormality in mechanisms affecting intracellular calcium homeostasis" ([Abnormal intracellular calcium ion concentration in platelets and lymphocytes of bipolar patients, 1992](Source: local_corpus)).

A 1994 study reinforced these findings, reporting elevated free intracellular calcium ion concentrations in blood platelets and lymphocytes of patients with mania and bipolar depression. Importantly, this study demonstrated that incubation with an ultrafiltrate of plasma from patients had no effect on calcium in normal platelets, suggesting the elevation is intrinsic to the cells and not due to a circulating factor ([Intracellular calcium signalling in peripheral cells of patients with bipolar affective disorder, 1994](Source: local_corpus)). In 1999, a study using a micro fura-2 fluorometric method observed significantly elevated basal calcium concentrations in bipolar patient platelets and lymphocytes compared to controls, with platelet calcium responses to thrombin, serotonin, and thapsigargin also significantly greater ([Elevated basal and thapsigargin-stimulated intracellular calcium of platelets and lymphocytes from bipolar affective disorder patients measured by a fluorometric microassay, 1999](Source: local_corpus)).

## Calcium Signaling Across Mood States

### Mania and Bipolar Depression

The consensus of the literature indicates that elevated intracellular calcium is a robust finding during acute mood episodes. The 2021 meta-analysis found the effect present in both mania and bipolar depression ([Cellular calcium in bipolar disorder: systematic review and meta-analysis, 2021](Source: local_corpus)). This aligns with earlier work showing elevated basal and stimulated intracellular calcium in patients with mania and bipolar depression ([Intracellular calcium signalling in peripheral cells of patients with bipolar affective disorder, 1994](Source: local_corpus)).

The extent of the abnormality appears linked to illness severity. A 2025 study examining valproate's effects noted that prior to incubation, platelet basal and stimulated calcium levels were generally lower than had been reported in previous studies. The authors attributed this to their sample of patients having at most moderate symptom severity, implying that more severe mood episodes are associated with greater calcium dysregulation ([Effects of Valproate on Intracellular Calcium Ion Signaling: Implications for Mechanistic Research, 2025](Source: local_corpus)). This suggests a gradient of dysfunction proportional to the clinical state.

### The Euthymic State: Trait or State Marker?

A critical question is whether calcium abnormalities persist during euthymia, which would designate them as a trait marker (a stable characteristic of the illness) rather than a state marker (fluctuating with symptoms). Evidence is mixed. The 2021 meta-analysis found data for euthymia to be equivocal ([Cellular calcium in bipolar disorder: systematic review and meta-analysis, 2021](Source: local_corpus)). Some studies support a state-dependent model; for instance, the 1991 study found that euthymic-treated bipolar patients had platelet calcium concentrations equivalent to control values ([Elevated platelet intracellular calcium concentration in bipolar depression, 1991](Source: local_corpus)). Similarly, a 1993 review noted that increased free intracellular calcium was found in patients when they were "acutely ill, but not after recovery" ([Calcium antagonists in manic-depressive illness, 1993](Source: local_corpus)).

However, other research complicates this view. A 1994 study found no significant differences between bipolar subjects in remission and matched controls for basal calcium, but did find that intracellular calcium measures were significantly higher in the lithium-treated group after stimulation with serotonin ([Platelet intracellular calcium in patients with recurrent affective disorders, 1994](Source: local_corpus)). Furthermore, a 2008 study found hyperactive intracellular calcium dynamics in B lymphoblasts from patients with bipolar I disorder, showing a significant effect of diagnosis on basal concentrations and response rates ([Hyperactive intracellular calcium dynamics in B lymphoblasts from patients with bipolar I disorder, 2008](Source: local_corpus)). As lymphoblast cell lines are immortalized and studied ex vivo, this finding supports the persistence of a cellular vulnerability (trait marker) even in the absence of acute symptoms. A 2012 review similarly argued that abnormality of cellular calcium signaling is regarded as a "replicable trait marker" for bipolar disorder ([Cellular calcium signaling in bipolar disorder, 2012](Source: local_corpus)).

## Specificity of Calcium Abnormalities to Bipolar Disorder

### Comparison with Major Depressive Disorder (Unipolar Depression)

Differentiating bipolar depression from unipolar depression (MDD) is a major clinical challenge. Biomarkers like intracellular calcium could potentially aid diagnosis. Several studies have sought to distinguish these conditions based on calcium signaling.

A 1996 study found an enhanced intracellular Ca²⁺ rise in response to serotonin in platelets from both bipolar patients and patients with major depression compared to healthy controls, suggesting the 5-HT2A receptor-mediated signaling mechanism may have a role in affective disorders broadly ([Enhanced calcium response to serotonin in platelets from patients with affective disorders, 1996](Source: local_corpus)). However, other studies report differences. The 1999 study found significant differences between bipolar and unipolar patients in basal and thapsigargin-stimulated calcium measures ([Elevated basal and thapsigargin-stimulated intracellular calcium of platelets and lymphocytes from bipolar affective disorder patients measured by a fluorometric microassay, 1999](Source: local_corpus)). Further, the 1991 study found baseline and thrombin-stimulated concentrations were significantly higher in untreated depressed bipolar patients than in untreated unipolar depressed patients ([Elevated platelet intracellular calcium concentration in bipolar depression, 1991](Source: local_corpus)). This suggests that while abnormalities may exist in MDD, the magnitude or profile of dysfunction may be distinct in BD.

### Comparison with Schizophrenia

Establishing specificity to BD, rather than a general feature of psychosis or severe mental illness, is crucial. A key 2014 study directly addressed this question. Researchers assessed platelet intracellular calcium in 15 inpatients with psychotic and nonpsychotic mania, 17 schizophrenia inpatients, and 17 matched controls. They found that baseline and agonist-stimulated calcium were significantly higher than controls in bipolar disorder but *not* in schizophrenia. Furthermore, the variability of baseline calcium was significantly increased in bipolar disorder regardless of the presence of psychosis, but this variability was not seen in schizophrenia ([Increased platelet intracellular calcium ion concentration is specific to bipolar disorder, 2014](Source: local_corpus)). This study provides strong evidence that the calcium abnormality is specific to the mood disorder phenotype of BD rather than being a general feature of psychotic illness.

### Serotonin-Stimulated Response Specificity

Investigations into serotonin (5-HT)-induced calcium mobilization have yielded detailed findings regarding specificity. A 2001 study examined 5-HT-induced platelet calcium response in 152 unmedicated outpatients with various psychiatric disorders (including BD, MDD, schizophrenia, panic disorder, OCD, and others) and normal controls. The study observed no significant differences in basal intracellular calcium among groups. However, the 5-HT-induced calcium response was specifically enhanced in bipolar disorder and melancholic major depression, but not in other disorders like schizophrenia or panic disorder ([Serotonin-induced platelet intracellular calcium mobilization in various psychiatric disorders: is it specific to bipolar disorder?, 2001](Source: local_corpus)). This supports the idea that enhanced serotonergic signaling coupling to calcium mobilization is relatively specific to affective disorders, particularly BD.

## Molecular Mechanisms and Genetic Correlates

### Endoplasmic Reticulum and Mitochondrial Dysfunction

The mechanisms underlying elevated intracellular calcium in BD have been linked to dysfunction in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and mitochondria. A 2003 study using lymphoblastoid cell lines from BD patients and controls examined responses to agents affecting these organelles. They found that the thapsigargin-induced cytosolic calcium response (which reflects ER calcium store release) was significantly higher in BD patients. This suggests that the ER calcium store content or the release mechanism from the ER is altered in BD cells ([Mechanisms of altered Ca2+ signalling in transformed lymphoblastoid cells from patients with bipolar disorder, 2003](Source: local_corpus)).

### The Bcl-2 Gene and Calcium Homeostasis

Genetic studies have linked the Bcl-2 gene, which modulates ER calcium dynamics, to BD. A 2011 study evaluated the impact of the Bcl-2 SNP rs956572 on intracellular calcium homeostasis. In B lymphoblast cell lines from BD-I patients, the "AA" genotype of rs956572 was associated with significantly higher basal intracellular calcium concentrations compared to "GG" carriers. The findings indicate this variant influences Bcl-2 mRNA and protein levels, thereby disrupting ER calcium homeostasis ([Bcl-2 SNP rs956572 associates with disrupted intracellular calcium homeostasis in bipolar I disorder, 2011](Source: local_corpus)).

### Calcium Channel Gene Variants

Genome-wide association studies have identified calcium channel genes as risk factors for BD. A 2016 study examined whether L-type calcium channels (LTCCs) account for the altered response to lithium in BD cells. While the study found that lithium stimulated intracellular calcium entry independently of LTCCs, it confirmed the involvement of calcium signaling pathways in the disorder's pathophysiology ([Calcium channel genes associated with bipolar disorder modulate lithium's amplification of circadian rhythms, 2016](Source: local_corpus)). Furthermore, a 2023 pilot pharmacogenetic study found that manic patients carrying specific intronic variants in the CACNA1B gene (rs2739258 and rs2739260) had better treatment responses to calcium channel blockers, linking genetic variants in calcium channels directly to treatment outcomes ([A pilot pharmacogenetic study of calcium channel blocker treatment of bipolar mania, 2023](Source: local_corpus)).

## Pharmacological Modulation of Intracellular Calcium

### Lithium and Carbamazepine

Established mood stabilizers have demonstrable effects on calcium signaling. Lithium has been shown to block the metabolism of the intracellular second messenger inositol-1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP3), which is involved in the rise in intracellular calcium ([Effects of lithium on platelet ionic intracellular calcium concentration in patients with bipolar (manic-depressive) disorder and healthy controls, 1990](Source: local_corpus)). Multiple studies confirm that lithium and carbamazepine lower elevated intracellular calcium in cells from affectively ill patients but not controls, effectively normalizing the hyperactive state ([Intracellular calcium signalling in peripheral cells of patients with bipolar affective disorder, 1994](Source: local_corpus)). However, findings can be complex; one study noted that lithium added *in vitro* tended to increase the thrombin-stimulated rise in calcium, highlighting that effects may differ based on assay conditions and chronicity of treatment ([Effects of lithium on platelet ionic intracellular calcium concentration in patients with bipolar (manic-depressive) disorder and healthy controls, 1990](Source: local_corpus)).

### Valproate

Results for valproate have been less consistent. The 2025 study specifically examined this issue, hypothesizing that inconsistent findings might be due to patient characteristics. In mildly to moderately ill patients, incubation with valproate actually raised basal calcium levels, contrasting with the normalization seen with lithium. The authors concluded that studies of subjects with "milder illness and normal baseline signaling" might miss or reverse the drug's effects seen in more severe cases ([Effects of Valproate on Intracellular Calcium Ion Signaling: Implications for Mechanistic Research, 2025](Source: local_corpus)). This underscores that correction of the abnormality may be a marker of therapeutic action relevant only when a significant baseline abnormality exists ([Intracellular calcium signalling in peripheral cells of patients with bipolar affective disorder, 1994](Source: local_corpus)).

### Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

Given the hyperactive calcium signaling, CCBs have been investigated as potential treatments. Verapamil, a CCB, has been used in BD for decades. A 2002 open-label series found that verapamil was effective for acute mania (100% response) and showed some efficacy for depression and mixed states ([Verapamil treatment for women with bipolar disorder, 2002](Source: local_corpus)). However, a 2016 systematic review identified six randomized controlled trials of verapamil for acute mania, finding "no evidence that it is effective." The review concluded that while CCBs are promising theoretically, robust clinical evidence is lacking, and more research is needed ([A systematic review of calcium channel antagonists in bipolar disorder and some considerations for their future development, 2016](Source: local_corpus)). Interestingly, not all anticonvulsants with calcium antagonist properties are effective; levetiracetam, which blocks N and P/Q-type calcium channels in animals, was shown *not* to alter elevated intracellular calcium in BD patients, and clinical trials have found no evidence of mood-stabilizing efficacy ([Levetiracetam, Calcium Antagonism, and Bipolar Disorder, 2015](Source: local_corpus)).

## Peripheral Versus Central Nervous System Findings

While most studies use platelets and lymphocytes due to accessibility, the relevance to brain pathophysiology is critical. Support for the translational validity of peripheral markers comes from studies using olfactory receptor neurons (ORNs), which are neurons of central origin. A 2005 study examined ORNs from BD patients and found aberrant signaling; medication-free patients' neurons responded to stimuli predominantly with *decreases* in intracellular calcium, unlike the increases seen in healthy subjects. Neurons from treated patients were less likely to respond, suggesting medication normalization of signaling ([Aberrant intracellular calcium signaling in olfactory neurons from patients with bipolar disorder, 2005](Source: local_corpus)). Additionally, a 2014 study using induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) from BD patients differentiated into neurons found significant alterations in the transcriptional profile, specifically increased expression of transcripts for membrane-bound receptors and ion channels, further linking peripheral findings to neuronal dysfunction ([Transcripts involved in calcium signaling and telencephalic neuronal fate are altered in induced pluripotent stem cells from bipolar disorder patients, 2014](Source: local_corpus)).

## Conclusion

The systematic review of the literature, anchored by the 2021 meta-analysis, confirms that basal free intracellular calcium is significantly elevated in individuals with bipolar disorder compared to healthy controls. The effect size is moderate (0.55) and represents an estimated 29% increase. This abnormality is well-replicated in platelets and lymphocytes, present in medication-free patients, and robust during manic and depressive episodes. The evidence for its persistence in euthymia is equivocal, suggesting that while a cellular vulnerability exists (supported by lymphoblast studies), the full manifestation of hyperactive calcium signaling is often state-dependent.

Crucially, the elevation in intracellular calcium and its response to serotonin stimulation appears specific to bipolar disorder and melancholic depression, distinguishing it from schizophrenia, panic disorder, and other psychiatric conditions. The mechanisms involve dysfunction in ER and mitochondrial calcium handling, influenced by genetic variants in genes such as Bcl-2 and CACNA1C/CACNA1B. Therapeutic modulation by lithium and carbamazepine effectively normalizes these elevations, providing a potential mechanistic explanation for their mood-stabilizing properties. While calcium channel blockers represent a logical therapeutic approach, clinical evidence remains mixed. Overall, aberrant intracellular calcium signaling stands as one of the most replicated and specific biological findings in bipolar disorder, bridging genetic risk, cellular pathophysiology, and pharmacological treatment.
