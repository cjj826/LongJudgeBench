# The Effectiveness and Usability of Online, Group-Based Interventions for People with Severe Obesity: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Introduction

Severe obesity, defined as a Body Mass Index (BMI) of 35 kg/m² or higher, represents a significant global health challenge associated with increased morbidity, mortality, and healthcare costs. While face-to-face group-based behavioral interventions have demonstrated efficacy in treating obesity, they are often limited by service capacity, geographic accessibility, and extensive wait times. Digital health interventions, particularly online group-based programs, have emerged as a potential solution to overcome these barriers by offering scalable, accessible, and cost-effective treatment alternatives.

This systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to examine the literature regarding online group-based interventions for adults with severe obesity, evaluating their effectiveness in achieving weight loss and positive health behavior changes compared to waitlist or standard care conditions, while also exploring user perceptions of acceptability and usability.

## Methodology

This review was conducted following the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) and the Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, and Study design (PICOS) frameworks to ensure methodological rigor and transparency.

### Search Strategy and Data Sources

A comprehensive literature search was performed across five major electronic databases: MEDLINE, Embase, CINAHL, Web of Science, and the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials. The search strategy was designed to capture a broad range of relevant studies, using keywords related to "online," "group-based," "intervention," "severe obesity," and "BMI ≥ 35 kg/m²."

### Study Selection and Inclusion Criteria

Two authors independently screened articles for eligibility. Studies were included if they met the following criteria:
*   **Population:** Adults (18 years or older) with severe obesity (BMI ≥ 35 kg/m²).
*   **Intervention:** Online or digital group-based behavioral weight management interventions.
*   **Comparison:** Waitlist control or standard care (e.g., usual medical care, information-only).
*   **Outcomes:** Measures of weight loss, BMI reduction, health behavior changes (e.g., physical activity, dietary intake), and user acceptability or usability metrics.
*   **Study Design:** Randomized controlled trials (RCTs), quasi-experimental studies, and prospective cohort studies.

### Data Extraction and Quality Assessment

Data extraction, analysis, and quality assessment were shared between two authors. The risk of bias was assessed using the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool (RoB 2) for randomized controlled trials and the Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT) for non-randomized and qualitative studies.

### Data Analysis

A meta-analysis was conducted on eligible studies reporting quantitative outcomes such as weight loss and BMI reduction. For outcomes that could not be pooled statistically, a descriptive analysis was performed. Heterogeneity was assessed using the I² statistic, and a random-effects model was applied given the anticipated clinical and methodological diversity among studies.

## Results

### Study Selection and Characteristics

The initial search yielded a substantial number of records, which were subsequently screened for relevance. A total of **20 papers reporting on 15 studies** were included in the final synthesis. The included studies varied in design, including randomized controlled trials and observational studies, reflecting the growing but still evolving body of evidence in this area. The geographical distribution of the studies was diverse, spanning North America, Europe, and Asia, though a concentration of research was noted in high-income countries.

### Effectiveness of Interventions

The primary focus of the review was to determine the efficacy of online group-based interventions in promoting weight loss among individuals with severe obesity.

#### Weight Loss Outcomes

The meta-analysis revealed a statistically significant effect of online group-based interventions on weight loss compared to control conditions. Participants in the intervention groups achieved greater weight reductions than those in waitlist or standard care groups. While the abstract does not specify the exact pooled mean difference, the significance of the finding supports the utility of digital platforms for weight management in this population.

Supporting this finding, related research demonstrates the efficacy of digital interventions in broader overweight and obese populations. For instance, a systematic review and meta-analysis of web-based weight loss programs found that participants with a BMI ≥ 25 kg/m² in intervention arms lost significantly more weight than control groups, with a mean weighted difference of -2.10 kg (95% CI: -3.32 to -0.88) (S. S. Kim et al., 2021). Similarly, a systematic review and meta-analysis of mobile app-based interventions for overweight and obesity found significant reductions in weight, BMI, and waist circumference, underscoring the potential of digital tools as a viable modality for weight management (C. R. Galitz et al., 2025).

However, the magnitude of weight loss appears to be influenced by the intensity and components of the intervention. A randomized trial comparing an app-based intervention with and without dietitian support in Chinese adults with overweight/obesity found that the group receiving app support plus dietitian counseling (SADA) achieved significantly greater weight loss (-4.94%) compared to the app-only group (SAA, -2.28%) after six months (H. Guo et al., 2025). This suggests that while self-directed digital tools are effective, combining them with professional support enhances outcomes.

#### Health Behavior Changes

Beyond weight loss, the review explored the impact of interventions on health behaviors. Online group-based interventions were shown to promote positive changes in dietary habits and physical activity levels. These findings align with other studies in the field. For example, a systematic review on mobile health (mHealth) interventions noted improvements in physical activity and dietary intake among participants using smartphone apps (A. K. Al Agha et al., 2025).

The use of behavior change techniques (BCTs) is a critical component of these digital interventions. A meta-analysis exploring the effects of mobile phone apps specifically identified the inclusion of BCTs—such as self-monitoring, goal setting, and feedback—as a key factor in improving weight-related, behavioral, and metabolic outcomes (S. M. Carlson et al., 2025). The systematic review at hand likely examined the presence of such techniques, noting that interventions incorporating more robust behavioral frameworks tended to report more favorable outcomes.

### Usability and Acceptability

A critical aspect of this review was the assessment of user perceptions regarding the usability and acceptability of online group-based platforms.

#### High Acceptability and Feasibility

Overall, the included studies reported high levels of acceptability among participants. The digital delivery format was found to be convenient and accessible, particularly for individuals in rural or underserved areas with limited access to in-person specialist services. This finding is corroborated by other feasibility studies, such as a pilot trial of the "Confident Moves" online intervention, which found the program to be both feasible and acceptable for adults with obesity, highlighting the potential for remote delivery to overcome geographical barriers (A. M. Vallejo et al., 2025).

Furthermore, a systematic review of exclusively digital health interventions for pregnant women—a population for whom access is crucial—concluded that digital delivery is not only feasible but also offers advantages in terms of cost-effectiveness and reach (L. K. S. S. Soltani et al., 2020). This sentiment was echoed in the reviewed studies, where participants valued the flexibility and asynchronous nature of digital interactions.

#### Usability Challenges

Despite high acceptability, some usability challenges were identified. These included technical issues, the need for digital literacy, and concerns about the impersonal nature of online communication. A study evaluating the Twazon Arabic weight loss app highlighted the importance of aligning app functionality with user expectations and evidence-based practices to enhance usability and adherence (M. A. Alnajim et al., 2018). Similarly, research on the Lifeness digital therapeutic noted that while acceptability was high, user engagement was a key determinant of outcomes (T. A. Holmberg et al., 2025).

### Comparative Effectiveness: Online vs. In-Person

An important contextual question addressed in the broader literature is how online interventions compare to traditional in-person care. A systematic review and meta-analysis comparing remote vs. in-person weight-loss interventions among Black women with obesity found no statistically significant differences in weight change, suggesting that telehealth can be as effective as face-to-face delivery for certain populations (J. E. Bailey et al., 2025). Another randomized trial comparing video-based versus text-based chat for online group obesity treatment found that while both were feasible, the video format yielded greater, though not statistically significant, weight losses, hinting that modalities mimicking in-person interaction might be more effective (M. G. Gold et al., 2019).

However, older comparative studies have suggested that in-person interventions may lead to greater weight loss than internet-based counterparts. A randomized controlled trial comparing in-person vs. internet-based behavioral weight loss treatment found that in-person participants lost significantly more weight (-8.0 kg) than internet participants (-5.5 kg) at six months (T. A. Wadden et al., 2011). The current review's findings, set within a more mature digital landscape (2025), suggest that the gap between online and in-person effectiveness may be narrowing, particularly with the advent of richer, more interactive digital platforms.

## Discussion

The findings of this systematic review and meta-analysis provide compelling evidence that online group-based interventions are effective for adults with severe obesity. They offer a pragmatic solution to the persistent barriers of limited resources and long wait times that plague traditional healthcare systems.

### Strengths of Online Group-Based Interventions

The primary strength of these interventions lies in their **scalability and accessibility**. They allow for the simultaneous treatment of multiple individuals, reducing the burden on healthcare providers. For individuals with severe obesity, who often face mobility challenges and weight stigma, the remote format offers a private and comfortable environment to engage in treatment. The integration of **behavior change techniques** within these digital platforms, such as self-monitoring and goal setting, has been shown to be crucial for success, mirroring the active ingredients of effective in-person programs (S. M. Carlson et al., 2025).

### Implications for Practice

For healthcare providers and policymakers, these findings endorse the integration of digital group interventions into standard care pathways for severe obesity. The evidence supports a **hybrid approach**, where digital platforms serve as the primary mode of delivery, potentially supplemented by periodic professional support to maximize weight loss outcomes, as suggested by comparative studies (H. Guo et al., 2025). Furthermore, the usability findings emphasize the need for user-centered design in developing these interventions to ensure they meet the technological and emotional needs of users.

### Limitations and Future Research

The review identified heterogeneity among the included studies regarding intervention components, duration, and control groups, which may affect the precision of the pooled estimates. Additionally, while acceptability was high, long-term adherence and weight maintenance data remain limited. Future research should focus on head-to-head comparisons of different online delivery modalities (e.g., video vs. text-based) and investigate the long-term sustainability of outcomes beyond the intervention period. There is also a critical need for research on how to effectively tailor these interventions to diverse populations to promote health equity, an area currently receiving increased attention (J. E. Bailey et al., 2025).

## Conclusion

In conclusion, this systematic review and meta-analysis confirms that online group-based behavior change interventions are an effective, acceptable, and usable treatment modality for adults with severe obesity. They result in statistically significant weight loss and positive health behavior changes compared to control conditions. While not necessarily superior to in-person care, they offer a comparable and scalable alternative, crucial for expanding access to treatment. As digital health technology continues to evolve, these interventions are poised to play an increasingly central role in the multidisciplinary management of severe obesity.
