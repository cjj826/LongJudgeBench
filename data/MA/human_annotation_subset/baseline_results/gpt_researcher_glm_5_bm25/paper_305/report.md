# Association Between Maternal Polycystic Ovary Syndrome and Adverse Birth Outcomes in Offspring: A Systematic Review of Evidence

## Introduction

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is recognized as the most prevalent endocrine disorder among women of reproductive age, affecting approximately 5% to 13% of this population globally. Characterized by a complex triad of hyperandrogenism, ovulatory dysfunction, and polycystic ovarian morphology, the syndrome has long been associated with subfertility and metabolic disturbances. However, the implications of maternal PCOS extend beyond conception difficulties, influencing the intrauterine environment and potentially programing fetal development. A critical question in obstetrics and gynecology is whether PCOS serves as an independent risk factor for adverse birth outcomes in offspring, or if the increased risks observed are merely secondary to confounding factors frequently co-occurring with the syndrome, such as obesity and the utilization of assisted reproductive technology (ART).

This report synthesizes evidence from a comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis to investigate the association between maternal PCOS and birth outcomes. The analysis critically examines the independence of PCOS as a risk factor by scrutinizing the roles of maternal age, body mass index (BMI), gestational weight gain, and ART, providing a structured evidence-based summary for healthcare professionals.

## Maternal Characteristics and Potential Confounding Variables

Understanding the baseline characteristics of women with PCOS is paramount to disentangling the syndrome's independent effects from those of confounding variables. A recent systematic review and meta-analysis encompassing 73 studies and 92,881 offspring highlighted distinct metabolic profiles in mothers with PCOS compared to controls ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome, 2024](local_corpus)).

Specifically, women with PCOS tend to be younger at conception and exhibit a significantly higher body mass index (BMI) compared to their non-PCOS counterparts. This elevated BMI is a central feature of the syndrome's metabolic phenotype, often accompanied by insulin resistance and compensatory hyperinsulinemia. Furthermore, gestational weight gain (GWG) is frequently greater in women with PCOS. These baseline disparities necessitate rigorous adjustment in statistical models to isolate the effect of PCOS diagnosis itself.

The interplay between PCOS and obesity is particularly complex. Obesity is not merely a confounder but a potential effect modifier. A nested case-control study utilizing propensity score matching found that pre-pregnancy overweight/obesity mediates the association between PCOS and neonatal birth weight, suggesting that the metabolic sequelae of PCOS drive growth outcomes ([The impact of pre-pregnancy overweight/obesity on the birth weight of offspring born from women with polycystic ovarian syndrome, 2025](local_corpus)). Similarly, research indicates that while underweight women face risks of low birth weight (LBW) and small for gestational age (SGA), women with PCOS often present the opposite challenge, though fetal growth restriction remains a concern ([Impact of Maternal Underweight on Infant and Maternal Outcomes in Japanese Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis, 2025](local_corpus)).

## Association with Preterm Birth

Preterm birth (PTB), defined as delivery before 37 weeks of gestation, is a primary adverse outcome associated with maternal PCOS. The systematic review of 73 studies confirmed that the odds of preterm birth are significantly higher in offspring of women with PCOS ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome, 2024](local_corpus)). This finding persists even after accounting for the higher rates of multiple gestations often seen in ART pregnancies.

Mechanistically, the link between PCOS and preterm delivery is multifaceted. It may involve:
1.  **Inflammatory Pathways:** Low-grade systemic inflammation characteristic of PCOS may trigger premature uterine contractility.
2.  **Placental Dysfunction:** Abnormal placentation, potentially driven by hyperandrogenism or insulin resistance, can lead to placental insufficiency and early delivery.
3.  **Cervical Insufficiency:** A retrospective study of Chinese women undergoing ART found that PCOS was associated with a higher prevalence of cervical insufficiency (CI), with an odds ratio of 2.05. Women with PCOS and CI had earlier gestational ages at delivery (average 26 weeks) compared to those without PCOS, indicating a severe phenotypic subset ([The prevalence of cervical insufficiency in Chinese women with polycystic ovary syndrome undergone ART treatment accompanied with negative prognosis, 2021](local_corpus)).

Furthermore, elevated Anti-Müllerian Hormone (AMH) levels, a proxy for PCOS severity, have been implicated. A secondary analysis of data from the Pregnancy in Polycystic Ovary Syndrome II (PPCOS II) trial indicated that highly elevated AMH levels were associated with an increased risk of spontaneous preterm delivery in women undergoing ovulation induction ([Highly elevated level of antimüllerian hormone associated with preterm delivery in polycystic ovary syndrome patients who underwent ovulation induction, 2021](local_corpus)). This suggests that the severity of the ovarian phenotype may directly influence gestational duration.

## Fetal Growth and Birth Weight

The association between maternal PCOS and fetal growth is complex, displaying a dichotomy between macrosomia and growth restriction.

### Small for Gestational Age (SGA) and Low Birth Weight (LBW)
The evidence robustly supports an association between PCOS and fetal growth restriction. The meta-analysis by [Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome, 2024](local_corpus) reports higher odds of fetal growth restriction and LBW, as well as lower mean birthweight in the PCOS cohort. Crucially, the study concludes that lower mean birthweight and higher rates of Small for Gestational Age (SGA) are "probably independent of BMI," asserting PCOS as an independent risk factor for suboptimal fetal growth.

This finding aligns with earlier observations, such as the 2005 study which found a significantly higher prevalence of SGA infants (12.8% vs. 2.8%) born to mothers with PCOS compared to controls matched for age and weight ([Birth weight in offspring of mothers with polycystic ovarian syndrome, 2005](local_corpus)). A 2024 cohort study combining data from clinical trials and the Norwegian Mother, Father, and Child Cohort (MoBa) confirmed these findings, noting that maternal PCOS was associated with lower birthweight z-scores and increased risk of SGA ([Growth Restriction in the Offspring of Mothers With Polycystic Ovary Syndrome, 2024](local_corpus)).

### Large for Gestational Age (LGA) and Macrosomia
Conversely, some studies highlight risks of overgrowth. Preconception impaired glucose tolerance (IGT) in women with PCOS is associated with a higher risk of Large for Gestational Age (LGA) infants ([Effect of Preconception Impaired Glucose Tolerance on Pregnancy Outcomes in Women With Polycystic Ovary Syndrome, 2017](local_corpus)). This is likely mediated by maternal hyperglycemia and fetal hyperinsulinemia. However, when examining the general PCOS population without specific stratification for glucose tolerance, the consensus leans towards growth restriction or normal weight, with the SGA signal remaining the most distinct "independent" signature of the PCOS phenotype in the systematic review context ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome, 2024](local_corpus)).

## The Impact of Assisted Reproductive Technology (ART)

Women with PCOS are frequent users of Assisted Reproductive Technology (ART), including In Vitro Fertilization (IVF) and Frozen Embryo Transfer (FET). Distinguishing the effect of the underlying diagnosis from the treatment itself is challenging.

A systematic review and meta-analysis focusing specifically on ART pregnancies found that women with PCOS undergoing IVF/ICSI had increased risks of adverse pregnancy and perinatal outcomes compared to non-PCOS controls undergoing ART ([Adverse pregnancy and perinatal outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome undergoing assisted reproductive technology: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](local_corpus)). Another meta-analysis confirmed these associations, noting higher risks of gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM), preterm birth, and low birth weight in PCOS patients using ART ([Association between maternal polycystic ovarian syndrome undergoing assisted reproductive technology and pregnancy complications and neonatal outcomes: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2024](local_corpus)).

However, specific nuances exist. For instance, in frozen embryo transfer cycles, while women with PCOS had higher rates of GDM and gestational hypertension, the live birth rates can be comparable or even favorable in some contexts compared to non-PCOS infertility patients ([Pregnancy complications and birth outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome undergoing frozen embryo transfer, 2024](local_corpus)). Interestingly, the risk of hypertensive disorders of pregnancy, a significant contributor to iatrogenic preterm birth, may be mitigated by using letrozole-induced ovulatory cycles rather than artificial cycles for FET, highlighting the role of the corpus luteum in vascular adaptation ([Letrozole-induced frozen embryo transfer cycles are associated with a lower risk of hypertensive disorders of pregnancy among women with polycystic ovary syndrome, 2021](local_corpus)).

The convergence of evidence suggests that PCOS remains a risk factor in ART settings, though the absolute risks may be modified by the specific ART protocol used.

## The Role of Metabolic and Endocrine Factors

The pathophysiology linking PCOS to adverse birth outcomes involves intricate metabolic and hormonal dysregulation.

### Hyperandrogenism
Hyperandrogenemia is a hallmark of PCOS. A systematic review and meta-analysis investigating the impact of hyperandrogenemia found that mothers with hyperandrogenic PCOS phenotypes had increased odds of gestational diabetes mellitus and other adverse outcomes compared to non-hyperandrogenic phenotypes ([The impact of hyperandrogenemia on pregnancy complications and outcomes in patients with PCOS: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2024](local_corpus)). Experimental models support this; maternal testosterone exposure in ewes resulted in intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR) and inhibited cardiac myocyte proliferation, suggesting direct fetal programming effects of androgens ([Cardiac myocyte proliferation and maturation near term is inhibited by early gestation maternal testosterone exposure, 2018](local_corpus)).

### Insulin Resistance
Insulin resistance is central to PCOS pathology. A retrospective cohort study demonstrated that insulin resistance (IR) in PCOS patients undergoing IVF/ICSI was associated with higher BMI and longer infertility duration ([Insulin resistance as a determinant of fertilization efficiency in polycystic ovary syndrome patients undergoing IVF/ICSI: a retrospective cohort study, 2025](local_corpus)). While IR primarily impacts conception and miscarriage rates, its downstream effects on maternal weight and glucose homeostasis indirectly influence fetal growth and timing of delivery. Notably, a prospective cohort study found that while maternal PCOS is associated with adverse neonatal outcomes, fetal insulin resistance (measured by cord blood HOMA-IR) was not significantly different between PCOS and control mothers, suggesting that the maternal environment, rather than fetal metabolic status per se, drives the growth discrepancies ([Lack of Fetal Insulin Resistance in Maternal Polycystic Ovary Syndrome, 2020](local_corpus)).

### Anti-Müllerian Hormone (AMH)
Elevated AMH is a diagnostic marker for PCOS. While high AMH levels are predictive of preterm delivery in ovulation induction cycles ([Highly elevated level of antimüllerian hormone associated with preterm delivery in polycystic ovary syndrome patients who underwent ovulation induction, 2021](local_corpus)), a retrospective cohort study analyzing IVF pregnancies found that in singleton pregnancies resulting in live birth, prepregnancy AMH levels did not significantly impact perinatal outcomes once the pregnancy was established ([Prepregnancy levels of antimüllerian hormone do not impact the perinatal outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome, 2025](local_corpus)). This discrepancy highlights that AMH may be a marker of risk for achieving or maintaining a pregnancy (miscarriage/preterm) rather than fetal growth once a stable pregnancy is achieved.

## Maternal Morbidity and Its Consequences for the Neonate

The adverse birth outcomes in PCOS offspring are inextricably linked to maternal obstetric complications. Women with PCOS have significantly higher odds of miscarriage, gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM), gestational hypertension, and pre-eclampsia ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of pregnancy outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome, 2024](local_corpus)).

*   **Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM):** The risk of GDM is markedly elevated (often 2- to 3-fold higher). A study involving women undergoing fertility treatments found a 2.94-fold higher likelihood of GDM in women with PCOS ([The Impact of Polycystic Ovary Syndrome on Gestational Diabetes Mellitus, Disease Knowledge, and Health Behaviors, 2025](local_corpus)). GDM increases the risk of macrosomia and LGA, yet, paradoxically, the overarching trend in PCOS leans toward SGA/LBW when adjusting for BMI, possibly due to the confounding effect of poor placentation.
*   **Hypertensive Disorders:** The increased risk of pre-eclampsia and gestational hypertension often necessitates iatrogenic preterm delivery, contributing to the higher PTB rates.
*   **Severe Maternal Morbidity:** A population-based cohort study in Ontario, Canada, covering over 1.5 million pregnancies, found that women with PCOS had a modest but significantly increased risk of severe maternal morbidity (adjusted relative risk 1.10), even after adjusting for age and other factors ([Diagnosed Polycystic Ovary Syndrome and Associated Severe Maternal Morbidity, 2026](local_corpus)). This systemic compromise inevitably impacts fetal well-being.

## Long-Term Offspring Development

The impact of maternal PCOS extends beyond the immediate neonatal period. A case-control study evaluating 1-2 year old offspring found that children born to mothers with PCOS had a significantly lower BMI compared to controls, suggesting a persistent trajectory of altered growth ([Development of 1-2 years Offspring Born to Mothers with Polycystic Ovary Syndrome, 2021](local_corpus)). This aligns with the concept of "fetal programming," where the intrauterine androgenic and metabolic environment predisposes offspring to long-term health issues, potentially including cardiovascular and metabolic disorders later in life.

## Conclusion

The synthesis of current evidence confirms that Polycystic Ovary Syndrome is an **independent risk factor** for adverse birth outcomes in offspring. While maternal characteristics such as higher BMI and the use of ART significantly contribute to obstetric risks, they do not fully account for the observed associations.

The diagnosis of maternal PCOS is independently associated with:
1.  **Increased Odds of Preterm Birth:** Driven by both spontaneous preterm labor (linked to inflammation and cervical insufficiency) and iatrogenic delivery (due to hypertensive disorders).
2.  **Fetal Growth Restriction:** Higher odds of SGA and LBW appear independent of BMI, distinguishing PCOS from the typical obesity-related macrosomia profile.
3.  **Adverse Maternal Complications:** The strong links to GDM and pre-eclampsia create a hostile intrauterine environment that precipitates early delivery and growth restriction.

Clinically, this necessitates that pregnant women with PCOS be managed as a high-risk obstetric population. Management strategies should focus on optimizing metabolic health pre-conception, vigilant screening for GDM and hypertensive disorders, and individualized monitoring of fetal growth. The evidence suggests that while interventions like metformin or specific ART protocols (e.g., letrozole-induced cycles) may mitigate some risks, the underlying PCOS phenotype remains a significant determinant of offspring health.
