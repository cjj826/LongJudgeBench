# Association Between Dietary Salt/Sodium Intake and Health Outcomes: A Systematic Review

## Introduction
Excessive dietary sodium intake is a significant global public health concern, recognized as a leading modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular morbidity and mortality. The widespread adoption of Westernized diets, characterized by substantially higher sodium consumption and lower potassium intake, has precipitated a rise in hypertension and related non-communicable diseases. While the linear relationship between sodium intake and blood pressure is well-established, the associations between sodium intake and other health outcomes—including cardiovascular disease (CVD) events, renal function, physical performance in aging populations, and all-cause mortality—remain areas of active investigation and, occasionally, scientific debate. This systematic review synthesizes evidence from recent high-quality studies to examine the multifaceted impact of dietary salt/sodium intake on various health outcomes in the general adult population.

## Sodium Intake and Blood Pressure Physiology

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms
The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) serves as a central regulator of the body's salt and water balance and arterial blood pressure. Acute sodium chloride loads or sustained high salt intakes suppress plasma renin activity, whereas reductions in NaCl intake stimulate it. This establishes a classical feedback loop between salt intake and renin release, conserving the body's salt content ([Salt feedback on the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system](local_corpus)). However, in salt-sensitive individuals, this regulatory system is impaired. Salt-sensitive hypertension (SS-HT) arises from multifactorial dysregulation of renal, vascular, and immune systems. Central to its pathophysiology is the aberrant activation of the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC), which drives sodium reabsorption in the distal nephron through both aldosterone-dependent and -independent mechanisms ([Integrated mechanisms linking sodium-potassium imbalance to salt-sensitive hypertension](local_corpus)). 

High dietary sodium intake also induces oxidative stress through NADPH oxidase-mediated reactive oxygen species (ROS) production, disrupts nitric oxide (NO) signaling, and activates antigen-presenting dendritic cells, triggering T-cell activation and vascular inflammation ([Integrated mechanisms linking sodium-potassium imbalance to salt-sensitive hypertension](local_corpus)). In the context of kidney disease, these mechanisms are exacerbated; for instance, kidney-specific deletion of the *Hsd11b2* gene in murine models caused systemic hypertension that was completely abolished by reducing salt intake, demonstrating the critical role of renal dysfunction in salt-dependent hypertension ([Renal Dysfunction Induced by Kidney-Specific Gene Deletion of Hsd11b2 as a Primary Cause of Salt-Dependent Hypertension](local_corpus)).

### Salt Sensitivity and Demographic Variations
The prevalence of salt sensitivity of blood pressure (SSBP) is notably high, affecting up to 50–60% of individuals with hypertension globally. This phenotype is even more prevalent among specific subgroups, including individuals of African ancestry, postmenopausal women, and those with obesity or metabolic syndrome ([Integrated mechanisms linking sodium-potassium imbalance to salt-sensitive hypertension](local_corpus)). Novel evidence from Zambia highlights that SSBP amplifies cardiovascular risk in people living with HIV, where chronic inflammation and cardiometabolic disturbances were uniquely associated with salt sensitivity ([Novel Insights into Salt-Sensitivity of Blood Pressure in African Adults with and without HIV: Comprehensive Inflammatory, renal and Cardiometabolic Profiling in a Zambian Cohort](local_corpus)). Genetic factors also play a role; microRNA gene polymorphisms (specifically in miR-26b-3p and miR-1307-5p) have been linked to blood pressure responses to salt intake and long-term hypertension incidence in Chinese populations ([Associations of microRNA Gene Polymorphisms With Salt Sensitivity, Longitudinal Blood Pressure Changes, and Hypertension Incidence in the Chinese Population](local_corpus)).

## Sodium Intake and Cardiovascular Disease

### Epidemiology and Global Burden
Elevated blood pressure remains the leading cause of preventable deaths globally, with diets high in sodium and low in potassium identified as primary contributors ([Replacing salt with low-sodium salt substitutes (LSSS) for cardiovascular health in adults, children and pregnant women](local_corpus)). A systematic review and meta-analysis covering 43 studies (n=62,940) quantified the dietary intake of sodium and potassium globally, finding substantial deviations from recommended levels, which correlates with cardiovascular morbidity ([Community-level dietary intake of sodium, potassium, and sodium-to-potassium ratio as a global public health problem: a systematic review and meta-analysis](local_corpus)). 

The burden is unevenly distributed. In Bangladesh, for example, a population-based survey revealed a mean urinary sodium excretion of 3.9 g/day, far exceeding the WHO recommendation of 2 g/day, correlating with a high prevalence of hypertension (20.9%) ([Urinary Sodium and Potassium Excretion in Bangladeshi Adults: Results from a Population-Based Survey with 24-Hour Urine Collections](local_corpus)). Similarly, in Kuwait, over 95% of adults exceeded sodium recommendations, contributing to escalating cardiovascular disease rates ([Diet quality of a population from a high-income gulf country in the era of transition: Kuwait as a model example](local_corpus)).

### Intervention Studies and Modeling
Evidence from clinical trials and modeling studies consistently supports sodium reduction as a preventive strategy for CVD. The 2025 American Heart Association/American College of Cardiology (AHA/ACC) hypertension guidelines emphasize dietary modification, including sodium reduction, as a cornerstone for reducing cardiovascular events and stroke ([The 2025 AHA/ACC hypertension guidelines: implications for cardiovascular and renal risk in patients with diabetes](local_corpus)). 

Modeling studies have projected significant health gains from sodium reduction. Implementing WHO's sodium benchmarks for packaged foods in India is estimated to substantially reduce cardiovascular and chronic kidney disease burden, proving highly cost-effective ([Estimated health benefits, costs, and cost-effectiveness of implementing WHO's sodium benchmarks for packaged foods in India: a modelling study](local_corpus)). In Cameroon, a 30% relative reduction in population-wide sodium intake is predicted to decrease the incidence of ischemic heart disease by 5.2% and hemorrhagic strokes by 6.6% over a lifetime ([Potential impact of a modest reduction in salt intake on blood pressure, cardiovascular disease burden and premature mortality: a modelling study](local_corpus)). Similar models in the Netherlands and Finland have demonstrated that modest reductions in sodium intake (e.g., via soup reformulation) can prevent thousands of CVD events and are cost-saving for healthcare systems ([A Modelling Approach to Estimate the Impact of Sodium Reduction in Soups on Cardiovascular Health in the Netherlands](local_corpus); [Health economic consequences of reducing salt intake and replacing saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat in the adult Finnish population: estimates based on the FINRISK and FINDIET studies](local_corpus)).

### Nocturnal Hypertension
Excessive salt intake is a major contributor to nocturnal hypertension, a condition highly prevalent in Asian populations. Nocturnal hypertension increases the risk of cardiovascular and all-cause mortality independent of daytime blood pressure. The Chinese Hypertension League has issued consensus recommendations identifying high salt intake and high salt sensitivity as primary attributors, recommending 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring for diagnosis and salt restriction as a management strategy ([Management of nocturnal hypertension: An expert consensus document from Chinese Hypertension League](local_corpus)). However, a cross-sectional study of treated hypertensive patients found no significant correlation between estimated daily salt intake from spot urine and nighttime systolic BP, suggesting that estimation methods and treatment status may complicate the relationship ([Association Between Estimated Daily Salt Intake from Spot Urine and Nocturnal Blood Pressure in Hypertensive Patients under Antihypertensive Treatment](local_corpus)).

## Sodium Intake and Kidney Health

### Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) Progression
The relationship between sodium intake and kidney health is complex and bidirectional. An umbrella review of 28 systematic reviews confirmed that dietary modifications, particularly salt restriction, are prioritized in CKD management to slow disease progression and prevent heart failure ([Dietary Patterns and Kidney Health: An Umbrella Review of Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses](local_corpus)). In patients with diabetes, effective blood pressure control via salt restriction lowers the risk of strokes and heart attacks and slows CKD progression ([Altered dietary salt intake for preventing diabetic kidney disease and its progression](local_corpus)).

Measurement of sodium intake in CKD patients is challenging due to alterations in circadian sodium handling and creatinine kinetics. A review of methods noted that while 24-hour urinary sodium excretion is the gold standard, spot urine estimation formulas are often used but may be less accurate in this population ([Sodium Intake Estimation in Chronic Kidney Disease: From Measurement Methods to Clinical Outcomes](local_corpus)). In a study of CKD patients, rapid weight loss observed during hospitalization with a low-salt diet was predicted by high baseline 24-hour urinary salt excretion, indicating that fluid retention driven by high salt intake is reversible with dietary restriction ([Twenty-four-hour Urinary Salt Excretion on Admission Predicts Significant Weight Loss with Seven-day Dietary Salt Restriction in Hospitalized Patients with Chronic Kidney Disease: A Single-center Study](local_corpus)).

### Cardiovascular Events in CKD
Kidney disease is an independent driver of CVD. In the Chronic Renal Insufficiency Cohort (CRIC), higher urinary sodium-to-potassium ratios were associated with increased risks of heart failure and myocardial infarction, underscoring the importance of the sodium-potassium balance in CKD patients ([Urinary Sodium and Potassium Excretion and the Risk of Cardiovascular Events in CKD](local_corpus)). However, data on the association between sodium excretion and mortality in CKD is inconclusive. Some studies suggest that the linear relationship seen in the general population may not hold for advanced CKD, potentially due to confounding by malnutrition or illness severity (reverse epidemiology), though recent large cohort studies continue to support the harms of excessive intake ([Sodium Intake Estimation in Chronic Kidney Disease: From Measurement Methods to Clinical Outcomes](local_corpus)).

## Sodium Intake and Physical Performance in Older Adults
Aging is associated with a progressive difficulty in maintaining ion regulation, which may impair muscle contraction capacity. A prospective study of 868 older adults in the Seniors-ENRICA cohort examined the longitudinal association between sodium intake and physical performance over five years. The study found that a 1 SD increase in sodium intake (0.73 g/d) was associated with a greater decline in Short Physical Performance Battery (SPPB) scores. This suggests that high sodium intake may accelerate the decline in physical performance in older adults, potentially through mechanisms unrelated to blood pressure, such as direct effects on muscle function or increased inflammation ([Longitudinal Association Between Sodium and Potassium Intake and Physical Performance in Older Adults](local_corpus)).

## Sodium Intake and Mortality
The association between sodium intake and all-cause mortality remains a topic of debate. A national population cohort study in China involving over 270,000 participants calculated 24-h urinary sodium excretion to estimate intake. The study aimed to examine associations with mortality and explore mediating factors, noting that while the link with blood pressure is linear, the research on sodium excretion and health outcomes has been inconclusive ([Associations and mediators of estimated sodium intake with cardiovascular mortality: data based on a national population cohort](local_corpus)). In contrast, a separate five-year cohort study in Japan found that hyperosmolarity and increased serum sodium concentration were risks for developing hypertension regardless of salt intake, suggesting that serum sodium levels might be an independent marker of risk ([Hyperosmolarity and Increased Serum Sodium Concentration Are Risks for Developing Hypertension Regardless of Salt Intake: A Five-Year Cohort Study in Japan](local_corpus)). While the totality of evidence generally supports that both very high and very low sodium intakes are associated with increased mortality (a U-shaped curve), the optimal range remains contentious. However, public health guidelines largely focus on reducing the upper tail of the distribution, where risk is unequivocally elevated.

## Intervention Strategies for Sodium Reduction

### Low-Sodium Salt Substitutes (LSSS)
Low-sodium salt substitutes (LSSS), which displace sodium chloride with potassium chloride, have emerged as a promising strategy. A systematic review confirms that LSSS can simultaneously decrease sodium intake and increase potassium intake, leading to blood pressure-lowering effects at low cost ([Replacing salt with low-sodium salt substitutes (LSSS) for cardiovascular health in adults, children and pregnant women](local_corpus)). The DECIDE-Salt trial in Chinese eldercare facilities demonstrated that replacing regular salt with a substitute was cost-saving, reducing systolic blood pressure and hypertension prevalence without adverse effects ([Cost-Effectiveness of Salt Substitute and Salt Supply Restriction in Eldercare Facilities: The DECIDE-Salt Cluster Randomized Clinical Trial](local_corpus)). In Indonesia, equity modeling suggested that a government-led LSSS strategy could prevent 1.5 million non-fatal CVD events over 10 years and reduce health inequities ([Cost-effectiveness analysis of low-sodium potassium-rich salt substitutes in Indonesia: an equity modelling study](local_corpus)).

### Digital and Personalized Interventions
Modern interventions leverage technology to promote behavioral change. A randomized clinical trial in Japan tested a sodium reduction program incorporating genetic profiles (specifically the sodium-sensitive AGT M235T genotype) and an AI-based mobile app. The intervention significantly reduced sodium intake in adults with elevated blood pressure, showcasing the potential of personalized, precision nutrition approaches ([Sodium Reduction Program Incorporating Genetic Profile and an AI-Based App: A Randomized Clinical Trial](local_corpus)). Similarly, a telehealth intervention using a dietary app and dietitian tele-counseling significantly reduced 24-hour urine sodium and improved diet quality in patients with diabetes and early-stage CKD ([Effects of Dietary App-Supported Tele-Counseling on Sodium Intake, Diet Quality, and Blood Pressure in Patients With Diabetes and Kidney Disease](local_corpus)). Furthermore, a randomized controlled trial found that a short, animated storytelling video significantly improved knowledge and behavioral expectation to reduce dietary sodium among US adults ([Digital storytelling boosts knowledge and behavioral expectation to reduce dietary sodium: a randomized controlled trial](local_corpus)).

## Other Health Outcomes and Interactions

### Osteoporosis and Diet-Cancer Links
While the provided texts focused heavily on cardiovascular and renal outcomes, the umbrella review on dietary fiber highlights the interconnectedness of diet and chronic disease prevention ([The impact of dietary fiber consumption on human health: An umbrella review of evidence from 17,155,277 individuals](local_corpus)). Regarding cancer, high-salt intake is known to disrupt the gastric mucosal defense barrier, promoting *Helicobacter pylori* colonization and acting as a risk factor for gastric carcinogenesis ([Diet and carcinogenesis of gastric cancer](local_corpus)). Additionally, a systematic review of Korean adults found protective associations of fruits and vegetables against gastric, colorectal, and breast cancers, indirectly supporting dietary patterns typically lower in processed sodium ([Dietary intake and cancer incidence in Korean adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis of observational studies](local_corpus)). While explicit data on sodium and osteoporosis was less prevalent in the provided texts, the mechanism of calcium loss with high sodium intake is established; the focus here remains on the strong evidence for BP, CVD, and CKD.

### The Sodium-to-Potassium Ratio
Current evidence increasingly points to the sodium-to-potassium ratio as a superior indicator of cardiovascular risk compared to sodium intake alone. In patients with rheumatoid arthritis, for instance, only 2 out of 61 patients presented an optimal ratio (≤1). The ratio was strongly associated with markers of arterial stiffness and myocardial perfusion, reinforcing the concept that increasing potassium intake is as critical as reducing sodium ([Sodium, potassium intake and urinary sodium-to-potassium ratio in rheumatoid arthritis: association with markers of cardiovascular dysfunction and disease-related parameters](local_corpus)).

## Conclusion
The evidence synthesized in this review establishes a clear and direct causal link between excessive dietary sodium intake and adverse health outcomes, primarily mediated through elevated blood pressure. High sodium intake is a major driver of hypertension, cardiovascular disease (including stroke and heart failure), and chronic kidney disease progression. The benefits of sodium reduction are supported by physiological mechanisms, observational epidemiology, clinical trials, and robust modeling studies. Furthermore, emerging evidence highlights the negative impact of high sodium intake on physical performance in older adults and the potential for genetic predisposition to influence salt sensitivity. The shift towards utilizing the sodium-to-potassium ratio as a risk marker underscores the importance of a holistic dietary approach. Intervention strategies, ranging from population-wide policies like LSSS and food reformulation to personalized digital health tools, demonstrate effectiveness and cost-effectiveness. Therefore, reducing dietary sodium remains a paramount public health imperative to reduce global morbidity and mortality.
