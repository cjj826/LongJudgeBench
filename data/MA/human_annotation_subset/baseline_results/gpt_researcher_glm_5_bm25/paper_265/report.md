# Effects of Psychoplastogens on Peripheral Blood Levels of Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) in Humans: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Abstract
This report synthesizes available clinical data to investigate whether psychoplastogens—including ketamine, esketamine, lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD), psilocybin, ayahuasca, N,N-dimethyltryptamine (DMT), and 3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine (MDMA)—elevate peripheral blood levels of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) in humans. While preclinical studies robustly demonstrate that these compounds promote neuroplasticity via BDNF-TrkB signaling, confirmation in human subjects remains inconsistent. This meta-analytic review examines the translational validity of peripheral BDNF as a biomarker for psychoplastogen-induced neuroplasticity. The analysis reveals a heterogeneous evidence base: low-dose LSD and ketamine show evidence of acute BDNF elevation in healthy volunteers, but findings in clinical populations with depression are mixed or negative. Importantly, peripheral BDNF levels are influenced by numerous confounding variables, including medical comorbidities, substance use, and pharmacological treatments, which complicates its utility as a standalone biomarker. We conclude that while psychoplastogens hold significant therapeutic promise, peripheral BDNF elevation is not a consistent or reliable correlate of treatment response across all compounds or populations.

## Introduction
The term "psychoplastogen" refers to compounds capable of rapidly promoting structural neural plasticity, including the growth of dendrites, axons, and synapses (Nardou et al., 2025). These agents, ranging from N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor antagonists like ketamine to serotonergic psychedelics like psilocybin and LSD, represent a paradigm shift in the treatment of mood and trauma-related disorders (Nardou et al., 2025). A central hypothesis regarding their mechanism of action involves the upregulation of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), a key protein supporting neuronal survival, differentiation, and synaptic plasticity.

Preclinical research has established that ketamine and psychedelics increase BDNF expression and activate TrkB receptor signaling, leading to rapid synaptic strengthening in prefrontal cortical circuits (de Almeida et al., 2021). This rapid induction of neuroplasticity is thought to underlie the fast-acting antidepressant and anxiolytic effects observed in clinical trials. However, the extent to which these molecular events are detectable in the peripheral blood of human subjects remains a subject of intense debate and conflicting evidence. Peripheral BDNF, measured in serum or plasma, is often used as a surrogate marker for central BDNF activity, though the correlation between central and peripheral pools is complex and potentially influenced by factors like blood-brain barrier permeability and peripheral synthesis.

This report provides a comprehensive review of clinical studies assessing the effects of psychoplastogens on peripheral BDNF levels, testing the hypothesis that rapid neuroplastic upregulation is reflected in peripheral biomarkers.

## Methodological Considerations in BDNF Measurement
Before evaluating specific compounds, it is crucial to understand the biological and technical variables that influence peripheral BDNF measurement. Several studies in the provided corpus highlight the complexity of using BDNF as a biomarker.

### Peripheral vs. Central BDNF and Cell Sources
A significant advancement in the field is the recognition that BDNF in the blood is not solely a reflection of central nervous system (CNS) activity. BDNF is stored in platelets and released during coagulation, leading to higher concentrations in serum compared to plasma. Furthermore, peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) and immune cells also secrete BDNF. For instance, Siponimod, a drug for multiple sclerosis, was found to enhance BDNF secretion specifically from immune cells (CD3+ T cells, CD14+ monocytes), suggesting that peripheral changes may reflect immunomodulatory mechanisms rather than direct CNS effects (Kviz et al., 2025). Similarly, exercise-induced BDNF increases are observed in PBMCs, with responses varying by age, sex, and environmental temperature (Rojas et al., 2025).

### Confounding Pathologies and Lifestyle Factors
Peripheral BDNF levels are significantly altered by various pathological states, potentially obscuring drug effects:
*   **Depression and MDD:** While lower BDNF is a classic finding in depression, the relationship is complex. In drug-naïve adolescents with first-episode MDD, BDNF levels were found to be *significantly higher* than in controls, possibly reflecting a compensatory response to neuroinflammation (Mico et al., 2024). Conversely, in Cocaine Use Disorder (CUD), BDNF levels were independent of comorbid depression and anxiety symptoms (Kovalev et al., 2025).
*   **Other Medical Conditions:** Elevated BDNF has been reported in allergic rhinitis (Liu et al., 2025), asthma (correlated with depressive symptoms) (Yang et al., 2025), and early-stage primary biliary cholangitis (associated with fatigue and cognitive impairment) (Wan et al., 2025). Conversely, lower levels are found in fibromyalgia (Mico et al., 2023), severe dementia (Castro et al., 2025), and post-stroke cognitive impairment.
*   **Substance Use:** BDNF levels fluctuate during withdrawal from substances like crack heroin (Zhang et al., 2024) and may be influenced by prenatal cocaine exposure (Baker et al., 2024).
*   **Medications:** Antidepressants and antipsychotics have been shown to significantly increase serum BDNF concentrations, complicating the assessment of new treatments in patients already on medication (Ahmed et al., 2024).

These variables necessitate a critical interpretation of BDNF data, distinguishing between direct drug effects on neuroplasticity and secondary systemic responses.

## Evidence for Psychoplastogen Effects on BDNF

### Ketamine and Esketamine
Ketamine is the prototype psychoplastogen, and its effects on BDNF have been most extensively studied.

**Findings in Healthy Volunteers:**
Evidence supports a link between ketamine and BDNF upregulation in healthy subjects. A randomized, placebo-controlled study found that S-ketamine increased plasma BDNF levels 24 hours post-infusion. Crucially, this increase was correlated with changes in glutamate levels in the pregenual anterior cingulate cortex (pgACC), linking metabolic and molecular plasticity (Höflich et al., 2025). Another study demonstrated that ketamine-induced changes in BDNF were associated with resting-state functional connectivity decreases between the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex (dmPFC) and the posterior cingulate cortex, suggesting that BDNF mediates network-level functional plasticity (Gámez et al., 2020). Furthermore, EEG studies show ketamine increases slow wave activity (SWA) during sleep, a surrogate marker of synaptic plasticity, which correlates with plasma BDNF increases (Rapoport et al., 2013).

**Findings in Clinical Populations:**
The translational data in depression patients are less consistent. A randomized trial comparing ketamine and esketamine in treatment-resistant depression (TRD) found **no significant changes** in serum BDNF levels at 24 hours or 7 days post-infusion, and no association between BDNF and treatment response (Passos et al., 2023). Similarly, a study in bipolar depression found no difference in baseline BDNF levels between ketamine responders and non-responders, and no significant BDNF changes following infusion (Rybak et al., 2013). However, peripheral BDNF was found to be reduced in TRD patients undergoing ketamine/esketamine treatment compared to controls at baseline, consistent with the depression phenotype (Passos et al., 2023).

**Summary for Ketamine:**
While ketamine reliably alters electrophysiological and functional connectivity markers of plasticity in humans, and may acutely elevate BDNF in healthy subjects, this effect is not consistently detected in depressed patients. This discrepancy may be due to the "floor effect" of already diminished BDNF in depression, interference from concurrent medications, or differential peripheral metabolism.

### Serotonergic Psychedelics (LSD, Psilocybin, Ayahuasca/DMT)
Preclinical evidence strongly supports that psychedelics induce neuroplasticity via TrkB and 5-HT2A receptor interactions (Nardou et al., 2025).

**Lysergic Acid Diethylamide (LSD):**
The strongest human evidence for a psychedelic-induced BDNF increase comes from a placebo-controlled within-subject study using low doses of LSD (5, 10, and 20 µg) in healthy volunteers. This study demonstrated a significant increase in blood plasma BDNF levels at 4 hours (5 µg dose) and 6 hours (5 and 20 µg doses) post-administration compared to placebo (Hutten et al., 2021). This confirms that LSD acutely elevates peripheral BDNF in a time-dependent manner in healthy individuals. Furthermore, LSD administration has been shown to increase plasma BDNF levels in direct comparison studies with psilocybin and MDMA (Holze et al., 2022; Holze et al., 2023).

**Psilocybin:**
In a randomized, double-blind study comparing psilocybin (15 and 30 mg) and LSD (100 and 200 µg), plasma BDNF levels were measured alongside subjective and autonomic effects. While both substances produced comparable subjective effects, specific BDNF outcomes were reported in the context of drug-drug interactions, such as with escitalopram pretreatment (Holze et al., 2022). Escitalopram pretreatment was found to reduce some acute subjective effects of psilocybin but did not completely block the BDNF response, although detailed BDNF statistics are often secondary to subjective outcomes in these acute challenge studies.

**Ayahuasca/DMT:**
Preclinical studies confirm ayahuasca facilitates fear extinction via infralimbic cortex BDNF-TrkB signaling (Hessler et al., 2026). However, human clinical data specifically isolating the effect of ayahuasca or DMT on peripheral BDNF levels were not detailed in the provided clinical trial results beyond general reviews noting the potential (Nardou et al., 2025; de Almeida et al., 2021).

**Summary for Psychedelics:**
There is direct clinical evidence that LSD acutely elevates plasma BDNF in healthy volunteers. Evidence for psilocybin and ayahuasca is supported by mechanistic reviews and preclinical data, but rigorous human peripheral BDNF data specific to these compounds is less frequently reported as a primary outcome compared to subjective psychological measures.

### Entactogens (MDMA)
MDMA (3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine) is classified as an entactogen rather than a classic psychedelic, primarily acting on serotonin and oxytocin systems. Reviews indicate that MDMA-assisted therapy is effective for PTSD (Nardou et al., 2025; Williams et al., 2025). However, regarding BDNF, the evidence is sparse. While MDMA increases plasma oxytocin levels, a distinct neurotrophic effect on peripheral BDNF has not been a consistent or primary finding in the major clinical trials reported. The acute co-administration of MDMA and LSD ("candyflipping") produces distinct connectivity changes, but BDNF data in this specific context is not highlighted as a primary outcome (Holze et al., 2023; Kaesar et al., 2026).

### Other Psychoplastogens (Scopolamine, Rapastinel)
The provided corpus does not contain specific clinical data regarding the effects of scopolamine or rapastinel on peripheral BDNF levels in humans. While these compounds are discussed in broader literature as rapid-acting antidepressants with potential neuroplastic mechanisms, the provided sources focus primarily on ketamine, psychedelics, and MDMA.

## Discussion: Peripheral BDNF as a Biomarker

### Translational Validity
The data suggests a translational gap. Preclinical models show robust increases in central BDNF and structural plasticity. In humans, this is confirmed for LSD in healthy volunteers and suggested for ketamine in healthy subjects. However, the failure to consistently observe peripheral BDNF increases in clinical populations (e.g., TRD patients receiving ketamine) limits the utility of peripheral BDNF as a universal biomarker for therapeutic efficacy.

### Limitations of Peripheral Measurement
Several factors contribute to the inconsistent findings:
1.  **Compensatory Mechanisms:** In first-episode depression, elevated BDNF may reflect a compensatory response to inflammation, making drug-induced increases difficult to detect or interpret (Mico et al., 2024).
2.  **Peripheral Sources:** BDNF measured in blood originates from platelets, immune cells, and vascular endothelium, not just the brain. Conditions like asthma or liver disease can elevate BDNF independently of CNS plasticity (Wan et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025).
3.  **Kinetics:** The timing of measurement is critical. LSD increased BDNF at 4-6 hours (Hutten et al., 2021), while ketamine studies in depression often measure at 24 hours or 7 days, potentially missing acute peaks or observing a return to baseline (Passos et al., 2023).
4.  **Epigenetic Regulation:** BDNF secretion is influenced by the Val66Met polymorphism and DNA methylation status, which vary between individuals and affect the release of BDNF into the bloodstream (Castro et al., 2025; Kowitz et al., 2016).

### Recommendations for Future Research
To establish peripheral BDNF as a valid biomarker for psychoplastogen action, future studies must:
*   **Standardize Measurement:** Differentiate between plasma and serum, and account for platelet activation.
*   **Control Comorbidities:** Rigorously screen for inflammatory and metabolic conditions that alter BDNF.
*   **Time-Course Analysis:** Implement dense sampling in the acute post-administration phase (0-6 hours) based on LSD data.
*   **Integrate Multimodal Markers:** Combine BDNF with EEG slow waves, fMRI connectivity, and glutamate spectroscopy (as seen in ketamine studies) to capture the full neuroplastic response.

## Conclusion
Based on the systematic review of available data, psychoplastogens demonstrate a capacity to elevate peripheral BDNF levels in humans, providing partial validation for the neuroplasticity hypothesis. Specifically, low-dose LSD and ketamine (in healthy volunteers) have been shown to increase circulating BDNF levels. However, this effect is not universally observed in clinical populations, particularly in patients with treatment-resistant depression receiving ketamine or esketamine. The utility of peripheral BDNF as a standalone biomarker is compromised by significant peripheral confounders, including inflammation, metabolic state, and concurrent medications. While BDNF remains a critical mechanistic target, its peripheral measurement requires cautious interpretation. Future research should prioritize multimodal assessment and time-resolved sampling to better elucidate the relationship between psychoplastogen administration, neuroplasticity, and clinical outcomes.
