## Conclusion (Expanded)

Based on a comprehensive synthesis of high-level evidence from randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and meta-analyses, the extraction of the primary maxillary canine stands as the definitive, evidence-based standard for the interceptive management of palatally displaced canines (PDCs). The literature robustly demonstrates that this intervention significantly alters the eruption trajectory of the permanent canine, increasing spontaneous eruption rates from a baseline of 13–42% in untreated controls to approximately 65–78% following intervention.

The clinical efficacy of this protocol is contingent upon strict adherence to patient selection criteria and timing. The evidence supports the following conclusions regarding implementation:

1.  **Optimal Timing:** Intervention is most effective during the late mixed dentition phase, specifically between the ages of 10 and 13 years. Success rates decline significantly in older adolescents, highlighting the necessity of early diagnosis.
2.  **Predictive Variables:** Radiographic assessment using panoramic radiographs remains the standard for predicting outcomes. Favorable prognostic indicators include a younger patient age, a mesioangular angle of the canine less than approximately 25–30 degrees, and a canine cusp tip located mesial to the lateral incisor but not crossing the midline of the lateral incisor root.
3.  **Protocol Selection:** Single extraction of the primary canine is the recommended first-line treatment. Current evidence does not support routine double extraction (primary canine and first molar), as it offers no statistically significant improvement in eruption rates and is associated with increased patient morbidity (pain and discomfort).
4.  **Adjunctive Therapies:** While Rapid Maxillary Expansion (RME) shows promise, particularly in patients with transverse maxillary deficiency, it does not universally outperform simple extraction in cases where arch width is normal. Furthermore, adjunctive use of cervical-pull headgear does not statistically enhance the efficacy of extraction alone.

From a safety perspective, interceptive extraction is a minimally invasive procedure with low reported pain levels, well-tolerated by pediatric patients. Crucially, it serves a protective function against root resorption of adjacent lateral incisors by facilitating the distal and vertical movement of the erupting canine away from the incisor root.

In summary, timely interceptive extraction of the primary canine prevents the sequelae of impaction, reducing the need for complex surgical exposure and prolonged orthodontic mechanics. General dental practitioners and orthodontists are advised to prioritize early screening for PDCs in the mixed dentition to leverage this critical window for interceptive correction.
