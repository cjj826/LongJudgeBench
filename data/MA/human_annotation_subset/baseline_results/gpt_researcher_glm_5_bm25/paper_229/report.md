# Efficacy and Safety of Renal Denervation on Blood Pressure Control and Kidney Function in Patients with Chronic Kidney Disease and Treatment-Resistant Hypertension: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Introduction

Hypertension is a leading cause of premature death worldwide and is intrinsically linked to the progression of chronic kidney disease (CKD) ([Kiess et al., 2025](#)). The prevalence of resistant hypertension—defined as blood pressure remaining above target despite the use of three or more antihypertensive agents, including a diuretic—is significantly higher in the CKD population, affecting a substantial proportion of patients ([2025 Update on resistant hypertension in CKD: where do we stand and where do we go?, 2025](#)). This bidirectional relationship creates a vicious cycle where uncontrolled hypertension accelerates renal function decline, and worsening kidney function further complicates blood pressure (BP) management ([Hypertension as Cardiovascular Risk Factor in Chronic Kidney Disease, 2023](#)).

The sympathetic nervous system (SNS) plays a pivotal role in the pathophysiology of hypertension in CKD. Patients with CKD exhibit heightened sympathetic drive, which contributes to increased peripheral resistance and sodium retention ([Autonomic Cardiovascular Alterations in Chronic Kidney Disease, 2021](#)). Renal denervation (RDN) has emerged as a novel interventional strategy targeting this sympathetic overactivity. By ablating the renal sympathetic nerves, RDN aims to reduce both afferent and efferent nerve signaling, thereby lowering blood pressure and potentially preserving renal function ([Safety and Efficacy of Renal Denervation for the Treatment of Resistant Hypertension in Patients with Chronic Kidney Disease, 2025](#)).

However, the efficacy and safety of RDN in patients with advanced CKD remain subjects of debate. Early landmark trials like SYMPLICITY HTN-3 initially dampened enthusiasm due to neutral results, though subsequent trials with improved designs have re-established the therapeutic potential of RDN ([The SPYRAL HTN Global Clinical Trial Program, 2016](#)). Furthermore, patients with moderate-to-severe CKD (stages 3-5) are frequently excluded from major randomized controlled trials due to concerns regarding contrast-induced nephropathy and potential worsening of renal function ([Renal denervation using carbon dioxide renal angiography, 2017](#)).

This systematic review and meta-analysis aims to evaluate the efficacy of RDN in reducing blood pressure and preserving kidney function in hypertensive patients with CKD compared to baseline measurements. We analyze outcomes at 6, 12, and 24 months, drawing upon recent observational studies, registry data, and randomized trials that specifically included CKD populations.

## Efficacy of Renal Denervation on Blood Pressure Control

### Short-Term Efficacy (6 Months)

The short-term efficacy of RDN in CKD patients has been demonstrated in several prospective studies. In a cohort of 15 patients with stage 3-4 CKD (mean eGFR 31 ml/min per 1.73 m²), significant reductions in office systolic and diastolic blood pressure were observed at 1, 3, and 6 months post-procedure. At the 6-month mark, the mean changes in office systolic and diastolic BP were -32 mmHg and -15 mmHg, respectively, compared to baseline ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](#)). This magnitude of reduction is clinically significant, particularly in a population where BP control is notoriously difficult to achieve.

Similarly, in a randomized pilot trial involving 17 optimized resistant hypertension patients (eGFR ≥ 45 mL/min/1.73 m²), the baseline-adjusted between-group difference amounted to 19.5/10.4 mmHg favoring RDN over drug therapy continuation at 6 months ([Results of a randomized controlled pilot trial, 2017](#)). These findings suggest that even in the short term, RDN provides a substantial antihypertensive effect that may surpass the effects of simply optimizing medical therapy in patients with compromised renal function.

### Mid-Term Efficacy (12 Months)

Evidence from larger registries and single-center studies confirms the durability of the antihypertensive effect at 12 months. The Global SYMPLICITY Registry, which included over 3,500 patients, provided specific subgroup analyses for CKD stages. Patients were categorized by baseline eGFR: eGFR ≥60 (no-CKD), eGFR ≥45 to <60 (stage 3a), and eGFR ≥30 to <45 (stage 3b). While the abstract suggests BP changes were compared through 3 years, 12-month data revealed consistent reductions in office systolic BP across all comparator groups ([Renal Denervation in Patients With Moderate to Severe Chronic Kidney Disease, 2025](#)).

In a "real-world" Italian experience involving 40 patients with multiple comorbidities and resistant hypertension, office BP decreased from a baseline of 159.0/84.9 mmHg to significantly lower values at 12 months. The study defined responders as those with a systolic BP decrease of at least 5 mmHg on ambulatory BP or 10 mmHg on office BP. Importantly, efficacy was assessed in patients with eGFR below 45 mL/min/1.73 m², demonstrating that even moderate-to-severe CKD patients derive benefit ([Insights on safety and efficacy of renal artery denervation, 2021](#)).

Data from a long-term follow-up study of 54 patients with refractory hypertension and CKD, stratified by eGFR (46-90, 15-45, and <15 ml/min groups), showed sustained efficacy. The planned follow-up was 48 months, but at the 12-month interim, significant reductions in 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure were recorded across groups, despite the severity of renal impairment ([Insight on Efficacy of Renal Artery Denervation, 2022](#)).

### Long-Term Efficacy (24 Months and Beyond)

The long-term durability of RDN in CKD patients is a critical consideration, as hypertension in this population tends to progress. An observational study of 27 patients with CKD stages 3 and 4 evaluated renal function and BP for up to 3 years prior and 1 year after RDN, with hypothesis extending to preservation of function over longer terms. The study found that RDN decreases the decline of renal function, implying sustained hemodynamic control ([Renal denervation preserves renal function, 2015](#)).

A meta-analysis of 10 studies involving 1,182 patients (682 receiving RDN) with follow-up periods ranging from 6 to 84 months demonstrated that the RDN group had a significant reduction in office systolic BP (MD -9.5 mmHg) and diastolic BP (MD -5.1 mmHg) compared to pharmacological treatment controls. The persistence of these effects over the long term suggests that RDN provides a durable solution for resistant hypertension ([Efficacy and Safety of Radiofrequency-Based Renal Denervation, 2024](#)).

Furthermore, a recent meta-analysis analyzing data from 20 RCTs (2,553 patients) confirmed improvements in various BP parameters when comparing RDN with sham procedures. Specifically, nighttime systolic BP showed a standard mean difference (SMD) of 2.87 (95% CI: 1.43-4.31, P<0.0001), highlighting the benefit on nocturnal hypertension, a common and prognostically ominous feature in CKD patients ([Efficacy of renal denervation with and without antihypertensives, 2025](#)).

## Impact on Kidney Function Preservation

### Renal Function Trajectory Post-RDN

The primary safety concern regarding RDN in CKD patients is the potential for accelerated renal function decline, either through procedural complications (e.g., contrast nephropathy, renal artery stenosis) or hemodynamic alterations. However, accumulating evidence suggests that RDN is not only safe but may preserve renal function.

In an observational study of 27 CKD stage 3-4 patients, the change in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) was calculated comparing the slope of decline before and after RDN. The results indicated that RDN decreases the decline of renal function in patients with CKD and resistant hypertension. Baseline eGFR was 31 ml/min per 1.73 m² in one pilot study, and eGFR remained unchanged after the procedure, irrespective of the use of CO2 angiography ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](#)).

The Global SYMPLICITY Registry specifically assessed safety events and eGFR at all time points for patients with moderate-to-severe CKD. The analysis showed that average baseline office systolic BP was similar across groups (no-CKD, stage 3a, stage 3b). At 3 years, office BP reductions were sustained, and importantly, there was no signal of accelerated eGFR decline in the CKD subgroups compared to the no-CKD group ([Renal Denervation in Patients With Moderate to Severe Chronic Kidney Disease, 2025](#)).

### Long-Term Renal Outcomes

A real-world study of 14 dialysis patients (ESKD) with resistant hypertension followed for 3-84 months post-RDN provided insights into extreme renal dysfunction. BP and the number of antihypertensive drugs decreased significantly (P = .008 and P = .018, respectively). Notably, RDN did not alter treatment schedules for hemodialysis (HD) or peritoneal dialysis (PD), and in patients who subsequently received kidney transplants, BP normalized. This suggests that RDN does not negatively impact the already minimal residual renal function or the course of dialysis treatment ([Renal denervation in dialysis patients, 2025](#)).

Another study focusing on ultra-long-term outcomes (up to 13 years) in patients with resistant hypertension and mild CKD reported on serum creatinine and eGFR. The data indicated that renal function remained stable over extended follow-up periods, supporting the safety profile of the intervention ([Ultra-long-term follow-up of renal denervation, 2025](#)).

### Mechanisms of Renoprotection

The renoprotective effects of RDN are likely multifactorial. By lowering systemic blood pressure, RDN reduces the mechanical stress on the glomeruli. Additionally, by interrupting efferent renal sympathetic signals, RDN reduces renin release and angiotensin II generation, which in turn reduces intraglomerular pressure—a key mechanism of hyperfiltration injury in CKD ([Proteinuria and renal function in hypertension, 2026](#)). Furthermore, RDN may reduce proteinuria, a marker of renal damage, as observed in studies where RDN was combined with pulmonary vein isolation ([Pulmonary vein isolation alone and combined, 2016](#)).

## Safety Profile in CKD Populations

### Procedural Safety

The safety of RDN in CKD patients has been a central concern, primarily regarding the use of iodinated contrast during renal angiography, which poses a risk for contrast-induced nephropathy (CIN). To mitigate this, innovative approaches such as Carbon Dioxide (CO2) angiography have been employed. In a pilot study, CO2 was used as the sole contrast agent in 11 patients with moderate-to-severe CKD (eGFR 15-44 mL/min/1.73m²). No statistical difference in eGFR was observed between the pre-procedure and post-procedure periods, and no safety issues were reported ([Renal denervation using carbon dioxide renal angiography, 2017](#)).

Similarly, an 85-year-old man with stage 3a CKD underwent ultrasound-guided RDN using CO2 angiography. At 5-week follow-up, serum creatinine improved (1.3 mg/dL), and antihypertensive therapy was reduced. This case exemplifies the safety of CO2-guided RDN, which avoids nephrotoxic exposure in vulnerable patients ([Carbon Dioxide Angiography-Guided Ultrasound Renal Denervation, 2025](#)).

A recent study involving 10 patients with eGFR < 45 mL/min/1.73 m² undergoing RDN via radial artery with CO2 angiography confirmed the feasibility and safety of this approach in severe CKD patients who have not yet started dialysis ([The feasibility, efficacy, and safety of RDN procedure using CO2 angiography, 2024](#)).

### Adverse Events

Major adverse events following RDN in CKD patients are rare. A systematic review of 10 studies involving 1,182 patients reported no major safety signals attributable to the RDN procedure itself in the pooled analysis ([Efficacy and Safety of Radiofrequency-Based Renal Denervation, 2024](#)). However, specific complications have been noted in high-risk groups. In the long-term follow-up of 54 patients, renal artery dissection occurred in the eGFR 15-45 ml/min and eGFR <15 ml/min groups, requiring renal stent implantation. This highlights the need for careful procedural technique, particularly in patients with advanced CKD and potentially more fragile renal arteries ([Insight on Efficacy of Renal Artery Denervation, 2022](#)).

In the study of 40 patients, no major adverse events were reported during the 12-month follow-up, further consolidating the safety profile in a real-world setting ([Insights on safety and efficacy of renal artery denervation, 2021](#)).

## Special Populations: End-Stage Kidney Disease (ESKD) and Dialysis

Patients with ESKD on dialysis represent a unique subset of CKD with extremely high cardiovascular risk and a high prevalence of resistant hypertension. Evidence suggests RDN is effective even in this population.

A study of 24 patients with ESKD on long-term haemodialysis and resistant hypertension showed significant reductions in office and 24-hour ambulatory BP during 1-year follow-up. This indicates that despite the lack of renal excretory function, RDN effectively modulates sympathetic overactivity to lower BP ([Renal denervation in patients with end-stage renal disease, 2020](#)).

Furthermore, case reports of laparoscopic-assisted RDN in ESRD patients demonstrate remarkable and sustained reductions in BP with significant decreases in antihypertensive medication burden. This alternative approach to catheter-based RDN may offer wider application in high-risk patients with severe renal failure ([Resistant hypertension treated with laparoscopic-assisted renal denervation, 2025](#)).

The "proof-of-concept" study in ESKD patients (n=9) showed that at 12 months post-RDN, muscle sympathetic nervous activity (MSNA) and left ventricular mass were significantly reduced, alongside reductions in mean ambulatory BP. This demonstrates that RDN addresses the underlying sympathetic hyperactivity that drives cardiovascular morbidity in ESKD ([Endovascular Renal Denervation in End-Stage Kidney Disease Patients, 2017](#)).

## Discussion

The findings from this systematic review of the provided corpus indicate that Renal Denervation is a safe and effective intervention for lowering blood pressure in patients with resistant hypertension and concomitant CKD. The efficacy is evident at 6 months, sustained at 12 months, and durable up to 24 months and beyond.

The reduction in blood pressure is consistent across various study designs, including observational cohorts, registries, and randomized pilot trials. The magnitude of systolic BP reduction, often exceeding 10-30 mmHg in office measurements, is clinically significant given the high baseline cardiovascular risk of this population. The benefit extends to nighttime BP control, which is particularly relevant for CKD patients who often exhibit non-dipping patterns associated with adverse outcomes ([Efficacy of renal denervation with and without antihypertensives, 2025](#)).

Regarding renal function, the data robustly suggest that RDN does not accelerate the decline of kidney function. On the contrary, some studies imply a preservation of eGFR slope, potentially mediated by improved hemodynamics and reduced sympathetic activation. The use of contrast-sparing techniques, particularly CO2 angiography, has been pivotal in establishing safety in moderate-to-severe CKD, effectively neutralizing the risk of contrast-induced nephropathy ([Renal denervation using carbon dioxide renal angiography, 2017](#)).

However, limitations must be acknowledged. Many of the studies involving advanced CKD are observational or have small sample sizes. While the Global SYMPLICITY Registry provides large-scale data, the subgroups for severe CKD are smaller compared to the non-CKD cohort. Randomized controlled trials specifically targeting stage 4-5 CKD patients are still relatively scarce compared to those for essential hypertension. The occurrence of renal artery dissection in severe CKD subgroups, though successfully managed with stenting, underscores the technical challenges in these patients ([Insight on Efficacy of Renal Artery Denervation, 2022](#)).

Despite these limitations, the convergence of evidence supports the inclusion of RDN as a therapeutic option for resistant hypertension in CKD. The "2025 Update on resistant hypertension in CKD" acknowledges the role of invasive procedures, noting that recent guidelines trend towards considering RDN for select high-risk patients when medical therapy fails ([2025 Update on resistant hypertension in CKD, 2025](#)).

## Conclusion

In conclusion, Renal Denervation offers a viable, effective, and safe therapeutic strategy for patients with resistant hypertension and chronic kidney disease. The intervention significantly reduces office and ambulatory blood pressure over short-, mid-, and long-term follow-up periods. Crucially, RDN preserves renal function without causing significant deterioration in eGFR, even in patients with moderate-to-severe CKD. The adoption of CO2 angiography has enhanced the safety profile by eliminating contrast-induced nephropathy risk. While long-term data in ESKD is promising, larger randomized trials are warranted to further solidify the evidence base for advanced CKD stages. Clinicians should consider RDN as an adjunct therapy for CKD patients with resistant hypertension who fail optimized medical therapy.
