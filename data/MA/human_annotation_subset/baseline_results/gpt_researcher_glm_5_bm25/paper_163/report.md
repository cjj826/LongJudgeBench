# Association Between Bisphosphonate Use and Risk of Cancers: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis Report

## Introduction

Bisphosphonates (BPs) are a primary class of antiresorptive drugs widely prescribed for the management of osteoporosis, Paget's disease, and metabolic bone diseases, as well as for the treatment of bone metastases and hypercalcemia associated with malignancy. By inhibiting osteoclast-mediated bone resorption, these agents significantly reduce the risk of skeletal-related events. Structurally, bisphosphonates are stable analogues of inorganic pyrophosphate, characterized by a P-C-P backbone that allows them to bind avidly to hydroxyapatite crystals in bone ([Russell, 2011](local_corpus)).

Bisphosphonates are broadly classified into two categories based on their chemical structure and mechanism of action: nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates (N-BPs) and non-nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates (non-N-BPs). N-BPs, such as alendronate, risedronate, ibandronate, pamidronate, and zoledronate, are significantly more potent. They act by inhibiting farnesyl diphosphate synthase (FPPS), a key enzyme in the mevalonate pathway, thereby disrupting protein prenylation in osteoclasts ([Russell et al., 2000](local_corpus); [Thompson et al., 2016](local_corpus)). Conversely, non-N-BPs, such as etidronate and clodronate, are metabolized into cytotoxic ATP analogues that induce osteoclast apoptosis.

Beyond their established skeletal effects, recent epidemiological and preclinical evidence has raised the possibility that bisphosphonates may exert non-skeletal effects, including a potential influence on cancer incidence and mortality. The mevalonate pathway, which is inhibited by N-BPs, is essential for the post-translational prenylation of small GTPases (e.g., Ras, Rho, Rac) that regulate cell proliferation, survival, and migration. Inhibition of this pathway may therefore confer anti-tumor properties ([Benefits of Bisphosphonate Therapy: Beyond the Skeleton, 2020](local_corpus)). Furthermore, preclinical studies suggest that bisphosphonates can affect angiogenesis, stimulate immune surveillance by activating gamma-delta T cells, and induce apoptosis in tumor cells.

However, the clinical evidence regarding the association between bisphosphonate use and cancer risk remains conflicting. While some studies suggest a protective effect against breast and colorectal cancers, concerns have been raised regarding a potential increased risk of esophageal and gastric cancers, particularly with oral formulations that may directly irritate the upper gastrointestinal mucosa. This report systematically synthesizes data from high-quality meta-analyses, systematic reviews, and epidemiological studies to evaluate the association between bisphosphonate use and the risk of overall and site-specific cancers, distinguishing between N-BPs and non-N-BPs and considering the influence of exposure duration.

## Methodological Synthesis and Evidence Quality

The evidence presented in this report is derived from comprehensive meta-analyses and systematic reviews published up to 2025. The methodological quality of these synthesized studies varies but generally adheres to rigorous standards, utilizing random-effects models to account for heterogeneity across observational datasets.

The most comprehensive evidence comes from a 2020 systematic review and meta-analysis that included 34 articles encompassing 4,508,261 participants and 403,196 cancer cases, providing high statistical power to detect associations ([Bisphosphonates and risk of cancers: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2020](local_corpus)). Additional high-quality evidence was drawn from nested case-control studies using large primary care databases such as QResearch and the Clinical Practice Research Datalink (CPRD) in the UK, as well as population-based cohorts like the Women’s Health Initiative (WHI) and the Nurses' Health Study (NHS).

A critical strength of the available literature is the distinction made between nitrogen-containing and non-nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates, allowing for mechanistic insights. Furthermore, dose-response and duration-response analyses in several studies have helped to elucidate whether observed associations are likely causal or confounded. For instance, the "healthy user bias"—where individuals prescribed osteoporosis medications may have better overall health behaviors—is a persistent confounder addressed through extensive adjustments for covariates such as BMI, smoking, and healthcare utilization.

## Bisphosphonate Use and Overall Cancer Risk

The aggregate evidence indicates **no significant association between bisphosphonate use and the risk of all-cause cancer**.

A large-scale meta-analysis encompassing over 4.5 million participants found that while bisphosphonate use was associated with significant risk reductions for specific cancer types, there was no statistically significant association with overall cancer risk ([Bisphosphonates and risk of cancers: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2020](local_corpus)). Similarly, a 2018 meta-analysis focusing on osteoporosis patients, involving 1,510,763 participants, reported a hazard ratio (HR) of 0.97 (95% CI: 0.80-1.18; I² = 92.5%), confirming the null effect on total cancer incidence ([Oral bisphosphonates and incidence of cancers in patients with osteoporosis: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2018](local_corpus)).

These findings are supported by prospective cohort data. An analysis from the Women’s Health Initiative found that bisphosphonate use was not associated with lung cancer incidence (adjusted HR = 0.91; 95% CI: 0.80-1.04) among postmenopausal women ([Oral bisphosphonate use and lung cancer incidence among postmenopausal women, 2018](local_corpus)). Additionally, a study utilizing the UK General Practice Research Database showed a reduced risk of all cancer (HR = 0.87; 95% CI: 0.77-0.97) in one cohort, but the broader consensus across larger meta-analyses suggests this reduction is driven by specific site-specific effects rather than a systemic anti-neoplastic effect.

The absence of a global reduction in cancer risk suggests that the chemopreventive potential of bisphosphonates is likely tissue-specific or contingent upon specific molecular pathways (e.g., hormone-related malignancies), rather than a general anti-proliferative effect across all cell types.

## Site-Specific Cancer Risk

### Breast Cancer

The association between bisphosphonate use and reduced breast cancer risk is one of the most consistent findings in the literature.

*   **Magnitude of Effect:** The 2020 meta-analysis reported a significant reduction in breast cancer risk, with a Relative Risk (RR) of 0.87 (95% CI: 0.82-0.93) ([Bisphosphonates and risk of cancers: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2020](local_corpus)). Similar findings were observed in a nested case-control study using UK primary care databases, where bisphosphonate use was associated with a reduced risk of breast cancer (OR: 0.92; 95% CI: 0.87-0.97) ([Exposure to bisphosphonates and risk of common non-gastrointestinal cancers, 2013](local_corpus)).
*   **Mechanism:** The mechanism likely involves the depletion of mevalonate pathway intermediates, which may impair the prenylation of signaling proteins required for tumor cell proliferation. Furthermore, nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates have been shown to inhibit cell viability and migration in breast cancer cell lines in vitro.
*   **Duration and Survival:** The protective effect appears to be duration-dependent. Long-term use (>1 year) has been associated with significantly reduced risks. Additionally, post-diagnostic use of oral bisphosphonates has been linked to improved breast cancer survival, suggesting a role in modifying disease progression ([Oral Bisphosphonates and Improved Survival of Breast Cancer, 2017](local_corpus)).

### Colorectal Cancer (CRC)

Bisphosphonate use is consistently associated with a statistically significant reduction in colorectal cancer risk. This association is biologically plausible because approximately 99% of an oral bisphosphonate dose remains unabsorbed in the intestine, allowing for direct topical exposure to the colonic mucosa, in addition to systemic effects.

*   **Magnitude of Effect:** A comprehensive meta-analysis reported a pooled Odds Ratio (OR) of 0.89 (95% CI: 0.80-0.99) for CRC risk in bisphosphonate users ([Oral bisphosphonates and the risk of colorectal cancer: a meta-analysis, 2013](local_corpus)). The 2020 meta-analysis confirmed this finding with a RR of 0.89 (95% CI: 0.81-0.98) ([Bisphosphonates and risk of cancers: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2020](local_corpus)).
*   **Dose-Response:** Strong evidence for causality comes from dose-response relationships. A significant inverse relationship has been noted for "10 or more prescriptions," with a pooled OR of 0.71 (95% CI: 0.60-0.84) ([Reduced risk of colorectal cancer with use of oral bisphosphonates: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2013](local_corpus)). Long-term exposure (>3 years) has been associated with even greater risk reductions (pooled OR: 0.73; 95% CI: 0.57-0.93) ([Oral bisphosphonates and the risk of colorectal cancer: a meta-analysis, 2013](local_corpus)).
*   **Comparative Data:** A nested case-control study in Kaiser Permanente Northern California found that ever use of oral bisphosphonates was associated with lower odds of CRC (OR: 0.82; 95% CI: 0.74-0.89) ([Oral bisphosphonates and colorectal cancer, 2017](local_corpus)). Interestingly, a Danish register-based cohort study also suggested lower mortality rates following CRC diagnosis in alendronate users, hinting at potential therapeutic benefits beyond prevention ([Reduced colon cancer incidence and mortality in postmenopausal women treated with an oral bisphosphonate, 2012](local_corpus)).

### Endometrial and Ovarian Cancers

Emerging evidence suggests a protective association for gynecological malignancies.

*   **Endometrial Cancer:** A meta-analysis of seven studies indicated that any use of bisphosphonates was associated with a significant reduction in endometrial cancer risk (RR = 0.75; 95% CI: 0.61-0.94) ([Bisphosphonates and risk of cancers: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2020](local_corpus)). A separate analysis confirmed this, showing a 27% risk reduction with any use ([Risk reduction of endometrial and ovarian cancer after bisphosphonates use: A meta-analysis, 2018](local_corpus)). The protective effect appears more pronounced with longer duration (>1 year), potentially mediated by modulation of estrogen signaling or immune surveillance mechanisms within the uterine microenvironment.
*   **Ovarian Cancer:** The data for ovarian cancer is less robust but suggestive. A meta-analysis estimated a significant risk reduction (RR = 0.73; 95% CI: 0.55-0.98) ([Risk reduction of endometrial and ovarian cancer after bisphosphonates use: A meta-analysis, 2018](local_corpus)). A case-control study (CITOUS) found that use of bisphosphonates for more than 1 year prior to diagnosis was associated with a significantly reduced risk of ovarian cancer (OR = 0.49; 95% CI: 0.26-0.93) ([The effect of bisphosphonates on the risk of endometrial and ovarian malignancies, 2014](local_corpus)).

### Gastrointestinal Cancers (Esophageal and Gastric)

The relationship between oral bisphosphonates and upper gastrointestinal cancers has been a subject of intense safety scrutiny due to the potential for mucosal irritation.

*   **Conflicting Evidence:** Early studies raised concerns. A case-control analysis within the UK General Practice Research Database suggested an increased risk of esophageal cancer (RR = 1.30; 95% CI: 1.02-1.66) for users with one or more prescriptions ([Oral bisphosphonates and risk of cancer of oesophagus, stomach, and colorectum, 2010](local_corpus)). A 2012 meta-analysis further fueled these concerns, reporting a positive relationship with an OR of 1.74 (95% CI: 1.19-2.55) ([Meta-analysis: oral bisphosphonates and the risk of oesophageal cancer, 2012](local_corpus)).
*   **Reassuring Evidence:** However, larger and more recent meta-analyses have largely refuted this risk. A 2015 systematic review of 11 studies found no association between bisphosphonate use and esophageal or gastric cancer ([Bisphosphonates and evidence for association with esophageal and gastric cancer: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2015](local_corpus)). Similarly, a large nested case-control study using QResearch and CPRD data found no association for esophageal (OR: 1.01) or gastric (OR: 0.88) cancer with overall bisphosphonate use ([Exposure to bisphosphonates and risk of gastrointestinal cancers, 2013](local_corpus)).
*   **Asian Populations:** Studies in Korean populations, where esophageal squamous cell carcinoma is more prevalent, also found no positive correlation between duration or frequency of bisphosphonate exposure and esophageal cancer risk ([Oral bisphosphonates and risk of esophageal cancer: a dose-intensity analysis, 2012](local_corpus)).

**Synthesis:** The weight of evidence suggests that if a true risk exists, it is likely very small and confined to specific subgroups. The initial signal may have been driven by protopathic bias (symptoms of undiagnosed cancer being treated with bisphosphonates for bone pain) or detection bias (increased endoscopic surveillance). Current consensus holds that bisphosphonates are not significantly associated with an increased risk of upper gastrointestinal cancers.

### Other Cancer Sites

*   **Pancreatic Cancer:** Bisphosphonate use has been associated with reduced risks of pancreatic cancer (OR: 0.79; 95% CI: 0.68-0.93) ([Exposure to bisphosphonates and risk of common non-gastrointestinal cancers, 2013](local_corpus)).
*   **Prostate Cancer:** Some studies indicate a reduced risk of prostate cancer (OR: 0.87; 95% CI: 0.79-0.96), though this is less consistently replicated than breast or colorectal cancer findings ([Exposure to bisphosphonates and risk of common non-gastrointestinal cancers, 2013](local_corpus)).

## Nitrogen-Containing vs. Non-Nitrogen-Containing Bisphosphonates

The chemical structure of bisphosphonates significantly modifies their biological activity, including potential anti-neoplastic effects. Nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates (N-BPs) are potent inhibitors of FPPS, leading to the accumulation of unprenylated proteins and disruption of cell signaling pathways critical for tumor survival and migration.

*   **Breast Cancer:** The protective effect appears to be specific to N-BPs. The 2020 meta-analysis found that N-BPs had a protective effect on breast cancer (RR = 0.94; 95% CI: 0.90-0.98), while non-N-BPs did not show a significant association ([Bisphosphonates and risk of cancers: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2020](local_corpus)). This supports the mechanistic hypothesis that inhibition of the mevalonate pathway is central to the chemopreventive effect.
*   **Cellular Mechanisms:** *In vitro* studies demonstrate that N-BPs (zoledronate, alendronate, pamidronate) inhibit the migration ability and differentiation of endothelial progenitor cells (EPCs), thereby exerting anti-angiogenic effects ([Investigation of inhibitory effects on EPC-mediated neovascularization, 2013](local_corpus)). They also induce apoptosis and inhibit cell viability in various cell lines, including osteoblasts, fibroblasts, and keratinocytes, which may contribute to both therapeutic effects and adverse events like osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ) ([Bisphosphonates affect migration ability and cell viability, 2011](local_corpus)).

## Duration of Use and Exposure-Response Relationships

The duration of bisphosphonate therapy is a critical determinant of cancer risk. The literature consistently demonstrates that longer exposure correlates with greater risk reduction for protective associations, reinforcing a potential causal link.

*   **Colorectal Cancer:** A systematic review found that while any use reduced risk (OR: 0.87), the association was significantly stronger for long-term exposure (OR: 0.73; 95% CI: 0.57-0.93) ([Oral bisphosphonates and the risk of colorectal cancer: a meta-analysis, 2013](local_corpus)). The Molecular Epidemiology of Colorectal Cancer study showed that use for more than 1 year was associated with a 50% reduction in risk (RR: 0.50; 95% CI: 0.35-0.71), whereas use for less than 1 year was not ([Use of bisphosphonates and reduced risk of colorectal cancer, 2011](local_corpus)).
*   **Endometrial Cancer:** A duration effect is also observed here, with use exceeding 1 year showing a significant protective effect (RR: 0.58; 95% CI: 0.47-0.72) ([Bisphosphonate use and the risk of endometrial cancer: a meta-analysis, 2016](local_corpus)).
*   **Esophageal Cancer:** For potential adverse effects, duration analyses generally do not support a cumulative risk. A Taiwanese nationwide study found no positive correlation between duration of exposure and esophageal cancer, suggesting that the initial transient signal observed in earlier studies may not reflect long-term toxicity ([Oral bisphosphonates and risk of esophageal cancer: a dose-intensity analysis, 2012](local_corpus)).

## Safety Profile and Mechanistic Context

While the primary focus of this report is cancer incidence, it is essential to contextualize these findings within the broader safety profile of bisphosphonates. The most serious adverse events associated with N-BPs are osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ) and atypical femoral fractures (AFF).

*   **Osteonecrosis of the Jaw (ONJ):** This adverse event is driven by the potent anti-resorptive and anti-angiogenic effects of N-BPs. The risk is higher with intravenous formulations (zoledronic acid) used in oncology compared to oral agents used for osteoporosis. The incidence of ONJ in osteoporosis patients is estimated at 5.1 to 9.6 cases per 100,000 patient-years for oral vs. intravenous N-BPs, respectively ([Zoledronate and osteonecrosis of the jaw in osteoporosis, 2023](local_corpus)). Interestingly, the anti-angiogenic properties implicated in ONJ may overlap with the anti-tumor mechanisms reducing cancer risk.
*   **Mechanistic Duality:** The mechanism of action—specifically the inhibition of protein prenylation and angiogenesis—appears to be a double-edged sword. While it may confer protection against breast and colorectal cancers, it simultaneously predisposes susceptible tissues (like the jaw) to impaired healing and necrosis ([Benefits of Bisphosphonate Therapy: Beyond the Skeleton, 2020](local_corpus)).

## Conclusion

Based on a comprehensive synthesis of epidemiological data, this systematic review concludes that **bisphosphonate use is associated with a significant reduction in the risk of specific cancers—namely breast, colorectal, and endometrial cancers—while having no significant effect on overall cancer risk**. The protective effects are most pronounced with long-term use (>1 year) and appear to be specifically mediated by nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates, likely through the inhibition of the mevalonate pathway and subsequent disruption of protein prenylation essential for tumor cell proliferation and survival.

Conversely, earlier concerns regarding an increased risk of esophageal cancer have been substantially alleviated by larger meta-analyses and Asian population studies, which found no consistent dose-response relationship. The evidence for reduced ovarian and pancreatic cancer risk is promising but requires further confirmation.

Clinically, these findings suggest that the prescription of bisphosphonates, particularly N-BPs like alendronate and zoledronic acid, may offer a "secondary benefit" in terms of chemoprevention for breast, colorectal, and endometrial cancers. However, this must be balanced against the established risks of ONJ and AFF. The decision to initiate bisphosphonate therapy should remain driven by skeletal health indications, with the potential cancer risk reduction considered a favorable ancillary effect rather than a primary indication.
