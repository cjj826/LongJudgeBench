# Neck Circumference as a Metabolic Health Marker and its Association with Metabolic Syndrome and Insulin Resistance in Women with Polycystic Ovary Syndrome

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is a pervasive endocrine-metabolic disorder affecting women of reproductive age, characterized by a complex interplay of hyperandrogenism, ovulatory dysfunction, and polycystic ovarian morphology. Beyond reproductive implications, PCOS is intrinsically linked to metabolic morbidities, including insulin resistance (IR), central obesity, and dyslipidemia, which collectively elevate the risk of type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) and cardiovascular disease (CVD). As the prevalence of obesity rises globally, the identification of convenient, reliable, and non-invasive anthropometric markers to screen for metabolic abnormalities in this population is of paramount clinical importance. This report synthesizes evidence from recent clinical studies to investigate whether women with PCOS exhibit larger neck circumference (NC) compared to non-PCOS controls and examines the robust associations between NC, metabolic syndrome (MetS), and insulin resistance.

## Anthropometric Phenotype: Neck Circumference in PCOS versus Non-PCOS Populations

The hypothesis that women with PCOS possess distinct anthropometric features compared to the general population is well-supported by literature regarding body mass index (BMI) and waist circumference (WC). However, the specific evaluation of neck circumference (NC) as a distinguishing marker between these groups reveals a nuanced relationship driven by the distribution of adiposity.

PCOS is frequently characterized by a predisposition toward "upper body" fat accumulation. A study focusing on **upper trunk fat assessment** noted that fat accumulation in the upper region of the body is common in PCOS and is associated with metabolic complications. In a cohort of 30 obese PCOS women compared to healthy controls, researchers identified positive correlations between neck circumference and total abdominal fat (TAF), suggesting that in PCOS, upper anthropometric measures like NC are significant indicators of visceral adiposity ([Upper trunk fat assessment and its relationship with metabolic and biochemical variables and body fat in polycystic ovary syndrome](local_corpus)).

While direct mean-comparison data of NC between PCOS and non-PCOS cohorts in the provided texts varies, the comparative prevalence of metabolic risk factors underscores the clinical divergence. A large-scale cross-sectional study analyzing 633 PCOS women and 2,172 non-PCOS women found that the prevalence of MetS was significantly higher in the PCOS group (28.0%) compared to the non-PCOS group (9.4%). The study highlighted that larger NC measurements were associated with the prevalence of MetS, hypertension, obesity, and hyperglycemia in both groups, but the absolute metabolic burden was consistently higher in the PCOS population ([Neck circumference is independently associated with metabolic syndrome in women with polycystic ovary syndrome](local_corpus)). This suggests that while NC is a marker of metabolic risk in the general population, its utility may be amplified in PCOS cohorts due to the higher baseline prevalence of upper-body adiposity and metabolic dysfunction.

## Neck Circumference as a Predictor of Insulin Resistance

Insulin resistance is a central pathophysiological feature of PCOS, affecting a substantial proportion of patients regardless of BMI. The utility of NC as a surrogate marker for IR has been rigorously evaluated, showing promising results that potentially surpass traditional markers like BMI.

In a dedicated cross-sectional study of 143 women with PCOS, the prevalence of IR was found to be 64.3%. The analysis revealed that patients with PCOS and IR had significantly greater values of NC compared to those without IR. Pearson correlation analysis demonstrated that NC was positively correlated with log-transformed HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment for Insulin Resistance). Crucially, multivariable linear regression models established that NC was significantly associated with HOMA-IR, confirming that NC is not merely a correlate of general obesity but an independent predictor of insulin resistance ([Neck circumference is a good predictor for insulin resistance in women with polycystic ovary syndrome](local_corpus)).

Furthermore, compelling evidence suggests that NC may offer diagnostic advantages over traditional anthropometric measurements. Research indicates that NC, as a reliable and convenient index, has "better predictive values of hyperuricemia and insulin resistance in women with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) compared with traditional anthropometric measurements" ([Neck circumference is independently associated with metabolic syndrome in women with polycystic ovary syndrome](local_corpus)). This finding positions NC as a potentially superior screening tool, capable of identifying metabolic disturbances that might be underestimated by BMI or waist circumference alone.

## Association with Metabolic Syndrome (MetS)

The relationship between NC and MetS in women with PCOS is profound. MetS represents a cluster of conditions—increased blood pressure, high blood sugar, excess body fat around the waist, and abnormal cholesterol or triglyceride levels—that increase the risk of heart disease, stroke, and diabetes.

A study involving 633 PCOS women demonstrated that NC is independently associated with MetS. Logistic regression analyses identified that larger NC measurements were significantly predictive of the syndrome. The prevalence of MetS components, including hypertension, central obesity, and dyslipidemia, was notably higher in women with larger NC measurements ([Neck circumference is independently associated with metabolic syndrome in women with polycystic ovary syndrome](local_corpus)).

In a study focusing on Asian demographics, specifically Bangladeshi women, researchers aimed to define specific cutoffs for NC to detect MetS and obesity. Among 200 women with PCOS (mean age 23.3 years, mean BMI 26.47 kg/m²), NC showed positive correlations with systolic and diastolic blood pressure, serum triglycerides, and the visceral adiposity index (VAI). This reinforces the concept that NC serves as a cumulative marker for various components of the metabolic syndrome, integrating vascular and lipidemic risk profiles into a single measurement ([Neck Circumference as a Predictor of Obesity and Metabolic Syndrome in Bangladeshi Women with Polycystic Ovary Syndrome](local_corpus)).

## Diagnostic Performance and Clinical Cutoff Values

Establishing standardized cutoffs is essential for translating research findings into clinical practice. The reported cutoff values provide specific thresholds that clinicians can utilize to flag high-risk patients.

### Obesity and Abdominal Obesity
In the cohort of 200 Bangladeshi women with PCOS, the mean NC was 34.6 (±3.04) cm. The study determined that an NC cutoff of **32.75 cm** offered the optimal balance for detecting abdominal obesity, yielding a sensitivity of 87.3% and a specificity of 74.4%, with an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.882. This high AUC value indicates excellent discriminatory power for identifying abdominal obesity in this population ([Neck Circumference as a Predictor of Obesity and Metabolic Syndrome in Bangladeshi Women with Polycystic Ovary Syndrome](local_corpus)).

### Metabolic Syndrome
The same study evaluated NC cutoffs for MetS detection. While specific sensitivity/specificity for MetS cutoffs were detailed in relation to ROC curves, the strong correlation coefficients between NC and VAI, TG, and BP imply that NC is a robust proxy for the syndrome. The identification of a specific threshold (e.g., the 32.75 cm mark for abdominal obesity) suggests that women exceeding this circumference should be screened more aggressively for metabolic abnormalities.

### Hyperandrogenism Link
Uniquely, NC also appears to bridge the metabolic and endocrine facets of PCOS. The Bangladeshi study found a positive correlation between NC and testosterone levels. Similarly, earlier research in obese premenopausal women found that NC was a better predictor of the Free Androgen Index (FAI) than BMI or fasting insulin alone, noting that NC and younger age were independent predictors of a higher FAI ([Neck circumference a good predictor of raised insulin and free androgen index in obese premenopausal women: changes with weight loss](local_corpus)). This indicates that upper body fat deposition, as measured by NC, may be more closely linked to the hyperandrogenic state of PCOS than general adiposity.

## Mechanistic Insights: Upper Body Fat and Visceral Adiposity

The clinical utility of NC is grounded in its anatomical representation of upper body subcutaneous fat. In PCOS, the physiological milieu of hyperandrogenism and insulin resistance drives a specific fat distribution pattern favoring the upper trunk and visceral depots.

A study utilizing computed tomography (CT) and bioelectrical impedance analysis in obese PCOS women provided mechanistic validation. It established a positive correlation between NC and Total Abdominal Fat (TAF) (r = 0.49, P < 0.0006). Furthermore, trunk circumference (TrC), a related metric, correlated with Visceral Fat (VF) (r = 0.62, P = 0.01). This demonstrates that a simple external measurement like NC reflects the internal deposition of visceral fat, which is the metabolically active tissue driving insulin resistance and inflammation in PCOS ([Upper trunk fat assessment and its relationship with metabolic and biochemical variables and body fat in polycystic ovary syndrome](local_corpus)).

The interaction between obesity phenotypes and inflammation further supports this. Research indicates that abdominal obesity (AO) exacerbates inflammation in PCOS, quantified by elevated tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α) in follicular fluid. The study noted that other obesity indicators, such as neck and arm circumference, occur alongside AO, painting a picture of a systemic "pro-inflammatory phenotype" where NC serves as a visible signpost of this metabolic dysfunction ([Abdominal Obesity May Play a Significant Role in inflammation Exacerbation in Polycystic Ovary Syndrome Patients](local_corpus)).

## Clinical Implications and Recommendations

The synthesis of this evidence leads to a concrete clinical opinion: **Neck circumference should be integrated as a standard anthropometric measurement in the routine evaluation of women with PCOS.**

Current practice often relies heavily on BMI and Waist Circumference (WC). However, BMI fails to distinguish between fat and muscle mass, and WC measurements can be variable. NC offers distinct advantages:
1.  **Simplicity and Reproducibility:** It requires only a measuring tape and minimal training, reducing inter-observer variability compared to WC.
2.  **Independent Predictive Value:** It correlates with insulin resistance and MetS independently of other factors.
3.  **Superiority in Specific Pathologies:** Evidence suggests it may be a better predictor of IR and hyperuricemia in PCOS than traditional measures.
4.  **Identification of "at-risk" Phenotypes:** It identifies women with the upper-body adiposity phenotype, who are at highest risk for cardiovascular sequelae.

Clinicians should be alerted by an NC measurement exceeding **32.75 cm** in young women with PCOS (based on Asian population data) to prompt further investigation into visceral fat accumulation, lipid profiles, and insulin sensitivity. This screening is particularly relevant given that adolescents with PCOS already show elevated metabolic markers, necessitating early detection tools ([Adolescent PCOS and metabolic health: An analysis of fat, muscle, and hormones](local_corpus)).

## Conclusion

The evidence strongly supports the hypothesis that neck circumference is a clinically significant marker in women with polycystic ovary syndrome. While PCOS is characterized by a higher prevalence of metabolic syndrome and upper-body fat distribution compared to non-PCOS populations, NC serves as a distinct and powerful predictor of these complications. It demonstrates a significant independent association with insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome, often showing better predictive capacity than traditional anthropometric indices.

Specifically, in women with PCOS:
*   NC correlates significantly with HOMA-IR, indicating its utility in screening for insulin resistance.
*   It is independently associated with MetS and its individual components (hypertension, dyslipidemia, hyperglycemia).
*   It correlates with visceral and total abdominal fat.
*   It shows associations with hyperandrogenism (testosterone levels and FAI).

Therefore, NC serves as a window into the metabolic health of the PCOS patient, reflecting the visceral adiposity and inflammatory state that underlie the syndrome. Incorporating NC into the standard workup for PCOS can facilitate early identification of metabolic risks, allowing for timely lifestyle or pharmacological interventions to mitigate long-term health consequences.
