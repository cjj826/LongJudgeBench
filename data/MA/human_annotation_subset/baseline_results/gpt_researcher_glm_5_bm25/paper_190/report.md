# A Systematic Review of Sexual Misconduct in Dentistry: Prevalence, Contributing Factors, Impacts, and Interventions

## Introduction
Sexual misconduct in healthcare settings represents a significant violation of professional ethics and patient trust. In the field of dentistry, the unique nature of the clinician-patient relationship—characterized by physical proximity, intimate examinations of the oral cavity, and the vulnerability of patients under anesthesia or sedation—creates a distinct environment where boundaries can be easily tested or crossed. A recent comprehensive review defines sexual misconduct in dentistry as including "inappropriate behaviours from patients or staff, such as unwanted advances, comments, gestures, or physical contact" (A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025). This report synthesizes current evidence regarding the prevalence, etiology, consequences, and management of sexual misconduct within dental care settings, drawing upon systematic reviews, cross-sectional studies, and climate surveys to provide a comprehensive overview of this occupational and professional hazard.

## Prevalence of Sexual Misconduct

### Prevalence Among Dental Students
Research indicates that sexual misconduct is a pervasive issue in dental education. A landmark 2025 systematic review screened 2,238 articles and identified 23 peer-reviewed papers detailing the scope of the problem (A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025). The findings reveal that dental students are frequently exposed to sexual misconduct from various perpetrators, including patients, faculty, and senior colleagues. Specific prevalence rates vary by region and study design. In a Brazilian dental school, 15% of undergraduate students reported personal experiences of sexual harassment, with a notable finding that male students had three times higher odds (OR=2.910) of being harassed compared to female students (Sexual harassment in dentistry: prevalence in dental school, 2010). Furthermore, 25.4% of these students reported witnessing sexual harassment within the institution. Similarly, an international survey of female dental students in the U.S., Bulgaria, Brazil, and India revealed that while a majority viewed admissions as fair, significant proportions perceived gender bias, with Bulgarian students (87.2%) particularly noting a lack of embrace of female students (An International Survey of Female Dental Students' Perceptions About Gender Bias and Sexual Misconduct at Four Dental Schools, 2018). In an Australasian dental school, a survey of clinical students found that harassment by patients is a frequent occurrence, with the response rate of 67% yielding significant data on the negative impacts of such events ("It's just inappropriate": Harassment of dental students by patients, 2022). Additionally, a study in Sri Lanka found that 18% of new dental students experienced sexual harassment during "ragging" (initiation rituals), with males reporting significantly higher rates than females (Harassment of newly admitted undergraduates by senior students in a Faculty of Dentistry in Sri Lanka, 2011).

### Prevalence Among Dental Hygienists and Practitioners
Dental hygienists, often working in close physical proximity to patients and dentists, report high rates of inappropriate behavior. A study of Virginia dental hygienists found that approximately one-third reported experiencing sexual harassment in the workplace within the previous 24 months (Sexual Harassment Issues Among Virginia Dental Hygienists, 2020). More alarmingly, a national survey in the United States found that the career occurrence of Inappropriate Patient Sexual Behavior (IPSB) among dental hygienists was 85.8%, with recent occurrence (past year) at 44% (Inappropriate Patient Sexual Behavior in the Dental Practice Setting: Experiences of dental hygienists, 2021). In Canada, a survey of 3,780 dental hygienists revealed that more than 70% experienced some form of mistreatment—including harassment, bullying, and sexual misconduct—over their careers (Healthy and Respectful Workplaces: The Experiences of Dental Hygienists in Canada, 2022). This indicates that sexual misconduct is part of a broader spectrum of workplace violence (WPV) affecting the profession.

### General Workplace Violence Context
Sexual misconduct does not occur in isolation but exists within a broader context of workplace violence in dentistry. In Nigeria, the prevalence of workplace violence in oral healthcare centers was reported at 31.9%, attributed largely to long waiting times and treatment outcomes (Occupational violence against dental professionals in southern Nigeria, 2011). In China, a survey of 1,109 dentists highlighted that workplace violence is a significant occupational hazard, though the breakdown of sexual violence specifically was less emphasized compared to physical and verbal abuse (A preliminary survey of occupational health and workplace violence among 1109 Chinese dentists: a call to action, 2025).

## Contributing Factors and Etiology

### Power Dynamics and Hierarchy
The hierarchical structure of dental practice and education is a primary contributing factor. The 2025 systematic review identified that power imbalances enable misconduct (A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025). In educational settings, "ragging" or hazing rituals perpetuate abuse, as seen in Sri Lanka where 50% of students experienced mistreatment, with verbal and emotional abuse being the most frequent (Harassment of newly admitted undergraduates by senior students in a Faculty of Dentistry in Sri Lanka, 2011). Faculty and senior colleagues are frequently identified as perpetrators. A UK-wide questionnaire pilot found that senior colleagues were identified as perpetrators in 88% of inappropriate behavior incidents (Inappropriate behaviours in a dental training environment: pilot of a UK-wide questionnaire, 2023). Similarly, in the U.S., faculty inconsistency and belittlement were cited as major issues affecting the learning environment (A humanistic environment for dental schools: what are dental students experiencing?, 2014).

### Patient-Perpetrated Misconduct
Patients are a significant source of sexual misconduct. The close physical contact required in dentistry, combined with the sedation or anesthesia used in some procedures, creates opportunities for inappropriate behavior. The study on IPSB among dental hygienists highlighted that healthcare professionals are at a 16-times higher risk of experiencing workplace violence and IPSB than other occupations (Inappropriate Patient Sexual Behavior in the Dental Practice Setting: Experiences of dental hygienists, 2021). In an Australasian dental school, students reported that patients often made inappropriate comments or gestures, which disrupted the professional relationship and caused emotional distress ("It's just inappropriate": Harassment of dental students by patients, 2022).

### Environmental and Structural Factors
The organization of dental practices and schools contributes to the problem. The PRECEDE-PROCEED model has been used to analyze behavioral and environmental factors contributing to WPV in dental institutions (Framework for managing inappropriate behavior in clinical dental learning environments, 2024). Factors include:
*   **Lack of Reporting Mechanisms:** In the UK pilot, 66% of respondents felt uncomfortable raising issues, and when they did, 30% felt unsupported (Inappropriate behaviours in a dental training environment: pilot of a UK-wide questionnaire, 2023).
*   **Institutional Culture:** Surveys of dental researchers at AADR conferences indicated that while the organization is committed to safety, incidents still occur, necessitating ongoing assessment (Survey of Dental Researchers' Perceptions of Sexual Harassment at AADR Conferences: 2015 to 2018, 2020).
*   **Physical Layout:** The privacy of treatment rooms, while necessary for patient dignity, can also shield misconduct.

### Gender and Minority Status
Gender plays a complex role. While women often occupy subordinate positions and are primary targets (Sexual boundaries in dental practice: Part 2, 1999), some studies, like the Brazilian study, found male students at higher risk of harassment (Sexual harassment in dentistry: prevalence in dental school, 2010). However, the 2022 ADEA Climate Study revealed that administrators with faculty appointments were significantly more likely to experience microaggressions, harassment, and discrimination (Microaggressions, harassment, and discrimination in dental education: Results from the 2022 American Dental Education Association Climate Study, 2025). Sexual and gender minorities (SGM) face distinct risks. A study on LGBQ+ faculty and staff found that they reported less fair treatment and more harassment compared to heterosexual counterparts (Exploring the climate for lesbian, gay, bisexual, and queer faculty, administrators, and staff in oral health education: Insights from the American Dental Education Association 2022 climate study, 2025). Additionally, dental students identifying as non-heterosexual reported worse experiences regarding welcomeness and belonging (Dental and allied dental students' cultural climate related perceptions and experiences: Does sexual orientation matter?, 2025). In Brazil, SGM individuals reported discrimination in dental services associated with disparities in oral health outcomes (Oral Health and Discrimination Experienced by Sexual and Gender Minorities in Brazil: A Cross-Sectional Study, 2025).

## Impacts of Sexual Misconduct

### Psychological and Professional Well-being
The consequences of sexual misconduct are profound. Victims often suffer from:
*   **Psychological Distress:** Harassment leads to anxiety, depression, and burnout. This is compounded by the general high-stress environment of dental training (Impact of COVID-19 on Depression, Anxiety and Stress of Dental Students: A Systematic Review, 2025).
*   **Career Attrition:** Victims may leave the profession or change jobs. In Canada, 67% of those mistreated considered leaving their job, and 8% left the profession entirely (Healthy and Respectful Workplaces: The Experiences of Dental Hygienists in Canada, 2022).
*   **Loss of Trust:** Inappropriate behaviors erode trust in educational institutions and the profession. A study of UK newspaper media showed that reports of sexual misconduct, while often sensationalized, affect public trust (Exploring dentists' professional behaviours reported in United Kingdom newspaper media, 2020).

### Impact on Educational Environment
A hostile learning environment impedes educational outcomes. The "hidden curriculum" of tolerated misconduct undermines the humanistic values dental schools aim to instill (A humanistic environment for dental schools: what are dental students experiencing?, 2014). When students are focused on survival and managing harassment, their ability to learn clinical skills is compromised.

### Patient Safety and Care
Misconduct distracts practitioners and can lead to medical errors. While specific links between sexual misconduct and dental errors are less documented, the broader literature on WPV suggests that stress and fear impair clinical judgment. Furthermore, silence regarding misconduct prevents the implementation of necessary safety protocols (Sexual boundaries in dental practice: Part 2, 1999).

## Interventions and Management Strategies

### Policy Development and Educational Interventions
The 2025 systematic review highlighted the need for evidence-based interventions (A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025). Currently, interventions are limited but evolving:
*   **Workplace Protocols:** Dental-specific WPV prevention protocols are being developed. One study used the PRECEDE-PROCEED model to create a comprehensive management protocol for dental settings, emphasizing education, policy, and support systems (Framework for managing inappropriate behavior in clinical dental learning environments, 2024).
*   **Training Workshops:** A pilot workshop in Germany for medical students, which is transferable to dental settings, showed that specific training on navigating harassment could improve students' confidence in responding to inappropriate behavior (Teaching medical students to navigate workplace harassment - preliminary experiences from a pilot workshop in Germany, 2025).
*   **Reporting Mechanisms:** Institutions must establish clear, confidential reporting channels. The survey of AADR conference attendees emphasized the need for organizations to facilitate reporting and support (Survey of Dental Researchers' Perceptions of Sexual Harassment at AADR Conferences: 2015 to 2018, 2020).

### Regulatory and Disciplinary Actions
Dental boards play a crucial role. An analysis of Texas dental board disciplinary actions identified sexual misconduct as a category of violation, though it was less frequent than issues like poor practice or fraud (An analysis of dental board disciplinary action among general dentists in Texas, 2025). In Australia, an analysis of tribunal cases under the National Law (2010-2017) showed that sexual misconduct cases, while fewer than other misconduct types like drug impairment, resulted in serious outcomes including deregistration (Serious misconduct of health professionals in disciplinary tribunals under the National Law 2010-17, 2020).

### Measuring and Monitoring
Validated tools are essential for assessing the extent of the problem. A study developed a 31-question tool for measuring workplace violence among clinical dental hygienists, which includes a specific domain for workplace sexual harassment (6 questions) (Measuring workplace violence for clinical dental hygienists, 2021). This allows for standardized data collection to inform policy.

### Organizational Climate Assessment
The 2022 ADEA Climate Study represents a significant step in monitoring the educational environment. By assessing perceptions of microaggressions, harassment, and discrimination across US and Canadian schools, it provides data to drive institutional change (The 2022 ADEA Climate Study in US and Canadian Dental Schools and Allied Dental Programs: Methodological Considerations, 2025).

## Discussion
The evidence confirms that sexual misconduct is a systemic issue in dentistry, affecting students, hygienists, and practitioners globally. The prevalence rates—ranging from 15% among students in Brazil to 85% career prevalence among hygienists in the US—indicate a widespread problem that requires urgent attention. A critical observation is the role of the "humanistic environment." Dental accreditation bodies (CODA) mandate a humanistic culture, yet surveys reveal a disconnect between policy and reality (A humanistic environment for dental schools: what are dental students experiencing?, 2014). The persistence of harassment suggests that current policies are insufficient or poorly enforced.

Furthermore, the high prevalence of IPSB underscores the need for better management of patient interactions, including training on boundary setting and defusion techniques. The intersectionality of identity—gender, sexual orientation, and race—exacerbates risk. The finding that LGBQ+ individuals face unique challenges (Exploring the climate for lesbian, gay, bisexual, and queer faculty, administrators, and staff in oral health education: Insights from the American Dental Education Association 2022 climate study, 2025) calls for targeted interventions that address the specific vulnerabilities of minority groups.

Comparatively, sexual misconduct in dentistry mirrors issues in medicine, where similar prevalence rates and power dynamics exist. However, the unique setting of the dental chair—intimate, prolonged physical contact—may necessitate specialized training beyond standard harassment education.

## Conclusion
Sexual misconduct in dentistry is a prevalent and impactful issue with deep roots in power dynamics, hierarchy, and institutional culture. It manifests through harassment by patients, peers, and faculty, affecting the well-being and career longevity of dental professionals. While systematic reviews are beginning to map the landscape of this issue, there remains a gap in robust, evidence-based interventions. Future efforts must focus on developing standardized measurement tools, implementing comprehensive WPV protocols, and fostering an institutional culture that prioritizes psychological safety and humanistic values. Only through a concerted effort to address the underlying causes of misconduct can the dental profession ensure a safe and respectful environment for all its members.
