# Effects of Time-Restricted Eating Combined with Exercise on Body Composition in Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Introduction

Time-restricted eating (TRE), a subtype of intermittent fasting, has gained significant traction as a dietary strategy for weight management and metabolic health improvement. Unlike traditional caloric restriction, TRE focuses on limiting the daily eating window to a specific timeframe, typically ranging from 4 to 10 hours, without necessarily mandating a reduction in caloric intake. The efficacy of TRE has been widely debated, particularly regarding its independent effects versus the effects of caloric restriction induced by shorter eating windows. However, the combination of TRE with structured exercise—encompassing aerobic, resistance, or concurrent training—presents a more complex physiological scenario. Exercise independently influences body composition by increasing energy expenditure and stimulating muscle protein synthesis. Consequently, combining these two interventions may yield synergistic or divergent effects on specific body composition metrics, such as fat mass (FM), body fat percentage (BF%), and fat-free mass (FFM).

This report synthesizes findings from a specific meta-analysis and supplementary randomized controlled trials to evaluate the effects of TRE when followed in combination with exercise. The primary objective is to determine whether this combined intervention significantly improves body composition in adults compared to unrestricted eating combined with exercise.

## Efficacy of TRE and Exercise on Body Composition: The Meta-Analytic Perspective

The cornerstone of this investigation is the 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis titled "Effects of time-restricted eating with exercise on body composition in adults." This study aimed to clarify the effects of TRE when combined with aerobic, resistance, or concurrent training on body composition ([Effects of time-restricted eating with exercise on body composition in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis.](local_corpus), 2025).

The meta-analysis included 15 studies comprising 338 participants who evaluated TRE versus unrestricted eating in individuals performing exercise. The researchers employed a random-effects model to calculate weighted mean effect sizes. The pooled results indicated that TRE, when combined with exercise, produced small but statistically significant reductions in fat mass and body fat percentage. Specifically, the analysis found an effect size of -0.20 (95% CI = -0.28 to -0.13, p < 0.001) for fat mass reduction and an effect size of -0.23 (95% CI = -0.35 to -0.11, p < 0.001) for body fat percentage reduction. Notably, the report suggests that these changes often occurred without a significant loss in fat-free mass, implying that the weight loss was primarily driven by adipose tissue reduction. The preservation of FFM is a critical finding, as it suggests that the combined intervention supports a favorable body composition phenotype—reducing adiposity while maintaining muscle mass—particularly when resistance training is involved.

## Mechanisms of Action: Beyond Caloric Restriction

The metabolic regulatory role of TRE has been explored in broader contexts, including a 2025 systematic review titled "Counting hours or calories?" This analysis highlighted that while TRE leads to significant weight loss, the mechanisms remain a subject of inquiry. When energy restriction strategies were conducted in both the TRE and control groups, the differences in weight loss were not statistically significant, suggesting that the benefits of TRE may largely stem from an implicit reduction in energy intake ([Counting hours or calories? Metabolic regulatory role of time-restricted eating in adults with overweight and obesity: a systematic review and meta-analysis.](local_corpus), 2025). However, when TRE is combined with exercise, the metabolic context shifts.

Exercise, particularly resistance training, stimulates anabolic pathways (e.g., mTOR signaling) that promote muscle hypertrophy, while TRE influences circadian rhythms and metabolic flexibility. A 2021 randomized controlled trial titled "Time-restricted eating and concurrent exercise training reduces fat mass and increases lean mass in overweight and obese adults" provided direct evidence of this synergy. This study demonstrated that overweight adults performing 8 weeks of concurrent aerobic and resistance training while adhering to TRE (12:00 p.m. to 8:00 p.m.) achieved significant reductions in fat mass while preserving or even increasing lean mass compared to normal eating controls ([Time-restricted eating and concurrent exercise training reduces fat mass and increases lean mass in overweight and obese adults.](local_corpus), 2021). This supports the hypothesis that TRE does not impair the anabolic response to resistance training, a concern often raised regarding intermittent fasting protocols.

## Specific Effects of Resistance Training Combined with TRE

Resistance training (RT) is the primary modality for increasing or preserving FFM. Several studies in the corpus examined the interaction between TRE and RT specifically.

### Preservation of Lean Mass
A 2023 study on trained participants examined four weeks of resistance training combined with TRE versus a habitual diet. The results showed that both interventions elicited decreases in fat mass (p < 0.05), but neither group experienced a loss in fat-free mass. This indicates that short-term TRE does not compromise the maintenance of muscle mass during resistance training in trained individuals ([Effect of Time-Restricted Eating and Resistance Training on High-Speed Strength and Body Composition.](local_corpus), 2023). Conversely, a 2020 study involving recreationally active males found no differential influence of TRE combined with resistance training on body composition compared to a normal diet, despite participants being in a caloric deficit. Both groups lost fat mass, but the addition of TRE did not provide a statistically significant additive benefit beyond the caloric deficit and exercise stimulus ([Four Weeks of Time-Restricted Feeding Combined with Resistance Training Does Not Differentially Influence Measures of Body Composition, Muscle Performance, Resting Energy Expenditure, and Blood Biomarkers.](local_corpus), 2020).

### High-Protein TRE and RT
Recent evidence suggests that the composition of the diet during the eating window matters significantly. A 2025 randomized controlled trial investigated TRE alongside a high-protein diet (1.6 g/kg/day) and resistance training in women with overweight. The study found significant reductions in visceral and subcutaneous adipose tissue, alongside the preservation of fat-free mass ([High-Protein Time-Restricted Eating Alongside Resistance Training Reduces Adipose Tissue While Preserving Fat-Free Mass in Women With Overweight: A Randomized Controlled Trial.](local_corpus), 2025). This highlights that while TRE provides the timing structure, adequate protein intake is essential for lean mass preservation during caloric deficits induced by fasting or dietary restriction.

### Hypertrophy in Hypercaloric States
Interestingly, a 2025 study investigated TRE during a hypercaloric state (10% surplus) combined with resistance training in well-trained individuals. The results indicated that total exercise volume was significantly lower in the TRE group compared to the control group (6,960 ± 287 vs. 7,334 ± 289 repetitions), and subjective energy ratings were poorer. This suggests that attempting to maximize muscle hypertrophy via a caloric surplus may be hindered by a restricted eating window, likely due to difficulties in consuming sufficient calories and nutrients within a short timeframe ([Hypercaloric 16:8 time-restricted eating during 8 weeks of resistance exercise in well-trained men and women.](local_corpus), 2025). Therefore, while TRE with exercise is effective for fat loss and lean mass maintenance, it may not be optimal for maximizing muscle hypertrophy during bulk phases.

## Effects of Aerobic and Concurrent Training with TRE

The literature also explores the impact of TRE on endurance and concurrent training adaptations, often in the context of athletes or physically active individuals.

### Endurance Training and Body Composition
In the context of aerobic training, such as in runners and cyclists, the results have been somewhat heterogeneous. A 2021 study on male middle- and long-distance runners found that 8 weeks of 16:8 TRE resulted in significant reductions in fat mass compared to habitual intake, without compromising aerobic performance ([Effects of 8 wk of 16:8 Time-restricted Eating in Male Middle- and Long-Distance Runners.](local_corpus), 2021). Similarly, a 4-week intervention in male long-distance runners found a significant reduction in whole-body fat mass (-0.8 ± 1.3 kg) with TRE, although cardiometabolic risk factors remained unchanged ([An Intervention of Four Weeks of Time-Restricted Eating (16/8) in Male Long-Distance Runners Does Not Affect Cardiometabolic Risk Factors.](local_corpus), 2023).

However, not all studies confirmed significant advantages over unrestricted eating. A 2024 crossover trial in male recreational runners found no significant differences in body composition changes between TRE and habitual diet groups, although both maintained performance ([Effects of Time-Restricted Eating on Aerobic Capacity, Body Composition, and Markers of Metabolic Health in Healthy Male Recreational Runners: A Randomized Crossover Trial.](local_corpus), 2024). This inconsistency suggests that in lean, active populations, the "fat loss" advantage of TRE may be attenuated compared to overweight populations.

### Flexible TRE and Aerobic Exercise
A 2025 randomized controlled trial investigated a flexible TRE protocol (flexTRE) combined with aerobic exercise in middle-aged women with overweight/obesity. The flexTRE group (8-hour window) combined with exercise achieved the greatest reductions in fat mass compared to flexTRE alone, exercise alone, or control groups ([Flexible time-restricted eating combined with exercise in a free-living setting for middle-aged women with overweight/obesity: a randomized controlled trial.](local_corpus), 2025). This finding is significant because it supports the efficacy of TRE in a "free-living" setting, where adherence to strict fasting windows can be challenging. The flexibility may improve long-term adherence, thereby enhancing body composition outcomes.

## Comparative Efficacy and Population Considerations

The efficacy of TRE combined with exercise appears to be moderated by the participant's baseline characteristics, specifically their weight status and sex.

### Overweight and Obese Populations
Evidence consistently supports the benefits of TRE combined with exercise in overweight and obese adults. A meta-analysis focusing on this population confirmed that TRE significantly reduces body weight and improves metabolic parameters ([Counting hours or calories? Metabolic regulatory role of time-restricted eating in adults with overweight and obesity: a systematic review and meta-analysis.](local_corpus), 2025). Furthermore, the synergy between TRE and resistance training is particularly potent here. A 2025 study on young adults with overweight/obesity found that TRE alone or in combination with resistance training significantly improved body composition variables, with the combined group showing superior outcomes ([Efficacy of time restricted eating and resistance training on body composition and mood profiles among young adults with overweight/obesity: a randomized controlled trial.](local_corpus), 2025).

### Female-Specific Responses
Several studies have focused on women, addressing sex-specific physiological responses. A meta-analysis of 13 RCTs involving 612 overweight and obese women found that TRE significantly reduced body weight and fasting insulin, though the magnitude of fat mass reduction varied ([Effects of time-restricted eating on body composition and metabolic parameters in overweight and obese women: a systematic review and meta-analysis.](local_corpus), 2025). Additionally, studies on female DanceSport dancers showed that 6 weeks of 16:8 TRE improved body composition and lipolytic hormone regulation without exacerbating eating disorder risks, even in a population prone to such conditions ([6-week time-restricted eating improves body composition, maintains exercise performance, without exacerbating eating disorder in female DanceSport dancers.](local_corpus), 2024).

### Athletes and Performance
For athletes, maintaining performance while optimizing body composition is crucial. The evidence suggests that TRE is generally compatible with maintaining endurance performance while facilitating fat loss. A study on elite cyclists found that 4 weeks of TRE reduced fat mass but noted that training quality and intensity must be monitored ([Time-restricted eating effects on performance, immune function, and body composition in elite cyclists: a randomized controlled trial.](local_corpus), 2020). However, as noted previously, TRE may impair the ability to consume the necessary calories for hypertrophy in strength athletes, potentially leading to lower training volumes ([Hypercaloric 16:8 time-restricted eating during 8 weeks of resistance exercise in well-trained men and women.](local_corpus), 2025).

## Safety and Psychological Factors

An often-overlooked aspect of dietary interventions is their psychological impact. A systematic review suggested that TRE might increase hunger in adults with overweight and obesity compared to isocaloric strategies, raising questions about long-term adherence ([Time-restricted eating increases hunger in adults with overweight and obesity: A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled studies.](local_corpus), 2025). However, the study on DanceSport dancers suggested that in a supervised, controlled setting, TRE did not exacerbate eating disorder attitudes ([6-week time-restricted eating improves body composition, maintains exercise performance, without exacerbating eating disorder in female DanceSport dancers.](local_corpus), 2024). Furthermore, TRE combined with exercise has been associated with improved mood profiles in some trials ([Efficacy of time restricted eating and resistance training on body composition and mood profiles among young adults with overweight/obesity: a randomized controlled trial.](local_corpus), 2025).

## Conclusion

Based on the synthesized evidence, the combined intervention of time-restricted eating and exercise effectively improves body composition in adults, primarily by reducing fat mass and body fat percentage while generally preserving fat-free mass. The meta-analytic data confirms statistically significant, though small, effect sizes for fat reduction when TRE is combined with aerobic, resistance, or concurrent training.

The efficacy appears most pronounced in overweight and obese populations, where the intervention facilitates a negative energy balance conducive to fat loss. The preservation of lean mass is a key advantage of combining TRE with resistance training, particularly when protein intake is adequate. However, in well-trained individuals or those aiming for hypertrophy via a caloric surplus, the restricted eating window may hinder the ability to consume sufficient calories and maintain optimal training volumes.

In conclusion, clinicians and practitioners should consider recommending TRE combined with exercise as a viable strategy for body recomposition, particularly for fat loss. However, the prescription should be tailored to the individual's goals: TRE is highly effective for cutting phases or weight management but may require modification (e.g., wider eating windows) for athletes focused on maximizing muscle hypertrophy.
