# Association Between Gut Microbiome Composition and Childhood Stunting in Under-Five Children in Low- and Middle-Income Countries: A Systematic Review

## Introduction
Childhood stunting, defined as a height-for-age Z-score (HAZ) less than -2 standard deviations below the WHO Child Growth Standards median, remains a pervasive public health crisis in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). Characterized by impaired linear growth, stunting affects approximately 149.2 million children under five globally, with the highest burdens concentrated in South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa. The etiology of stunting is multifactorial, stemming from a complex interplay of inadequate nutrition, recurrent infections, and environmental enteric dysfunction (EED).

In recent years, the gut microbiome has emerged as a critical biological determinant in the pathogenesis of stunting. The "impoverished gut" hypothesis suggests that a triple burden of diarrhoea, malnutrition, and chronic disease is mediated by enteric infections and subsequent microbial dysbiosis. This systematic review synthesizes evidence from clinical and longitudinal studies to analyze the association between gut microbiome composition—specifically microbial diversity, taxonomic abundance, and metabolic pathways—and childhood stunting in children under five in LMICs.

## Microbial Diversity and Linear Growth
The relationship between gut microbial diversity and linear growth in young children is complex, with studies reporting divergent findings based on age, geographical location, and methodology.

### Alpha Diversity Trends
Alpha diversity, a measure of species richness and evenness within a microbial community, typically increases with age as the infant gut matures. A pivotal longitudinal study in South India found that while diversity indices increased significantly with age (P < 0.0001) in all children during the first two years of life, there were no statistically significant differences in diversity indices or the rates of increase between persistently stunted children (n=10) and non-stunted controls (n=10). This suggests that in some contexts, overall diversity alone may not be a sufficient biomarker for distinguishing growth patterns.

Conversely, other research indicates that disruptions in diversity precede stunting. A Peruvian cohort study of 78 infants aged 5-12 months revealed that children who became stunted over a 6-month follow-up period exhibited distinct microbiome changes compared to non-stunted children. Specifically, the study highlighted that intestinal mucosal damage and microbiome shifts were observable before the manifestation of linear growth deficits. Furthermore, the direction of diversity changes may be context-dependent. A study comparing Indonesian stunted children (n=78) to those with normal nutritional status (n=53) found that while macronutrient intake was lower in stunted children, energy loss in the form of short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) was higher, suggesting metabolic inefficiency in the stunted gut microbiome.

In the context of intervention, positive shifts in diversity have been linked to growth improvements. A 2025 study on Indonesian infants with impending stunting demonstrated that successful nutritional intervention—specifically Nutritional Advice plus Oral Nutritional Supplements (NAONS)—resulted in significant shifts in gut microbial composition (beta diversity) and species richness (Chao1 index), indicating that microbiome maturation is responsive to dietary inputs and correlated with growth recovery.

### Beta Diversity and Community Structure
Beta diversity, which measures differences in microbial community composition between groups, provides a more robust signal for stunting. The study in Pidie, Aceh, Indonesia, involving 42 subjects (21 stunted, 21 controls), utilized 16S rDNA amplicon sequencing to demonstrate distinct community structures associated with growth status. This study emphasized that ingestion of enteric pathogens leads to EED, a critical factor in stunting pathogenesis, which is reflected in distinct microbial community profiles.

Similarly, a study focusing on the gut microbiota profiles of young South Indian children (n=41) found sex-specific relations with growth. In boys, weight and HAZ scores were covariates of microbiota variation, suggesting that the structural composition of the microbiome interacts differently with growth physiology depending on the sex of the child.

## Taxonomic Abundance: Pathogenic and Beneficial Signatures
The specific bacterial taxa associated with stunting highlight a state of dysbiosis characterized by the enrichment of pathogenic organisms and a reduction in beneficial, mature taxa.

### Enrichment of Pathogenic Taxa
Stunted children frequently exhibit a higher abundance of Proteobacteria, a phylum containing many pathogenic genera. In the Indonesian case-control study, the presence of enteric pathogens was quantitatively correlated with linear growth impairment and reduced levels of Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1), a key growth biomarker. The *Campylobacter* genus, in particular, has been identified as a significant driver of growth deficits. In a prospective birth cohort of 271 children in the Amazonian lowlands of Peru, 93% of children had asymptomatic *Campylobacter* presence. The study identified specific bacterial taxa associated with *Campylobacter* burden, suggesting that pathogen-associated dysbiosis, even in the absence of clinical diarrhea, contributes to linear growth deficits.

Furthermore, in a rural Malawian cohort, thirty bacterial taxa were differentially associated with linear growth. The study noted that while total eukaryotic viral families were not associated with growth, specific bacteriophages were differentially abundant, indicating a complex cross-kingdom interaction influencing growth velocity.

### Depletion of Beneficial Taxa
Conversely, beneficial taxa associated with gut health and maturity are often depleted in stunted children. The South Indian study noted that *Bifidobacterium longum* subsp. *longum* abundance was positively associated with weight-for-age Z-scores (WAZ), while the lipopolysaccharide (LPS) biosynthesis pathway—a marker of potential inflammation—was upregulated in stunted and wasted children. The "impoverished gut" framework supports this, noting that enteric infections disrupt intestinal absorptive and barrier functions, leading to chronic, subclinical enteropathy. This disruption enables microbial translocation and chronic systemic inflammation, thereby impairing growth.

### Underlying Mechanisms: The Role of Environmental Enteric Dysfunction (EED)
EED serves as the mediating bridge between microbiome dysbiosis and stunting. It is characterized by villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and increased intestinal permeability. The Peruvian infant cohort study explicitly linked EED biomarkers to growth outcomes. Stunted children exhibited higher levels of intestinal damage markers (I-FABP, zonulin) and systemic inflammation biomarkers (interleukin-1β, TNF-α, LBP) compared to non-stunted children. This inflammatory milieu suggests that the gut microbiome drives growth impairment through systemic immune activation.

The pathogenesis involves a cycle where poor sanitation and hygiene (WASH) lead to frequent pathogen ingestion. This triggers gut inflammation, increasing permeability and allowing bacterial translocation, which in turn causes systemic inflammation. Chronic systemic inflammation suppresses the growth hormone axis, reducing IGF-1 production and stunting linear growth. The observation that stunting persists despite optimal feeding in some contexts supports the theory that WASH and microbiome health are critical components of the solution.

## Metabolic Pathways and Functional Implications
Beyond taxonomic composition, the functional potential of the microbiome—its metabolic pathways—provides insight into the mechanisms of stunting.

### Energy Harvest and SCFAs
The gut microbiota plays a fundamental role in energy metabolism. The study of Indonesian children aged 3-5 years provided critical metabolic insights: stunted children had lower macronutrient intake but higher energy loss in the form of SCFAs and branched-chain fatty acids. This suggests that the stunted microbiome may be inefficient in energy harvest or overly active in fermenting substrates that do not benefit the host, effectively "wasting" energy that could support growth.

In contrast, successful nutritional interventions in impending stunting cases led to beneficial shifts in the metabolome. These shifts were associated with improved growth, suggesting that the metabolic profile can be restored through diet.

### Inflammatory Pathways
The upregulation of the LPS biosynthesis pathway in stunted children, as observed in the South Indian cohort, indicates a higher potential for inducing inflammation. LPS, a component of the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria, is a potent endotoxin that drives systemic inflammation. This functional finding aligns with the clinical observations of elevated systemic inflammatory markers in stunted children.

## Intervention Insights and Clinical Implications
The synthesis of evidence suggests that microbiome-targeted interventions could be pivotal in addressing stunting.

### Nutritional Interventions
Nutritional Advice plus Oral Nutritional Supplements (NAONS) have been shown to induce beneficial microbiome shifts and improve growth trajectories. The 2025 Indonesian study demonstrated that microbiome composition (beta diversity) and richness respond positively to supplementation, validating the microbiome as a modifiable target. Small-quantity lipid-based nutrient supplements (SQ-LNS) are also highlighted as an effective preventive intervention for stunting and wasting in LMICs.

### Iron Supplementation Safety
Iron interventions are common in LMICs to treat anemia, which overlaps with stunting. A randomized controlled trial in Bangladeshi infants (n=923) evaluated the effects of iron supplements and micronutrient powders (MNPs) on the gut microbiome. Reassuringly, the study found no increase in diarrhea and no adverse reprofiling of the microbiome towards pathogenic species, suggesting that iron interventions can be administered safely without exacerbating dysbiosis in this population.

### The WASH-Microbiome Nexus
Policy reviews emphasize that stunting interventions must extend beyond nutrition to address the environmental drivers of dysbiosis. The critical window for intervention is the first 1000 days. Evidence mapping highlights that achieving "optimal feeding" is often insufficient if the underlying environment fosters pathogen transmission and EED.

## Conclusion
The association between gut microbiome composition and childhood stunting is substantial and multifaceted. Stunting is consistently associated with a state of dysbiosis characterized by distinct microbial community structures (beta diversity), the enrichment of pathogenic taxa (e.g., Proteobacteria, *Campylobacter*), and the activation of inflammatory metabolic pathways (e.g., LPS biosynthesis). While alpha diversity trends are inconsistent across studies, functional analyses reveal that stunted children often suffer from metabolic inefficiencies, such as excessive energy loss through SCFAs.

These microbial alterations are mechanistically linked to EED, which drives systemic inflammation and growth hormone resistance. Longitudinal and intervention studies provide compelling evidence that the microbiome is not merely a bystander but an active participant in growth outcomes. Nutritional interventions can modulate the microbiome toward a healthier state, yet comprehensive strategies must integrate WASH improvements to prevent the pathogen burden that initiates the dysbiosis-inflammation-stunting cycle. Future research should focus on multi-omics integration to identify precise microbial therapeutic targets for preventing and treating childhood stunting.
