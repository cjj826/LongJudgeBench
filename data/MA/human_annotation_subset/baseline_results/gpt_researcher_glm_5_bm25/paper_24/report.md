# Comparative Effectiveness of Very Low Calorie Diets and Low-Energy Liquid-Formula Diets for Weight Loss in Obese Patients With and Without Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus: A Systematic Review

## Abstract

**Background:** Weight management is a cornerstone of treatment for obesity and type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). Very low calorie diets (VLCD) and low-energy liquid-formula diets (LED) are potent interventions, yet their comparative effectiveness in patients with T2DM versus those without remains a subject of clinical inquiry.

**Objective:** To synthesize clinical data regarding the comparative effectiveness of VLCDs (<800 kcal/day) and LEDs (>800 kcal/day) for weight loss in obese patients, specifically contrasting outcomes between those with T2DM and those without.

**Methods:** A comprehensive systematic review was conducted using provided clinical data sources. Studies were selected based on their relevance to dietary interventions (VLCD/LED), patient populations (obese with and without T2DM), and weight loss outcomes.

**Results:** Evidence suggests that both VLCDs and LEDs induce significant weight loss in obese individuals, with magnitude ranging from 8.5 kg to 18 kg depending on duration and adherence. Crucially, the presence of T2DM does not appear to impair total weight loss potential; patients with and without diabetes achieve near-identical weight reduction outcomes. However, distinct physiological differences exist. Patients with T2DM exhibit less fat mass reduction per unit of weight lost compared to non-diabetic counterparts, suggesting a divergence in body composition changes during caloric restriction. Furthermore, VLCDs demonstrate high efficacy in inducing diabetes remission and cardiovascular risk reduction, with outcomes often superior to conventional dietary advice.

**Conclusions:** VLCDs and LEDs are effective for weight loss in both diabetic and non-diabetic obese populations. While total weight loss is comparable, metabolic and body composition responses differ. These findings support the use of intensive dietary interventions in T2DM management, provided medical supervision is maintained.

## Introduction

Obesity and type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) are intertwined global health pandemics that significantly increase the risk of cardiovascular disease and mortality. Dietary intervention remains the primary modality for weight management, ranging from standard hypocaloric diets to intensive medical nutrition therapy utilizing formula diets. Two specific categories of energy-restricted diets are utilized for rapid and substantial weight loss: very low calorie diets (VLCDs), providing fewer than 800 kcal/day, and low-energy diets (LEDs), typically providing approximately 800 kcal/day or slightly more, often delivered as liquid formulas.

Historically, clinicians have questioned whether the metabolic milieu of T2DM—characterized by insulin resistance and altered substrate oxidation—impedes weight loss efficacy compared to non-diabetic individuals. This systematic review aims to evaluate the comparative effectiveness of these intensive dietary strategies, specifically contrasting weight loss outcomes between obese patients with T2DM and those without. Understanding these distinctions is vital for tailoring patient expectations and optimizing clinical management protocols.

## Methods

### Data Sources and Study Selection
This review synthesized data from a provided corpus of clinical studies, randomized controlled trials (RCTs), systematic reviews, and meta-analyses. Studies were selected if they evaluated VLCD or LED interventions in obese adults (>18 years) with a comparison or control group involving non-diabetic participants, or if they reported outcomes stratified by diabetes status.

### Outcomes of Interest
Primary outcomes included absolute weight loss (kg), change in Body Mass Index (BMI), and percentage excess weight loss. Secondary outcomes included changes in body composition (fat mass vs. fat-free mass), glycemic control (HbA1c, fasting plasma glucose), and cardiometabolic risk factors.

### Data Synthesis
Due to heterogeneity in study designs and reporting, a narrative synthesis with quantitative extraction was performed. Priority was given to randomized controlled trials and prospective studies that directly compared diabetic and non-diabetic cohorts.

## Results

### Comparative Weight Loss: Diabetic vs. Non-Diabetic Patients

A pivotal study addressing the core query of this review was conducted by Han et al. (2012), which directly compared weight loss and body composition changes in obese subjects with and without T2DM during a 24-week VLCD program. In a study population of 70 weight-matched subjects, intention-to-treat analysis revealed no statistically significant difference in weight loss between the two groups at 24 weeks. The per-protocol analysis confirmed near-identical weight change trajectories, with the diabetes group losing 8.5 ± 1.3 kg and the control group losing 9.4 ± 1.2 kg (P = .64) ([Han et al., 2012](url)).

These findings are corroborated by the early work of Wing et al. (1998), which evaluated an intensive weight loss program including a VLCD in patients with established T2DM and non-diabetic controls. At the 1-year follow-up, non-diabetic participants reduced their BMI by 6 kg/m², while diabetic participants reduced their BMI by 5 kg/m². While the non-diabetic group showed a slightly numerically superior reduction, the difference was not clinically substantial, indicating that patients with diabetes can achieve comparable weight loss success under intensive intervention ([Wing et al., 1998](url)).

Further support is found in a multicenter trial by Miyashita et al. (2013), which evaluated a low-caloric diet with partial use of a formula diet (MicroDiet®) in obese type 2 diabetic patients. While this study compared a formula diet group to a conventional diet group rather than a non-diabetic control, it highlighted that diabetic patients utilizing formula replacements lost significantly more weight (-3.5 kg) compared to those on a conventional subcaloric diet (-1.4 kg) over 24 weeks, demonstrating the efficacy of formula diets in this population ([Miyashita et al., 2013](url)).

### Body Composition Dynamics

While total weight loss appears comparable, the physiological response to caloric restriction differs. Han et al. (2012) provided critical insight into body composition, finding that while total weight loss was similar, there was "less fat reduction per unit weight loss in type 2 diabetic compared with nondiabetic obese individuals." The change in fat mass index correlated with BMI change in both groups (diabetes: r = 0.878; control: r = 0.920), but the slope of this relationship was attenuated in the diabetic group. This suggests that patients with T2DM may lose more lean tissue or water relative to fat compared to non-diabetics during rapid weight loss, a factor that necessitates careful monitoring of body composition in clinical practice ([Han et al., 2012](url)).

### Efficacy of VLCDs and Formula Diets in T2DM

The reviewed literature consistently demonstrates the superior efficacy of VLCDs and LEDs over conventional dietary advice for weight loss in T2DM. A case report by Paisey et al. (2000) documented a 40-year-old woman with T2DM and a BMI of 40 kg/m² who lost 18 kg over 9 months on a VLCD. This intervention was profound enough to allow the discontinuation of all medications, achieving normoglycaemia, normotension, and normolipidaemia ([Paisey et al., 2000](url)).

Similarly, an observational study by Ahlqvist et al. (2014) evaluated a 4-week VLCD (Slanka) in 25 obese subjects, 7 of whom had T2DM. The study noted improved metabolic control, including reductions in fasting plasma glucose and HbA1c, alongside weight loss. The efficacy was noted in both the diabetic and non-diabetic subsets, confirming the utility of VLCDs as a preoperative or standalone weight management tool ([Ahlqvist et al., 2014](url)).

In the context of bariatric surgery, the use of preoperative VLCDs has been shown to predict long-term success. A study by Tsuda et al. (2025) involving 198 participants found that preoperative glycemic response to a VLCD (600 kcal/day) predicted diabetes remission. Patients achieving blood glucose levels below 143 mg/dL on the second day of the VLCD had a >70% likelihood of complete remission 2 years post-surgery ([Tsuda et al., 2025](url)).

### Metabolic and Cardiovascular Outcomes

The impact of intensive diet therapy extends beyond weight loss. Wing et al. (1998) reported that diabetic patients on a VLCD experienced significant reductions in waist-hip ratios (-0.05) and increases in the HDL/total cholesterol ratio (+0.06), indicating improved atherosclerosis risk profiles. Notably, 14 diabetic subjects in the VLCD group were able to discontinue insulin ([Wing et al., 1998](url)).

A more recent umbrella review by Wharton et al. (2022) synthesized data on diets for weight management in adults with T2DM. It highlighted that total diet replacement (TDR) programs, typically utilizing low-energy formulas, result in significant weight loss and remission of diabetes. The review emphasized that weight reduction is fundamental for T2DM management and that intensive dietary approaches like VLCDs are among the most effective strategies for achieving remission ([Wharton et al., 2022](url)).

Comparative analyses also exist regarding the mechanism of weight loss. A study by Honisett et al. (2021) compared brain activation patterns in weight loss via VLCD versus gastric bypass. While the VLCD group achieved significant weight loss, the brain's response to food cues differed from surgical patients, suggesting that adherence and physiological adaptation might vary by intervention type ([Honisett et al., 2021](url)). Furthermore, Hoeks et al. (2025) demonstrated that weight loss induced by behavioral diet therapy (VLCD) resulted in a doubling of insulin-mediated glucose disposal, whereas surgical weight loss did not change this parameter, highlighting a potentially superior metabolic advantage to diet-induced weight loss in some contexts ([Hoeks et al., 2025](url)).

## Discussion

### Equivalence in Weight Loss Potential
The synthesis of available data allows for a concrete clinical conclusion: **Obese patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus are capable of achieving weight loss magnitudes comparable to their non-diabetic counterparts when adhering to very low calorie or low-energy formula diets.** The findings of Han et al. (2012) effectively dispel the historical clinical misconception that insulin resistance or diabetic pharmacotherapy inevitably blunts weight loss potential. The caloric deficit induced by VLCDs appears to override the metabolic conserving mechanisms typical of T2DM to a sufficient degree that total weight loss trajectories converge with those of non-diabetics.

### Distinctions in Tissue Loss and Metabolism
While total mass loss is similar, the quality of weight loss differs. The finding of reduced fat mass loss per unit weight loss in diabetics is significant. This may be attributed to altered oxidative priorities, fluid shifts, or the loss of lean mass. Clinicians must be vigilant in recommending adequate protein intake and resistance training during VLCD protocols for diabetic patients to preserve lean body mass, a concern highlighted in general VLCD reviews ([The impact and utility of very low-calorie diets, 2023](url)).

### Clinical Implications for Diabetes Remission
The data strongly support the role of VLCDs in achieving diabetes remission. The Direct and DiRECT-adjacent trials summarized in the umbrella review by Wharton et al. (2022) and the specific remission data from Paisey et al. (2000) and Wing et al. (1998) demonstrate that substantial weight loss via these diets can reverse the underlying pathophysiology of T2DM. The "remission" rate correlates strongly with the magnitude of weight loss, which is achievable via VLCD/LED protocols.

### Comparison with Other Interventions
It is important to contextualize these diets against other medical interventions. While VLCDs are effective, surgical interventions generally yield greater absolute weight loss. However, comparing behavioral diet therapy (VLCD) to gastric bypass, Hoeks et al. (2025) found that diet-induced weight loss doubled insulin-mediated glucose disposal, a metabolic benefit not seen to the same extent in the surgical group ([Hoeks et al., 2025](url)). This suggests that while surgery yields more weight loss, the *metabolic quality* of the weight loss via diet may have specific glycemic advantages.

### Safety and Tolerability
Safety is a paramount concern with VLCDs, particularly in T2DM where hypoglycemia is a risk if medications are not adjusted. The reviewed studies, such as Ahlqvist et al. (2014) and Wing et al. (1998), indicate that with proper medical supervision, these diets are safe. The transient changes in blood pressure and antioxidant status noted by Wing et al. (1998) were manageable ([Wing et al., 1998](url)). Furthermore, patient preferences are evolving; a survey by Heald et al. (2025) indicated that while Continuous Low Energy Diets (CLED) are standard, patients express interest in Intermittent Low Energy Diets (ILED), suggesting a need for flexible delivery models to enhance adherence ([Heald et al., 2025](url)).

## Conclusion

This systematic review concludes that very low calorie diets and low-energy liquid-formula diets are highly effective for weight loss in both obese patients with type 2 diabetes and those without. The presence of T2DM does not constitute a barrier to achieving clinically significant weight reduction. However, the physiological response is distinct, with diabetic patients potentially exhibiting a different body composition profile during weight loss.

The evidence supports the integration of VLCDs and LEDs as a first-line intensive intervention for T2DM, particularly for patients seeking remission. The primary determinant of success appears to be adherence to the caloric restriction rather than the diabetic status itself. Future research should focus on long-term maintenance strategies post-VLCD and strategies to optimize body composition preservation in the diabetic population during rapid weight loss.
