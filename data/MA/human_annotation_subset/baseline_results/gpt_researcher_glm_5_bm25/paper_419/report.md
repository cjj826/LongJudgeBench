# Effectiveness of Internet- and Mobile-Based Interventions for Improving Mental Health in Adults with Overweight/Obesity and Comorbid Depressive Symptoms

## Introduction
The global burden of obesity and depression represents a significant public health challenge, with the two conditions frequently presenting as comorbidities. Individuals with obesity are at a heightened risk of developing depressive symptoms, a relationship that is often bidirectional, where each condition exacerbates the severity and treatment resistance of the other. This comorbidity is linked to poorer health outcomes, including an increased risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, and a higher mortality rate. Traditional face-to-face interventions, while effective, often face barriers such as limited accessibility, stigma, and a shortage of trained specialists. Consequently, there has been a growing interest in internet- and mobile-based interventions (IMIs) as scalable, accessible, and cost-effective alternatives for delivering psychological support to this vulnerable population. This report systematically evaluates the effectiveness of internet- and mobile-based interventions in reducing depressive symptom severity in adults with overweight or obesity who experience comorbid depressive symptoms. Drawing upon high-quality systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and randomized controlled trials (RCTs), the analysis examines clinical outcomes, intervention characteristics, and the quality of the available evidence.

## Methodological Considerations and Evidence Base
The evaluation of IMIs for comorbid depression and obesity relies on a diverse body of evidence ranging from large-scale meta-analyses to specific RCT protocols. Methodological quality varies across studies, with recent publications (2024–2026) generally adhering to more rigorous standards such as the PRISMA guidelines and the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool. The evidence specifically targeting individuals with both conditions, while growing, remains less extensive than research focusing on depression or obesity alone. Therefore, this report prioritizes studies specifically designed for the comorbid population while integrating findings from broader reviews on digital health for depression and weight management where applicable.

## Effectiveness of Digital Interventions on Depressive Symptoms

### Overall Efficacy of Digital Mental Health Interventions
The broader literature on digital mental health interventions provides a strong foundation for their efficacy. A systematic review and meta-analysis of digital health interventions for university students found significant reductions in stress, anxiety, and depression ([Fang et al., 2026](local_corpus)). Similarly, a meta-analysis focusing on mobile applications for patients with severe mental illness demonstrated that mobile app interventions significantly reduced disease-related symptoms of depression and anxiety ([Wong et al., 2022](local_corpus)). These findings establish that digital delivery of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) and other evidence-based psychological treatments is a viable modality for reducing depressive symptom severity in general adult populations.

### Efficacy in the Comorbid Population
Direct evidence for the effectiveness of IMIs in adults with both obesity and depressive symptoms is promising but highlights specific nuances regarding intervention intensity and duration.

**RCT Evidence:** A randomized controlled trial investigating a self-guided eHealth program (SHED-IT: Recharge) for men with overweight/obesity and depressive symptoms (PHQ-9 ≥5) found that the intervention was effective in reducing both weight and depressive symptoms. The study, which compared the intervention group to a wait-list control group over a 3-month period, demonstrated significant improvements in mental health outcomes, suggesting that tailored eHealth programs can successfully address both physical and psychological parameters simultaneously ([Morgan et al., 2021](local_corpus)). Similarly, a pilot randomized controlled trial evaluated two versions of a digital behavioral health product, Vira, among young adults with depressive symptoms and obesity risk factors. The study compared a self-guided version (Vira Self-Care) with a coach-supported version (Vira+Coaching). Results indicated that both versions were feasible and acceptable, with preliminary effectiveness in improving mental health. Notably, the version with coach support showed higher engagement, underscoring the potential value of human augmentation in digital interventions ([Kornfield et al., 2024](local_corpus)).

**Internet-Based CBT (iCBT):** Internet-based cognitive behavioral therapy has shown particular promise. A study on the "Lighten Your Life Program," which combined an educational support group with a mobile app (Pacifica) for managing depressive symptoms and chronic pain (often comorbid with obesity), found statistically significant reductions in Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) scores over time ([Chen et al., 2019](local_corpus)). Furthermore, a retrospective study of a 12-week, therapist-supported, app-based CBT program in a "polychronic" population (adults with obesity, type 2 diabetes, and depression) reported significant improvements in depressive symptoms among those who completed the follow-up assessment ([Newton et al., 2022](local_corpus)).

**Protocol and Ongoing Trials:** The growing interest in this field is evidenced by several study protocols aimed at rigorously evaluating these interventions. The "LightMood" trial protocol describes a manualized, user-centered digital intervention for patients with obesity, containing elements of CBT and mindfulness, aimed at reducing depression symptoms ([Bentz et al., 2024](local_corpus)). Another protocol, the "@ktivPLUS" research project, outlines a randomized controlled trial for an internet-based self-help intervention, "Fit4Mood," specifically tailored for adults with obesity ([Schmutzer et al., 2025](local_corpus)). These studies emphasize tailoring interventions to the specific needs of the target group to improve adherence and acceptance.

### Specific Depressive Symptoms and Obesity
The relationship between specific depressive symptoms and obesity has been explored to refine intervention targets. A study analyzing data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS) found that elevated depressive symptoms were associated with an increased risk of incident diabetes, with overweight or obesity mediating this relationship. Symptoms such as loneliness and restless sleep were identified as significant contributors ([Jiang et al., 2025](local_corpus)). This suggests that digital interventions could potentially be optimized by targeting these specific symptom clusters, such as through sleep hygiene modules or social connectivity features often found in comprehensive digital platforms.

## Impact on Weight and Cardiometabolic Outcomes
While the primary focus of this review is depressive symptom severity, the bidirectional nature of the comorbidity necessitates an examination of weight-related outcomes. Improvements in weight often correlate with improvements in mood, and vice versa. A systematic review and meta-analysis of behavior change resources in mobile app-based interventions for adults with overweight and obesity found that mobile phone apps yielded significant reductions in body weight (Mean Difference [MD] = -1.32 kg) and fat mass ([Chen et al., 2025](local_corpus)). Another umbrella review confirmed that mobile app interventions resulted in significant weight loss (MD = -1.32 kg) and noted that long-term eHealth interventions were effective ([Lee et al., 2025](local_corpus)). In the context of comorbid serious mental illness (SMI), a systematic review of digital health interventions for cardiometabolic risk reduction found that most studies reported improvements in depressive symptoms, physical activity, diet, and cardiometabolic indicators like weight and A1c ([Hoffmann et al., 2025](local_corpus)). This supports the utility of integrated digital interventions that target lifestyle behaviors to improve mental health outcomes in populations at high cardiometabolic risk.

## Comparative Effectiveness and Intervention Characteristics

### Standalone vs. Human-Supported Interventions
A critical distinction in the effectiveness of IMIs is the level of human support. Standalone digital interventions offer high scalability but may suffer from lower adherence. A meta-analysis of stand-alone digital mental health applications for anxiety and depression found a significant therapeutic effect, supporting their viability ([Firth et al., 2023](local_corpus)). However, research in the comorbid population often favors guided or supported interventions. The Vira pilot study highlighted that while both self-guided and coach-supported versions were effective, the coach-supported version likely benefits from enhanced accountability ([Kornfield et al., 2024](local_corpus)). This aligns with broader findings in digital health, where "human-centered" or highly interactive tools often show higher effectiveness. For instance, a review of digital tobacco cessation modalities found that highly interactive tools had higher effectiveness at 6 months compared to purely automated ones ([Gomis-Berger et al., 2025](local_corpus)).

### Theoretical Basis
Theoretical frameworks, particularly CBT, dominate the landscape of effective digital interventions for this population. The efficacy of CBT in treating depression is well-established, and its translation to digital formats (iCBT) has been validated in numerous studies ([Hedman et al., 2014](local_corpus); [Cuijpers et al., 2024](local_corpus)). Interventions like "Fit4Mood" and "LightMood" are explicitly based on CBT principles, often integrating behavioral activation techniques that are relevant to both depression (e.g., increasing engagement) and obesity (e.g., increasing physical activity) ([Schmutzer et al., 2025](local_corpus); [Bentz et al., 2024](local_corpus)).

## Quality of Evidence
The quality of evidence varies by outcome and study design.
1.  **RCTs:** High-quality RCTs, such as the SHED-IT: Recharge trial, provide direct evidence of efficacy for weight and depression outcomes in men ([Morgan et al., 2021](local_corpus)). However, many studies in this specific subfield are pilot trials (e.g., Vira trial) with smaller sample sizes, which limits the statistical power and generalizability of the findings ([Kornfield et al., 2024](local_corpus)).
2.  **Meta-Analyses and Systematic Reviews:** Reviews like those by Lee et al. (2025) and Chen et al. (2025) provide high-quality evidence for the effect of digital tools on weight loss, which supports the potential for holistic management ([Lee et al., 2025](local_corpus); [Chen et al., 2025](local_corpus)). The review on digital interventions for university students by Fang et al. (2026) adheres to rigorous meta-analytic standards ([Fang et al., 2026](local_corpus)).
3.  **Study Protocols:** While protocols (e.g., for the Fit4Mood or LightMood trials) do not provide outcome data, they indicate a trend toward more rigorous, confirmatory trials in the field, adhering to SPIRIT and CONSORT guidelines ([Schmutzer et al., 2025](local_corpus); [Bentz et al., 2024](local_corpus)).

**Heterogeneity and Limitations:** A significant limitation across the evidence base is high heterogeneity in intervention components, control groups, and outcome measures. This is frequently noted in meta-analyses (e.g., high I² statistics in weight loss reviews) ([Lee et al., 2025](local_corpus)). Additionally, there is a risk of selection bias in digital health studies, as participants are often more technologically literate and motivated. Long-term sustainability of effects beyond the immediate intervention period remains a gap, though ongoing trials are increasingly incorporating longer follow-up periods.

## Conclusion
Based on the systematic evaluation of the available literature, internet- and mobile-based interventions represent a promising and evidence-based approach for reducing depressive symptom severity in adults with overweight or obesity. The evidence supports the effectiveness of digital CBT and behavioral activation programs, particularly those that integrate weight management components with psychological support. The current consensus suggests that while standalone digital interventions can be effective, interventions with some degree of human support (coaching or therapist guidance) may offer superior adherence and potentially better outcomes in this complex population. The quality of evidence is bolstered by several randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses, though the field is characterized by heterogeneity in intervention design. Future research should focus on long-term maintenance of effects and the personalization of digital interventions to address the specific behavioral and psychological needs of individuals with comorbid depression and obesity.
