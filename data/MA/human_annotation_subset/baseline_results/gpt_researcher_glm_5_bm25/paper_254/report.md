# Meta-Analysis of Cortical Thickness Abnormalities in Medication-Free Patients with Major Depressive Disorder

## Introduction

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) represents a significant global health burden, characterized by persistent low mood, anhedonia, and cognitive deficits. Neuroimaging techniques, particularly structural Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), have been extensively utilized to elucidate the neurobiological substrates of this disorder. Among various morphometric measures, cortical thickness—a sensitive marker of cortical integrity reflecting the distance between the white matter and pial surfaces—has emerged as a crucial metric for understanding the neuropathophysiology of MDD.

Investigating medication-free patients is of paramount importance in psychiatric neuroimaging. Pharmacological treatments, particularly antidepressants, are known to exert neuroplastic effects, potentially confounding the interpretation of structural alterations. For instance, Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT), a potent intervention for treatment-resistant depression, has been shown to induce significant bilateral increases in cortical thickness, particularly in the temporal pole, inferior and middle temporal cortex, and the insula ([Bilateral ECT induces bilateral increases in regional cortical thickness](local_corpus)). Similarly, longitudinal studies on antidepressant use indicate complex interactions with age and brain structure ([Regional brain morphology and current antidepressant use: findings from 32 international cohorts from the ENIGMA major depressive disorder working group](local_corpus)). Therefore, focusing on medication-naive or medication-free populations allows researchers to isolate disease-specific pathology from treatment-induced plasticity.

This report synthesizes findings from a broad corpus of literature to investigate whether medication-free patients with MDD exhibit alterations in cortical thickness compared to healthy controls (HC). Furthermore, it examines the associations between these morphological changes and key demographic and clinical variables, aiming to provide a comprehensive overview of the structural landscape of depression in the unmedicated state.

## Methodological Considerations in Cortical Thickness Analysis

Understanding the methodological landscape is essential for interpreting the heterogeneity in findings. The provided literature primarily utilizes two approaches: Voxel-Based Morphometry (VBM) and Surface-Based Morphometry (SBM), often implemented via the FreeSurfer software suite. SBM is generally preferred for measuring cortical thickness as it provides a direct estimate of the cortical mantle's width, whereas VBM typically assesses gray matter volume or density, which can be influenced by cortical folding ([Gray Matter Abnormalities in Non-comorbid Medication-naive Patients with Major Depressive Disorder or Social Anxiety Disorder](local_corpus)).

Recent meta-analyses, such as the preregistered analysis by the ENIGMA consortium and other collaborative groups, have emphasized the necessity of large sample sizes to overcome issues of statistical power and reproducibility that plagued earlier, smaller studies. The standardization provided by consortia like ENIGMA allows for the harmonization of data across multiple international sites, providing more robust estimates of effect sizes.

## Global and Regional Cortical Thickness Alterations

The literature presents a complex and sometimes contradictory picture of cortical thickness alterations in medication-free MDD patients. However, converging evidence from large-scale studies and meta-analyses points towards specific regional vulnerabilities, primarily involving the fronto-limbic networks.

### Cortical Thinning in MDD

The most consistent finding across multiple studies and meta-analyses is the presence of cortical thinning in patients with MDD compared to healthy controls. A prominent 2025 meta-analysis examining shared cortical characteristics across MDD, anxiety disorders, and chronic pain identified four common clusters of significant cortical thinning. These regions included the **right insula**, **left anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)**, triangular part of the **left inferior frontal gyrus (IFG)**, and the **left middle temporal gyrus (MTG)** ([Shared cortical characteristics in major depressive disorder, anxiety disorder, and chronic pain: a structural MRI meta-analysis study](local_corpus)). The identification of the insula and ACC is particularly noteworthy given their established roles in interoception, emotional regulation, and the "salience network" of the brain.

Further supporting these findings, a cross-sectional study of first-episode MDD patients reported cortical thinning in the **right dorsomedial prefrontal cortex (PFC)** and **superior temporal gyrus**. Crucially, this study employed a multi-modal approach, demonstrating that these local cortical thinning patterns were linked to resting-state functional disconnectivity, specifically affecting the default mode network and fronto-parietal network ([Local cortical thinning links to resting-state disconnectivity in major depressive disorder](local_corpus)).

A study focusing on first-episode MDD patients found that the cortex was thinner in the **frontal, temporal, parietal, and insular regions**. This study also noted that subcortical volumes (putamen and amygdala) tended to be smaller, and there were altered associations between cortical thickness and subcortical volumes, suggesting a decoupling of structural covariance networks early in the illness course ([Altered patterns of association between cortical thickness and subcortical volume in patients with first episode major depressive disorder: A structural MRI study](local_corpus)).

In late-life depression, cortical thinning has also been observed. A study on patients with late-life minor depression found thinning in the **right anterior cingulate cortex** and **left medial orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)**, regions critical for mood regulation and executive function ([Cortical thinning in patients with late-life minor depression](local_corpus)).

### Reports of Cortical Thickening

Interestingly, the literature also contains reports of increased cortical thickness in medication-naive, first-episode patients, presenting an apparent paradox. A study investigating first-episode, treatment-naïve, mid-life MDD patients observed **increased cortical thickness** in the right hemisphere, specifically in the **medial orbitofrontal gyrus, pars opercularis, rostral middle frontal gyrus, and supramarginal gyrus**. Notably, the increased thickness of the rostral middle frontal gyrus was negatively correlated with depression severity on the Hamilton Depression Rating Scale, suggesting a potential compensatory mechanism in the early stages of the disorder ([Regional increases of cortical thickness in untreated, first-episode major depressive disorder](local_corpus)).

Similarly, another study of untreated first-episode MDD patients found increased thickness in the **right orbital frontal cortex** and the **left inferior parietal gyrus**, though this thickening did not correlate with anhedonia scores ([Increased prefrontal and parietal cortical thickness does not correlate with anhedonia in patients with untreated first-episode major depressive disorders](local_corpus)). A surface-based morphometry study also reported increased thickness in the **right posterior cingulate region** and **parietal cortex**, alongside decreased thickness in other parietal sub-regions ([Surface vulnerability of cerebral cortex to major depressive disorder](local_corpus)).

These findings of increased thickness may reflect neurodevelopmental variations, acute neuroinflammatory responses, or compensatory plasticity in the early phase of illness, contrasting with the neurodegenerative thinning observed in chronic or recurrent depression.

## Association with Demographic Factors

### Age and Developmental Trajectories

The relationship between cortical thickness and age in MDD is non-linear and complex. The ENIGMA-MDD working group's analysis of 32 international cohorts provided crucial insights. They found that **younger individuals** taking antidepressants showed lower bilateral middle temporal gyrus thickness compared to non-medicated patients and healthy controls, a finding not observed in older individuals ([Regional brain morphology and current antidepressant use: findings from 32 international cohorts from the ENIGMA major depressive disorder working group](local_corpus)).

In adolescents and emerging adults, distinct patterns emerge. A study on emerging adults (18-25 years) with first-episode MDD found that compared to HCs, patients exhibited increased cortical thickness in the **left lateral occipital cortex**, which correlated positively with childhood trauma scores ([Surface-based analysis of early cortical gyrification and thickness alterations in treatment-Naïve, first-episode depressive patients during emerging adulthood](local_corpus)). Furthermore, baseline cortical thickness has been shown to predict the first onset of depression in adolescence with 70% accuracy, suggesting that structural deviations precede clinical symptoms ([Cortical thickness predicts the first onset of major depression in adolescence](local_corpus)).

### Sex Differences

Sex-specific alterations are increasingly recognized as a critical factor in MDD neurobiology. A study of 123 treatment-naïve MDD patients revealed significant sex-by-group interactions. While both sexes showed higher local gyrification indices in the posterior cortex compared to controls, **female patients** specifically showed significantly lower surface area in the left ventrolateral prefrontal cortex and inferior temporal cortex. **Male patients**, conversely, showed a unique pattern of increased cortical thickness in the right lateral orbitofrontal cortex ([Sex-specific alterations of cortical morphometry in treatment-naïve patients with major depressive disorder](local_corpus)).

These findings are corroborated by studies on suicidal ideation, which found distinct sex-specific cortical structural changes in MDD patients ([Sex Differences in Cortical Structural Alterations in Major Depressive Disorder With Suicidal Ideation](local_corpus)).

## Association with Clinical Characteristics

### Depression Severity and Symptom Dimensions

The relationship between cortical thickness and global depression severity is not always linear, prompting researchers to investigate specific symptom dimensions.

*   **Anhedonia:** Anhedonia, the inability to feel pleasure, is a core symptom. However, in untreated first-episode patients, increased prefrontal and parietal thickness did not correlate with anhedonia scores measured by the Temporal Experience of Pleasure Scale ([Increased prefrontal and parietal cortical thickness does not correlate with anhedonia in patients with untreated first-episode major depressive disorders](local_corpus)). Conversely, functional connectivity studies have linked anhedonia to abnormal connectivity of the superior temporal gyrus ([Anhedonia correlates with abnormal functional connectivity of the superior temporal gyrus and the caudate nucleus in patients with first-episode drug-naive major depressive disorder](local_corpus)). Furthermore, a 2025 study utilizing 7T MRI found specific cortical thickness alterations in high anhedonia MDD patients, linking these changes to specific gene expression profiles ([Transcriptional substrates of cortical thickness alterations in anhedonia of major depressive disorder](local_corpus)).
*   **Suicidality:** Structural alterations are prominent in MDD patients with suicidality. Widespread decreases in cortical thickness have been noted in suicide attempters with MDD ([Distinct neural correlates between suicide attempters with major depressive disorder and other psychiatric disorders: a multimodal imaging study](local_corpus)). In adolescents with MDD and suicidality, abnormal coupling between glymphatic activity and brain structure-function was observed, implicating neuroinflammatory processes ([Abnormal coupling between glymphatic activity and brain structure-function in adolescents with major depressive disorder and suicidality](local_corpus)). Additionally, non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI) in youth with mood disorders has been linked to anterior cingulate cortex thickness ([Cingulate cortex cortical thickness associated with non-suicidal self-injury and suicide risk in youth with mood disorders](local_corpus)).
*   **Melancholic Features:** A study comparing first-episode drug-naive patients with melancholic vs. non-melancholic depression found no significant group differences in cortical thickness. However, in the melancholic group, the cortical thickness of the **left postcentral gyrus and right precuneus** was negatively correlated with somatic anxiety scores ([Changes of cortical thickness in the first episode, drug-naive depression patients with and without melancholic features](local_corpus)).

### Childhood Trauma

Childhood maltreatment (CM) significantly moderates cortical morphology in MDD. In a study of early adults with MDD, those with a history of CM exhibited significantly **reduced bilateral precuneus volume and left cuneus thickness** compared to MDD patients without CM. This suggests that trauma imposes an additional layer of structural deficit, possibly through stress-induced mechanisms ([Childhood maltreatment-related cortical structural differences between treatment-naïve early adults with major depressive disorder and healthy controls](local_corpus)).

### Biotype and Diagnostic Prediction

Recent research has attempted to identify neuroanatomical dimensions to classify MDD heterogeneity. The COORDINATE-MDD consortium utilized machine learning to identify neuroanatomical biotypes. One such biotype showed blunted treatment response to SSRIs, suggesting that structural alterations may predict clinical outcomes ([Neuroanatomical dimensions in medication-free individuals with major depressive disorder and treatment response to SSRI antidepressant medications or placebo](local_corpus)).

Differentiating MDD from Bipolar Disorder (BD) is a significant clinical challenge. Cortical thickness signatures have been explored to predict the transition from MDD to BD. Patients who transitioned to BD showed distinct patterns, sharing similarities with BD patients, particularly cortical thinning in the **left inferior temporal cortex** ([Cortical thickness signature as a predictor of diagnostic transition from major depressive disorder to bipolar disorder in late adolescence and early adulthood: a prospective nested case-control study](local_corpus)). Machine learning models have achieved moderate success in distinguishing MDD from BD using cortical morphological features ([Machine learning-based assessment of morphometric abnormalities distinguishes bipolar disorder and major depressive disorder](local_corpus)).

## Causality and Biological Mechanisms

Establishing causality remains a challenge. A Mendelian Randomization study provided evidence for a causal association, demonstrating that MDD causes a **decrease in cortical thickness in the medial orbitofrontal region**. This study also identified associations with inflammatory markers like C-reactive protein and tumor necrosis factor, suggesting a pathway involving neuroinflammation ([Causal Association Between Major Depressive Disorder and Cortical Structure: A Bidirectional Mendelian Randomization Study and Mediation Analysis](local_corpus)).

Furthermore, the "Virtual Histology" approach has linked cortical thickness differences to specific gene expression profiles. In MDD, the profile of cortical thinning was associated with genes expressed in specific cell types, suggesting that glial and neuronal dysfunction underpins the observed macroscopic changes ([Virtual Histology of Cortical Thickness and Shared Neurobiology in 6 Psychiatric Disorders](local_corpus)).

## Discussion and Conclusion

The meta-analytic synthesis of the provided literature confirms that medication-free patients with MDD exhibit significant alterations in cortical thickness, though the pattern is heterogeneous. The weight of evidence, particularly from recent large-scale meta-analyses and consortium data, supports the presence of **cortical thinning in key paralimbic and prefrontal regions**—specifically the anterior cingulate cortex, insula, and temporal cortices. These regions are integral to emotional regulation, salience detection, and cognitive control, and their structural compromise likely underpins the core affective and cognitive symptoms of depression.

However, the findings of **increased cortical thickness** in specific prefrontal and parietal regions in first-episode, treatment-naïve patients cannot be dismissed. These opposing findings likely reflect the **heterogeneity of the disorder** and the dynamic nature of brain changes across the illness trajectory. It is plausible that early-stage depression involves initial hyperplasia or compensatory thickening in regulatory regions (like the dorsolateral prefrontal and parietal cortices), which eventually gives way to neurodegenerative thinning with chronicity, recurrence, or in the presence of specific risk factors like childhood trauma.

Demographic and clinical variables significantly moderate these structural alterations. **Age** is a critical factor, with distinct patterns in adolescents, emerging adults, and elderly populations. **Sex differences** are evident, suggesting that the neurobiological mechanisms of depression differ between males and females. Clinical characteristics, particularly **anhedonia, suicidality, and childhood trauma**, are associated with specific regional deficits. The causal link between MDD and cortical thinning, particularly in the medial orbitofrontal region, is supported by genetic evidence, emphasizing the role of inflammatory pathways.

In conclusion, cortical thickness serves as a valuable, though complex, biomarker for MDD in the medication-free state. Future research should prioritize longitudinal designs in drug-naive cohorts to track the evolution of these structural changes and integrate multi-modal data (genetic, inflammatory, and functional) to disentangle the compensatory versus pathological nature of observed alterations. The identification of distinct neuroanatomical subtypes ("biotypes") represents a promising step towards precision psychiatry, potentially guiding treatment selection based on structural brain profiles.
