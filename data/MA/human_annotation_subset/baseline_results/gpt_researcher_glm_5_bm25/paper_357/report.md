# Systematic Review of Postmortem Evidence for Neuroinflammatory Markers in Schizophrenia

## Introduction
Schizophrenia is a severe neuropsychiatric disorder characterized by complex interactions between genetic susceptibility and environmental factors. In recent decades, the neuroinflammatory hypothesis of schizophrenia has gained substantial traction, suggesting that immune dysregulation and chronic low-grade inflammation within the central nervous system (CNS) may contribute to the disorder's pathogenesis. Neuroinflammation refers to inflammatory processes occurring within the brain parenchyma, involving the activation of resident immune cells such as microglia and astrocytes, the release of cytokines and chemokines, and potential compromise of the blood-brain barrier (BBB).

Postmortem brain studies provide a unique and direct window into the neuropathological substrate of schizophrenia. While in vivo neuroimaging techniques like positron emission tomography (PET) with translocator protein (TSPO) ligands and diffusion magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) offer valuable insights, they are indirect measures. Postmortem investigations allow for the precise quantification of cellular markers, protein expression, and gene transcripts that are otherwise inaccessible in living patients.

This systematic review synthesizes postmortem evidence regarding alterations in neuroinflammatory markers—including microglia, astrocytes, cytokines, and related molecular pathways—in individuals with schizophrenia compared to healthy controls. The evidence is evaluated to determine the validity of the neuroinflammatory hypothesis and to explore the potential heterogeneity of inflammatory subtypes within the disorder.

## Methodological Landscape of Postmortem Neuroinflammatory Research
The interpretation of postmortem neuroinflammatory findings in schizophrenia requires a critical understanding of the inherent methodological challenges. The literature is marked by heterogeneity and, at times, inconsistency. Several key factors influence the reliability and comparability of these studies.

First, sample characteristics vary significantly. Studies often differ in sample size, age of subjects, duration of illness, and cause of death. Crucially, the effects of antipsychotic medication and comorbid substance use disorder (SUD) represent significant confounding variables. For instance, research has demonstrated that antipsychotic-treated subjects exhibit increased α2A-adrenoceptor expression in the prefrontal cortex compared to antipsychotic-free subjects, suggesting that treatment status can alter molecular profiles (Trémeau et al., 2021). Similarly, lipidomic and proteomic analyses of extracellular vesicles (EVs) from the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) reveal that SUD induces distinct neuroinflammatory signatures, complicating the interpretation of primary psychiatric pathology (Trask et al., 2025).

Second, technical factors such as tissue pH, RNA integrity number (RIN), postmortem interval (PMI), and storage duration can profoundly impact gene expression profiles. A recent study emphasized that pH and RIN significantly affect immune-related genes, necessitating rigorous control for these variables to avoid spurious associations (Prasad et al., 2025). Furthermore, the reliability of data has been questioned in specific cases; for example, a prominent study reporting increased neuroinflammatory and arachidonic acid cascade markers was retracted due to research misconduct, highlighting the need for verification of findings (Rao et al., 2013).

Finally, the selection of brain regions varies. The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC), anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), hippocampus, and temporal cortex are most frequently studied due to their implicated roles in the cognitive and emotional deficits of schizophrenia. However, findings are not always uniform across regions, suggesting localized inflammatory responses.

## Microglial Activation and Markers in Schizophrenia
Microglia are the resident macrophages of the CNS and serve as the primary mediators of neuroinflammation. Their activation results in morphological changes (from ramified to amoeboid) and increased expression of surface markers such as Human Leukocyte Antigen-DR (HLA-DR) and CD68.

### Evidence for Increased Microglial Activity
A significant portion of postmortem literature supports the presence of microglial activation in schizophrenia. An early and foundational study by Radewicz et al. (2000) found a significant 28% increase in HLA-DR+ microglial numerical density in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (area 9) of chronic schizophrenia patients compared to controls. Increases were also noted in the superior temporal gyrus (area 22). These findings were corroborated by Steiner et al. (2006), who investigated HLA-DR expression across multiple regions. They observed region-specific increases, noting that HLA-DR+ cell density was increased in a hemisphere-specific manner, potentially reflecting impaired cerebral lateralization in schizophrenia.

Furthermore, microglial density appears to be linked to clinical variables. A study examining the posterior hippocampus found that microglial activation was more prominent in the "paranoid" subgroup (with prominent positive symptoms) compared to the "residual" subgroup, suggesting that microglial activation may be a state marker associated with acute psychosis rather than a trait marker of the disease itself (Steiner et al., 2012). This aligns with the hypothesis of distinct immunophenotypes.

### Evidence for Macrophage Infiltration and Complement Activation
Recent molecular investigations have expanded the focus from resident microglia to include peripheral immune involvement. A pivotal study by Catts et al. (2020) identified a "high inflammatory biotype" in schizophrenia, characterized by increased transcripts related to phagocytosis (CD163, CD64) and macrophage adhesion (ICAM1) in the midbrain. The density of CD163+ perivascular macrophages was significantly increased, suggesting a breach of the BBB and infiltration of peripheral immune cells. This study also found elevated complement pathway transcripts (C1qA, C3, C4), which are critical for synaptic pruning. Overactivation of the complement system could lead to excessive synaptic elimination, a mechanism proposed to underlie cortical thinning and connectivity deficits in schizophrenia.

### Contrasting Evidence and Null Findings
However, the evidence is not unidirectional. Some earlier studies utilizing ligand binding for peripheral-type benzodiazepine receptors (PBRs), a marker of gliosis, reported decreased binding in several brain areas of schizophrenia patients, suggesting a potential reduction in glial activity or cellular density rather than activation. Additionally, a systematic review of glial markers in suicide noted that while microglial activation is observed in suicides with affective disorders, the pattern in schizophrenia specifically requires nuanced interpretation regarding suicide as a confounding factor (Wang et al., 2024).

## Astrocyte Alterations in Schizophrenia
Astrocytes provide trophic support, regulate neurotransmitter homeostasis, and contribute to BBB integrity. The astrocyte marker Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein (GFAP) is a primary focus of postmortem studies.

### GFAP Expression and Astrocyte Morphology
The findings regarding astrocytes in schizophrenia are notably contradictory. Some studies report increased GFAP expression. For example, increased inflammatory markers identified in the DLPFC included SERPINA3, an astrocyte-derived acute-phase protein (van Kesteren et al., 2017). Furthermore, a 2023 meta-analysis focusing on the S100 gene family found significant up-regulation of several S100 genes (e.g., S100A10, S100A16). These proteins are involved in astrocyte activation and neuroinflammation. Notably, this up-regulation was concentrated in a specific subgroup of patients, reinforcing the concept of an "inflammatory subtype" of schizophrenia (Leanza et al., 2023).

In contrast, other prominent studies report decreased astrocyte density. A study analyzing the subgenual cingulate cortex and callosal genu found a significant decrease in astrocyte density in schizophrenia (Torres-Platas et al., 2013). Similarly, a layer-specific analysis of the DLPFC revealed a 32% reduction in the GFAP-area fraction in layer V, alongside an increase in GFAP-positive cell density, suggesting a shift towards smaller, more numerous astrocytes potentially indicative of a reactive but stressed glial population.

### Reconciling Astrocyte Findings
These discrepancies may be resolved by considering the concept of "reactive astrogliosis" versus "astrocyte pathology." A 2014 study by Catts et al. proposed that astrocyte activation is specifically associated with the subgroup of patients exhibiting high neuroinflammation. They found that GFAP mRNA and protein levels were only elevated in patients who also showed increased inflammatory cytokine expression, suggesting that reactive astrocytes are a feature of a specific inflammatory phenotype rather than a universal finding in schizophrenia (Catts et al., 2014). This aligns with the general observation that while total glial cell numbers often appear normal, specific markers reveal complex, disease-specific patterns of change (Zhang et al., 2022).

## Cytokine and Molecular Inflammatory Markers
Beyond cellular morphology, the quantification of cytokine transcripts and proteins provides direct evidence of inflammatory signaling.

### Pro-inflammatory Cytokine Elevation
Increased transcripts of pro-inflammatory cytokines are a consistent finding in multiple brain regions. A study utilizing Next Generation Sequencing (SOLiD) on the DLPFC identified 798 differentially regulated transcripts, with pathway analysis highlighting the inflammatory response. Specific increases in IL-6, IL-8, and IL-1β were confirmed via qPCR (van Kesteren et al., 2017). This is supported by peripheral findings; a large meta-analysis confirmed elevated blood levels of IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-1RA in acute schizophrenia, providing peripheral validation for central inflammation hypotheses.

### The Kynurenine Pathway
The kynurenine pathway, which links inflammation to neurotransmission, is critically involved. Inflammation activates the enzyme indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO), shifting tryptophan metabolism towards kynurenine. A study by Catts et al. (2020) demonstrated that patients with elevated inflammatory cytokine mRNA in the prefrontal cortex had altered levels of kynurenine pathway enzymes and metabolites, specifically increased kynurenic acid (KYNA). KYNA is an NMDA receptor antagonist, and its elevation is hypothesized to contribute to the glutamatergic dysfunction observed in schizophrenia.

### Viral Associations and Environmental Triggers
Environmental pathogens may trigger or exacerbate neuroinflammation. A 2023 study investigated the role of Cytomegalovirus (CMV), a neurotropic herpesvirus. It found that anti-CMV antibody levels in blood were associated with microglial density and markers of neuroinflammation in postmortem DLPFC samples, suggesting that viral reactivation could drive a subset of neuroinflammatory pathology (Schizophrenia Research, 2023).

## Implications for the Neuroinflammatory Hypothesis

### Heterogeneity and Inflammatory Subtypes
The most significant conclusion derived from the postmortem literature is that schizophrenia is not a homogeneous entity regarding neuroinflammation. The consistent identification of a subgroup of patients with marked inflammatory changes—often termed the "high inflammatory biotype"—suggests that inflammation is a core pathological mechanism for some, but not all, individuals. Machine learning analyses of inflammatory markers have identified distinct subgroups with specific neuroanatomical and cognitive profiles (Panizzon et al., 2023).

### Interaction with White Matter and Synaptic Pathology
Neuroinflammation is not an isolated event; it interacts with structural brain changes. A systematic review found consistent evidence of microglial activation in white matter regions, potentially contributing to the oligodendrocyte dysfunction and myelin pathology observed in schizophrenia. Microglial activation and complement cascades are known to drive synaptic pruning. The finding of increased complement transcripts (C1qA, C3, C4) in the midbrain and cortex supports the hypothesis that excessive immune-mediated synaptic elimination contributes to the reduced neuropil and cortical thinning characteristic of schizophrenia (Catts et al., 2020).

### Vascular and BBB Compromise
Finally, postmortem evidence points to vascular contributions. In the subependymal neurogenic niche, patients with the neuroinflammatory type of schizophrenia showed altered expression of BBB-related transcripts (CLDN5, OCLN) and angiogenesis-related mRNAs (ANGPT2, VEGFA), alongside increased diapedesis-related transcripts (Kanaan et al., 2025). This indicates that BBB permeability may be compromised, facilitating the infiltration of peripheral immune factors that potentiate central inflammation.

## Conclusion
The postmortem evidence provides robust but nuanced support for the neuroinflammatory hypothesis of schizophrenia. It confirms that a substantial subgroup of patients exhibits marked alterations in key inflammatory markers, including increased density of HLA-DR+ microglia, elevated transcripts for pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, IL-8, IL-1β), complement proteins (C1q, C3, C4), and astrocyte-associated proteins (S100 family). These inflammatory changes are not uniform but appear concentrated in a "high inflammatory biotype," often associated with markers of peripheral immune infiltration and BBB compromise.

The data refutes the notion of simple gliosis (a reactive proliferation of all glia) and instead paints a picture of complex, cell-type-specific alterations. Microglia show signs of activation, particularly in white matter and in patients with acute symptomatology. Astrocyte findings are more variable, likely reflecting either regional differences or distinct reactive states that do not always conform to classic "gliosis" markers like GFAP.

Future research must prioritize the stratification of patients into inflammatory subtypes to validate these postmortem findings. The integration of postmortem molecular profiles with in vivo imaging biomarkers (e.g., free-water imaging) will be crucial for translating these findings into therapeutic strategies, such as the use of anti-inflammatory agents (e.g., minocycline) or microbiome modulation, specifically targeted to the inflammatory phenotype of schizophrenia.
