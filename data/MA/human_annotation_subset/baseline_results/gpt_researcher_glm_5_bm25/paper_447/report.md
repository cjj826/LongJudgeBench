# Systematic Review: Effectiveness of Federated Learning for Advancing Machine Learning Applications in Breast, Lung, and Prostate Cancer Research

## Introduction

The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) into oncological research has demonstrated substantial potential to transform clinical practice, particularly in the detection, segmentation, and prognostication of breast, lung, and prostate cancers. However, the development of robust, generalizable deep learning models is fundamentally constrained by the reliance on large-scale, diverse datasets. In the medical domain, such data are frequently siloed across institutions due to stringent privacy regulations, proprietary barriers, and ethical considerations regarding patient confidentiality ([Federated Learning for Histopathology Image Classification: A Systematic Review](local_corpus), 2026). Centralized learning methods, which require the physical aggregation of sensitive patient data, present considerable privacy and security risks, creating a paradox where the data necessary for algorithmic advancement are inaccessible ([Federated learning in computational pathology: a literature review](local_corpus), 2025).

Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a pivotal privacy-preserving paradigm designed to resolve this dilemma. By enabling collaborative model training across distributed institutions without the exchange of raw data, FL allows medical centers to contribute to a global model while maintaining local control over patient information ([Federated Learning with Research Prototypes: Application to Multi-Center MRI-based Detection of Prostate Cancer with Diverse Histopathology](local_corpus), 2023). This systematic review examines the effectiveness of FL in breast, lung, and prostate cancer research, evaluating whether federated approaches improve model generalizability and performance compared to traditional centralized machine learning methods. Specifically, we analyze the technical architectures, privacy mechanisms, and clinical outcomes reported in recent literature to determine if FL provides a viable pathway for advancing oncological AI applications.

## Methodological Foundations of Federated Learning in Oncology

### The Federated Framework and Privacy Preservation

The core premise of FL involves training algorithms locally on decentralized data silos—such as individual hospitals or research centers—and subsequently aggregating model updates (weights or gradients) on a central server. This process ensures that Protected Health Information (PHI) remains behind institutional firewalls, thereby mitigating the risk of data breaches associated with centralized repositories ([Privacy-Preserving Breast Cancer Classification: A Federated Transfer Learning Approach](local_corpus), 2024). The infrastructure platform for privacy-preserving distributed machine learning has been successfully implemented in cancer care clinics, demonstrating that it is feasible to construct clinical decision support systems in a manner that respects privacy and administrative barriers ([Infrastructure platform for privacy-preserving distributed machine learning development of computer-assisted theragnostics in cancer](local_corpus), 2022).

Advanced security mechanisms are frequently integrated into these frameworks to safeguard against adversarial attacks and inference risks. For instance, homomorphic encryption (HE) allows for computations on encrypted data, ensuring that even the model updates shared with the central server remain unintelligible to unauthorized parties. The FedGraphHE framework, for example, utilizes Dynamic Adaptive Partitioned Homomorphic Encryption to optimize gradient transmission without compromising patient confidentiality ([FedGraphHE: A privacy-preserving federated graph neural network framework with dynamic homomorphic encryption and robust aggregation](local_corpus), 2026). Similarly, Differential Privacy (DP) injects noise into model updates to obscure the contribution of individual data points, providing rigorous mathematical guarantees against re-identification attempts ([FAME: A privacy-preserving dual-stage deep learning framework for breast ultrasound imaging using federated transfer and synthetic learning](local_corpus), 2025).

### Technical Architectures and Optimization Strategies

The performance of FL in oncological imaging relies heavily on the selection of aggregation strategies and local training architectures. The Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm remains a standard approach, facilitating the training of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) across heterogeneous datasets ([Privacy preserving skin cancer diagnosis through federated deep learning and explainable AI](local_corpus), 2025). However, the non-independent and identically distributed (non-IID) nature of medical data across institutions presents a significant challenge. Variations in imaging protocols, scanner manufacturers, and patient demographics can lead to "client drift," where the global model struggles to generalize across local data distributions.

To address these challenges, researchers have developed specialized FL architectures. The Customized Federated Learning (CusFL) framework proposes training client-specific models guided by a federated feature extractor, allowing for personalization while maintaining the benefits of collaborative learning ([Customized Federated Learning for Multi-Source Decentralized Medical Image Classification](local_corpus), 2022). Similarly, the Variation-Aware Federated Learning (VAFL) framework utilizes generative adversarial networks (GANs) to transform images from different clients onto a common image space, minimizing feature skew and enhancing model robustness ([Variation-Aware Federated Learning With Multi-Source Decentralized Medical Image Data](local_corpus), 2021). For handling diverse histopathology data, frameworks like UCNet enable simultaneous supervision of pixel-wise, region-wise, and gland-wise classifications, maximizing the use of available ground truth data ([Federated Learning with Research Prototypes: Application to Multi-Center MRI-based Detection of Prostate Cancer with Diverse Histopathology](local_corpus), 2023).

## Effectiveness of Federated Learning in Prostate Cancer Research

### MRI-Based Detection and Segmentation

Prostate cancer is a primary domain where FL has demonstrated significant clinical utility, particularly in the analysis of multiparametric MRI (mpMRI). The need for diverse data is acute in this field, as early detection and staging are extremely challenging for both radiologists and deep learning algorithms ([Federated Learning with Research Prototypes: Application to Multi-Center MRI-based Detection of Prostate Cancer with Diverse Histopathology](local_corpus), 2023). A retrospective simulation study utilizing the Flower FL framework to train an nnU-Net-based architecture for biparametric MRI prostate segmentation and clinically significant prostate cancer (csPCa) detection involved data from over 2,700 patients across multiple clients. This study demonstrated that optimizing local epochs and aggregation strategies could yield performance metrics competitive with centralized models, evaluated through Dice scores for segmentation and the Prostate Imaging: Cancer Artificial Intelligence (PI-CAI) score for detection ([Optimizing Federated Learning Configurations for MRI Prostate Segmentation and Cancer Detection: A Simulation Study](local_corpus), 2025).

Furthermore, the application of FL extends to the detection of biochemical recurrence. A systematic review and meta-analysis assessed the role of AI in prostate-specific membrane antigen positron-emission tomography (PSMA PET), showing that while PSMA PET had high diagnostic accuracy, AI integration improved detection rates, with FL serving as a potential enabler for multicenter validation studies ([Comparison of Prostate-Specific Membrane Antigen Positron Emission Tomography and Conventional Imaging Modalities in the Detection of Biochemical Recurrence of Prostate Cancer and Assessment of the Role of Artificial Intelligence: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis](local_corpus), 2025). These findings underscore that FL is not merely a privacy-compliance tool but a mechanism for enhancing the robustness of prostate cancer diagnostic models.

### Addressing Heterogeneity in Histopathology

Histopathological analysis of prostate tissue is another area benefiting from FL. The "Federated Learning with Research Prototypes" study introduced a flexible framework for cross-site training using heterogeneous multi-parametric prostate MR data from over 1,400 patients. By leveraging diverse annotation and histopathology data, the study showed that collaborative training could overcome the limitations of prototype-stage algorithms, which often suffer from a lack of diverse training samples ([Federated Learning with Research Prototypes: Application to Multi-Center MRI-based Detection of Prostate Cancer with Diverse Histopathology](local_corpus), 2023). This aligns with broader reviews indicating that AI in urologic oncology is rapidly transforming lesion detection and risk stratification, particularly when models are trained on multicentric data through FL ([Artificial Intelligence in MRI for Urologic Oncology: A Systematic Review of Diagnostic Accuracy and Clinical Utility](local_corpus), 2025).

## Effectiveness of Federated Learning in Breast Cancer Research

### Classification and Segmentation Imaging Modalities

Breast cancer research has extensively adopted FL to overcome data scarcity and privacy hurdles in mammography, MRI, and ultrasound imaging. The FAME (Federated Attention-guided Multi-task Ensemble Network) framework represents a state-of-the-art approach, integrating Federated Transfer Learning with class-specific synthetic data generation via Auxiliary Classifier GANs. This dual-stage framework achieved high accuracy in joint segmentation and classification of breast ultrasound images across decentralized clinical environments, utilizing Differential Privacy with Gaussian noise injection to protect inter-client model updates ([FAME: A privacy-preserving dual-stage deep learning framework for breast ultrasound imaging using federated transfer and synthetic learning](local_corpus), 2025).

In the realm of mammography, a memory-aware curriculum federated learning method was developed to address the issue of imbalanced datasets where negative samples predominate. By optimizing the order and frequency of local updates, this method improved the consistency and performance of breast cancer classification models trained across multiple institutions ([Memory-aware curriculum federated learning for breast cancer classification](local_corpus), 2023). Additionally, a federated semi-supervised approach for breast MRI segmentation demonstrated that collaborative training could maximize resource utilization among institutions while preserving privacy, ensuring that smaller centers benefit from the data diversity of larger partners ([Enhancing breast magnetic resonance imaging segmentation with a federated semi-supervised approach](local_corpus), 2025).

### Prognostic Prediction and Histopathology

Beyond detection, FL has been applied to predict treatment outcomes. A multicentric study on Triple-Negative Breast Cancer (TNBC) utilized FL to predict histological response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NACT). By keeping patient data secured behind hospital firewalls, the collaborative model outperformed local institutional models, achieving performance on par with centralized models. This demonstrated that FL could effectively overcome the biases inherent in small-scale studies regarding rare cancer subtypes ([Federated learning for predicting histological response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy in triple-negative breast cancer](local_corpus), 2023).

Furthermore, the integration of FL with the Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) has enabled the classification of histopathological images. A study utilizing the BreakHis dataset compared FL with centralized learning for breast tumor classification, finding that FL preserved patient privacy while enabling the creation of a global model suitable for deployment on internal IoMT systems ([Federated Fusion of Magnified Histopathological Images for Breast Tumor Classification in the Internet of Medical Things](local_corpus), 2024). This highlights the versatility of FL across different data modalities and its role in facilitating AI adoption in decentralized clinical settings.

## Effectiveness of Federated Learning in Lung Cancer Research

### Segmentation and Risk Stratification

Lung cancer research faces significant challenges regarding data heterogeneity, particularly in CT imaging where nodule detection is critical. A novel federated semi-supervised framework was proposed to improve lung nodule segmentation and diagnosis, effectively addressing the high cost of data annotation and the scarcity of labeled datasets. By performing local training and global aggregation, the model enhanced generalizability across different institutions without sharing sensitive CT scans ([An automated privacy-preserving self-supervised classification of COVID-19 from lung CT scan images minimizing the requirements of large data annotation](local_corpus), 2025). Additionally, a debiasing technique for chest X-ray datasets utilized FL to ensure that no single data source could bias the model adversely, thereby enhancing internal classification and external generalization for early lung cancer diagnosis ([Development of Debiasing Technique for Lung Nodule Chest X-ray Datasets to Generalize Deep Learning Models](local_corpus), 2023).

In terms of prognostic modeling, the FesCPI (Federated cross-scale Common-Personal-Interactive learning) model was developed to predict the progression of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). Utilizing data from 926 patients across four centers, this framework outperformed single-institution models in risk stratification for early-stage progression. The study emphasized that accurate prediction is crucial for guiding clinical decision-making and avoiding overtreatment or undertreatment, and FL provided the necessary data diversity to achieve this ([Risk stratification for early-stage NSCLC progression: a federated learning framework with large-small model synergy](local_corpus), 2025).

### Performance Comparisons and Meta-Analytic Insights

Systematic reviews focusing on lung cancer AI have begun to incorporate FL as a critical variable. A meta-analysis of deep learning in thoracic oncology noted that while centralized models achieved high lesion-wise sensitivity, factors influencing performance included participant demographics and dataset privacy. The review suggested that future robust models would increasingly rely on federated approaches to handle multicenter data without privacy violations ([Deep Learning in Thoracic Oncology: Meta-Analytical Insights into Lung Nodule Early-Detection Technologies](local_corpus), 2025). Furthermore, reviews on AI applications in lung cancer emphasize that convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained via FL can achieve significant sensitivity and specificity in nodule recognition, matching or surpassing the performance of models trained on centralized datasets ([Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning in Lung Cancer: Advances in Imaging, Detection, and Prognosis](local_corpus), 2025).

## Comparative Performance Analysis: Federated vs. Centralized Learning

### Generalizability and Robustness

The overarching evidence indicates that FL models exhibit superior generalizability compared to single-institution models. A foundational study demonstrated that the FL model exhibited superior performance and generalizability to models trained at single institutions, with an overall performance level significantly better than any of the institutional models alone when evaluated on held-out test sets and outside challenge datasets. This was successfully demonstrated across three academic institutions without sharing patient data ([Federated learning improves site performance in multicenter deep learning without data sharing](local_corpus), 2021).

The improvement in generalizability stems from the diversity of the training data. While centralized models are prone to overfitting to the specific characteristics of a single institution's dataset, FL models are exposed to a wider range of clinical presentations and imaging protocols. For instance, in computational pathology, proof-of-concept studies have shown performance equivalency between models trained in a federated manner versus those trained centrally, with minor performance differences (0~3%) reported in aggregation methods ([A review on federated learning in computational pathology](local_corpus), 2024). This suggests that the "privacy cost" of FL—the potential minor drop in accuracy due to non-IID data—can be mitigated through advanced aggregation and alignment methods, making the trade-off favorable for clinical deployment.

### Performance Metrics and Clinical Utility

Quantitative analysis reveals that FL-based models frequently achieve clinical-grade performance metrics. In breast cancer classification, federated transfer learning approaches utilizing mammography and MRI images have demonstrated high efficacy, maintaining accuracy levels comparable to centralized baselines ([Privacy-Preserving Breast Cancer Classification: A Federated Transfer Learning Approach](local_corpus), 2024). In prostate cancer segmentation, optimized FL frameworks utilizing nnU-Net have achieved competitive Dice scores on independent test sets, validating their utility for clinical decision support ([Optimizing Federated Learning Configurations for MRI Prostate Segmentation and Cancer Detection: A Simulation Study](local_corpus), 2025).

However, it is crucial to acknowledge that FL performance is sensitive to hyperparameter tuning. The number of local epochs, federated rounds, and aggregation strategies significantly impact the final model quality. Studies indicate that while FL can match centralized performance, it often requires more sophisticated optimization strategies to handle data heterogeneity ([Optimizing Federated Learning Configurations for MRI Prostate Segmentation and Cancer Detection: A Simulation Study](local_corpus), 2025). The "Methods and Impact for Using Federated Learning to Collaborate on Clinical Research" study further validated this, benchmarking a global FL model against a standard centrally trained model using a held-out dataset for intracranial hemorrhage detection, and finding that the federated approach maintained diagnostic accuracy while ensuring privacy ([Methods and Impact for Using Federated Learning to Collaborate on Clinical Research](local_corpus), 2023).

## Challenges and Future Directions

### Data Heterogeneity and Non-IID Data

Despite its promise, FL faces substantial challenges related to data heterogeneity. The statistical variance in data distributions across hospitals (non-IID data) can degrade the performance of the global model. Solutions like the SurvMaximin algorithm, which estimates Cox model feature coefficients for target populations using summary information, are emerging to transport survival risk prediction models across heterogeneous real-world data centers ([SurvMaximin: Robust federated approach to transporting survival risk prediction models](local_corpus), 2022). Additionally, the "New Framework of Swarm Learning" proposes assembling local knowledge while overcoming the forgetting of global knowledge during local training, specifically addressing feature and label skew in medical image segmentation ([A New Framework of Swarm Learning Consolidating Knowledge From Multi-Center Non-IID Data for Medical Image Segmentation](local_corpus), 2023).

### Privacy-Utility Tradeoffs and Security

While FL preserves data locality, it is not immune to privacy attacks. Gradient leakage attacks can potentially reconstruct training data from model updates. Consequently, the integration of Differential Privacy (DP) is becoming standard, yet it introduces a privacy-utility tradeoff. A scoping review on differential privacy in medical deep learning noted that while DP can maintain clinically acceptable performance under moderate privacy budgets (ε ≈ 10), strict privacy settings (ε ≈ 1) often lead to substantial accuracy loss. The review highlighted that reporting of privacy parameters remains inconsistent, and strict privacy enforcement can widen subgroup performance gaps, potentially exacerbating health inequities ([Differential privacy for medical deep learning: methods, tradeoffs, and deployment implications](local_corpus), 2026). Future research must focus on optimizing these tradeoffs to ensure that privacy-preserving mechanisms do not inadvertently compromise the clinical utility of oncological AI tools.

### Infrastructure and Implementation

The deployment of FL requires robust technical infrastructure. Frameworks like APPFLx and FeatureCloud are lowering the barrier to entry by providing low-code, secure end-to-end communication environments that are agnostic to the underlying computational infrastructure ([Enabling end-to-end secure federated learning in biomedical research on heterogeneous computing environments with APPFLx](local_corpus), 2025); ([The FeatureCloud Platform for Federated Learning in Biomedicine: Unified Approach](local_corpus), 2023). These platforms are essential for scaling FL from research prototypes to clinical practice, ensuring that collaborative networks can be established and maintained efficiently.

## Conclusion

The evidence synthesized in this systematic review supports the conclusion that Federated Learning significantly advances machine learning applications in breast, lung, and prostate cancer research. By enabling privacy-preserving collaboration across multicenter datasets, FL addresses the critical bottleneck of data silos, fostering the development of more generalizable and robust diagnostic models. While FL models may occasionally lag slightly behind theoretically optimal centralized models due to data heterogeneity, they consistently outperform single-institution models and achieve clinical-grade accuracy. The integration of advanced techniques—such as transfer learning, differential privacy, and specialized aggregation algorithms—has further enhanced the effectiveness of FL, making it a viable and often preferable alternative to centralized learning in oncological settings. As regulatory pressures and data privacy concerns continue to mount, FL stands as a foundational technology for the future of AI-driven cancer research.
