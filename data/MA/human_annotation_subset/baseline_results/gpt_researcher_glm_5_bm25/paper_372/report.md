# Association Between Agricultural Land-Use Exposure and Infectious Disease Risks in Southeast Asia: A Meta-Analytic Review

## Introduction
The interface between human activity and the environment is a critical determinant of public health, particularly in regions undergoing rapid economic and ecological transition. Southeast Asia represents a global hotspot for emerging infectious diseases, a status inextricably linked to its dynamic landscape where extensive agricultural expansion interfaces with remaining natural habitats. The conversion of forests and wetlands into agricultural land—ranging from rubber and oil palm plantations to rice paddies and aquaculture—alters ecological dynamics, often bringing human populations into closer contact with wildlife reservoirs and arthropod vectors. While individual studies have long implicated agriculture as a driver of specific infectious diseases, a systematic quantification of this risk across the region has historically been lacking. This report synthesizes findings from a comprehensive meta-analysis and supporting literature to rigorously examine the association between agricultural land-use exposure and infectious disease risk in Southeast Asia. The analysis specifically addresses whether occupational or residential exposure to agricultural environments significantly increases the odds of pathogen infection compared to unexposed populations, detailing the magnitude of these risks across specific land-use types and disease categories.

## Methodological Overview
The core evidence base for this report is derived from a landmark meta-analysis conducted by Wells et al. (2019), which systematically assessed the generality of disease-agriculture relationships in Southeast Asia. The researchers performed a comprehensive literature search, identifying 34 eligible studies that met stringent inclusion criteria. These studies provided data on human infection rates categorized by exposure status—defined as living or working in agricultural land versus being unexposed. The analytical approach employed a random-effects meta-analysis model to calculate pooled Odds Ratios (OR) with 95% Confidence Intervals (CI). This statistical method accounts for between-study variance, providing a more conservative and generalizable estimate of effect size than a fixed-effect model. Subgroup analyses were conducted to differentiate risks by specific agricultural land-use types (e.g., oil palm, rubber, livestock) and specific disease categories (e.g., vector-borne, soil-transmitted helminths). The robustness of the findings was evaluated through sensitivity analyses, ensuring that the overall conclusions were not driven by individual outliers ([Wells et al., 2019](#)).

## General Risk Assessment: Agricultural Exposure
The meta-analysis provides unequivocal statistical evidence that agricultural land-use is a significant driver of infectious disease risk in Southeast Asia. The pooled analysis of 34 studies revealed that individuals who live or work in agricultural lands are, on average, **1.74 times more likely** to be infected with a pathogen compared to those who are unexposed (OR: 1.74, CI: 1.47-2.07). This confidence interval is narrow and statistically significant, ruling out the null hypothesis of no effect. These findings corroborate the hypothesis that agricultural environments function as "disease promoters" by altering the ecological balance. The consistent nature of this association across multiple study sites suggests a fundamental epidemiological link between land conversion and human pathogen exposure. The risk is not merely incidental but systemic, likely driven by the creation of novel habitats for vectors and reservoirs, as well as the increased exposure of susceptible human hosts during agricultural activities ([Wells et al., 2019](#)).

## Disease-Specific Risk Profiles
The association between agricultural exposure and disease risk is highly heterogeneous depending on the pathogen involved. The meta-analysis identified specific diseases with significantly elevated odds ratios, while others showed no significant change.

### Vector-Borne and Mite-Borne Diseases
The most substantial risks were observed for vector-borne diseases, particularly those transmitted by mites and mosquitoes.
*   **Spotted Fever Group (SFG) Rickettsioses:** The highest effect size was recorded for SFG diseases, with exposed individuals having nearly four times the odds of infection (OR: 3.91, CI: 2.61-5.85). This suggests that agricultural habitats in Southeast Asia, particularly scrubland or plantation edges, are highly conducive to the life cycles of trombiculid mites (chiggers), the vectors for these rickettsial pathogens.
*   **Scrub Typhus:** Similarly, scrub typhus, caused by *Orientia tsutsugamushi*, showed a strong association with agricultural land use (OR: 2.37, CI: 1.41-3.96). This aligns with the ecology of the vector, which favors transitional vegetation zones often found in agricultural landscapes.
*   **Malaria:** The analysis indicated that agricultural exposure doubles the risk of malaria infection (OR: 2.00, CI: 1.46-2.73). This finding is supported by broader ecological studies indicating that deforestation and land-use change facilitate the zoonotic transmission of malaria species such as *Plasmodium knowlesi*. Variations in temperature and precipitation, exacerbated by land conversion, impact mosquito vector populations, creating new transmission zones ([Wells et al., 2019](#); [Plasmodium knowlesi study, 2025](#)).

### Soil-Transmitted Helminths
*   **Hookworm:** The risk of hookworm infection was significantly elevated among those exposed to agricultural land (OR: 2.42, CI: 1.56-3.75). This strong association is biologically plausible, as agricultural soils—particularly in plantation settings where sanitation infrastructure may be limited—serve as a direct reservoir for hookworm larvae. Transmission occurs via skin penetration, a route of exposure highly relevant to agricultural workers who frequently work barefoot in moist, contaminated soils.

### Faecal-Oral Route Diseases
In stark contrast to the vector-borne and soil-transmitted pathogens, the meta-analysis detected **no significant change in infection risk** for diseases transmitted via the faecal-oral route. This distinction is critical. It implies that the increased risk associated with agriculture is driven primarily by ecological and zoonotic mechanisms (vector expansion, habitat change) rather than solely by poor water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) conditions, which are often correlated with rural poverty but are not unique to the agricultural land-use interface itself ([Wells et al., 2019](#)).

## High-Risk Agricultural Land-Uses
Not all agricultural practices confer equal risk. The meta-analysis highlighted specific land-use types that act as potent amplifiers of disease transmission.

### Plantation Crops: Oil Palm and Rubber
The effect sizes were greatest for exposure to **oil palm** and **rubber** plantations. These monocultures have expanded rapidly across Southeast Asia, often replacing diverse tropical forests. The transition from forest to plantation creates a "forest-agriculture interface" that disrupts native biodiversity while favoring generalist vector species and reservoir hosts. For instance, the modification of drainage and canopy structure in these plantations can create ideal breeding sites for malaria vectors, while the rodent populations that thrive in these landscapes can sustain rickettsial pathogens ([Wells et al., 2019](#)).

### Livestock Farming
**Non-poultry based livestock farming** was also identified as a high-risk activity. This category typically involves close contact with mammals such as pigs, cattle, or goats, which serve as reservoirs or amplifying hosts for zoonotic pathogens (e.g., leptospirosis, certain rickettsioses). The study on Rickettsial seropositivity in Peninsular Malaysia supports this, showing significantly higher seropositivity rates for *Rickettsia conorii* and *Rickettsia felis* among animal farm workers compared to urban blood donors ([Wells et al., 2019](#); [Rickettsial seropositivity study, 2017](#)).

### Aquaculture and Rice Cultivation
While the meta-analysis focused on terrestrial agriculture, supporting literature highlights the risks associated with aquaculture and rice farming. The conversion of mangroves and wetlands to aquaculture ponds, prevalent across Southeast Asia, has been linked to altered land-use patterns that support planetary health issues, including infectious diseases. Furthermore, the conversion of aquaculture ponds to rice monoculture in southeast China has been shown to alter biogeochemical cycles, potentially influencing the environmental persistence of pathogens. Specific to Southeast Asia, *Opisthorchis viverrini* (liver fluke) prevalence in northeast Thailand is geographically linked to land-use compositions around villages, particularly those associated with wetlands and agriculture, though altitude also plays a mitigating role ([Planetary Health Consequences of Telecoupled Shrimp Farming, 2025](#); [Opisthorchis viverrini study, 2013](#)).

## Epidemiological Dynamics and Mechanisms
The elevated disease risks identified in the meta-analysis are underpinned by complex ecological mechanisms.

### Deforestation and Spillover Risk
Land-use change, primarily deforestation for agriculture, is a fundamental driver of zoonotic spillover. As natural habitats are fragmented, the diversity of potential reservoir hosts often shifts. Research indicates that known wildlife hosts of human-shared pathogens comprise a greater proportion of local species richness and abundance in sites under substantial human use (secondary, agricultural, and urban lands) compared to pristine forests. This suggests that land-use change systematically favors species that are more likely to harbor zoonotic pathogens, thereby increasing the probability of spillover to humans ([Zoonotic host diversity study, 2020](#)).

### The Case of Bat-Borne Coronaviruses
The spillover risk of bat SARS-related coronaviruses (SARSr-CoVs) in Southeast Asia illustrates the connection between land use and emerging diseases. A probabilistic risk assessment estimated that a median of **66,280 people** are infected with SARSr-CoVs annually in Southeast Asia. This high number is attributed to the overlap between bat host distributions and human populations, a dynamic intensified by deforestation and encroachment of human settlements into forest fringes for agricultural purposes ([Bat SARS-related coronavirus study, 2022](#)).

### Water Quality and Environmental Drivers
Agricultural intensification also affects water quality, which can indirectly influence disease transmission. Structural equation modeling in South Korea showed that agricultural land use significantly increased biochemical oxygen demand and nutrient concentrations in streams, affecting benthic macroinvertebrate indices. While this primarily impacts environmental health, the degradation of water resources can facilitate waterborne diseases, although the meta-analysis noted that faecal-oral risks were not significantly higher in agricultural settings compared to unexposed controls, suggesting that other transmission pathways (vector-borne, soil-mediated) are more characteristic of the agricultural risk profile ([Structural Equation Model study, 2020](#)).

## Public Health Implications
The findings of this meta-analysis have profound implications for land-use planning and public health policy in Southeast Asia.

### Integration of Health into Land-Use Policy
The consistent exacerbation of disease risks by agricultural land-uses necessitates the integration of human health considerations into agricultural development and land-use policies. Current policies often prioritize economic yields and production efficiency without adequately accounting for the external costs of disease burden. The data suggest that "agricultural intensification" should be re-evaluated to incorporate "One Health" principles, recognizing the interconnection between human, animal, and environmental health.

### Targeted Interventions
Public health interventions should be specifically tailored to the agricultural sector.
1.  **Occupational Safety:** For workers in oil palm, rubber, and livestock sectors, interventions should include provision of protective clothing (to prevent chigger bites and hookworm penetration), improved sanitation facilities, and regular health screenings.
2.  **Vector Control:** In plantation landscapes, integrated vector management strategies should be implemented to control mites and mosquitoes, potentially modifying the vegetation structure to reduce vector habitats.
3.  **Zoonotic Surveillance:** Enhanced surveillance for zoonotic pathogens in wildlife and livestock at the forest-agriculture interface is essential for early detection of emerging threats, such as novel coronaviruses or rickettsial agents.

### Addressing Environmental Determinants
Efforts to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from tropical peatlands—often converted for oil palm and pulpwood—by preserving forests could yield co-benefits for public health by maintaining natural ecological barriers against disease emergence ([Opportunities for reducing greenhouse gas emissions, 2010](#)).

## Limitations and Future Directions
While the meta-analysis provides robust evidence, limitations exist. The included studies vary in their definitions of "exposure" and "unexposed" populations, and residual confounding factors (such as socioeconomic status) may influence the estimates. Additionally, the majority of studies focus on rural agricultural settings; the risks associated with urban agriculture or peri-urban interfaces require further elucidation. Future research should focus on mechanistic studies to understand precisely how specific agricultural practices alter vector ecology and host-pathogen dynamics. Longitudinal studies are needed to track the health impacts of land-use change over time, particularly in areas undergoing rapid conversion.

## Conclusion
The evidence synthesized in this report leads to a concrete conclusion: **Agricultural land-uses consistently exacerbate infectious disease risks in Southeast Asia.** The statistical association is robust, with individuals in agricultural settings being 1.74 times more likely to be infected by a pathogen. This risk is driven by specific land-uses—oil palm, rubber, and livestock farming—and specific diseases, namely hookworm, malaria, scrub typhus, and spotted fever group rickettsioses. Conversely, the risk of faecal-oral diseases appears unaffected by agricultural exposure specifically, pointing to the unique ecological nature of the agricultural threat. These findings compel a paradigm shift in how agricultural development is approached in the region. It is no longer sufficient to view agriculture solely as a production system; it must be recognized as a complex socio-ecological system with profound public health externalities. Mitigating these risks requires a cross-sectoral approach that bridges agriculture, public health, and environmental conservation to ensure that the drive for economic development does not come at an unacceptable cost to human health.
