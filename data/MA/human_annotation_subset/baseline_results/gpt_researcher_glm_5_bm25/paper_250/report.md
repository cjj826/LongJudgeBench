# Comparative Effectiveness of Antidepressant Treatments for Treatment-Resistant Depression: A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis

## Introduction

Treatment-resistant depression (TRD) represents a significant clinical challenge in modern psychiatry, affecting approximately 30% of individuals with major depressive disorder (MDD) ([Pharmacotherapy for Treatment-Resistant Depression: Antidepressants and Atypical Antipsychotics](source_url), 2023). Defined typically as an inadequate response to at least two adequate trials of antidepressant medications, TRD is associated with increased morbidity, mortality, and substantial healthcare costs ([Treatment-resistant depression: definition, prevalence, detection, management, and investigational interventions](source_url), 2023). The management of TRD often necessitates a shift from first-line pharmacological monotherapy to more complex strategies, including pharmacological augmentation, combination therapies, and neuromodulatory interventions.

This report synthesizes findings from a comprehensive systematic review and network meta-analysis (NMA) that compared the relative effectiveness of various pharmacological and neuromodulatory treatments for TRD. The primary focus is on adult patients who have not responded to at least two antidepressant trials, with response rate serving as the primary outcome measure. By integrating direct and indirect comparisons from randomized controlled trials (RCTs), this analysis provides a ranked hierarchy of treatment efficacy to inform evidence-based clinical decision-making.

## Methodology and Scope of the Analysis

The primary source for this synthesis is a systematic review and network meta-analysis conducted by [Relative effectiveness of antidepressant treatments in treatment-resistant depression: a systematic review and network meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials](source_url) (2025). The investigators searched PubMed, the Cochrane Library, and Embase from inception to April 13, 2023, identifying RCTs of adults with depression who met stringent criteria for treatment resistance. Specifically, participants were required to have failed at least two antidepressant trials, and those with bipolar or psychotic depression were excluded to ensure a homogeneous unipolar TRD population.

The analysis included 69 RCTs encompassing 10,285 participants (5,662 females, 4,623 males) and evaluated 25 separate treatments. The Cochrane Risk of Bias Tool-2 was employed to assess study quality. Response rate was the primary outcome, defined typically as a 50% or greater reduction in depressive symptom severity scores. Odds ratios (ORs) were estimated using a random-effects NMA model, allowing for the ranking of interventions based on their probability of efficacy.

## Relative Efficacy of Pharmacological Augmentation Strategies

Pharmacological augmentation—adding a second agent to an existing antidepressant—is a cornerstone of TRD management. The NMA identified several pharmacological agents that demonstrated superior efficacy compared to placebo.

### Atypical Antipsychotics

Atypical antipsychotics are among the most widely studied augmentation agents for TRD. The analysis revealed that several agents in this class significantly outperformed placebo in inducing a treatment response.

**Aripiprazole** was identified as a significantly effective augmentation agent. This finding is consistent with its FDA approval for adjunctive therapy in MDD. The analysis indicated an Odds Ratio (OR) of approximately 2.16 for aripiprazole augmentation relative to placebo ([Relative effectiveness of antidepressant treatments in treatment-resistant depression](source_url), 2025). Similarly, **brexpiprazole** demonstrated significant efficacy with an OR of 2.53, and **cariprazine** showed an OR of 2.52. These findings align with previous meta-analytic data suggesting that second-generation antipsychotics (SGAs) are effective for TRD, with a Number Needed to Treat (NNT) of approximately 11 ([Efficacy and Tolerability of Combination Treatments for Major Depression: Antidepressants plus Second-Generation Antipsychotics vs. Esketamine vs. Lithium](source_url), 2021).

**Quetiapine** (extended-release) also showed significant efficacy (OR 2.02). The **Olanzapine-Fluoxetine Combination (OFC)**, marketed as Symbyax, is another approved treatment for TRD. The NMA estimated an OR of 2.54 for OFC, corroborating evidence from acute-phase studies supporting its effectiveness in patients who have responded inadequately to antidepressant monotherapy ([Olanzapine and fluoxetine combination therapy for treatment-resistant depression](source_url), 2009; [Symbyax for acute-phase management of treatment-resistant depression](source_url), 2010).

While effective, the use of atypical antipsychotics is limited by their adverse effect profiles, including weight gain, metabolic disturbances, akathisia, and potential long-term risks such as tardive dyskinesia ([Pharmacotherapy for Treatment-Resistant Depression: Antidepressants and Atypical Antipsychotics](source_url), 2023). Consequently, the benefits of efficacy must be weighed against these tolerability concerns.

### Mood Stabilizers and Other Agents

**Lithium** augmentation has historically been considered a gold-standard strategy. The NMA confirmed its enduring relevance, showing a significant effect with an OR of 1.88. This finding is supported by older specific trials, such as those demonstrating the efficacy of lithium augmentation of nortriptyline in patients resistant to multiple antidepressants ([Lithium augmentation of nortriptyline for subjects resistant to multiple antidepressants](source_url), 2003). Furthermore, the NMA identified **liothyronine** (thyroid hormone) as a potentially effective augmentation agent, ranked among the top treatments (OR 2.98), highlighting the value of established, lower-cost options.

### Novel Pharmacotherapies: Ketamine and Esketamine

**Intravenous (IV) ketamine** has emerged as a transformative treatment for TRD due to its rapid onset of action. The network meta-analysis found IV ketamine to be highly effective, with an OR of 4.03 compared to placebo/sham ([Relative effectiveness of antidepressant treatments in treatment-resistant depression](source_url), 2025). This high effect size underscores its potency.

Recent comparative evidence suggests that IV ketamine may be non-inferior to electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) for nonpsychotic TRD. A randomized trial (ELEKT-D) demonstrated noninferiority, with clinical features potentially guiding selection between the two ([Ketamine vs Electroconvulsive Therapy for Treatment-Resistant Depression: A Secondary Analysis of a Randomized Clinical Trial](source_url), 2024; [In treatment-resistant major depression, ketamine was noninferior to ECT for treatment response](source_url), 2023). Furthermore, ketamine may be better tolerated than many second-line augmentation strategies, leading some experts to argue for its evaluation as a second-line rather than third-line agent ([Ketamine for refractory depression: Save the best for last?](source_url), 2025).

**Esketamine**, the S-enantiomer of ketamine administered intranasally, is FDA-approved for TRD. The NMA found it effective (OR 1.92). While both IV ketamine and esketamine are effective, head-to-head meta-analyses suggest comparable acute effectiveness, though IV ketamine might offer specific advantages in speed of onset or specific symptom clusters ([Intravenous ketamine versus esketamine for depression: a systematic review and meta-analysis](source_url), 2025). Another novel pharmacotherapy, the **dextromethorphan-bupropion** combination, has shown promise in case reports for TRD, representing a potential future addition to the therapeutic arsenal ([Fixed dose combination dextromethorphan-bupropion: A hope for treatment resistant depression (TRD)](source_url), 2025).

### Other Pharmacological Agents

The NMA evaluated numerous other agents. **Minocycline**, for instance, was included in the network but its specific effect size in the summary hierarchy was not among the top-tier treatments compared to the primary interventions of interest like ketamine or antipsychotics. **Modafinil** and **Stimulants** were also analyzed; modafinil showed significant efficacy (OR 2.12) as an augmentation agent ([Augmentation strategies for treatment resistant major depression](source_url), 2022).

## Relative Efficacy of Neuromodulatory Interventions

For patients with severe TRD or those who prefer non-pharmacological approaches, neuromodulation offers critical alternatives. The NMA provided clear rankings for these interventions.

### Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)

**Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)** was identified as the most effective treatment for TRD in the network meta-analysis, achieving an Odds Ratio of 4.18 for response compared to placebo/sham ([Relative effectiveness of antidepressant treatments in treatment-resistant depression](source_url), 2025). ECT has long been considered the gold standard for severe, resistant depression. Comparative studies consistently show that while rTMS is effective, ECT demonstrates superior efficacy. For instance, a randomized trial found ECT led to greater reductions in depression and suicidal behavior scores than rTMS ([Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation versus electroconvulsive therapy for the treatment of major depressive disorder](source_url), 2011). Real-world data from Taiwan confirm that among completers, ECT has significantly higher response (75.0% vs. 50.0%) and remission (50.0% vs. 34.4%) rates compared to standard rTMS ([A Comparison of Electroconvulsive Therapy and Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation for Nonpsychotic Treatment-Resistant Depression: A Real-World Study in Taiwan](source_url), 2025).

However, ECT is associated with cognitive side effects, which remain a primary deterrent for patients. Interestingly, recent research points to shared mechanisms between ECT and ketamine, specifically involving adenosine signalling in the prefrontal cortex, which may open avenues for refining therapeutic precision ([Adenosine signalling drives antidepressant actions of ketamine and ECT](source_url), 2026).

### Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (rTMS)

**Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (rTMS)** is a non-invasive neuromodulation technique with a favorable safety profile. The NMA found standard rTMS to be effective (OR 1.59). While generally less potent than ECT, rTMS is better tolerated and lacks the cognitive impairments associated with ECT. A systematic review and network meta-analysis of non-surgical brain stimulation confirmed that ECT ranks highest for efficacy, but rTMS remains a viable option, particularly for those ineligible for ECT ([Comparative efficacy and acceptability of non-surgical brain stimulation for the acute treatment of major depressive episodes in adults](source_url), 2019).

### Theta Burst Stimulation (TBS)

**Theta Burst Stimulation (TBS)** is a newer form of rTMS that can be administered more rapidly. **Intermittent TBS (iTBS)** has received FDA clearance for TRD. The NMA indicated significant efficacy for iTBS (OR 1.79). Accelerated iTBS (aiTBS) protocols, such as Stanford Neuromodulation Therapy (SNT), which condenses treatment into 5 days, have shown remarkably high remission rates (up to ~90% in open-label, and significant efficacy in sham-controlled trials) for TRD ([Stanford Neuromodulation Therapy (SNT): A Double-Blind Randomized Controlled Trial](source_url), 2022; [Accelerated Theta-Burst Stimulation for Treatment-Resistant Depression: A Randomized Clinical Trial](source_url), 2025).

Comparative studies, such as the BEAT-D trial, have investigated the non-inferiority of bilateral TBS (BL-TBS) to standard bilateral rTMS, finding that shorter TBS protocols are efficient and effective alternatives for TRD across various age groups ([Effectiveness of sequential bilateral repetitive transcranial stimulation versus bilateral theta burst stimulation for patients with treatment-resistant depression (BEAT-D)](source_url), 2025).

### Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS)

**Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS)** is another non-invasive technique. While included in broader NMAs of neuromodulation, its specific efficacy for TRD was less robust than ECT or high-frequency rTMS in the primary NMA of pharmacological and neuromodulatory treatments. A comparative study suggested that while tDCS is beneficial, ECT remains more effective for reducing symptoms in TRD ([A comparative study of the effect of electroconvulsive therapy and transcranial direct current stimulation in the treatment of persons suffering from treatment-resistant depression](source_url), 2022).

### Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)

For highly refractory cases, **Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)** offers an invasive but potentially effective option. A network meta-analysis of DBS targets identified that stimulation of the nucleus accumbens and ventral capsule/ventral striatum showed favorable efficacy for reducing depressive symptoms in TRD ([Deep brain stimulation for treatment-resistant major depressive disorder: a network meta-analysis of stimulation targets](source_url), 2025).

## Statistical Synthesis and Hierarchy of Treatments

The network meta-analysis provides a ranked hierarchy of the 25 treatments based on response rates. The top treatments demonstrating statistically significant higher response rates compared to placebo or sham were:

1.  **Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)** (OR 4.18)
2.  **Intravenous Ketamine** (OR 4.03)
3.  **Liothyronine** (OR 2.98)
4.  **Olanzapine-Fluoxetine Combination** (OR 2.54)
5.  **Brexpiprazole** (OR 2.53)
6.  **Cariprazine** (OR 2.52)

## Discussion and Differentiation of Interventions

The choice between pharmacological and neuromodulatory interventions is nuanced. ECT and IV ketamine stand out as having the highest effect sizes, making them preferred options for rapid stabilization in severe TRD. However, the invasiveness of ECT and the monitoring requirements for IV ketamine influence practical deployment.

Pharmacological augmentation with atypical antipsychotics (aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, quetiapine, OFC) remains a first-line approach for TRD due to ease of administration and robust evidence. The NMA confirms their significant benefits over placebo. However, the favorable tolerability profile of ketamine—particularly its lack of weight gain and sexual dysfunction compared to antidepressants and antipsychotics—may shift the risk-benefit calculus ([Ketamine for refractory depression](source_url), 2025).

For patients prioritizing non-invasive, non-pharmacological options, rTMS and particularly accelerated iTBS protocols offer compelling alternatives. While the efficacy of standard rTMS is lower than ECT, the advent of accelerated, high-dose iTBS (like SNT) has narrowed this gap, offering rapid relief within days rather than weeks ([Stanford Neuromodulation Therapy (SNT)](source_url), 2022).

It is also noteworthy that the effectiveness of interventions can be influenced by patient history. For example, a history of ECT non-response in the current episode was associated with a significantly worse response to iTBS (OR 0.252), suggesting that severe refractoriness may predict resistance across modalities ([Response to Intermittent Theta Burst Stimulation in Treatment-Resistant Depression: Comparison of Patients With and Without History of Electroconvulsive Therapy](source_url), 2025).

## Conclusion

This comprehensive network meta-analysis confirms that several treatments are significantly more effective than placebo for adults with TRD who have failed at least two antidepressants. ECT and IV ketamine demonstrate the highest efficacy, followed by specific pharmacological augmentations (liothyronine, olanzapine-fluoxetine, brexpiprazole, cariprazine) and neuromodulatory techniques like iTBS. The ranking provided allows clinicians to weigh the magnitude of benefit against the burden of treatment administration and side effects. For rapid and robust response, ECT and IV ketamine are paramount. For maintenance or preference for non-invasive approaches, atypical antipsychotic augmentation or accelerated TBS protocols represent effective, evidence-based strategies. The evolution of accelerated neuromodulation and rapid-acting pharmacotherapies continues to reshape the TRD treatment landscape, offering hope for remission in this difficult-to-treat population.
