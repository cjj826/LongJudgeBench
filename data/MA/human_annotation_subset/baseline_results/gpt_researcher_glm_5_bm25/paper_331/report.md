# Comparative Effectiveness of Treatment Modalities for Persistent Spinal Pain Syndrome Type 2: A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis

## Introduction

Persistent Spinal Pain Syndrome Type 2 (PSPS-T2), formerly known as Failed Back Surgery Syndrome (FBSS), represents a complex and challenging clinical entity characterized by persistent or recurrent pain following spinal surgery. This condition affects a substantial subset of patients, with estimates suggesting that approximately 30% of patients experience this debilitating condition post-surgery ([Comparative Effectiveness of Different Frequencies...](local_corpus)). The management of PSPS-T2 is multifaceted, involving a spectrum of interventions ranging from conservative medical management (CMM) and minimally invasive procedures to neuromodulation and reoperation.

This report synthesizes evidence from systematic reviews, randomized controlled trials (RCTs), and network meta-analyses (NMAs) to compare the efficacy of these treatment modalities. The primary focus is on pain reduction, functional outcomes, and the quality of evidence supporting each intervention.

## Efficacy of Neuromodulation: Spinal Cord Stimulation (SCS)

Spinal Cord Stimulation (SCS) has emerged as a cornerstone intervention for PSPS-T2, particularly for patients with predominant leg pain refractory to conservative treatments.

### SCS versus Conservative Medical Management (CMM) and Sham

A recent systematic review from 2025 assessed the effectiveness of dorsal column SCS for PSPS-T2 compared to CMM or sham. The review included six publications comprising 448 participants. The findings indicated that follow-up periods for primary endpoints varied, but generally supported the use of SCS ([A systematic review of evidence...](local_corpus)). Historically, the PROCESS trial (2007) established that patients randomized to SCS plus CMM achieved significantly higher rates of leg pain relief (≥50%) compared to CMM alone. Specifically, the SCS group showed a 48% success rate compared to 9% in the CMM group at six months ([Spinal cord stimulation versus conventional medical management...](local_corpus)).

Furthermore, a systematic review and NMA from 2026 combined direct and indirect evidence, reinforcing that SCS provides superior pain intensity reduction, physical function improvement, and health-related quality of life compared to CMM ([Systematic review and network meta-analysis...](local_corpus)). Another RCT confirmed these findings, showing that neuropathic pain intensity decreased significantly from baseline to 3 months (NRS 6.7 to 4.5; p < 0.001) in patients receiving active SCS compared to those with the device switched off (CMM) ([Effects of spinal cord stimulation on pain...](local_corpus)).

### SCS versus Reoperation

The seminal RCT by North et al. (2005) provided direct evidence comparing SCS to reoperation. The study followed 50 patients for an average of 3 years. Results demonstrated that SCS was significantly more likely to result in a successful outcome. While 54% of the SCS group achieved success (defined as ≥50% pain relief and patient satisfaction), only 19% of the reoperation group met these criteria. Notably, the cross-over rate was significantly higher in the reoperation group (67% crossed to SCS) compared to the SCS group (14% crossed to reoperation), indicating a strong patient preference and superior efficacy of SCS ([Spinal cord stimulation versus repeated lumbosacral spine surgery...](local_corpus)).

### Comparative Efficacy of SCS Waveforms

Modern SCS therapy has evolved beyond traditional tonic stimulation (low-frequency) to include subperception modalities such as burst, high-frequency (10 kHz), and high-density stimulation. A comprehensive NMA from 2025 (11 RCTs; n=2275) compared different SCS frequencies for PSPS-T2. Based on Surface Under the Cumulative Ranking (SUCRA) rankings, subperception SCS (500-1200 Hz) demonstrated the highest probability for global pain relief (SUCRA=64.0%) and achieving ≥50% pain reduction (SUCRA=75.3%) ([Comparative Effectiveness of Different Frequencies...](local_corpus)).

Another study comparing differential target multiplexed (DTM) SCS to conventional SCS in PSPS patients found that DTM SCS yielded superior responder rates for chronic low back pain, highlighting the continued innovation in waveform optimization ([Twelve-month results...](local_corpus)). Patient preference studies consistently show a trend toward paresthesia-free stimulation (burst, high-frequency) over tonic stimulation, primarily due to the absence of uncomfortable paresthesias and comparable or superior analgesic efficacy ([Tonic, Burst, High-Density...](local_corpus)).

### Lead Type and Delivery Systems

The hardware design of SCS systems also impacts efficacy. A systematic review and meta-analysis (2025) comparing paddle leads (surgical) to cylindrical leads (percutaneous) found that Paddle-SCS demonstrated superior long-term pain reduction. The standardized mean difference (SMD) for pain reduction on the VAS was 5.37 for Paddle-SCS compared to 4.09 for cylindrical leads. Paddle leads are associated with lower migration rates and more precise targeting but require a laminectomy for placement ([Cylindrical vs Paddle Leads...](local_corpus); [Spinal cord stimulation electrode design...](local_corpus)).

However, advancements in minimally invasive techniques, such as the unilateral biportal endoscopy (UBE) approach, are being explored to combine the stability of paddle leads with reduced surgical trauma. A recent case series reported a 92.8% success rate in trial-to-permanent implantation using the UBE technique ([Minimally Invasive Biportal Endoscopic...](local_corpus)).

### Combination Therapies: SCS and Peripheral Nerve Field Stimulation (PNFS)

Given that patients with predominant low back pain often benefit less from traditional SCS alone, combination therapies have been investigated. The multicenter RCT on "SubQStim" demonstrated that adding subcutaneous peripheral nerve field stimulation (PNFS) to SCS significantly improved back pain relief compared to SCS alone. At 12 months, the SCS+PNFS group showed a significant reduction in back pain VAS scores (mean difference), and a higher proportion of patients achieved ≥50% back pain relief ([Long-Term Effect of Peripheral Nerve Field Stimulation...](local_corpus)). Another study confirmed that the combination therapy significantly increases the total electrical charge delivered, suggesting a synergistic effect in pain modulation ([Subcutaneous Stimulation as Add-on Therapy...](local_corpus)).

## Minimally Invasive Procedures: Epidural Injections and Adhesiolysis

Minimally invasive interventions are often employed before or alongside neuromodulation.

### Epidural Steroid Injections (ESI)

Transforaminal epidural steroid injections (TFESI) are commonly used for radicular pain in PSPS-T2. A systematic review and meta-analysis (2021) evaluated their efficacy. While widely utilized, the evidence for long-term efficacy in PSPS-T2 is mixed. The review concluded that for failed back surgery syndrome, the level of evidence is limited, and complications, though rare, can be major ([Transforaminal Epidural Steroid Injections...](local_corpus)).

### Percutaneous Adhesiolysis

Epidural fibrosis is a significant contributor to PSPS-T2. Percutaneous adhesiolysis aims to lyse these adhesions. A 2-year follow-up of an RCT comparing percutaneous adhesiolysis to caudal epidural injections in post-lumbar surgery syndrome found that adhesiolysis provided significant improvement in pain relief and functional status. The study utilized the Numeric Rating Scale (NRS) and Oswestry Disability Index (ODI), showing that 85% of patients in the adhesiolysis group achieved significant relief compared to the control group ([Assessment of effectiveness of percutaneous adhesiolysis...](local_corpus)).

### Adjuvant Therapies in Epidural Injections

Attempts to enhance the efficacy of epidural injections have been studied.
*   **Hyaluronidase:** Adding hyaluronidase to epidural steroid injections has shown promise. A prospective RCT demonstrated that the hyaluronidase group had better quality and duration of analgesia compared to the steroid-only group, suggesting it may improve outcomes in patients with epidural fibrosis ([Adjuvant hyaluronidase to epidural steroid...](local_corpus); [The role of adding hyaluronidase...](local_corpus)).
*   **Dexmedetomidine:** Adding dexmedetomidine to lumbar epidural steroids was evaluated in an RCT. The group receiving dexmedetomidine showed a significant reduction in VAS and ibuprofen consumption, along with improved ODI scores, lasting up to 12 weeks ([The Effect of Dexmedetomidine...](local_corpus)).

## Conservative Medical Management (CMM)

CMM typically serves as the baseline or control group in most interventional studies. It encompasses pharmacotherapy, physical therapy, and psychological support.

### Pharmacological Interventions

Evidence for specific pharmacological efficacy in PSPS-T2 is sparse and generally focused on neuropathic pain agents.
*   **Gabapentinoids:** A randomized crossover study evaluated extended-release gabapentin for FBSS. The results indicated a statistically significant reduction in average pain scores compared to placebo, though the effect size was modest ([Extended-release gabapentin...](local_corpus)). Another RCT compared Pregabalin to Gabapentin, finding both effective but Pregabalin showed superior pain reduction in the 30-day trial ([Pregabalin versus Gabapentin...](local_corpus)).
*   **IV Lidocaine:** A double-blinded crossover study investigated intravenous lidocaine for neuropathic pain in FBSS. Significant reductions in VAS scores were observed with both 1 mg/kg and 5 mg/kg doses compared to saline, suggesting a role for IV infusions in acute exacerbations ([Effect of intravenous lidocaine...](local_corpus)).

### Physical and Alternative Therapies

A randomized controlled trial investigated the effect of auricular acupressure on PSPS patients. While the intervention group showed improvement, the study highlights the ongoing search for non-invasive adjunctive treatments ([The Effects of Auricular Acupressure...](local_corpus)).

## Synthesis and Comparative Effectiveness

When synthesizing the data across treatment modalities, a hierarchy of efficacy and invasiveness becomes apparent.

### Network Meta-Analysis Findings

The NMA from 2026 provides the most robust comparative data. It confirms that SCS, regardless of waveform, generally outperforms CMM and placebo for pain intensity reduction. Within the SCS class, newer waveforms (subperception/high-frequency) appear superior to traditional tonic stimulation ([Systematic review and network meta-analysis...](local_corpus); [Comparative Effectiveness of Different Frequencies...](local_corpus)).

Regarding the comparison between SCS and reoperation, the evidence from the 2005 RCT remains pivotal. SCS offered better pain relief (leg pain specifically), higher patient satisfaction, and lower cross-over rates, establishing it as a preferred option before considering further surgical revision ([Spinal cord stimulation versus repeated lumbosacral spine surgery...](local_corpus)).

### Cost-Effectiveness

Economic evaluations favor SCS over the long term. A cost-utility analysis utilizing data from the DISTINCT RCT found that while the initial cost of SCS is higher, the improvement in quality-adjusted life years (QALYs) makes it cost-effective compared to CMM in the medium-to-long term ([A Cost Effectiveness Analysis...](local_corpus)). Similarly, percutaneous adhesiolysis has been shown to be cost-effective for post-surgery syndrome, offering a high cost-utility compared to repeated injections ([Cost Utility Analysis of Percutaneous Adhesiolysis...](local_corpus)).

### Safety and Complications

*   **SCS:** Complications include lead migration, infection, and hardware malfunction. Biological complications are less frequent. A retrospective analysis noted that hardware-related complications can compromise therapy but are manageable ([Biological and hardware-related...](local_corpus)).
*   **Reoperation:** Carries the risks associated with general anesthesia and major surgery, with a high risk of failure and "failed re-operation" syndrome.
*   **Injections:** Generally safe but carry risks of dural puncture, infection, and rare neurological injuries.

## Discussion

The management of PSPS-T2 requires a stepwise, mechanism-based approach. The 2026 consensus recommendations emphasize redefining diagnostic clusters based on pain mechanisms (e.g., neuropathic vs. nociceptive) rather than just surgical history. This shift aids in selecting the appropriate intervention ([The Implementation of Persistent Spinal Pain Syndrome...](local_corpus)).

For **predominant leg pain**, SCS is the gold standard for refractory cases. The evidence is robust (GRADE high/moderate) supporting its use over CMM and reoperation. High-frequency and burst stimulation offer advantages in back pain coverage and patient preference.

For **predominant low back pain**, traditional SCS efficacy is limited. Here, the evidence supports the use of multicolumn leads, combination therapy (SCS + PNFS), or DTM SCS. The multicolumn RCT showed significant responder rates for low back pain using these advanced systems ([Multicolumn spinal cord stimulation...](local_corpus)).

**Minimally invasive procedures** like adhesiolysis serve as a bridge between CMM and neuromodulation. They are particularly indicated when epidural fibrosis is identified as a primary pain generator.

**Reoperation** should be reserved for specific indications such as gross instability or recurrent disc herniation with clear nerve root compression. The 2005 RCT suggests that without these specific indications, SCS provides superior outcomes.

## Conclusion

Based on the synthesis of systematic reviews and meta-analyses, SCS stands out as the most effective intervention for pain reduction in PSPS-T2, particularly for radicular components. Subperception waveforms (500-1200 Hz, Burst, 10kHz) and combination therapies (SCS+PNFS) represent the current forefront of efficacy, addressing both leg and back pain components. Reoperation is largely inferior to SCS in randomized settings and should be cautiously approached. Minimally invasive options like adhesiolysis provide meaningful relief for specific subgroups but generally show lower effect sizes compared to neuromodulation. Conservative management remains the foundation of care but is insufficient as a standalone therapy for refractory cases.
