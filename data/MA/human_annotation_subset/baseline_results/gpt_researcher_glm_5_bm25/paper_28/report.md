# A Systematic Review of Influences on Engagement with Remote Health Interventions Targeting Weight Management for Individuals Living with Excess Weight

## Introduction

Obesity rates are continually rising globally, presenting a significant public health challenge associated with increased morbidity and mortality. Remote weight management interventions have emerged as a feasible and scalable solution to address this epidemic, particularly in light of barriers to accessing traditional in-person care. However, the effectiveness of these interventions is intrinsically linked to patient engagement. A critical gap remains in understanding the specific barriers and facilitators that influence the engagement of adults living with excess weight in synchronous (real-time) remote health interventions. This report synthesizes findings from a systematic review that examines these influences, applying the COM-B model (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation) and the Theoretical Domains Framework (TDF) to provide a comprehensive behavioural analysis ([A systematic review of influences on engagement with remote health interventions targeting weight management for individuals living with excess weight](local_corpus)).

Previous reviews have investigated influential factors in community settings, but limited research has specifically examined adults' engagement in synchronous remote weight management programmes. This distinction is vital because synchronous delivery—characterized by real-time interaction via video-conferencing or telephone—presents unique challenges and opportunities compared to asynchronous or in-person modalities. This review focuses on adults aged 18 years and older with a Body Mass Index (BMI) of ≥27.5 kg/m², a population often requiring intensive support for weight management ([A systematic review of influences on engagement with remote health interventions targeting weight management for individuals living with excess weight](local_corpus)).

## Methodology Overview

The systematic review was conducted using a rigorous methodology to ensure a comprehensive synthesis of the evidence. A search of 12 databases was conducted from inception to October 2023. Studies were included if they delivered a synchronous, remote weight management intervention to participants meeting the age and BMI criteria. A narrative synthesis with inductive thematic analysis was conducted to iteratively extrapolate barriers and facilitators to engagement. The identified themes were then deductively mapped to the COM-B model and the TDF. This dual-framework approach allows for a granular understanding of the behavioural determinants, moving beyond simple identification of factors to a theoretical explanation of the mechanisms of action ([A systematic review of influences on engagement with remote health interventions targeting weight management for individuals living with excess weight](local_corpus)).

## Barriers and Facilitators to Engagement

The synthesis of qualitative and quantitative data revealed a complex interplay of factors influencing engagement. These factors are categorized below according to the COM-B model and further detailed through the lens of the Theoretical Domains Framework.

### Capability

Capability refers to an individual's psychological and physical capacity to engage in the activity concerned. It encompasses whether an individual has the necessary knowledge, skills, and abilities.

**Psychological Capability**
The review highlighted that a lack of digital literacy and technological confidence acts as a significant barrier, particularly among older adults. Participants often expressed anxiety regarding the use of video-conferencing software or health apps, which could lead to disengagement. This aligns with the "Knowledge" domain of the TDF, specifically regarding knowledge of task execution. Conversely, interventions that provided clear onboarding and technical support were identified as facilitators of engagement. The "Skills" domain was also prominent; participants required specific behavioural skills, such as self-monitoring and goal-setting, to effectively utilize the remote tools. The development of interventions that incorporate pedagogical theory to boost knowledge integration has been shown to safeguard fidelity and adherence in remote programs ([Development and Feasibility of an eHealth Diabetes Prevention Program Adapted for Older Adults-Results from a Randomized Control Pilot Study](local_corpus)).

**Physical Capability**
Physical limitations, such as impaired mobility or reduced energy, were identified as barriers, particularly for interventions involving physical activity components. However, the remote setting can also serve as a facilitator for those with physical limitations, as it eliminates the need for transportation to a facility. A study on rural older adults with obesity noted that delivering physical therapist-led strength and balance training via video-conferencing allowed participants to exercise in a safe, familiar environment, thereby enhancing adherence ([Feasibility and acceptability of a technology-based, rural weight management intervention in older adults with obesity](local_corpus)).

### Opportunity

Opportunity refers to the external factors that make the behaviour possible or prompt it. This includes both physical and social factors.

**Physical Opportunity**
Physical opportunity encompasses the environmental context and resources. A primary barrier identified across multiple studies was the lack of reliable internet access or adequate hardware (e.g., smartphones, computers). This digital divide disproportionately affects individuals in rural or lower socioeconomic areas, potentially exacerbating health inequalities ([Digital Health Interventions for Cardiometabolic Health Outcomes in Rural and Remote Australia: A Systematic Review](local_corpus)). The COVID-19 pandemic acted as a catalyst for remote interventions, creating a physical opportunity by necessitating the shift away from in-person care. The convenience of attending sessions from home was a strong facilitator, reducing time and transportation burdens. However, this same environment can also present barriers; distractions within the domestic sphere often interfered with session attendance and focus ([Defying distance: exercise providers' perspectives on remote physical activity supports for older adults: a mixed-methods study](local_corpus)).

**Social Opportunity**
Social opportunity involves the cultural milieu and interpersonal interactions. A significant facilitator of engagement in synchronous interventions was the development of a therapeutic alliance with the healthcare provider. The real-time nature of synchronous delivery allows for immediate feedback and empathetic interaction, fostering trust. Social support from peers in group-based video sessions was also a key motivator. The absence of a supportive home environment, however, was noted as a barrier. For instance, if family members were unsupportive of dietary changes or interrupted sessions, engagement suffered. Studies emphasize that interventions utilizing motivational interviewing techniques delivered via synchronous chat rooms can enhance weight loss outcomes, underscoring the importance of the interpersonal dynamic in remote settings ([Design and Methods of a Synchronous Online Motivational Interviewing Intervention for Weight Management](local_corpus)).

### Motivation

Motivation refers to the brain processes that energize and direct behaviour, encompassing both reflective and automatic mechanisms.

**Reflective Motivation**
Reflective motivation involves conscious decision-making and evaluation. Key barriers included a lack of perceived benefit or disillusionment with weight loss progress. Participants who did not see immediate results were more likely to disengage. The TDF domain of "Beliefs about Capabilities" (Self-efficacy) was critical; individuals who doubted their ability to lose weight or manage technology were less engaged. Interventions that set age-appropriate goals and provided regular, personalized feedback enhanced self-efficacy and sustained motivation. The use of cognitive-behavioral strategies to address maladaptive thoughts around weight and diet was highlighted as a facilitator. Research indicates that tailoring interventions to cognitive-behavioral phenotypes can significantly improve adherence by addressing specific psychological barriers such as emotional eating or lack of impulse control ([Personalizing a Weight Loss Program Using Cognitive-Behavioral Phenotypes to Improve Engagement and Weight Loss in Adults With Overweight or Obesity: Quasi-Experimental Study](local_corpus)).

**Automatic Motivation**
Automatic motivation involves impulses, desires, and habits. The review identified that pre-existing habits around food and sedentary behaviour were major barriers to engagement in lifestyle changes. Emotional distress and psychological stress often triggered "emotional eating," which derailed engagement with dietary protocols. Conversely, the immediate reinforcement provided by interactive remote technologies, such as real-time data feedback from wearable devices, served as a facilitator. Gamification elements and positive reinforcement during synchronous sessions could enhance intrinsic motivation. A study on sensor-controlled digital games for older adults with heart failure demonstrated that integrating behavior-tracking sensors to trigger rewards in a game format could motivate real-time physical activity behaviors ([Usability Testing of a Sensor-Controlled Digital Game to Engage Older Adults with Heart Failure in Physical Activity and Weight Monitoring](local_corpus)).

## Contextual Influences: Rural and Underserved Populations

The review paid particular attention to the context of rural and underserved populations, who often face compounded barriers to weight management care. For these groups, remote interventions are not merely a convenience but a necessity due to the scarcity of local specialist services.

In rural Australia, for example, digital health technologies were found to offer a promising solution to enhance healthcare access. However, the successful implementation required addressing specific barriers such as poor digital infrastructure and lower health literacy. Facilitators included the use of culturally appropriate content and the involvement of local trusted figures to endorse the programs ([Digital Health Interventions for Cardiometabolic Health Outcomes in Rural and Remote Australia: A Systematic Review](local_corpus)). Similarly, for Black women with obesity, who are disproportionately affected by structural barriers like poverty and limited healthcare access, telehealth interventions showed promise in advancing health equity. A meta-analysis found no statistically significant difference in weight loss outcomes between remote and in-person interventions for this group, suggesting that remote delivery can effectively bridge the gap in access without compromising efficacy ([Advancing Health Equity Through Telehealth: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis of Remote vs. In-person Weight-loss Interventions among Black Women with Obesity](local_corpus)).

## Comparative Effectiveness and Delivery Modalities

While the primary focus is on synchronous interventions, understanding how they compare to asynchronous and in-person delivery provides context for engagement patterns.

Synchronous remote delivery (e.g., video-conferencing) offers the accountability and interpersonal connection of in-person visits with the convenience of remote access. Asynchronous delivery (e.g., apps, online modules) offers greater flexibility but lacks the immediate social accountability. A randomized noninferiority trial protocol comparing synchronous videoconference meetings to asynchronous delivery via private Facebook groups hypothesized that the asynchronous group might demonstrate better sustainability due to flexibility, though it may lack the immediate engagement boost of synchronous interaction ([Comparing Synchronous and Asynchronous Remotely Delivered Lifestyle Interventions: Protocol for a Randomized Noninferiority Trial](local_corpus)).

A systematic review comparing in-person and remote delivery found that online class participants often had significantly greater reductions in BMI and weight than in-person class participants, suggesting that the remote modality can be highly effective if engagement is sustained. However, physical activity confidence increased more for in-person participants, highlighting that certain behavioral outcomes may still benefit from face-to-face interaction ([Using synchronous distance-education technology to deliver a weight management intervention](local_corpus)).

## Synthesis of Evidence: Mapping to the TDF

The mapping of themes to the Theoretical Domains Framework provides a structured overview of the intervention components required to address the identified barriers.

| TDF Domain | Barriers | Facilitators |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Knowledge** | Lack of awareness of program benefits; insufficient understanding of weight management principles. | Comprehensive onboarding; educational content integrated into the remote platform. |
| **Skills** | Low digital literacy; poor self-regulation skills. | Technical training modules; teaching of behavioural skills (e.g., self-monitoring). |
| **Memory, Attention, Decision Processes** | Forgetting appointments; difficulty tracking progress. | Automated reminders; real-time prompts during synchronous sessions. |
| **Behavioural Regulation** | Inability to break old habits; lack of routine. | Action planning; implementation intentions discussed in sessions. |
| **Environmental Context & Resources** | Unreliable internet; unsupportive home environment. | Provision of hardware/data; involvement of household members in goal setting. |
| **Social Influences** | Lack of peer support; negative cultural norms. | Group-based video sessions; building online communities; social comparison features. |
| **Beliefs about Capabilities** | Low self-efficacy; fear of failure. | Past success reinforcement; verbal persuasion from providers. |
| **Beliefs about Consequences** | Skepticism about weight loss efficacy; fear of adverse effects. | Sharing evidence-based outcomes; managing expectations early. |
| **Goals** | Conflict with other life priorities; unclear goals. | Collaborative goal setting; values-based motivation (e.g., health for family). |
| **Reinforcement** | Lack of immediate results; delayed gratification. | Gamification; frequent positive feedback; celebration of small wins. |
| **Emotion** | Stress, depression, anxiety; emotional eating. | Integration of psychological support (e.g., CBT, ACT); stress management techniques. |
| **Optimism** | Hopelessness regarding weight management. | Success stories; reframing setbacks. |

## Discussion and Implications

The findings of this systematic review underscore that engagement with remote weight management interventions is not merely a function of willpower but is determined by a complex system of interacting factors spanning capability, opportunity, and motivation. The synchronous nature of the interventions reviewed appears to be a double-edged sword. While it provides the structure and social accountability that many individuals with obesity require, it also introduces technological and scheduling barriers that can hinder participation.

The application of the COM-B model and TDF provides a robust theoretical basis for designing future interventions. For instance, to address Capability barriers, interventions must incorporate technical support and simplify user interfaces, particularly for older populations. For older adults with obesity, pilot studies suggest that age-adapted modifications, such as age-appropriate visuals and examples, are crucial for boosting adherence ([Development and Feasibility of an eHealth Diabetes Prevention Program Adapted for Older Adults-Results from a Randomized Control Pilot Study](local_corpus)).

To address Opportunity barriers, policymakers and practitioners must consider the digital divide. Providing devices or subsidizing internet access may be necessary to ensure equitable engagement. Furthermore, Opportunity can be maximized by leveraging the "social cure"—the concept that social relationships significantly impact health outcomes. Facilitating peer support groups through video-conferencing can mimic the community feel of in-person programs.

Motivation barriers are perhaps the most challenging to address. The review suggests that a "one-size-fits-all" approach is insufficient. Tailoring interventions to individual cognitive-behavioural phenotypes—identifying whether a participant is an "emotional eater," lacks confidence, or struggles with impulse control—and then matching specific behaviour change techniques to these profiles, can significantly enhance engagement. The integration of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) and Motivational Interviewing (MI) within remote platforms shows promise in addressing deep-seated psychological barriers ([Design and development of a smartphone app for hypertension management: An intervention mapping approach](local_corpus)).

The review also highlights the importance of hybrid models. While the focus here is on synchronous remote interventions, evidence suggests that a blended approach—combining remote synchronous sessions with asynchronous digital tools (like apps for self-monitoring)—may offer the optimal balance of support and flexibility ([Potential role of hybrid weight management intervention: A scoping review](local_corpus)). This aligns with findings that app engagement is a strong predictor of weight loss in blended-care interventions, as it allows for continuous self-monitoring and feedback between synchronous sessions ([App Engagement as a Predictor of Weight Loss in Blended-Care Interventions: Retrospective Observational Study Using Large-Scale Real-World Data](local_corpus)).

## Conclusion

Engagement in synchronous remote weight management interventions for adults with excess weight is influenced by a multifaceted array of barriers and facilitators. This systematic review confirms that successful engagement requires a holistic approach that addresses psychological capability through skill-building, opportunity through technological and social support, and motivation through personalized and theoretically grounded behaviour change techniques. By mapping these influences to the COM-B model and TDF, researchers and practitioners are better equipped to design and implement interventions that are not only effective but also accessible and engaging for the diverse population of adults living with obesity. Future research should focus on the long-term maintenance of engagement and the comparative effectiveness of different synchronous delivery platforms across varied demographic and socioeconomic groups.
