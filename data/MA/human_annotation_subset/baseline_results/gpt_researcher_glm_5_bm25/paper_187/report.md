# Effectiveness of Team-Based Learning Versus Other Teaching Pedagogies in Undergraduate Dental Education: A Scoping Review

## Introduction

The landscape of dental education has undergone a significant paradigm shift in recent decades, moving away from passive, instructor-centered models toward active, student-centered learning environments. Among the various innovative pedagogies, Team-Based Learning (TBL) has emerged as a prominent strategy designed to enhance student engagement, collaboration, and clinical reasoning. TBL is a structured form of small-group learning that emphasizes student preparation outside of class followed by in-class application activities. In undergraduate dental education, where the acquisition of both theoretical knowledge and practical clinical skills is paramount, the effectiveness of TBL compared to traditional lecture-based learning (LBL) and other dialectic approaches remains a subject of rigorous inquiry.

This scoping review synthesizes evidence from recent literature to investigate whether TBL is an effective alternative teaching pedagogy for delivering undergraduate dental education. Specifically, this report examines outcomes related to **student satisfaction** and **academic performance**, comparing TBL against traditional didactic teaching and other active learning methods such as the flipped classroom (FC) and problem-based learning (PBL).

## Effectiveness of Team-Based Learning on Academic Performance

### Comparison with Traditional Lecture-Based Learning (LBL)

The evidence strongly suggests that TBL positively influences academic performance in dental education, often yielding superior outcomes compared to traditional lectures. A meta-analysis by [Application of Emerging Teaching Models in Dental Education: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (2024)]() provided a comprehensive evaluation of emerging teaching methods, including TBL, PBL, and flipped classrooms, compared to LBL. The study, which analyzed randomized controlled trials across dental disciplines, found that these active learning methods generally resulted in better academic performance than traditional lectures. Specifically, the analysis indicated a statistically significant improvement in performance scores for students exposed to active learning strategies.

In the specific context of dental prosthodontics, a seminal study by [Effects of team-based learning on fixed prosthodontic education in a Japanese School of Dentistry (2015)]() evaluated 36 fourth-year dental students. The study design involved a transition from traditional lectures to TBL sessions within the same course. Results from the term-end examination revealed significant differences in performance. While high-achieving students performed well in both settings, low-achieving students showed marked improvement in the TBL setting compared to the lecture setting. This suggests that TBL may have a "leveling-up" effect, providing greater support and engagement for students who might struggle in traditional passive learning environments.

Similarly, a cross-sectional study on local anesthesia education by [Team-based learning versus traditional lectures in local anesthesia course: A cross-sectional study exploring dental students' perception and performance (2025)]() compared student performance after TBL sessions and traditional lectures. The study, conducted over two academic years, utilized Pearson's correlation test and found a significant relationship (r = 0.780) between TBL activities and student performance outcomes. The active nature of TBL, which includes tests and group discussions, was credited with enhancing the retention and application of complex procedural knowledge.

### Comparison with Other Active Learning Pedagogies

When comparing TBL to other active learning strategies, the results are nuanced but indicative of TBL's robust effectiveness. A comparative study by [Comparison between flipped classroom and team-based learning in fixed prosthodontic education (2017)]() directly compared the effectiveness of TBL and the flipped classroom (FC) model in a fixed prosthodontics course. The researchers compared term-end examination scores between curriculum segments covered by FC and those covered by TBL. Interestingly, the study found that while both methods were effective, TBL was associated with higher efficiency and high student satisfaction. The term-end examination results suggested that the collaborative problem-solving inherent in TBL might be more effective for certain types of clinical application than the individual pre-class preparation emphasized in FC.

Furthermore, a quasi-experimental study discussed in [Development and Impact of a Case and Team-Based Applied Physiology Course for Pre-Dental Graduate Students (2025)]() demonstrated the efficacy of a combined "Case and Team-Based" approach. Pre-dental students enrolled in an Applied Physiology course using TBL scored significantly higher on Dental Physiology exams compared to those who did not receive the intervention. This highlights that TBL is not only effective as a standalone pedagogy but also potent when integrated with case-based learning (CBL) to reinforce complex scientific concepts relevant to dental practice.

However, it is important to note that TBL is not always significantly superior to other active methods in all contexts. A systematic review and network meta-analysis by [Which novel teaching strategy is most recommended in medical education? A systematic review and network meta-analysis (2024)]() evaluated various strategies including TBL, FC, and PBL. While TBL was found to be effective, the study highlighted that the "optimal" method often depends on the specific learning objectives (e.g., theoretical knowledge vs. practical skills). Nevertheless, TBL consistently ranked high for fostering comprehensive competency and clinical reasoning.

### Impact on Knowledge Retention and Clinical Reasoning

TBL appears to foster deeper learning and better retention. A study by [Validity of Peer Evaluation for Team-Based Learning in a Dental School in Japan (2017)]() investigated the correlations between peer evaluation, Individual Readiness Assurance Tests (IRAT), Group Readiness Assurance Tests (GRAT), and term-end examinations. Significant positive correlations were found between peer evaluations and examination records, suggesting that the accountability and peer interaction inherent in TBL contribute to sustained academic performance. The study implies that the social dynamics of TBL—where students are accountable to their peers—drive a higher level of engagement and preparation, which translates to better academic outcomes.

## Student Satisfaction and Perceptions of Team-Based Learning

### Student Satisfaction with TBL vs. Traditional Lectures

Student satisfaction is a critical metric for evaluating the viability of a teaching pedagogy. The literature indicates a generally high level of satisfaction with TBL among dental students, often exceeding that for traditional lectures. In the [Effects of team-based learning on fixed prosthodontic education (2015)](), student questionnaires revealed "high student approval for TBL-style learning," specifically noting that "active group discussion among students during TBL was a key factor in these ratings." Students appreciated the shift from passive listening to active participation, finding the collaborative environment more stimulating and conducive to learning.

Similarly, the study on local anesthesia ([Team-based learning versus traditional lectures in local anesthesia course, 2025]()) utilized a 10-item questionnaire to gauge student perception. The mean average scores of responses were favorable towards TBL, with students reporting higher engagement and a preference for the interactive nature of the sessions over traditional didactic lectures. The significant correlation found between student perception and performance suggests that the enjoyment of the learning process may be a mediating factor in the academic gains observed.

### Student Satisfaction with TBL vs. Flipped Classrooms

The comparison between TBL and flipped classrooms (FC) regarding satisfaction reveals interesting dynamics. The [Comparison between flipped classroom and team-based learning in fixed prosthodontic education (2017)]() noted that while both methods garnered positive feedback, TBL was reported to have "higher efficiency with high student satisfaction." A potential reason for this preference is the structural support TBL provides; while FC relies heavily on individual self-discipline for pre-class preparation, TBL enforces preparation through the IRAT/GRAT process and leverages peer support during in-class sessions.

However, some studies indicate that the "best" approach may be a hybrid one. [Research on the application and effect of flipped-classroom combined with TBL teaching model (2023)]() investigated a combined model in biochemistry teaching. The results showed that the "flipped classroom combined with TBL" model improved undergraduate satisfaction and learning effectiveness more than traditional methods. This suggests that students appreciate the flexibility of FC (learning at their own pace) combined with the social, interactive benefits of TBL.

### Perception of Active Learning and Soft Skills

Beyond immediate academic satisfaction, TBL is perceived as beneficial for developing "soft skills" essential for dental practice. [Rethinking active learning in medical education: a comparative study of inquiry-based and team-based learning on student performance and satisfaction (2026)]() compared TBL with Inquiry-Based Learning (IBL). While the study found IBL to be effective, TBL was specifically highlighted for fostering "team efficacy" and collaborative skills. This aligns with the findings of [The Effects of Simulation Problem-Based Learning on the Empathy, Attitudes toward Caring for the Elderly, and Team Efficacy of Undergraduate Health Profession Students (2021)](), which, while focusing on simulation PBL, emphasized the importance of interprofessional and team-based activities in boosting student confidence in teamwork—a core component of TBL.

Furthermore, [Adapting Dental Education for the Gen Z: An Overview of Active Learning Strategies (2025)]() points out that Generation Z dental students prefer active, collaborative learning environments. The review confirms that TBL aligns well with the digital-native's preference for interaction and social learning, leading to higher engagement and satisfaction levels compared to the isolation often felt in traditional lecture halls.

## Discussion

### Synthesis of Findings

The synthesis of the provided literature confirms that Team-Based Learning is a highly effective pedagogy in undergraduate dental education. The evidence demonstrates two primary advantages of TBL over traditional Lecture-Based Learning (LBL):
1.  **Academic Performance:** TBL consistently leads to equal or better academic performance, particularly in application-based assessments. It appears to provide significant benefits for lower-achieving students, potentially by distributing cognitive load through group interaction and peer teaching.
2.  **Student Satisfaction:** TBL is generally preferred over traditional lectures. Students value the active engagement, the opportunity for discussion, and the development of teamwork skills, which are directly relevant to their future clinical practice.

When compared to other dialectic methods like Flipped Classrooms (FC), TBL holds its own. While FC is effective for knowledge transmission, TBL may offer superior outcomes in terms of student accountability and in-class engagement efficiency. The literature suggests that a blended approach (FC + TBL) might represent the "gold standard," leveraging the strengths of both asynchronous preparation and synchronous team application.

### Methodological Considerations and Limitations

While the findings are positive, there are limitations to consider. Many studies, such as the [Effects of team-based learning on fixed prosthodontic education (2015)](), utilized quasi-experimental designs or cross-sectional surveys rather than randomized controlled trials (RCTs). The [Application of Emerging Teaching Models in Dental Education (2024)]() meta-analysis highlighted the need for more rigorous RCTs to minimize heterogeneity. Additionally, the "novelty effect"—where students respond positively simply because the method is new and different—could influence satisfaction scores. However, the consistent correlation found between satisfaction and objective performance metrics (e.g., term-end exams) strengthens the validity of these positive perceptions.

Furthermore, the effectiveness of TBL is contingent upon implementation fidelity. [The Active Classroom: A Narrative Review (2025)]() notes that active learning methods require significant faculty training and resource investment. Poorly implemented TBL, where cases are not clinically relevant or group dynamics are mismanaged, could lead to student dissatisfaction.

### Implications for Dental Education

The shift towards TBL aligns with broader educational reforms emphasizing competency-based education. As noted in [Contemporary educational methods in periodontology (2024)](), periodontal education has successfully integrated student-centered methods like TBL and simulation to address the gap between theory and clinical practice. Dental educators are encouraged to transition from being "sages on the stage" to "guides on the side," facilitating deeper learning through structured team activities.

The emphasis on teamwork in TBL also prepares students for the realities of modern dental practice, where interprofessional collaboration is increasingly required. [Team-based learning versus traditional lectures in local anesthesia course (2025)]() underscores that the skills practiced in TBL—communication, negotiation, and collective decision-making—are the very skills needed in clinical settings.

## Conclusion

Based on the comprehensive review of the provided literature, Team-Based Learning (TBL) is substantiated as an effective and often superior alternative to traditional lecture-based learning in undergraduate dental education. TBL demonstrably enhances academic performance, particularly in the application of clinical knowledge, and achieves higher levels of student satisfaction by fostering an engaging, collaborative, and clinically relevant learning environment. While comparable to other active methods like the flipped classroom, TBL's unique structure of individual and group readiness assurance tests provides a mechanism for accountability and peer-supported learning that is distinctively beneficial. Therefore, dental institutions seeking to modernize their curricula should consider TBL as a primary pedagogical strategy, either as a standalone approach or integrated with flipped classroom elements, to optimize both student satisfaction and educational outcomes.
