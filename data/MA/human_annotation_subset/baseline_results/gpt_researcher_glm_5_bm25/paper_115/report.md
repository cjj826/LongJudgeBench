# Survival Benefit of Cytoreductive Surgery in Primary Stage IV Endometrial Cancer: A Systematic Review

## Introduction

Endometrial cancer (EC) is the most common gynecological malignancy in high-income countries, with its incidence steadily increasing over the past three decades. While the majority of women are diagnosed with early-stage disease and have a favorable prognosis, approximately 3% to 13% of patients present with or are diagnosed with stage IV disease at the time of surgical staging. The management of advanced-stage endometrial cancer, particularly stage IV disease, remains a significant clinical challenge due to a lack of prospective randomized evidence to guide treatment strategies.

The concept of cytoreductive surgery (CRS), which aims to reduce the tumor burden to minimal or no residual disease, has been a cornerstone in the management of advanced ovarian cancer. This paradigm has been extrapolated to the management of stage IV endometrial cancer, with the hypothesis that optimal surgical cytoreduction may similarly improve survival outcomes. However, unlike in ovarian cancer, where the survival benefit of optimal cytoreduction is well-established, the evidence for endometrial cancer is primarily derived from retrospective cohort studies and, more recently, systematic reviews and meta-analyses of these studies.

This report synthesizes the available evidence from systematic reviews and meta-analyses to investigate whether complete or optimal cytoreductive surgery improves overall survival compared to incomplete or suboptimal CRS in patients with primary stage IV endometrial cancer.

## Defining Optimal Cytoreduction in Endometrial Cancer

A critical aspect of interpreting the evidence is the lack of a standardized definition for "optimal" cytoreduction in endometrial cancer. The literature reveals variability in the threshold used to define optimal cytoreduction:

-   **Residual Disease ≤1 cm:** This definition is commonly used, aligning with the traditional ovarian cancer standard. Studies such as the 2000 analysis of Stage IVB endometrial carcinoma by Greer et al. and the 2019 study on advanced-stage endometrial cancer utilized this threshold.
-   **Residual Disease ≤2 cm:** Earlier studies, including a 1997 review and the 2010 study on extra-abdominal metastasis, defined optimal cytoreduction as residual tumor ≤2 cm.
-   **No Gross Residual Disease (R0):** More recent and contemporary analyses, including a 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis and a 2015 retrospective cohort study, emphasize "maximal" or "complete" cytoreduction, defined as no gross residual disease (R0), as the most significant prognostic goal.

The evolution from ≤2 cm to no gross residual disease reflects a growing understanding that in endometrial cancer, similar to ovarian cancer, survival outcomes improve incrementally as the amount of residual disease decreases.

## Evidence from Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

The most comprehensive evidence comes from a 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis titled "Primary cytoreductive surgery for advanced stage endometrial cancer." This study aimed to summarize the evidence about primary cytoreductive surgery in the treatment of advanced-stage endometrial cancer. The researchers searched MEDLINE, Embase, and Scopus databases for full-text English reports that included ≥10 patients undergoing primary cytoreductive surgery for advanced-stage endometrial cancer and reported survival rates based on residual disease burden. The key finding was that the percentage of cases reaching maximal cytoreduction (no gross residual disease) was a primary outcome, with the meta-analysis providing pooled survival data. The study concluded that achieving no gross residual disease offers the most significant survival benefit, reinforcing the principle that "the less residual disease, the better" applies to endometrial cancer.

Another relevant meta-analytic approach is seen in a 2025 study on the prognostic value of molecular classification in stage IV endometrial cancer, which retrospectively analyzed patients treated with primary cytoreductive surgery or cytoreductive surgery after induction chemotherapy. While its primary focus was molecular classification, the study context confirms the continued use and evaluation of cytoreductive surgery in this population.

## Evidence from Major Retrospective Cohort Studies

The conclusions of systematic reviews are underpinned by numerous retrospective cohort studies, which provide the granular data on survival outcomes based on cytoreduction status.

### The Impact of Residual Disease on Survival

A seminal 2015 retrospective cohort study titled "Survival impact of cytoreduction to microscopic disease for advanced stage cancer of the uterine corpus" specifically assessed the impact of cytoreduction to no gross residual disease (R0) on overall survival in patients with stage III-IV uterine cancers. The cohort included patients with carcinosarcoma (CS), papillary serous/clear cell (UPSC/CC), and endometrioid carcinoma (EC). The study found that R0 was achieved in a majority of patients (64% CS, 53% UPSC/CC, 68% EC) and that achieving R0 was a significant predictor of improved overall survival.

A 2019 study on advanced stage (IIIC/IV) endometrial cancer analyzed 45 patients and found that optimal cytoreduction to ≤1 cm visible disease was achieved in 64.4% of women. The study identified optimal cytoreduction as a key factor influencing survival outcomes.

### Large Multi-Institutional Analyses

Several large multi-institutional studies from Japan have provided robust data on this topic. A 2012 retrospective multi-institutional analysis of 248 patients with surgical stage IVb endometrial cancer found a median overall survival of 24 months. The most common histological types were endometrioid and serous. The study concluded that complete resection of extra-abdominal metastases and optimal cytoreduction were significant prognostic factors.

A follow-up study in 2013 by the same group expanded the analysis to 426 patients with clinical and surgical stage IVb endometrial cancer. The median overall survival for all patients was 14 months. Crucially, the study compared initial treatment strategies: primary surgery (n=279), primary chemotherapy (n=125), and palliative care (n=22). The median overall survival times were significantly different: 21 months for the primary surgery group, 12 months for the primary chemotherapy group, and 1 month for the palliative care group (p<0.0001). While this demonstrates a survival advantage for the primary surgery group, it is important to note that patients selected for primary surgery generally had better performance status and potentially more resectable disease.

### Studies Focused on Specific Histologies

The benefit of cytoreductive surgery appears consistent across aggressive histological subtypes of endometrial cancer:

-   **Uterine Papillary Serous Carcinoma (UPSC):** A 2001 study evaluated 31 patients with Stage IV UPSC. The median survival for all patients was 14.4 months. The only significant predictor of suboptimal surgical outcome was disease in three or more anatomic regions. A 2022 multicenter retrospective analysis of 68 patients with stage IVB uterine serous carcinoma (USC) further confirmed the survival impact of cytoreductive surgery. This study defined optimal cytoreduction (R1) as residual disease ≤1 cm and suboptimal cytoreduction (R2) as >1 cm. Despite a poor overall prognosis with a reported 5-year survival of <20%, optimal cytoreduction followed by chemoradiation was associated with improved survival outcomes.
-   **Uterine Carcinosarcoma (CS):** A 2011 study assessed the role of cytoreductive surgery in 44 patients with stage IIIC-IVB uterine CS. Complete gross resection was achieved in 57% of patients. The median progression-free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS) for the entire cohort were 8.6 and 18.5 months, respectively. Complete gross resection was significantly associated with improved survival compared to incomplete resection.

### Studies on Extra-Abdominal Metastasis

A unique study published in 2010 specifically examined the benefits of cytoreductive surgery for 33 stage IVb cases with extra-abdominal metastases, a scenario with an extremely poor prognosis. Cytoreduction was conducted in 30 cases. The median PFS and OS for patients with optimal cytoreduction (defined as residual masses ≤2 cm in this study) were significantly better than those with suboptimal reduction (residual masses >2 cm). This benefit was observed not only among patients with only intra-abdominal metastasis but also, notably, among those with extra-abdominal metastases. This finding suggests that even in the presence of distant disease, optimal cytoreduction of the intra-abdominal tumor burden can confer a survival advantage.

## Prognostic Factors Influencing Survival

While cytoreduction status is a strong independent prognostic factor, other variables also significantly influence survival in stage IV endometrial cancer:

-   **Lymphovascular Space Invasion (LVSI):** A 2016 single oncology centre review of 104 cases with stage III-IV endometrial cancer confirmed that LVSI is an independent poor prognostic factor for both PFS and OS.
-   **Molecular Classification:** Emerging evidence highlights the prognostic value of molecular classification. A 2024 study determined which molecular subclasses are found in stage IV endometrial cancer and correlated them with survival. The 2025 PORTEC-3 trial's post-hoc analysis further emphasized that molecular classification can guide adjuvant therapy decisions, which indirectly impacts survival outcomes.
-   **Histological Subtype:** Non-endometrioid histologies, such as serous carcinoma and carcinosarcoma, are associated with a poorer prognosis compared to endometrioid adenocarcinoma. However, the benefit of cytoreduction appears consistent across these subtypes.

## Comparison of Surgical Approaches: Primary vs. Interval Debulking

The optimal timing of surgery—whether primary debulking surgery (PDS) or interval debulking surgery (IDS) after neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NACT)—remains a subject of debate.

-   **Primary Debulking Surgery (PDS):** Traditionally considered the criterion standard, PDS is often recommended if complete resection can be achieved, as per 2023 clinical practice recommendations.
-   **Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy (NACT):** A 2020 cohort study using the National Cancer Database identified women with stage IV endometrial cancer treated with NACT or PDS. The study found that NACT is increasingly used and offers an alternative for patients where PDS might be associated with significant morbidity or where optimal cytoreduction is not initially feasible. A 2025 real-world study from India further evaluated NACT in patients deemed unfit for primary surgery, finding that radiologic response to NACT could predict interval debulking surgery outcomes.

The decision often hinges on the surgeon's ability to achieve optimal cytoreduction and the patient's overall condition. If PDS can achieve no gross residual disease, it remains the preferred approach. However, NACT followed by IDS is a valid strategy for selected patients.

## Conclusion and Clinical Implications

Based on the comprehensive review of the available evidence, primarily from systematic reviews and large retrospective cohort studies, the following conclusions can be drawn:

1.  **Survival Benefit is Established:** There is strong and consistent evidence that achieving optimal cytoreduction, particularly no gross residual disease (R0), significantly improves overall survival in patients with primary stage IV endometrial cancer compared to suboptimal or incomplete cytoreduction.
2.  **Definition Matters:** The definition of "optimal" has evolved. Current evidence supports aiming for no gross residual disease (R0) as the goal, as survival outcomes improve with decreasing volumes of residual disease.
3.  **Benefit Across Subtypes:** The survival advantage of optimal cytoreduction is observed across different histological subtypes, including aggressive variants like uterine papillary serous carcinoma and carcinosarcoma.
4.  **Benefit in Selected Extra-Abdominal Disease:** Even in patients with extra-abdominal metastases, optimal cytoreduction of intra-abdominal disease can provide a meaningful survival benefit.
5.  **Role of NACT:** NACT followed by interval debulking surgery is a viable alternative for patients who are poor candidates for primary surgery or where optimal cytoreduction is unlikely at presentation.

In summary, cytoreductive surgery plays a crucial role in the management of stage IV endometrial cancer. The primary goal should be maximal surgical effort to achieve no gross residual disease whenever feasible, as this is the most significant modifiable prognostic factor for improved survival in this advanced-stage disease.
