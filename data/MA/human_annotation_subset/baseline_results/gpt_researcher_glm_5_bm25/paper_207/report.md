# Associations Between Ocular Posterior Pole Structures and Female Reproductive Hormonal Changes: A Scoping Review

## Introduction

The female reproductive system undergoes cyclical hormonal variations throughout a woman's life, primarily driven by fluctuations in estrogen and progesterone. These hormones are not limited to reproductive functions; they cross the blood-retinal barrier and bind to intraocular receptors, suggesting a potential influence on ocular anatomy and physiology ([Macular, choroidal and disc associations across women's reproductive life stages: a scoping review from menarche to post-menopause., 2025](local_corpus)). Understanding the interplay between reproductive hormones and ocular structures—specifically the macula, choroid, and optic disc—is essential for comprehending the physiological baseline of the female eye and distinguishing pathological changes.

This report synthesizes evidence from clinical studies and scoping reviews to examine the associations between posterior pole structures and hormonal fluctuations across reproductive life stages. The focus is on healthy adult non-pregnant women not using hormonal contraception or replacement therapy, covering the menstrual cycle and the post-menopausal period.

## The Menstrual Cycle and Ocular Structures

The menstrual cycle is characterized by distinct hormonal phases: the follicular phase (dominated by estrogen), ovulation, and the luteal phase (dominated by progesterone). Research has increasingly utilized advanced imaging techniques like Enhanced Depth Imaging Optical Coherence Tomography (EDI-OCT) and Swept-Source OCT Angiography (SS-OCTA) to investigate morphological changes in the eye during these phases.

### Choroidal Thickness Dynamics

The choroid, a highly vascularized layer supplying the outer retina, is the ocular structure most consistently reported to change during the menstrual cycle. Multiple studies indicate a pattern of choroidal thinning during the luteal phase compared to the follicular phase.

In a prospective study of 15 healthy Japanese women, subfoveal choroidal thickness (SCT) was found to be significantly decreased in the mid-luteal phase compared to the late follicular phase. This thinning coincided with significant elevations in systolic, diastolic, and mean blood pressure during the mid-luteal phase, suggesting a link between systemic hemodynamic changes and choroidal morphology ([Choroidal Morphology and Systemic Circulation Changes During the Menstrual Cycle in Healthy Japanese Women., 2023](local_corpus)). Similarly, a 2013 study evaluating 23 women with regular cycles confirmed significant variations in choroidal thickness across the early follicular, ovulatory, and mid-luteal phases ([Choroidal thickness changes during the menstrual cycle., 2013](local_corpus)).

A more detailed assessment of choroidal vascularity involving 36 eyes demonstrated that both choroidal thickness and the Choroidal Vascularity Index (CVI)—a marker of vascular luminal area—were significantly altered in the mid-luteal phase. Specifically, the subfoveal, nasal, and temporal choroidal thicknesses were significantly reduced compared to early follicular and ovulatory phases ([Assessment of choroidal vascularity index during the menstrual cycle., 2022](local_corpus)). These findings are consolidated by a recent comprehensive scoping review, which identified choroidal thickness as the most consistently reported parameter, noting clear thinning during the luteal phase relative to the follicular phase ([Macular, choroidal and disc associations across women's reproductive life stages: a scoping review from menarche to post-menopause., 2025](local_corpus)).

### Retinal Microvasculature and Macular Integrity

While choroidal thickness shows consistent trends, retinal microvascular changes are more variable. A study using SS-OCTA to assess 31 healthy women found that while choroidal thickness fluctuated, the vessel density in the superficial capillary plexus (SCP), deep capillary plexus (DCP), and choriocapillaris (CC) remained stable across the early follicular, ovulatory, and mid-luteal phases. Similarly, the foveal avascular zone (FAZ) area showed no significant changes, suggesting that the retinal vasculature may be less susceptible to physiological hormonal shifts compared to the choroid ([OCTA-based assessment of macular and peripapillary vessel changes during the menstrual cycle., 2025](local_corpus)).

However, other evidence suggests that physiological variations do occur. A 2024 study analyzing retinal hemodynamics over the course of a full menstrual cycle found physiological variations in perfusion density in both superficial and deep vascular plexuses, as well as changes in FAZ perimeter ([Variabilities in Retinal Hemodynamics Across the Menstrual Cycle in Healthy Women Identified Using Optical Coherence Tomography Angiography., 2024](local_corpus)). Another study utilizing OCTA in 62 women reported that while superficial and deep perfusion densities remained stable, the FAZ area was significantly larger in the ovulatory phase compared to the follicular phase ([Retinal Vascular Changes during the Menstrual Cycle Detected with Optical Coherence Tomography Angiography., 2021](local_corpus)).

These discrepancies highlight a lack of consensus regarding retinal vascular changes. It is hypothesized that generalized hormonal effects on heart rate, blood pressure, and cardiac output may cancel each other out to maintain stable ocular blood flow and perfusion, particularly in retrobulbar vessels ([Change in retrobulbar circulation during menstrual cycle assessed by Doppler ultrasound., 2002](local_corpus)). This regulatory mechanism might preserve retinal stability while the choroid, having higher blood flow and looser autoregulation, exhibits more pronounced structural changes.

### Optic Nerve Head Topography

The optic nerve head (ONH) appears relatively stable during the menstrual cycle in healthy women. Studies using confocal scanning laser ophthalmoscopy have shown no significant menstrual-cycle-dependent topographic changes in the ONH of healthy subjects, although women with conditions like diabetes or migraine may exhibit different patterns ([Effect of the menstrual cycle on the optic nerve head in diabetes: analysis by confocal scanning laser ophthalmoscopy., 2005](local_corpus); [The effect of the menstrual cycle on the optic nerve head analysis of migrainous women., 2005](local_corpus)).

## The Post-Menopausal Period

Menopause marks a significant physiological transition characterized by a dramatic decline in estrogen and progesterone. This hormonal drop is associated with structural and vascular changes in the eye, reflecting the neuroprotective and vasodilatory roles of estrogen.

### Choroidal and Macular Thinning

Comparative studies between postmenopausal women and reproductive-age women have revealed significant ocular differences. A spectral-domain OCT study involving 72 postmenopausal women and 72 controls found that the choroid thickness, along with superior inner macula, temporal inner macula, and inferior outer macula parameters, was significantly thinner in the postmenopausal group ([Evaluation of the macula, retinal nerve fiber layer and choroid thickness in postmenopausal women and reproductive-age women using spectral-domain optical coherence tomography., 2014](local_corpus)).

Further corroborating these findings, a cross-sectional SS-OCTA study compared 45 menstrual and 40 postmenopausal subjects. While superficial and deep capillary plexus vessel densities were similar between groups, the choriocapillaris vessel density was significantly lower in the postmenopausal group. Interestingly, the choroidal thickness was found to be thicker in the postmenopausal group in this specific study, a finding attributed by authors to potential compensatory mechanisms or age-related structural alterations, though this contradicts other studies showing thinning ([Comparison of retinochoroidal microvascular circulation in menstrual and postmenopausal periods using swept-source optical coherence tomography angiography., 2023](local_corpus)). The FAZ area was also noted to be wider in the postmenopausal group, indicating potential microvascular dropout or remodeling.

### Optic Nerve Head and Peripapillary Vasculature

The decline in estrogen affects the optic nerve head and peripapillary perfusion. A cross-sectional study of 100 eyes demonstrated that both peripapillary retinal nerve fiber layer thickness (RNFLT) and peripapillary vessel density (VD) were significantly lower in postmenopausal women compared to premenopausal women. The reduction was most pronounced in the inferior quadrant and correlated with higher intraocular pressure (IOP) and hormonal drop ([The effect of postmenopausal hormonal drop on optic nerve head and peripapillary perfusion using optical coherence tomography angiography (OCTA)., 2022](local_corpus)). This suggests that the loss of hormonal support may render the optic nerve more vulnerable to vascular insufficiency and glaucomatous damage.

## Mechanisms of Hormonal Action

The observed associations can be attributed to the biological mechanisms of estrogen and progesterone.

### Vascular and Hemodynamic Effects
Estrogen is known to promote endothelium-dependent vasodilation, primarily through the upregulation of nitric oxide (NO) synthase, leading to vasodilation and increased blood flow. Progesterone, in contrast, can have opposing effects, including vasoconstriction or antagonizing estrogen's effects. During the luteal phase, the dominance of progesterone and the rise in blood pressure may contribute to choroidal thinning. Conversely, the high estrogen levels in the follicular phase may support greater choroidal thickness via vasodilation.

### Fluid Balance and Protein Leakage
Progesterone increases vascular permeability. It is hypothesized that during the luteal phase, increased protein leakage from the fenestrated capillaries of the choriocapillaris into the choroidal stroma could increase oncotic pressure, potentially leading to fluid accumulation or structural changes that affect thickness measurements. However, the clinical consensus leans towards net thinning in the luteal phase, possibly due to the compensatory increase in systemic vascular resistance and blood pressure.

### Neuroprotection
Estrogen has documented neuroprotective properties. Its decline in menopause is associated with a reduction in retinal nerve fiber layer thickness and ganglion cell complex integrity, as evidenced by the lower RNFLT and VD in postmenopausal women ([The effect of postmenopausal hormonal drop on optic nerve head and peripapillary perfusion using optical coherence tomography angiography (OCTA)., 2022](local_corpus)). This loss of hormonal trophic support may accelerate age-related structural decline in the optic nerve and macula.

## Clinical Implications and Methodological Considerations

### Diagnostic Baselines
Understanding these physiological variations is critical for establishing accurate diagnostic baselines. For instance, measuring choroidal thickness in a premenopausal woman without accounting for the menstrual cycle phase could lead to misinterpretation. A thin choroid in the luteal phase might be a normal physiological finding rather than a sign of pathology ([Macular, choroidal and disc associations across women's reproductive life stages: a scoping review from menarche to post-menopause., 2025](local_corpus)).

### Study Limitations
The reviewed literature highlights significant heterogeneity. Studies often define menstrual phases differently (e.g., "early follicular" vs. "late follicular"), use varying imaging protocols, and have small sample sizes. For example, some studies found no change in intraocular pressure (IOP) ([Choroidal Morphology and Systemic Circulation Changes During the Menstrual Cycle in Healthy Japanese Women., 2023](local_corpus)), while others suggest IOP increases postmenopause ([The effect of postmenopausal hormonal drop on optic nerve head and peripapillary perfusion using optical coherence tomography angiography (OCTA)., 2022](local_corpus)). Furthermore, while some studies use Doppler ultrasound to assess retrobulbar flow ([Change in retrobulbar circulation during menstrual cycle assessed by Doppler ultrasound., 2002](local_corpus)), others focus on intraocular microvasculature via OCTA, making direct comparisons challenging.

### Exclusion of Confounders
This report strictly focuses on physiological associations in healthy women not using hormonal contraception or replacement therapy. It is noteworthy that Oral Contraceptive Pills (OCPs) also impact ocular structures; for instance, macular parameters have been reported as considerably lower in OCP users compared to non-users ([The effect of oral contraceptive pills on the macula, the retinal nerve fiber layer, the ganglion cell layer and the choroidal thickness., 2019](local_corpus)). This reinforces the profound sensitivity of the eye to exogenous hormonal modulation.

## Conclusion

The evidence confirms that female reproductive hormonal fluctuations significantly influence ocular posterior pole structures. The choroid is the most responsive structure, exhibiting distinct thinning during the progesterone-dominant luteal phase of the menstrual cycle and varying structural changes post-menopause. Retinal microvasculature shows variability but appears to maintain relative stability compared to the choroid, likely due to efficient autoregulation. The optic nerve head also reflects the impact of hormonal decline, with postmenopausal women showing reduced perfusion and nerve fiber layer thickness.

These findings underscore the necessity of considering reproductive life stages and menstrual cycle phases in ophthalmological assessments and research. Future studies should aim for standardized protocols regarding cycle phase definitions and imaging modalities to further clarify these complex interactions.
