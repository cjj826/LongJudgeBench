# Effectiveness of Very-Low-Calorie and Low-Energy Liquid-Formula Diets in Obese Adults With Versus Without Type 2 Diabetes: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis

## Abstract

Objective: To compare weight loss efficacy of very-low-calorie diets (VLCDs; <800 kcal/day) and low-energy liquid-formula diets (LEDs; >800 kcal/day) between obese adults with type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2D) and obese adults without diabetes, and to summarize body composition and safety outcomes.

Methods: PRISMA-aligned systematic review of randomized and nonrandomized clinical studies in adults with obesity receiving total diet replacement VLCDs or LED formula programs, reporting weight outcomes in diabetic and nondiabetic cohorts. Primary outcome was change in body weight at 4–52 weeks. Secondary outcomes included body composition and adverse events. Random-effects meta-analysis pooled between-group mean differences where feasible; GRADE assessed certainty.

Results: Two comparative VLCD cohorts (n≈51 completers in one, sample size not explicit in the other) directly contrasted obese adults with and without T2D on identical VLCD programs for 24–52 weeks. Pooled quantitative synthesis was limited by sparse, heterogeneous data; however, both studies showed no clinically meaningful difference in weight loss between T2D and nondiabetic participants at program end. In a 24-week VLCD, mean weight loss was 8.5 ± 1.3 kg (T2D) versus 9.4 ± 1.2 kg (non-diabetic), mean difference (MD) −0.9 kg (favoring non-diabetic), P=0.64 (imprecise) ([“Less fat reduction per unit weight loss…” 2012](local_corpus/less-fat-reduction-2012)). At 1 year, VLCD reduced BMI by ~5 kg/m² (T2D) vs ~6 kg/m² (non-diabetic), again indicating broadly comparable weight loss magnitudes ([“An intensive weight loss programme…” 1998](local_corpus/intensive-weight-loss-1998)). Notably, the 2012 study reported less fat mass reduction per unit BMI loss in T2D, suggesting relatively greater lean mass loss risk without countermeasures ([“Less fat reduction per unit weight loss…” 2012](local_corpus/less-fat-reduction-2012)). Direct head-to-head evidence for LED formula diets (>800 kcal/day) comparing T2D versus non-diabetic patients was not identified; single-condition trials indicated clinically meaningful weight losses with partial or total formula use across populations, but cross-study comparisons are confounded by differing designs and durations ([Kjøllesdal et al., 2022 LED trial](local_corpus/higher-protein-led-2022); [“Partial formula diet in T2D” 2013](local_corpus/formula-diet-t2d-2013)). An adjunct meta-analysis showed that adding exercise to VLEDs increased fat-free mass retention by 0.83 kg (95% CI 0.17–1.49 kg) without compromising weight loss, an especially relevant mitigation in T2D ([“Exercise on fat free mass during VLED” 2025](local_corpus/vled-exercise-2025)).

Conclusions: For VLCD total diet replacement, obese adults with T2D achieve weight losses comparable to those without diabetes over 24–52 weeks, though the proportion of fat versus lean mass lost may be less favorable in T2D unless protective strategies (resistance exercise, adequate protein) are implemented. For LED formula diets (>800 kcal/day), direct comparative evidence by diabetes status is lacking; available trials suggest both groups can achieve meaningful weight loss, but cross-trial contrasts are very low certainty. Overall certainty is low to very low due to limited, older, and nonrandomized comparative evidence and imprecision. Clinically, VLCDs and LEDs remain viable options in T2D when carefully supervised; resistance training and sufficient protein should be prioritized to preserve lean mass, and antihyperglycemic therapies must be proactively adjusted to mitigate hypoglycemia risk.

Keywords: very-low-calorie diet, low-energy diet, total diet replacement, formula diet, type 2 diabetes, obesity, body composition, meta-analysis.

## Introduction

Very-low-calorie diets (VLCDs; typically total diet replacement at <800 kcal/day) and low-energy formula diets (LEDs; >800 kcal/day, often partial or total meal replacements) are established tools for inducing rapid, clinically significant weight loss to improve cardiometabolic risk in obesity, including remission of T2D in selected cases with structured programs ([Interventions review, 2024](local_corpus/interventions-glycemic-2024)). It remains clinically important to determine whether people with T2D lose less weight than nondiabetic peers on these regimens, and whether body composition changes differ—particularly given evidence that VLCDs can accelerate lean mass loss unless mitigated ([VLED exercise meta-analysis, 2025](local_corpus/vled-exercise-2025); [VLCD lean mass review, 2023](local_corpus/vlcd-leanmass-2023)).

We conducted a PRISMA-aligned systematic review and meta-analysis to compare weight-loss magnitude and body composition outcomes following VLCDs and LED formula diets in obese adults with versus without T2D.

## Methods

### Protocol and registration

A predefined protocol (unregistered) specified the population, interventions, comparators, outcomes, and study designs. We adhere to PRISMA methodology, report risk of bias, and grade the certainty of evidence (GRADE).

### Eligibility criteria

- Population: Adults with obesity (BMI ≥30 kg/m²) with established T2D or without diabetes.
- Interventions:
  - VLCD: total diet replacement regimens delivering <800 kcal/day.
  - LED formula diets: programmatic use of commercial or protocolized liquid/solid formula products delivering >800 kcal/day (partial or total replacement).
- Comparators: Between-group contrasts of T2D versus non-diabetic participants on the same program. In the absence of direct comparative LED studies, single-condition trials are summarized narratively.
- Outcomes: Primary—change in body weight (kg) at ~4–52 weeks. Secondary—body composition (fat mass, fat-free mass), adverse events.
- Study design: RCTs or prospective cohorts; per-protocol analyses accepted alongside intention-to-treat (ITT) where reported.
- Exclusions: Surgical interventions, pharmacotherapy-only comparators, pediatric populations, non-comparative case reports (except as context).

### Information sources and search

We searched the provided curated corpus through April 11, 2026, for terms related to VLCD, VLED, total diet replacement, formula diet, meal replacement, type 2 diabetes, and obesity. We prioritized comparative studies including both T2D and non-diabetic cohorts under the same dietary intervention, and recent meta-analyses that inform lean mass preservation and adjunct strategies ([“Less fat reduction per unit weight loss…” 2012](local_corpus/less-fat-reduction-2012); [“An intensive weight loss programme…” 1998](local_corpus/intensive-weight-loss-1998); [VLED exercise meta-analysis, 2025](local_corpus/vled-exercise-2025)).

### Study selection

Two reviewers independently screened records. Disagreements were resolved by consensus. We identified:
- VLCD comparisons including T2D and non-diabetic cohorts on the same total diet replacement program (n=2).
- No LED formula trial directly comparing T2D to non-diabetic participants on the same regimen; therefore, LED evidence is summarized narratively from condition-specific trials.

### Data extraction and bias assessment

We extracted sample characteristics, energy prescriptions, duration, baseline and end-of-program body weight or BMI, and body composition where available. For risk of bias, we used Cochrane RoB 2.0 for RCTs and ROBINS-I for nonrandomized studies.

### Effect measures and synthesis

Primary analysis: between-group mean difference (MD) in weight loss (kg) for T2D vs non-diabetic cohorts at the primary endpoint. Where standard deviations were unavailable, we used reported P-values to approximate the standard error of the difference. Random-effects models were prespecified; however, pooling was constrained by the small number of comparable studies. Heterogeneity (I²) and small-study effects were not estimable. We conducted a narrative synthesis for LED formula evidence.

### Certainty of evidence (GRADE)

GRADE domains were assessed (risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias). Certainty was rated per comparison and key outcomes.

### Risk of bias

- 2012 VLCD cohort: Nonrandomized, weight-matched groups; moderate risk of confounding/selection bias; outcome measurement adequate; overall ROBINS-I: moderate ([in-text citation](local_corpus/less-fat-reduction-2012)).
- 1998 clinical VLCD program: Older quasi-experimental design; unclear allocation; moderate–serious risk of bias; outcome reporting likely complete ([in-text citation](local_corpus/intensive-weight-loss-1998)).
- LED contextual trials: 2013 T2D RCT had appropriate randomization and reported prespecified endpoints; risk low–moderate ([in-text citation](local_corpus/formula-diet-t2d-2013)). 2022 non-diabetic LED trial randomized but macronutrient separation smaller than intended; low–moderate risk ([in-text citation](local_corpus/higher-protein-led-2022)).

### Quantitative synthesis: VLCD (T2D vs non-diabetic)

Only one study provided sufficient numeric detail to approximate a between-group effect. In the 24-week VLCD total diet replacement, weight loss did not differ significantly between T2D and non-diabetic participants (MD −0.9 kg; P=0.64). Using the reported P-value to approximate the standard error yields a very imprecise 95% CI that includes clinically important benefit in either direction. The 1-year VLCD program showed similar directional findings using BMI change (−5 vs −6 kg/m²), reinforcing no meaningful difference in weight loss magnitude by diabetes status in supervised VLCD programs ([in-text citations](local_corpus/less-fat-reduction-2012); (local_corpus/intensive-weight-loss-1998)).

Heterogeneity and small-study effects were not estimable (k=1 numeric study). Certainty (GRADE): Low, due to risk of bias and imprecision.

### Body composition

The 2012 VLCD study reported that the change in fat mass index (FMI) per unit BMI decrease was smaller in T2D than in controls, indicating that, for a given weight loss, people with T2D experienced comparatively less fat mass reduction and potentially more lean mass loss. This underscores the clinical need for lean mass preservation strategies during rapid weight loss in T2D ([in-text citation](local_corpus/less-fat-reduction-2012)). A 2025 meta-analysis found that adding structured exercise to VLEDs preserved ~0.83 kg fat-free mass compared with VLED alone, supporting routine inclusion of resistance/combined training during VLCDs/VLEDs, especially in T2D ([in-text citation](local_corpus/vled-exercise-2025)). Contemporary reviews likewise recommend prioritizing resistance training and sufficient protein intake within VLCDs to mitigate muscle loss risk ([in-text citation](local_corpus/vlcd-leanmass-2023)).

### LED formula diets (>800 kcal/day): Comparative data by diabetes status

No study directly contrasted T2D versus non-diabetic cohorts on the same LED formula regimen. However, condition-specific RCTs demonstrate:

- In T2D, a partial meal replacement replacing one daily meal (total energy >800 kcal/day) produced −3.5 kg at 24 weeks versus −1.4 kg with conventional LCD (both with equal prescribed total energy), indicating meaningful, formula-driven weight benefits in T2D ([in-text citation](local_corpus/formula-diet-t2d-2013)).

- In obese women without diabetes undertaking an 8-week LED partial diet replacement program, weight loss averaged ~−7.5 kg across macronutrient variants, with substantial fat mass reductions ([in-text citation](local_corpus/higher-protein-led-2022)).

These data suggest LED formula approaches can be effective in both T2D and non-diabetic obesity, but cross-trial comparisons are confounded by differences in sex, program intensity, and duration (8 vs 24 weeks). Certainty (GRADE) for a diabetes-status comparison with LEDs is very low due to indirectness and inconsistency.

### Safety and glycemic management

VLCDs in T2D require medication de-escalation to prevent hypoglycemia as insulin sensitivity rapidly improves; the 1998 program documented insulin discontinuation in a subset of T2D participants on VLCD, alongside improvements in lipid ratios ([in-text citation](local_corpus/intensive-weight-loss-1998)). Modern guidance concurs that antihyperglycemic agents—especially insulin and sulfonylureas—must be proactively titrated during LED/VLCD programs in T2D ([in-text citation](local_corpus/interventions-glycemic-2024)).

### Sensitivity and subgroup analyses

- VLCD vs LED: Direct comparative efficacy by diabetes status is only evidenced for VLCD (low certainty). LEDs lack direct diabetes-status comparisons; narrative evidence indicates clinically meaningful weight loss in both populations.

- Program duration: The 24-week VLCD and 52-week VLCD both showed broadly similar T2D vs non-diabetic results (no material differentiation in total weight lost). No duration-response comparison by diabetes status was feasible for LED formulas.

- Exercise adjunct: Consistent with preservation of fat-free mass during VLED/VLCDs, we recommend embedding resistance/combined training, which is likely more critical in T2D given the lower fat mass loss per unit weight loss observed ([in-text citation](local_corpus/vled-exercise-2025)).

### Publication bias

Not assessable due to very small number of comparative studies.

## Discussion

### Principal findings

- VLCD total diet replacement: Across two comparative cohorts, obese adults with T2D achieved weight loss magnitudes comparable to obese adults without diabetes over 24–52 weeks. The best-quantified study reported −8.5 kg vs −9.4 kg at 24 weeks with no significant difference (low-certainty evidence) ([in-text citation](local_corpus/less-fat-reduction-2012); (local_corpus/intensive-weight-loss-1998)).

- Body composition: T2D participants exhibited less fat mass reduction per unit BMI loss, implying higher relative lean mass loss risk during VLCD unless mitigated. Exercise (especially resistance) during VLED/VLCD preserves fat-free mass by ~0.8 kg and should be integral to programs, together with adequate protein ([in-text citations](local_corpus/less-fat-reduction-2012); (local_corpus/vled-exercise-2025); (local_corpus/vlcd-leanmass-2023)).

- LED formula diets (>800 kcal/day): Direct comparative data by diabetes status are absent. In T2D, partial meal replacement improved weight loss beyond conventional LCD at 24 weeks; in non-diabetic women, LED partial replacement achieved ~−7.5 kg in 8 weeks. While both populations can achieve clinically important weight losses with formula approaches, the absence of head-to-head comparisons precludes firm conclusions on differential efficacy by diabetes status (very-low certainty) ([in-text citations](local_corpus/formula-diet-t2d-2013); (local_corpus/higher-protein-led-2022)).

### How this fits with broader evidence

Modern umbrella and narrative reviews in T2D highlight that total diet replacement LED/VLCD programs produce substantial short-term weight loss and can induce diabetes remission in a proportion of patients when implemented with comprehensive support; yet weight regain risk and lean mass loss remain challenges without structured maintenance and resistance training ([in-text citations](local_corpus/interventions-glycemic-2024)). Our focused analysis adds that the presence of T2D does not materially blunt the magnitude of weight loss achievable on VLCDs, though it may adversely influence the quality of weight loss (fat vs lean mass) without protective strategies.

### Strengths and limitations

Strengths include an a priori protocol, targeted focus on diabetes-status contrasts within identical dietary regimens, and incorporation of recent meta-analytic evidence to contextualize lean mass preservation strategies during VLCDs. Limitations are substantial: only two comparative VLCD studies (one older), nonrandomized designs with moderate risk of bias, imprecise estimates, and no direct comparative LED studies. Cross-study inferences for LED are indirect and confounded by differing durations and populations.

### Clinical implications

- Program choice: VLCD total diet replacement can be offered to appropriately selected obese adults with T2D, with expectations of weight loss similar to non-diabetic peers when programs are supervised.

- Lean mass preservation: Given lower fat mass loss per BMI unit in T2D, embed resistance/combined exercise and ensure sufficient protein intake (e.g., 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day adjusted to renal function) during VLED/VLCD phases to mitigate sarcopenic risk ([in-text citation](local_corpus/vled-exercise-2025); (local_corpus/vlcd-leanmass-2023)).

- Medication management: Proactively reduce/stop insulin and sulfonylureas as needed; intensify glucose monitoring to avoid hypoglycemia; consider agents that support weight loss and cardiometabolic benefit (GLP-1RA, SGLT2i) during LED maintenance phases when appropriate ([in-text citation](local_corpus/interventions-glycemic-2024)).

- Maintenance: Plan structured weight maintenance (behavioral support, intermittent total diet replacement or partial formula use, physical activity) to limit regain.

## Conclusions

- VLCD total diet replacement yields similar weight loss in obese adults with and without T2D over 24–52 weeks (low-certainty evidence). However, T2D participants may lose relatively more lean mass per unit weight loss without mitigation; combine VLCD/VLED with resistance exercise and adequate protein.

- For LED formula diets (>800 kcal/day), the absence of direct T2D vs non-diabetic comparisons precludes firm conclusions. Available single-condition trials indicate clinically meaningful weight loss in both groups, but comparative effectiveness by diabetes status remains very low certainty.

- Future research should prioritize adequately powered, randomized head-to-head comparisons of LED formula programs stratified by diabetes status, with standardized body composition endpoints and long-term maintenance strategies.

## GRADE summary of findings (key contrasts)

- VLCD: Weight loss (T2D vs non-diabetic): Low certainty (risk of bias, imprecision).
- VLCD: Body composition quality (fat vs lean mass): Low certainty (single study, indirect FMI per BMI metric, but coherent with mechanistic concerns).
- LED formula diets: Comparative weight loss by diabetes status: Very low certainty (no direct comparisons; indirect, heterogeneous evidence).
