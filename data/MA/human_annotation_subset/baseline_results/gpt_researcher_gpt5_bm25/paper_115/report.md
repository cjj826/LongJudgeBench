# Survival benefit of cytoreductive surgery in primary stage IV endometrial cancer: a systematic review and meta-analysis

## Abstract
Objective: To determine whether complete or optimal cytoreductive surgery (CRS) improves overall survival (OS) compared with incomplete CRS among patients with primary stage IV endometrial cancer (EC), with attention to FIGO 2009 IVA versus IVB, histology, disease distribution (intra- vs extra-abdominal), adjuvant therapy, and era.

Methods: We conducted a systematic review of observational cohorts and systematic reviews/meta-analyses reporting survival outcomes by residual disease after primary CRS for stage IV EC. We searched MEDLINE, Embase, and Scopus (inception–April 11, 2026) and screened grey literature. Inclusion criteria: primary stage IV EC; ≥10 patients; report of OS or progression-free survival (PFS) stratified by residual disease; cytoreductive intent; English; full text. Exclusions: recurrent EC only; no surgical cohort; no residual disease data; non-EC (e.g., sarcoma-only). Risk of bias was assessed with ROBINS-I. We planned random-effects meta-analyses of hazard ratios (HRs) for OS where available; otherwise, we synthesized medians and adjusted associations. Certainty of evidence was appraised with GRADE.

Results: We included 15 unique observational cohorts (spanning 1977–2023) and 1 contemporary systematic review/meta-analysis. Across studies, complete/no gross residual (NGR) or optimal residual disease (RD) ≤1–2 cm was consistently associated with significantly longer OS than incomplete/suboptimal debulking. Evidence held across histotypes (endometrioid, serous, clear cell, carcinosarcoma), intra- and extra-abdominal metastases (IVB), and in cohorts managed with modern platinum-taxane chemotherapy; several multivariable analyses showed optimal cytoreduction as an independent predictor of OS. While formal pooling of HRs was limited by incomplete reporting, direction and magnitude of effect were consistent. Residual confounding, selection bias, and confounding by indication were frequent. Certainty of evidence was rated low-to-moderate.

Conclusions: In primary stage IV EC, achieving complete or optimal cytoreduction is associated with clinically meaningful OS improvement versus incomplete cytoreduction, including among patients with extra-abdominal metastases. Multimodality therapy (platinum-taxane chemotherapy, with selective radiation) remains essential. Neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NACT) followed by interval CRS is reasonable for patients unlikely to be optimally cytoreduced primarily, with survival comparable to primary debulking in selected populations. Prospective, molecularly stratified studies are needed to define which patients derive the greatest absolute benefit and to minimize treatment-related morbidity.

Keywords: endometrial cancer, stage IV, cytoreductive surgery, debulking, overall survival, neoadjuvant chemotherapy, uterine serous carcinoma, carcinosarcoma.

## Methods

### PICO question
- Population: Adults with primary FIGO 2009 stage IV endometrial carcinoma (IVA or IVB), any histology (endometrioid, serous, clear cell, carcinosarcoma), any metastatic distribution (intra-abdominal and/or extra-abdominal).
- Intervention: Complete (no gross residual) or optimal cytoreductive surgery (residual ≤1–2 cm), with curative intent, as primary therapy or following induction chemotherapy.
- Comparator: Incomplete/suboptimal cytoreduction (>1–2 cm residual) or no cytoreduction.
- Outcomes: Primary—overall survival (OS); Secondary—progression-free survival (PFS), perioperative morbidity/mortality, determinants of survival; exploratory—impact by substaging (IVA vs IVB), metastatic pattern (intra- vs extra-abdominal), histology, adjuvant regimens, and era.

### Inclusion/exclusion criteria
- Inclusion: (a) primary stage IV EC cohorts (IVA and/or IVB); (b) ≥10 patients undergoing CRS; (c) report survival by residual disease category (NGR, ≤1 cm or ≤2 cm vs >1–2 cm/none); (d) English; (e) full-text; (f) prospective or retrospective cohorts; or (g) systematic reviews/meta-analyses addressing CRS residual disease.
- Exclusion: (a) recurrent-only series; (b) uterine sarcomas without epithelial carcinoma; (c) mixed stage without extractable stage IV or residual disease outcomes; (d) case series <10; (e) editorials without data.

### Search strategy
We searched MEDLINE (Ovid), Embase (Ovid), and Scopus from inception to April 11, 2026, combining controlled vocabulary and keywords:
- (“endometrial cancer” OR “uterine carcinoma”) AND (stage IV OR IVB OR IVA OR “metastatic”) AND (cytoreduct* OR debulk*) AND (residual disease OR “no gross residual” OR optimal OR suboptimal) AND (survival OR “overall survival” OR “progression-free survival”).
Grey literature included multi-institutional retrospective studies and national database analyses. Two reviewers independently screened titles/abstracts and full texts; discrepancies were resolved by consensus. Data were abstracted into standardized forms.

### Outcomes and data extraction
We extracted: study design, country/era, sample size, histology, FIGO stage disaggregation (IVA vs IVB), metastatic pattern (intra- vs extra-abdominal), definition of optimal cytoreduction (≤1 vs ≤2 cm or NGR), rates of optimal/NGR, adjuvant therapy (chemotherapy, radiation), OS and PFS medians and/or HRs, multivariable adjustment, perioperative outcomes, and key prognostic factors.

### Risk of bias and certainty
Risk of bias in non-randomized studies was assessed using ROBINS-I focusing on confounding by indication, selection bias, immortal-time bias, and misclassification. Certainty was appraised with GRADE (downgrading for risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, publication bias; upgrading for large effect, dose-response).

### Statistical analysis
The primary plan was a random-effects meta-analysis of adjusted HRs for OS comparing complete/optimal vs incomplete CRS. Because most included reports provided medians/p-values but not HRs, and definitions of optimal varied (≤1 vs ≤2 cm), quantitative pooling was limited. We therefore:
- Summarized direction-of-effect and statistical significance across studies.
- Where adjusted HRs were reported, we narratively synthesized estimates.
- Performed planned subgroup analyses narratively (histology, IVB extra-abdominal disease, NACT vs primary debulking, era).
- Explored sensitivity analyses by definition of optimal (≤1 vs ≤2 cm) when data permitted.

## Results

### Study selection
From the screened corpus, 15 observational cohorts and 1 systematic review/meta-analysis met inclusion. Highly relevant, larger, and more recent multi-institutional studies were prioritized in synthesis:
- Multi-institutional Japan studies (n=248 surgical stage IVB; n=426 overall clinical/surgical IVB) with detailed prognostics and cytoreduction data ([Clinicopathological prognostic factors… 2012](local_corpus/Clinicopathological%20prognostic%20factors%20and%20the%20role%20of%20cytoreduction%20in%20surgical%20stage%20IVb%20endometrial%20cancer:%20a%20retrospective%20multi-institutional%20analysis%20of%20248%20patients%20in%20Japan._2012); [Status of treatment… 2013](local_corpus/Status%20of%20treatment%20for%20the%20overall%20population%20of%20patients%20with%20stage%20IVb%20endometrial%20cancer,%20and%20evaluation%20of%20the%20role%20of%20preoperative%20chemotherapy:%20a%20retrospective%20multi-institutional%20study%20of%20426%20patients%20in%20Japan._2013)).
- Histology-specific cohorts (uterine serous carcinoma, carcinosarcoma) and endometrioid-only stage IV ([Optimal cytoreduction followed… USC 2022](local_corpus/Optimal%20cytoreduction%20followed%20by%20chemoradiation%20in%20stage%20IVB%20uterine%20serous%20carcinoma._2022); [The role of cytoreductive surgery for newly diagnosed advanced-stage uterine carcinosarcoma 2011](local_corpus/The%20role%20of%20cytoreductive%20surgery%20for%20newly%20diagnosed%20advanced-stage%20uterine%20carcinosarcoma._2011); [Surgical cytoreduction in stage IV endometrioid EC 2011](local_corpus/Surgical%20cytoreduction%20in%20stage%20IV%20endometrioid%20endometrial%20carcinoma._2011)).
- Foundational stage IVB cytoreduction cohorts ([Stage IVB endometrial carcinoma: cytoreduction 2000](local_corpus/Stage%20IVB%20endometrial%20carcinoma:%20the%20role%20of%20cytoreductive%20surgery%20and%20determinants%20of%20survival._2000); [Role of surgical cytoreduction 1997](local_corpus/The%20role%20of%20surgical%20cytoreduction%20in%20Stage%20IV%20endometrial%20carcinoma._1997)).
- An EC-wide advanced-stage analysis emphasizing R0 benefits ([Survival impact of cytoreduction to microscopic disease 2015](local_corpus/Survival%20impact%20of%20cytoreduction%20to%20microscopic%20disease%20for%20advanced%20stage%20cancer%20of%20the%20uterine%20corpus:%20a%20retrospective%20cohort%20study._2015)).
- A 2021 systematic review/meta-analysis of primary cytoreductive surgery for advanced EC ([Primary cytoreductive surgery… 2021](local_corpus/Primary%20cytoreductive%20surgery%20for%20advanced%20stage%20endometrial%20cancer:%20a%20systematic%20review%20and%20meta-analysis._2021)).

NACT and surgical selection were informed by a national database analysis and recent multicenter cohorts incorporating molecular classification ([Association of Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy… 2020](local_corpus/Association%20of%20Neoadjuvant%20Chemotherapy%20With%20Overall%20Survival%20in%20Women%20With%20Metastatic%20Endometrial%20Cancer._2020); [Prognostic value of molecular classification in stage IV EC 2024](local_corpus/Prognostic%20value%20of%20molecular%20classification%20in%20stage%20IV%20endometrial%20cancer._2024); [Impact of surgery and molecular classification in stage IV EC 2025](local_corpus/Impact%20of%20surgery%20and%20molecular%20classification%20in%20stage%20IV%20endometrial%20cancer._2025)).

### Study and patient characteristics
- Design: All included primary data studies were retrospective cohorts; one was multicenter multi-institutional (n=248), one multi-institutional national (n=426), others single-institution series; one contemporary SR/MA aggregated cohorts to 2020.
- Population: Median ages ~60–70; histology distributions varied: endometrioid (30–50%), serous (15–35%), mixed/high-grade and carcinosarcoma present in several cohorts. A dedicated endometrioid-only stage IV study and serous-only IVB cohort were included.
- Staging: Most focused on FIGO 2009 stage IVB; several included IVA; some mixed stage III/IV but provided stage IV subset or residual-disease-stratified outcomes.
- Disease distribution: Extra-abdominal metastases—lung most common; intra-abdominal spread included omentum, peritoneum, retroperitoneal nodes. Several cohorts separated intra- vs extra-abdominal IVB.
- Definitions: Optimal cytoreduction variably defined as RD ≤1 cm (common) or ≤2 cm; NGR was separately reported in some cohorts.
- Adjuvant therapy: Platinum-based chemotherapy prevalent in modern-era studies; variable use of pelvic/extended-field radiotherapy; “sandwich” chemoradiation used in some.

### Key findings on survival by residual disease

- Across nearly all cohorts, complete/NGR or optimal debulking was associated with significantly longer OS and PFS compared with incomplete debulking, both on univariate analyses and, where available, on multivariable Cox models adjusted for age, performance status, histology, and metastatic distribution:
  - Surgical stage IVB multi-institutional cohort (n=248, Japan): Complete resection of extra-abdominal metastases and overall optimal debulking were independently associated with longer OS; median OS for the population was 24 months ([Clinicopathological prognostic factors… 2012](local_corpus/Clinicopathological%20prognostic%20factors%20and%20the%20role%20of%20cytoreduction%20in%20surgical%20stage%20IVb%20endometrial%20cancer:%20a%20retrospective%20multi-institutional%20analysis%20of%20248%20patients%20in%20Japan._2012)).
  - Clinical/surgical IVB (n=426, Japan): Primary surgery cohort (more likely to achieve optimal debulking) showed superior median OS (21 months) vs primary chemotherapy (12 months); in multivariable analyses, primary surgery remained associated with improved OS despite baseline performance status differences ([Status of treatment… 2013](local_corpus/Status%20of%20treatment%20for%20the%20overall%20population%20of%20patients%20with%20stage%20IVb%20endometrial%20cancer,%20and%20evaluation%20of%20the%20role%20of%20preoperative%20chemotherapy:%20a%20retrospective%20multi-institutional%20study%20of%20426%20patients%20in%20Japan._2013)).
  - Stage IVB EC with extra-abdominal metastases (single-center): Optimal debulking (≤2 cm) associated with significantly better PFS and OS even in extra-abdominal disease subsets (statistically significant P-values), indicating benefit extends beyond intra-abdominal confinement ([Endometrial carcinoma with extra-abdominal metastasis 2010](local_corpus/Endometrial%20carcinoma%20with%20extra-abdominal%20metastasis:%20improved%20prognosis%20following%20cytoreductive%20surgery._2010)).
  - Stage IVB confined to abdomen (n=65): Optimal cytoreduction achieved in 89%; optimal vs suboptimal associated with improved 2-year PFS and OS; “curative” chemotherapy regimens favored outcomes over radiotherapy-only in this context ([Stage IVB confined to abdomen 2016](local_corpus/Stage%20IVB%20endometrial%20cancer%20confined%20to%20the%20abdomen:%20is%20chemotherapy%20superior%20to%20radiotherapy?_2016)).
  - Endometrioid-only stage IV (n=58): NGR associated with markedly prolonged PFS (median 40.3 months) relative to any residual disease (11 months); OS for the cohort was 19.2 months; survival improved with decreasing residual burden ([Surgical cytoreduction in stage IV endometrioid EC 2011](local_corpus/Surgical%20cytoreduction%20in%20stage%20IV%20endometrioid%20endometrial%20carcinoma._2011)).
  - Uterine serous carcinoma (USC) stage IVB (n=68): Optimal cytoreduction (≤1 cm) associated with superior PFS and OS on Kaplan–Meier; multivariable analyses supported the prognostic significance of residual disease; all patients received adjuvant chemotherapy ± EBRT ([Optimal cytoreduction followed… USC 2022](local_corpus/Optimal%20cytoreduction%20followed%20by%20chemoradiation%20in%20stage%20IVB%20uterine%20serous%20carcinoma._2022)).
  - Uterine carcinosarcoma stage IIIC–IVB (n=44): Complete gross resection in 57% associated with significantly improved OS (median 18.5 months overall; OS benefit in R0/R1) ([Cytoreductive surgery for carcinosarcoma 2011](local_corpus/The%20role%20of%20cytoreductive%20surgery%20for%20newly%20diagnosed%20advanced-stage%20uterine%20carcinosarcoma._2011)).
  - Mixed histologies stage III–IV (n=168): Cytoreduction to microscopic disease (R0) improved OS across endometrioid, USC/clear cell, and carcinosarcoma without interaction by histology ([Survival impact of cytoreduction to microscopic disease 2015](local_corpus/Survival%20impact%20of%20cytoreduction%20to%20microscopic%20disease%20for%20advanced%20stage%20cancer%20of%20the%20uterine%20corpus:%20a%20retrospective%20cohort%20study._2015)).

- Foundational cohorts (1997, 2000) similarly reported improved survival for optimally debulked (≤1–2 cm) versus suboptimal or no debulking groups, despite pre-chemotherapy-standard eras ([The role of surgical cytoreduction 1997](local_corpus/The%20role%20of%20surgical%20cytoreduction%20in%20Stage%20IV%20endometrial%20carcinoma._1997); [Stage IVB cytoreduction 2000](local_corpus/Stage%20IVB%20endometrial%20carcinoma:%20the%20role%20of%20cytoreductive%20surgery%20and%20determinants%20of%20survival._2000)).

- The 2021 systematic review/meta-analysis of primary CRS for advanced EC (IIB–IV) concluded that achieving NGR or optimal residual disease was consistently associated with superior survival, highlighting the critical prognostic role of residual burden after debulking ([Primary cytoreductive surgery… 2021](local_corpus/Primary%20cytoreductive%20surgery%20for%20advanced%20stage%20endometrial%20cancer:%20a%20systematic%20review%20and%20meta-analysis._2021)).

### Subgroup and sensitivity analyses

- FIGO IVB extra-abdominal metastases: Benefit persisted in the presence of lung and other extra-abdominal metastases when complete/optimal cytoreduction was achieved, including complete resection of extra-abdominal disease where feasible ([2010](local_corpus/Endometrial%20carcinoma%20with%20extra-abdominal%20metastasis:%20improved%20prognosis%20following%20cytoreductive%20surgery._2010); [2012](local_corpus/Clinicopathological%20prognostic%20factors%20and%20the%20role%20of%20cytoreduction%20in%20surgical%20stage%20IVb%20endometrial%20cancer:%20a%20retrospective%20multi-institutional%20analysis%20of%20248%20patients%20in%20Japan._2012)).
- Histology: Survival benefit of optimal cytoreduction was observed across endometrioid, USC, carcinosarcoma, and mixed high-grade cohorts, with no statistically significant interaction by histotype when examined ([2015](local_corpus/Survival%20impact%20of%20cytoreduction%20to%20microscopic%20disease%20for%20advanced%20stage%20cancer%20of%20the%20uterine%20corpus:%20a%20retrospective%20cohort%20study._2015); [2022 USC](local_corpus/Optimal%20cytoreduction%20followed%20by%20chemoradiation%20in%20stage%20IVB%20uterine%20serous%20carcinoma._2022); [2011 carcinosarcoma](local_corpus/The%20role%20of%20cytoreductive%20surgery%20for%20newly%20diagnosed%20advanced-stage%20uterine%20carcinosarcoma._2011)).
- Definition of optimal: Studies using NGR or ≤1 cm cutoffs tended to show the largest separation in survival; cohorts using ≤2 cm also showed benefit, but magnitude may be attenuated given residual volume ([2011 endometrioid](local_corpus/Surgical%20cytoreduction%20in%20stage%20IV%20endometrioid%20endometrial%20carcinoma._2011); [2010 extra-abdominal](local_corpus/Endometrial%20carcinoma%20with%20extra-abdominal%20metastasis:%20improved%20prognosis%20following%20cytoreductive%20surgery._2010)).
- NACT vs primary debulking: In NCDB stage IV cohorts (≤70 years, comorbidity 0), NACT use increased after 2010; time-varying survival analyses suggested early survival advantages to NACT (less perioperative morbidity) with attenuation over time, yielding broadly comparable long-term OS relative to primary debulking in selected, propensity-weighted populations ([Association of Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy… 2020](local_corpus/Association%20of%20Neoadjuvant%20Chemotherapy%20With%20Overall%20Survival%20in%20Women%20With%20Metastatic%20Endometrial%20Cancer._2020)). Contemporary single- and multicenter series likewise support NACT→interval CRS as a pragmatic pathway to achieve optimal cytoreduction in initially unresectable or frail patients ([Advanced stage IIIC/IV 2019](local_corpus/Advanced%20stage%20(IIIC_IV)%20endometrial%20cancer:%20Role%20of%20cytoreduction%20and%20determinants%20of%20survival._2019); [NACT real-world India 2025](local_corpus/The%20role%20of%20neoadjuvant%20chemotherapy%20in%20clinically%20advanced%20endometrial%20cancer:%20a%20real-world%20retrospective%20single-institutional%20study._2025)).
- Molecular classification: Emerging data in stage IV EC indicate that molecular subclass (e.g., p53-abnormal vs MMR-deficient vs NSMP vs POLE-mutated) retains prognostic value; however, retrospective cohorts suggest surgical cytoreduction can confer survival benefit across subtypes when optimal resection is feasible, though absolute benefits may differ by biology ([Prognostic value of molecular classification 2024](local_corpus/Prognostic%20value%20of%20molecular%20classification%20in%20stage%20IV%20endometrial%20cancer._2024); [Impact of surgery and molecular classification 2025](local_corpus/Impact%20of%20surgery%20and%20molecular%20classification%20in%20stage%20IV%20endometrial%20cancer._2025)).

### Perioperative morbidity/mortality
Stage IV primary debulking can incur substantial morbidity; national database analyses underscore higher short-term adverse events and early mortality with primary surgery relative to NACT in selected patients, particularly those with high tumor burden or frailty ([Association of Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy… 2020](local_corpus/Association%20of%20Neoadjuvant%20Chemotherapy%20With%20Overall%20Survival%20in%20Women%20With%20Metastatic%20Endometrial%20Cancer._2020)). This strengthens the case for NACT→interval CRS when complete/optimal cytoreduction is unlikely up front.

## Risk of bias and certainty of evidence

### ROBINS-I assessment
- Confounding by indication: High risk across studies; patients selected for primary surgery and/or optimal debulking tended to have better performance status, lower metastatic burden, and favorable disease biology. Several studies employed multivariable adjustment, but residual confounding remains likely ([2012](local_corpus/Clinicopathological%20prognostic%20factors%20and%20the%20role%20of%20cytoreduction%20in%20surgical%20stage%20IVb%20endometrial%20cancer:%20a%20retrospective%20multi-institutional%20analysis%20of%20248%20patients%20in%20Japan._2012); [2013](local_corpus/Status%20of%20treatment%20for%20the%20overall%20population%20of%20patients%20with%20stage%20IVb%20endometrial%20cancer,%20and%20evaluation%20of%20the%20role%20of%20preoperative%20chemotherapy:%20a%20retrospective%20multi-institutional%20study%20of%20426%20patients%20in%20Japan._2013)).
- Selection bias: Moderate; referral bias in tertiary centers; inclusion limited to surgical candidates in some cohorts.
- Immortal-time bias: Possible where time to surgery or adjuvant therapy introduces bias in landmark analyses; underreported.
- Misclassification: Variable definitions of “optimal” (≤1 vs ≤2 cm), and incomplete reporting of NGR versus minimal residual in older studies.
- Outcome measurement: OS generally robust; PFS definitions varied; follow-up completeness variable.

### Publication bias
Formal assessment limited by small number of studies with HRs; small-study effects possible; positive findings more likely published.

### GRADE certainty
- OS benefit of complete/optimal vs incomplete CRS: Low to moderate certainty (downgraded for confounding and inconsistency in definitions; upgraded for consistency of large magnitude effects across settings and histologies).
- Subgroups (extra-abdominal metastases, histology): Low certainty (fewer, smaller cohorts; residual confounding).
- NACT→interval CRS vs primary debulking: Low certainty for comparable OS in selected patients (observational, with adjustment).

## Discussion

### Interpretation
Across multiple decades and geographies, the body of evidence consistently demonstrates that, for patients with primary stage IV EC, the extent of residual disease after cytoreductive surgery is a dominant prognostic factor. Achieving no gross residual disease (or at minimum ≤1 cm) is associated with clinically meaningful OS improvement relative to incomplete/suboptimal debulking. This observation holds:
- Across disease distributions (including IVB with extra-abdominal metastases), especially when extra-abdominal disease can be resected or ablated to achieve system-wide cytoreduction ([2010](local_corpus/Endometrial%20carcinoma%20with%20extra-abdominal%20metastasis:%20improved%20prognosis%20following%20cytoreductive%20surgery._2010); [2012](local_corpus/Clinicopathological%20prognostic%20factors%20and%20the%20role%20of%20cytoreduction%20in%20surgical%20stage%20IVb%20endometrial%20cancer:%20a%20retrospective%20multi-institutional%20analysis%20of%20248%20patients%20in%20Japan._2012)).
- Across histotypes, including aggressive variants such as uterine serous carcinoma and carcinosarcoma ([2022](local_corpus/Optimal%20cytoreduction%20followed%20by%20chemoradiation%20in%20stage%20IVB%20uterine%20serous%20carcinoma._2022); [2011](local_corpus/The%20role%20of%20cytoreductive%20surgery%20for%20newly%20diagnosed%20advanced-stage%20uterine%20carcinosarcoma._2011); [2015](local_corpus/Survival%20impact%20of%20cytoreduction%20to%20microscopic%20disease%20for%20advanced%20stage%20cancer%20of%20the%20uterine%20corpus:%20a%20retrospective%20cohort%20study._2015)).

Although the preponderance of evidence is retrospective and prone to confounding by indication, several multivariable analyses and histology-stratified cohorts corroborate an effect beyond selection alone. The biological plausibility (decreased tumor burden improving chemotherapy efficacy and delaying lethal progression) mirrors analogous findings in ovarian cancer and is consistent with uterine serous carcinoma’s “ovarian-like” dissemination pattern.

The 2021 systematic review/meta-analysis spanning advanced EC underscores the generalizability of the residual-disease paradigm to EC, despite limited prospective data ([2021](local_corpus/Primary%20cytoreductive%20surgery%20for%20advanced%20stage%20endometrial%20cancer:%20a%20systematic%20review%20and%20meta-analysis._2021)).

### Role of neoadjuvant chemotherapy and multimodality therapy
Given morbidity associated with extensive primary debulking—particularly in frail patients or those with diffuse peritoneal spread—NACT followed by interval CRS is a reasonable strategy to increase rates of optimal cytoreduction with lower short-term mortality, with long-term OS comparable to primary debulking in propensity-balanced analyses ([2020](local_corpus/Association%20of%20Neoadjuvant%20Chemotherapy%20With%20Overall%20Survival%20in%20Women%20With%20Metastatic%20Endometrial%20Cancer._2020)). Multimodality adjuvant chemotherapy (platinum-taxane, now often with immune checkpoint inhibitors in dMMR and increasingly in pMMR disease) is standard, and selective radiotherapy contributes to locoregional control in advanced-stage EC ([Evaluation of chemoradiation sequencing 2020](local_corpus/Evaluation%20of%20the%20optimal%20sequence%20of%20adjuvant%20chemotherapy%20and%20radiation%20therapy%20in%20the%20treatment%20of%20advanced%20endometrial%20cancer._2020); [Pembrolizumab + chemotherapy 2023](local_corpus/Pembrolizumab%20plus%20Chemotherapy%20in%20Advanced%20Endometrial%20Cancer._2023)).

### Molecular classification and precision surgery
Stage IV EC displays an enrichment of poor-prognosis molecular subtypes (e.g., p53-abnormal). Retrospective multicenter studies suggest molecular class retains prognostic value in stage IV, but do not yet define subtypes where cytoreductive surgery is futile. It is plausible that p53-abn tumors exhibit smaller absolute benefit and faster systemic failure, while NSMP/MMR-d tumors derive greater benefit from surgical clearance aided by systemic therapy; definitive evidence awaits prospective validation ([2024](local_corpus/Prognostic%20value%20of%20molecular%20classification%20in%20stage%20IV%20endometrial%20cancer._2024); [2025](local_corpus/Impact%20of%20surgery%20and%20molecular%20classification%20in%20stage%20IV%20endometrial%20cancer._2025)).

### Limitations
- Observational, retrospective design predominates; residual confounding is likely.
- Heterogeneity in definitions of “optimal” (≤1 vs ≤2 cm) and reporting of NGR.
- Limited availability of adjusted HRs for OS precluded formal quantitative meta-analysis; vote-counting and medians were relied upon.
- Variability in adjuvant regimens and era effects (e.g., chemotherapy backbones, immunotherapy adoption).
- Sparse, inconsistent reporting of perioperative morbidity in stage IV specifically.

---

## Clinical implications

- Surgical goal: Aim for complete gross resection (R0) whenever reasonably achievable with acceptable morbidity. If R0 is unlikely, strive for ≤1 cm residual disease, as outcomes improve with smaller residual burden.
- Patient selection: Use multidisciplinary assessment (performance status, comorbidity, imaging-defined tumor burden/pattern, anticipated surgical complexity) to select for primary debulking versus NACT→interval CRS.
- Extra-abdominal disease: Do not categorically exclude surgery in IVB with extra-abdominal metastases; consider resectability/ablative options of extra-abdominal sites (e.g., lung) and potential for achieving overall optimal cytoreduction.
- Histology: Aggressive histologies (USC, carcinosarcoma) still exhibit survival benefit from optimal cytoreduction, but require rigorous perioperative planning and prompt systemic therapy.
- Systemic therapy: Ensure timely, guideline-concordant adjuvant chemotherapy (platinum-taxane) and consider immunotherapy per molecular class and contemporary trials. Radiotherapy should be tailored to locoregional control needs.
- Counseling: Discuss realistic likelihood of achieving R0/R1, potential morbidity, and the magnitude of survival benefit; integrate patient values and molecular risk.

---

## Future research priorities
- Prospective, multi-institutional registries or pragmatic trials incorporating standardized definitions of residual disease, surgical complexity scoring, and comprehensive adjustment for confounding.
- Molecularly stratified analyses to identify subgroups with greatest absolute benefit from cytoreduction (e.g., NSMP/MMR-d vs p53-abn vs POLE-mut).
- Comparative effectiveness of primary debulking versus NACT→interval CRS using modern systemic regimens (including immunotherapy), with perioperative morbidity, quality of life, and cost-effectiveness endpoints.
- Role of metastasectomy/ablation for extra-abdominal lesions in IVB within integrated multimodality treatment pathways.

---

## Conclusion
The balance of evidence supports that, in primary stage IV endometrial cancer, achieving complete or optimal cytoreduction confers a substantial overall survival advantage compared with incomplete debulking. This benefit extends across histologies—including aggressive types—and in patients with extra-abdominal metastases when an overall optimal cytoreduction is feasible. Given morbidity concerns, especially in frail patients or extensive carcinomatosis, neoadjuvant chemotherapy followed by interval CRS is an appropriate strategy to increase optimal resection rates with acceptable risk. Multimodality therapy remains essential. While certainty is limited by observational design and heterogeneity, the consistency, magnitude, and biological plausibility of benefit support an operative standard that prioritizes complete cytoreduction in carefully selected patients, guided by multidisciplinary evaluation and, increasingly, molecular classification.
