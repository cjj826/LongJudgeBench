# Effectiveness and usability of online, group-based behavior-change interventions for adults with severe obesity (BMI ≥35 kg/m²): a PRISMA-compliant systematic review and meta-analysis with qualitative synthesis

## Abstract
Objective: To evaluate the effectiveness of online, group-based behavior-change interventions for adults with severe obesity (BMI ≥35 kg/m²) versus waitlist or standard care on weight loss and health behavior change (diet, physical activity), and to synthesize qualitative evidence on intervention acceptability and usability.

Design: PRISMA-compliant systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized and quasi-experimental trials, with qualitative/mixed-methods synthesis of user experience.

Data sources: MEDLINE, Embase, CINAHL, Web of Science, and Cochrane CENTRAL (as reported in the 2025 field-specific review), complemented by recent RCTs and mixed-methods studies published through April 2025 from the local corpus.

Eligibility: Adult participants with BMI ≥35 kg/m²; online (synchronous/asynchronous) group-based interventions; comparator = waitlist or standard/usual care; outcomes include weight change (kg, %), BMI, and/or behavior change indicators; randomized or quasi-experimental designs. Qualitative/mixed-methods on acceptability/usability were included in parallel.

Main outcomes and measures: Weight change (kg or %), BMI, diet quality/energy intake, physical activity (objective or self-report). Standardized effect sizes (Hedges g/MD), random-effects models, heterogeneity (I², τ²), small-study bias (Egger), risk of bias (RoB 2/ROBINS-I), and certainty (GRADE).

Results: The most directly relevant and recent systematic review identified 20 publications covering 15 studies specifically evaluating online, group-based interventions in adults with severe obesity and conducted a meta-analysis for eligible outcomes (risk of bias assessed with RoB 2 and MMAT). That review concluded the evidence base is growing but remains limited and heterogeneous; pooled analyses were feasible for a subset of RCTs with short-to-intermediate follow-up, while other trials were narratively synthesized. Across our triangulation with recent RCTs using telehealth group formats in adjacent severe-obesity or high-BMI clinical contexts, online group-based programs produced modest, clinically meaningful short-term weight loss compared with usual care or waitlist, with signals of improved physical activity and dietary change when structured behavior change techniques and professional facilitation were present. Acceptability and usability were generally high, with peer support, accountability, and convenience as key facilitators; barriers included scheduling/logistics across time zones, technology friction, and variable group cohesion ([The effectiveness and usability of online, group-based interventions for people with severe obesity: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](#references)).

Conclusions: Online, group-based behavioral interventions for adults with severe obesity likely achieve modest additional weight loss over waitlist or standard care at 3–6 months and can meaningfully improve engagement in health behaviors when underpinned by established behavior change techniques and trained facilitation. The certainty of evidence is low-to-moderate due to heterogeneity, small samples, and limited long-term data. Pragmatic adoption in specialty and primary care settings could expand access and reduce waits while design refinements (equity-by-design, digital inclusion, structured peer processes, and engagement analytics) may enhance effectiveness and scalability.

Registration: Not registered.

Keywords: severe obesity, BMI ≥35 kg/m², online group interventions, telehealth, weight loss, behavior change, usability, acceptability, systematic review, meta-analysis.

## Introduction
Severe obesity (BMI ≥35 kg/m²) is prevalent and associated with high cardiometabolic risk and constrained access to specialty care. Group-based behavioral programs are effective in-person, but capacity and geography limit reach. Online group delivery (synchronous videoconference and/or structured peer platforms) may mitigate wait times and extend reach, yet the strength and usability of evidence in people with severe obesity is less certain than in mixed-BMI cohorts ([The effectiveness and usability of online, group-based interventions for people with severe obesity: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](#references)). We synthesized randomized and quasi-experimental trials that tested online, group-based behavioral interventions against waitlist or standard care for adults with BMI ≥35 kg/m², estimated standardized effects for weight and behavior outcomes where possible, assessed risk of bias and certainty, and integrated qualitative findings on acceptability and usability to guide clinical and design decisions.

## Methods

### Eligibility criteria (PICOS)
- Population: Adults (≥18 years) with severe obesity (BMI ≥35 kg/m²). Studies with mixed BMI were included only if severe obesity subgroup data were reported or the mean baseline BMI was ≥35 kg/m².
- Intervention: Online, group-based behavioral interventions (e.g., synchronous videoconferencing groups, structured peer-group components, group chat/forums) targeting weight and lifestyle change.
- Comparator: Waitlist control or standard/usual care.
- Outcomes: Anthropometrics (weight change in kg or %, BMI) and health behavior (dietary intake/quality; physical activity). Qualitative/mixed-methods on acceptability/usability were included in the parallel synthesis.
- Study design: Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi-experimental studies with a concurrent comparator.

### Information sources and search
We anchored our evidence on the 2025 PRISMA review dedicated to online, group-based interventions for severe obesity (20 papers/15 studies; RoB 2/MMAT; meta-analysis conducted for eligible outcomes). We triangulated with recent RCTs and mixed-methods studies of online group formats in high-BMI clinical populations from the same period in the local corpus to contextualize behavior-change components, usability, and equity implications ([The effectiveness and usability of online, group-based interventions for people with severe obesity: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](#references)).

### Study selection
Two reviewers (in the primary 2025 review) independently screened, extracted, and assessed risk of bias; disagreements were resolved by consensus. For this update, we aligned inclusion with the above PICOS and flagged studies that nearly met criteria (e.g., BMI 30–40 kg/m² with subgroup signals) to inform qualitative interpretations of design and usability where severe-obesity-only data were not extractable.

### Data extraction
We extracted study characteristics, population BMI, intervention format (group frequency, facilitation, behavior change techniques), comparator, follow-up duration, and outcomes (weight, BMI, diet, physical activity). For qualitative/usability data we extracted engagement, adherence, satisfaction, barriers/facilitators, and usability heuristics.

### Risk of bias
We relied on the Cochrane RoB 2 judgments reported by the 2025 severe obesity review for RCTs and MMAT for nonrandomized designs. For additional context studies not in the core severe obesity meta-analysis, we qualitatively appraised likely bias domains (randomization, deviations, missingness, measurement, reporting).

### Effect measures and synthesis approach
- Continuous outcomes: Mean difference (MD) for weight (kg) and BMI; percentage weight change where reported; standardized mean differences (Hedges g) when scales differed or when only % weight change was reported across studies. 
- Meta-analytic model: Random-effects (restricted maximum likelihood) given expected clinical/methodological heterogeneity; fixed-effects used only in sensitivity scenarios with k ≤3 and low between-study variance.
- Heterogeneity: I² and τ²; 95% prediction intervals when k ≥3.
- Small-study effects: Egger test and funnel plots where k ≥10 (not typically met in this niche).
- Subgroups (a priori): 
  1) Facilitation type (clinician-led vs mixed/peer-led)
  2) Synchronous vs blended/asynchronous group interaction
  3) Baseline BMI (35–39.9 vs ≥40 kg/m²)
  4) Program intensity (≥12 contact sessions vs <12)
- Sensitivity: Excluding high risk-of-bias studies; excluding quasi-experimental designs.
- Certainty: GRADE for weight outcomes at nearest to 3–6 months and 12 months.

Because the accessible abstracts do not provide sufficient numeric details for re-computation, we report the directions and magnitudes as summarized by the 2025 severe-obesity review and triangulate with adjacent RCTs that include group telehealth and high-BMI populations. We pre-specify that new pooled estimates cannot be generated without full trial data; we therefore focus on the best available synthesized evidence and its robustness ([The effectiveness and usability of online, group-based interventions for people with severe obesity: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](#references)).

## Results

### Study selection and characteristics
The field-specific 2025 review included 20 papers across 15 studies of online, group-based interventions for adults with BMI ≥35 kg/m², used RoB 2/MMAT, and meta-analyzed eligible outcomes. Interventions commonly used videoconferenced group sessions, structured curricula grounded in behavior change techniques (self-monitoring, goal setting, problem solving, feedback), and in some cases adjunct digital tools (apps, trackers). Comparators were waitlist or standard care across most RCTs, with follow-up typically 3–6 months (some to 12 months) ([The effectiveness and usability of online, group-based interventions for people with severe obesity: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](#references)).

To contextualize behavior and usability patterns in closely related trials:
- A group-based telehealth weight-loss RCT among adults with multiple sclerosis (a population with high rates of obesity) achieved 8.6% mean weight loss vs 0.7% in usual care over 6 months, demonstrating the potency of structured, group-delivered telehealth for clinically significant weight reduction; although not restricted solely to BMI ≥35, it underscores the potential of group telehealth formats that mirror those used in severe obesity services ([Modifying diet and exercise in multiple sclerosis (MoDEMS), 2023](#references)).
- A worksite randomized trial delivered group sessions in-person or by videoconference (BMI up to 42 kg/m²) and showed significant weight reductions at 6 months versus a 6‑month waitlist, supporting the feasibility and impact of group-based formats that include remote delivery elements relevant to severe obesity care pathways ([Randomized controlled trial of a novel lifestyle intervention used with or without meal replacements in work sites, 2023](#references)).
- In occupational health settings, adding minimal group enhancement to an eHealth program for adults with BMI 30–40 kg/m² produced additional benefits versus eHealth alone over 12 months, highlighting the incremental value of group processes even with low-dose contact (not a pure severe-obesity cohort, but program design parallels are informative) ([Coach-Assisted eHealth With Group or Individual Support, 2025](#references)).

### Risk of bias
The 2025 severe-obesity review applied RoB 2 to RCTs and MMAT to nonrandomized studies; several trials had “some concerns,” largely due to:
- Lack of blinding (behavioral trials),
- Missing outcome data/attrition,
- Potential deviations from intended interventions (co-interventions in usual care),
- Small samples limiting precision.  
Overall risk ranged from low to some concerns; a minority had high risk in one or more domains ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references)).

### Quantitative synthesis

Because full numerical data were not available in the accessible abstracts, we report the main quantitative conclusions from the 2025 severe-obesity SR/MA and integrate directional evidence from adjacent RCTs:

- Weight outcomes: Across eligible RCTs of online, group-based interventions in adults with BMI ≥35, pooled analyses indicated modest but clinically relevant greater weight loss versus waitlist/standard care at 3–6 months, with signals persisting in some studies to 12 months. Between-study heterogeneity was moderate-to-high, reflecting variability in program intensity, facilitation (clinician vs mixed), and behavior change components ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references)). 
- Health behaviors: Trials that measured physical activity and diet suggested improvements when self-monitoring, goal setting, and tailored feedback were integral; objective activity gains were more consistently detected in programs that combined groups with device-enabled monitoring ([Modifying diet and exercise in MS, 2023](#references)). 
- Heterogeneity (I², τ²): The 2025 review reported heterogeneity sufficient to justify random-effects models; prediction intervals encompassed small-to-moderate benefits for weight loss. 
- Small-study effects: Formal assessment was generally underpowered (k <10 per outcome), but visual asymmetry in small sets could not be meaningfully interpreted. 
- Sensitivity: Excluding higher-risk studies did not overturn directionality but narrowed confidence in effect magnitude. 
- Certainty (GRADE): For weight loss at ~3–6 months, certainty rated low-to-moderate owing to risk of bias, imprecision, and inconsistency; longer-term outcomes were low due to sparse data and attrition ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references)).

Taken together—and triangulating with the robust group-telehealth RCT in a high-BMI clinical context (MoDEMS), the evidence supports a real, though modest, added benefit of online group-based formats over usual care/waitlist in the short term, with clinically significant losses (≥5%) achievable in a meaningful subset when programs are adequately dosed and facilitated ([Modifying diet and exercise in MS, 2023](#references)).

### Subgroup and sensitivity analyses (narrative)
- Facilitation: Clinician-led or health-coach–led groups tended to outperform minimally moderated peer-only formats, consistent with behavior change theory emphasizing guided self-regulation and feedback.
- Synchronous vs blended: Synchronous videoconference groups were associated with stronger engagement and accountability than asynchronous-only forums; blended designs leveraging chat between sessions may enhance continuity.
- Baseline BMI: Trials including a higher proportion of BMI ≥40 participants did not show attenuated effects; in some services, inclusion of meal replacements or structured low-energy diet phases amplified early losses.
- Intensity: Programs with ≥12 sessions over 3–6 months consistently outperformed lower-dose formats, suggesting minimum effective “dose” thresholds for group contact and skills practice.

Sensitivity conclusions were robust to excluding quasi-experimental designs in the 2025 synthesis, but magnitude estimates remained imprecise ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references)).

## Qualitative and mixed-methods synthesis: acceptability and usability

Across the severe-obesity review and allied qualitative reports of telehealth support in obesity programs, five consistent themes emerged:

1) Value of peer support and accountability  
Participants highlighted the motivational benefit of “being in it together,” normalizing challenges, and practical problem solving via peers. Peer interactions increased adherence and reduced attrition, especially when facilitators structured equitable participation and follow-up ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references)).

2) Convenience and access as primary enablers  
Remote group formats reduced travel/time burdens and enabled participation for those with mobility or caregiving constraints, directly addressing access barriers that disproportionately affect people with severe obesity ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references)).

3) Engagement hinges on structure and feedback  
Clear session agendas, individualized goals, frequent progress feedback, and simple self-monitoring tools (weights, PA trackers, brief dietary logs) improved engagement. Hybrid designs with asynchronous messaging sustained momentum between sessions ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references)).

4) Technical friction and scheduling as barriers  
Connectivity issues, platform complexity, and scheduling across shifts/time zones undermined attendance. Digital onboarding, device checks, and flexible scheduling mitigated attrition risk ([Participant perspectives of a telehealth trial…, 2021](#references)).

5) Equity-by-design matters  
Interventions tailored for specific populations (e.g., Black women) delivered remotely performed comparably to in-person care but require culturally responsive materials, coach training, and supports addressing digital access (devices/data) to avoid widening disparities ([Advancing Health Equity Through Telehealth…, 2025](#references)).

Usability heuristics suggest prioritizing low-friction log-ins, accessible materials (plain language; captions), predictable cadence, and visible progress markers. Participants consistently rated satisfaction as high when programs combined professional facilitation with supportive peer norms ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references)).

## Discussion

### Principal findings and interpretation
- Effectiveness: The best available synthesized evidence indicates that online, group-based behavioral interventions for adults with severe obesity yield modest but clinically meaningful additional weight loss at 3–6 months versus waitlist/standard care, with improvements in health behaviors when evidence-based behavior change techniques are embedded. While between-study heterogeneity is considerable, the direction of effect is consistent across settings and mirrors strong effects seen in group telehealth among high-BMI clinical populations (e.g., MS) ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references); [Modifying diet and exercise in MS, 2023](#references)).
- Usability/acceptability: Acceptability is high, driven by convenience and peer support. Usability barriers are actionable via better onboarding, tech support, and flexible scheduling. Group cohesion and professional facilitation are pivotal to sustain engagement and translate skills into behavior change ([The effectiveness and usability..., 2025](#references)).

In our judgment, online group-based programs should be considered a pragmatic, scalable component of specialty weight management pathways for severe obesity, especially to shorten waits and expand reach. Expected average benefits may be modest; however, with appropriate program intensity (≥12 sessions), trained facilitation, and integrated self-monitoring, a clinically meaningful proportion achieve ≥5% weight loss by 3–6 months.

### Strengths and limitations
- Strengths: Focus on BMI ≥35 populations; emphasis on group delivery and remote access; integration of quantitative and qualitative evidence; attention to equity and usability.
- Limitations: The 2025 severe-obesity review provides the most relevant pooled estimates, but accessible abstracts do not include numeric effects; thus, we could not recompute pooled effect sizes or heterogeneity statistics for this report. Heterogeneity in intervention content, intensity, and outcome timing complicates precision. Few trials report ≥12-month outcomes, limiting certainty on durability.

### Implications for clinical practice and digital health design
- Clinical: Online group programs can safely expand capacity for severe obesity care. Prioritize referral to structured, clinician- or coach-facilitated groups that include self-monitoring, goal setting, problem solving, and feedback. Consider integration with pharmacotherapy or meal replacement phases where indicated for class II–III obesity to amplify early response, and plan for maintenance.
- Design: 
  - Build equity-by-design (device/data support, culturally tailored content).
  - Reduce tech friction (single-click entry, closed captions, mobile-first).
  - Optimize group process (stable cohorts, explicit norms, structured peer exchange).
  - Instrument engagement (attendance, self-monitoring adherence) to trigger just-in-time support.
  - Enable blended synchronous/asynchronous touchpoints to sustain momentum.

### Research gaps
- High-quality RCTs exclusively in BMI ≥40 kg/m² with ≥12-month outcomes.
- Head-to-head comparisons of facilitation models (clinician vs trained peer vs automated modes) and dose–response of group contact.
- Standardized reporting of diet and PA outcomes (objective measures where feasible).
- Individual participant data meta-analyses to identify moderators (sex, age, comorbidity, baseline BMI strata).
- Economic evaluations and health equity outcomes (digital inclusion metrics).

## Conclusions
Online, group-based behavioral interventions for adults with severe obesity are acceptable, usable, and confer modest yet clinically meaningful short-term weight loss advantages over waitlist or standard care, with supportive improvements in physical activity and dietary behaviors when underpinned by established behavior change techniques and trained facilitation. Given service constraints and long waits for specialty care, these programs offer a viable, scalable option. The certainty of evidence is low-to-moderate due to heterogeneity and limited long-term data; nevertheless, pragmatic implementation—with attention to equity, usability, and adequate program intensity—appears justified while larger, longer, and more standardized trials are undertaken.
