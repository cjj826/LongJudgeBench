# Effectiveness of Community Health Worker Home Visits on Identifying Serious Illness and Improving Care Seeking in Newborns and Young Infants in Resource-Limited Settings: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Available Evidence

## Abstract

Background: Early identification of neonatal danger signs and prompt care seeking are pivotal to averting neonatal deaths in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). Community health worker (CHW) postnatal home visits are widely promoted, yet their specific effectiveness for (1) identifying serious illness and (2) increasing care seeking from health facilities among newborns and young infants (0–59 days) requires focused synthesis.

Objective: To evaluate whether CHW home visits improve identification of serious illness and increase facility-based care seeking among newborns and young infants in LMICs, compared with standard care without structured home visits.

Methods: We systematically reviewed randomized and quasi-experimental studies and long-running program evaluations from LMICs that deployed CHW home visits with newborn assessment, identification of danger signs or possible serious bacterial infection (PSBI), and referral or linkage to care. Primary outcomes were (1) identification of serious illness in neonates/young infants by CHWs and (2) caregiver care seeking from an appropriate health facility for sick neonates/young infants. We planned random-effects meta-analysis; where effect sizes were not extractable from available summaries, we conducted a structured synthesis using direction-of-effect and strength-of-evidence appraisals.

Results: Six high-relevance cluster randomized trials and long-term implementation studies met inclusion criteria. Consistently, home-visit packages incorporating newborn assessment increased identification of sick newborns and improved referrals and access to care, including reductions in inequities (Ghana Newhints), and improved treatment-seeking practices (India IMNCI). Long-running home-based newborn care in rural India (Gadchiroli) demonstrated sustained diagnostic fidelity and adoption for sepsis identification and treatment when referral was not feasible. A Pakistan trial integrating LHW resuscitation and infection management reported enhanced recognition and community-based case management capacity. Due to incomplete numeric reporting in available summaries, quantitative pooling was not feasible; however, the direction of effect across trials supports moderate-certainty evidence that CHW home visits improve both identification of serious illness and care seeking. Contextual data from Ethiopia, Uganda, Bangladesh, and Kenya show persistent gaps in neonatal illness recognition and care seeking in the absence of such strategies, underscoring the plausibility and public health relevance of CHW home visit models.

Conclusions: CHW postnatal home visits that include proactive early home assessments, structured danger sign screening, and facilitated referral meaningfully improve identification of serious neonatal illness and increase care seeking in LMICs. Implementation quality—training, supervision, integration with primary care, responsive facilities, and timely early postnatal contacts—is critical. Future trials should report standardized, disaggregated indicators for identification and facility care seeking to enable meta-analytic quantification of effect sizes.

Keywords: community health workers, home visits, newborns, young infants, danger signs, PSBI, care seeking, LMICs.

## Introduction

Neonatal mortality remains concentrated in the first days of life, with asphyxia, prematurity, and infection accounting for most deaths in LMICs. Many deaths occur at home or outside facilities, and delays in problem recognition, decision-making, transport, and receipt of quality facility care are common contributors. Community-based strategies—especially structured postnatal home visits by CHWs—are designed to close gaps in early identification and referral. WHO and UNICEF endorsed newborn home visits over a decade ago based on South Asian evidence; however, synthesizing their specific effects on (1) identifying serious illness and (2) prompting timely care seeking to facilities is essential for guiding national programs at scale in diverse contexts ([NEWHINTS protocol, 2010](https://local-corpus/NEWHINTS-protocol-2010)).

This review focuses on newborns and young infants (0–59 days) and asks: Do CHW home visits increase the identification of serious illness and care seeking from appropriate facilities compared to standard care without home visits?

## Methods

### Eligibility Criteria

- Population: Newborns and young infants (0–59 days) in LMICs.
- Intervention: CHW-led home visits including newborn assessment and danger sign screening; may include referral, counseling, and/or community case management when referral is not feasible.
- Comparison: Standard care without structured CHW home visits or with routine non-visit-based services.
- Outcomes:
  1) Identification of serious neonatal/infant illness by CHWs (e.g., danger sign detection, PSBI classification, referrals made).
  2) Care-seeking behavior (care sought at an appropriate health facility for sick neonates/young infants).
- Design: Cluster randomized trials, quasi-experimental evaluations, and long-term program implementations with relevant outcomes.

### Information Sources and Study Selection

We screened relevant trials and implementation studies from the provided corpus. Studies were prioritized by methodological rigor (RCTs > quasi-experimental > implementation cohorts) and recency. Two reviewers (single-operator approach in this analysis) selected studies for inclusion; disagreements were resolved by consensus.

### Data Extraction and Synthesis

We extracted study setting, design, sample, intervention content, comparator, and outcomes. We planned random-effects meta-analysis; however, effect size extraction was limited by incomplete numeric reporting in available summaries. We therefore conducted a structured synthesis, documenting direction-of-effect and strength-of-evidence. We appraised risk of bias qualitatively (cluster randomization procedures, masking, outcome measurement) and assessed certainty of evidence by outcome.

### Quantitative Synthesis

Planned meta-analysis could not be performed due to the absence of extractable, standardized effect sizes for the two outcomes of interest in the available summaries. Specifically:

- Identification of serious illness: Most trials reported intervention content (danger sign screening) and process measures (referrals) in full texts; numeric yields were not presented in the available abstracts for extraction.
- Care seeking: Directional improvements in treatment-seeking and access to care were reported, but without the specific proportions or adjusted effect estimates necessary for pooling.

Given this constraint, we conducted a structured synthesis by direction-of-effect and consistency across studies.

## Effectiveness Findings

### Identification of Serious Illness by CHWs

- Structured newborn assessments during home visits increased detection of danger signs and PSBI, enabling referral. The Ghana Newhints package trained community-based surveillance volunteers to assess and refer newborns with danger signs in the first week; subsequent analysis documented improved access—an intermediary reflecting successful identification and triage ([Newhints access to care, 2016](https://local-corpus/Newhints-access-2016)).
- Longitudinal HBNC in Gadchiroli demonstrated sustained CHW capacity to screen and diagnose neonatal sepsis over 23 years, with metrics of coverage, diagnostic fidelity, and adoption, validating the feasibility of accurate CHW identification in real-world settings when adequately trained and supervised ([Gadchiroli HBNC, 2022](https://local-corpus/Gadchiroli-2022)).
- In rural Pakistan, training LHWs to recognize and treat suspected neonatal respiratory infections with oral antibiotics (and basic resuscitation) operationalized identification and initial management at community level, confirming that CHW platforms can reliably identify serious illness within integrated packages ([Pakistan LHW curative, 2017](https://local-corpus/Pakistan-2017)).

Overall, the direction of effect across settings (West Africa, South Asia) indicates that CHW home visits with structured assessment increase identification of serious neonatal illness. Certainty is moderate given multiple cluster trials and a robust long-term program showing diagnostic fidelity.

### Care Seeking from Appropriate Health Facilities

- The Newhints access-to-care analysis found that CHV assessments and referrals improved access to facility care for sick newborns and reduced inequities, implying higher proportions of sick newborns being taken to or reaching facilities after CHW referral. The study highlighted equity gains among disadvantaged subgroups, a critical programmatic benefit ([Newhints access to care, 2016](https://local-corpus/Newhints-access-2016)).
- The Haryana IMNCI cluster trial reported improvements in caregiver treatment-seeking practices for neonatal and infant morbidities, detected through structured household surveys at 29 days, 6 months, and 12 months—a strong indication that home visit strategies coupled with systems strengthening shift care-seeking behaviors ([IMNCI Haryana, 2014](https://local-corpus/IMNCI-2014)).
- Pakistan’s community-based strategies trial reported mortality reductions and described strengthened community-facility linkages through LHWs, TBAs, and community committees—mechanisms likely to have increased care seeking, although specific care-seeking metrics were not available in the summary ([Pakistan community strategies, 2011](https://local-corpus/Pakistan-2011)).

Contextual evidence emphasizes the need for improved care seeking: in Ethiopia during the COVID-19 period, only 8–12% of mothers reported recognition of serious newborn illness and/or care seeking, pointing to large gaps that CHW home visit programs aim to fill ([Ethiopia PSBI formative, 2023](https://local-corpus/Ethiopia-PSBI-2023)). In Uganda, major contributors to neonatal death included caregiver delays in problem recognition and decision-making (50%) and facility care delays (30%), reinforcing the plausibility that CHW home visits reduce first-delay barriers and trigger timely referral ([Uganda three delays, 2010](https://local-corpus/Uganda-three-delays-2010)).

Taken together, moderate-certainty evidence indicates that CHW home visits increase care seeking for sick neonates, with equity benefits when interventions are properly supervised and linked to functioning facilities.

## Heterogeneity, Implementation Factors, and Equity

- Region and platform: Evidence spans sub-Saharan Africa (Ghana, South Africa) and South Asia (India, Pakistan). Effects were directionally consistent across regions.
- Intervention intensity: Packages combining early (day 1–3) postnatal visits, structured danger sign checklists, proactive referrals, and follow-up (e.g., Newhints; IMNCI; HBNC) showed the most consistent improvements.
- Health system linkage: Strong referral pathways and responsive facilities appear necessary to translate identification into effective care. Newhints found reduced inequities, suggesting that CHW visits can mitigate socioeconomic and geographic barriers when linkages function well ([Newhints access to care, 2016](https://local-corpus/Newhints-access-2016)).
- Task scope: Adding community-based curative functions (e.g., oral antibiotic initiation for PSBI when referral is not feasible) increased the potential to avert deterioration, but depends on training, supervision, and policy frameworks ([Pakistan LHW curative, 2017](https://local-corpus/Pakistan-2017); [Gadchiroli HBNC, 2022](https://local-corpus/Gadchiroli-2022)).
- Digital augmentation: Emerging protocols (e.g., CHV-NEO integrated with Kenya’s eCHIS) may further strengthen bidirectional communication and timely CHW support; outcomes are pending trial completion ([CHV-NEO protocol, 2024](https://local-corpus/CHV-NEO-2024)).

## Risk of Bias and Certainty of Evidence

- Risk of bias: Cluster randomization with restricted allocation (Newhints) and large-scale implementation strengthen internal and external validity. Lack of blinding is inherent but mitigated by objective behavioral outcomes. IMNCI and Pakistan cluster trials had robust designs; however, outcome ascertainment via caregiver interviews can introduce reporting bias.
- Certainty: Moderate for both outcomes given multiple high-quality trials and consistent direction of effect. Downgraded for imprecision in effect sizes due to unavailability of numeric outcomes in accessible summaries.

---

## Publication Bias

Formal assessment was not possible. The inclusion of large, well-known trials and long-term program evaluations reduces the risk that positive-only findings drive conclusions, but future reviews should include complete numeric data to enable funnel plot assessments.

## Program and Policy Implications

1. Scale structured CHW postnatal home visits with early timing:
   - Schedule at least one visit within the first 24–48 hours and additional contacts within the first week to capture the period when nearly 60–80% of neonatal deaths occur ([Cause-specific mortality analysis, 2015](https://local-corpus/Cause-specific-2015)).

2. Standardize danger sign screening and referral:
   - Implement simple, validated danger sign algorithms and referral slips; track referrals and outcomes.

3. Strengthen linkages and equity:
   - Coordinate with primary health facilities to ensure responsiveness; monitor equity indicators to ensure gains among the poorest and most remote families ([Newhints access to care, 2016](https://local-corpus/Newhints-access-2016)).

4. Enable community-based PSBI treatment when referral is not feasible:
   - Adopt WHO PSBI guidance and national adaptations to initiate simplified antibiotic regimens at primary level, with clear escalation pathways ([PSBI implementation Ethiopia/Kenya, 2024](https://local-corpus/PSBI-implementation-2024)).

5. Invest in CHW training, supervision, and data systems:
   - Continuous supportive supervision, competency refreshers, and integration with digital tools (eCHIS, SMS platforms) can maintain diagnostic fidelity and accelerate response ([Gadchiroli HBNC, 2022](https://local-corpus/Gadchiroli-2022); [CHV-NEO protocol, 2024](https://local-corpus/CHV-NEO-2024)).

6. Improve facility quality:
   - Address “third delay” with readiness for newborn care, including triage, antibiotics, resuscitation, and referral transport to ensure that increased care seeking translates to improved survival ([Uganda three delays, 2010](https://local-corpus/Uganda-three-delays-2010)).

---

## Research Priorities

- Report standardized indicators: Proportions of newborns assessed, danger signs identified, referrals issued, and proportion and timeliness of facility attendance following referral.
- Disaggregate by timing and equity: Effects by day of life, birth setting, socioeconomic status, and distance to facility.
- Hybrid trials: Evaluate combined CHW home visit plus digital communication models (e.g., CHV-NEO) on identification and care seeking.
- Cost-effectiveness: Complement outcomes with incremental cost per additional sick newborn brought to appropriate care and per death averted in program settings.

---

## Conclusions

Based on consistent findings from cluster randomized trials and long-term implementations, CHW home visits in LMICs improve identification of serious neonatal illness and increase care seeking to appropriate facilities. While pooled effect estimates could not be calculated from available summaries, the direction and coherence of evidence across contexts support adopting and scaling structured CHW postnatal home visiting with strong referral linkages and supportive facility readiness. Integrating PSBI management where referral is not feasible and augmenting CHW workflows with digital communication platforms hold promise to further improve timeliness and equity of neonatal care.

In our judgment, the weight of current evidence provides moderate certainty that CHW home visits materially strengthen neonatal illness identification and caregiver care-seeking behaviors in resource-limited settings. Program success will depend on early timing of visits, robust supervision, clear referral pathways, and facility quality to complete the continuum of care.
