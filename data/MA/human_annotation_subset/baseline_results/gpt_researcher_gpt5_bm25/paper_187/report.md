# Effectiveness of Team‑Based Learning Versus Other Pedagogies in Undergraduate Dental Education: A Scoping Review

## Executive Summary

This scoping review maps and appraises evidence on whether team‑based learning (TBL) is an effective alternative to traditional didactic lectures and other active pedagogies for undergraduate dental education, focusing on student satisfaction (Kirkpatrick Level 1) and academic performance/knowledge outcomes (Level 2). Across dental disciplines and geographies, TBL is consistently associated with higher student satisfaction and, in most studies, improved or comparable academic performance relative to lecture. Gains are most apparent when classic TBL design features (readiness assurance sequence with IRAT/GRAT, significant application exercises, peer evaluation, purposeful team formation, meaningful grading stakes) are implemented with fidelity and assessments are aligned to higher‑order outcomes. Direct head‑to‑head comparisons of TBL with flipped classroom (FC) are scarce in dentistry; limited within‑course evidence is inconclusive, while broader dental and health professions literature indicates FC can also improve performance. Overall, the weight of dental evidence—supported by high‑quality syntheses from adjacent health professions—supports TBL as an effective, learner‑centred alternative to lectures for cognitive outcomes and satisfaction, with contextual and implementation factors moderating effect sizes ([Effects of TBL on fixed prosthodontics, 2015](https://example.org/local_corpus/tokushima_prosth_tbl_2015); [Validity of peer evaluation in dental TBL, 2017](https://example.org/local_corpus/peer_eval_tbl_prosth_2017); [TBL vs lecture in dental education, 2021](https://example.org/local_corpus/tbl_vs_lecture_dentistry_2021); [Local anesthesia course TBL vs lecture, 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/local_anesthesia_tbl_2025); [Emerging teaching models in dental education meta‑analysis, 2024](https://example.org/local_corpus/emerging_models_dental_meta_2024)).

## Methods

### Review question and scope

- Review question: Among undergraduate dental learners, is TBL more effective than traditional didactic lectures and other pedagogies (e.g., flipped classroom, case‑based and problem‑based learning) in improving student satisfaction and academic performance?  
- Population: Undergraduate dental students.  
- Intervention: Team‑based learning (core elements per classic TBL framework).  
- Comparators: Lecture‑based learning (LBL), flipped classroom (FC), other active pedagogies.  
- Outcomes: Student satisfaction/attitudes (Kirkpatrick Level 1) and academic performance/knowledge/skills (Level 2).  
- Context: Preclinical and clinical dental courses.

### Sources and selection

This report draws from dental education studies in a curated corpus, prioritising peer‑reviewed studies with clear comparators and outcomes and, secondarily, high‑quality syntheses in closely related health professions to contextualise findings. Studies were mapped to designs (RCTs, quasi‑experimental, before‑after, cross‑sectional). Priority was given to more recent and discipline‑relevant sources.

### Data charting and quality appraisal

We charted: setting/discipline, design, sample, comparator, outcomes, main effects, and TBL fidelity features where available. Given heterogeneity and limited randomized dental trials, a narrative synthesis was performed; meta‑analytic effect sizes from high‑quality reviews were used for triangulation when pertinent. Risk of bias was appraised qualitatively by design (e.g., single‑site quasi‑experiments carry higher risk of confounding; cross‑sectional perceptions studies are not causal).

### Syntheses and broader contextual evidence

- Dental meta‑analysis of emerging teaching models (RCT‑only) found that PBL, CBL, TBL, and FC, as a group, improved performance versus LBL; method‑specific effect sizes were not fully delineated in the abstract, but pooled evidence supports replacing some lectures with structured active methods in dental curricula ([Emerging models dental meta‑analysis, 2024](https://example.org/local_corpus/emerging_models_dental_meta_2024)).  
- Periodontology scoping review (2025) catalogued multiple active methods (including TBL and FC) used in dental periodontology teaching with generally favorable learner outcomes, while underscoring heterogeneity in design and outcome measures ([Periodontology scoping review, 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/periodontology_scoping_2025)).  
- Outside dentistry, recent meta‑analyses consistently show TBL outperforms LBL in clinical medicine and nursing on knowledge and satisfaction (e.g., SMD≈0.46 for short‑term theory, SMD≈0.88 for practice; large effects for satisfaction), reinforcing the direction of effect seen in dental studies ([TBL vs LBL in clinical medicine meta‑analysis, 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/tbl_clinical_medicine_meta_2025); [TBL vs LBL in nursing RCTs, 2022](https://example.org/local_corpus/tbl_nursing_rcts_2022)).  
- Health professions flipped‑classroom meta‑analyses show average exam scores ≈5.4 percentage points higher than lectures, indicating that other active methods besides TBL can also improve outcomes if well implemented ([HPE flipped meta‑analysis, 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/hpe_flipped_meta_2025)).

### Student satisfaction (Kirkpatrick Level 1)

- In prosthodontics, students rated TBL highly; active discussion was cited as a key driver of satisfaction ([Tokushima 2015](https://example.org/local_corpus/tokushima_prosth_tbl_2015)).  
- Saudi dentistry students reported favorable perceptions of TBL compared with lectures, aligning with observed grade improvements in TBL components ([Qassim 2021](https://example.org/local_corpus/tbl_vs_lecture_dentistry_2021)).  
- In local anesthesia, students’ positive perceptions were strongly correlated with performance (r=0.780) in TBL‑covered topics ([Local anesthesia 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/local_anesthesia_tbl_2025)).  
- Comparative dental pharmacology data show FC also improves satisfaction relative to lectures, underscoring that active pedagogies broadly tend to raise satisfaction ([Dental pharmacology FC 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/dental_pharm_fc_2025)).

Overall, satisfaction consistently favors TBL over lectures across dental contexts; differences between TBL and other active pedagogies (e.g., FC) are less well quantified but appear generally positive for both approaches.

### Academic performance (Kirkpatrick Level 2)

- Fixed prosthodontics: TBL was associated with significantly higher term‑end examination performance relative to lecture in a within‑course design ([Tokushima 2015](https://example.org/local_corpus/tokushima_prosth_tbl_2015)).  
- Across dental blocks, grade distributions often favored TBL components, though block‑level heterogeneity and non‑randomized designs limit causal inference ([Qassim 2021](https://example.org/local_corpus/tbl_vs_lecture_dentistry_2021)).  
- Local anesthesia: performance gains on topics taught via TBL were reported alongside strong perception–performance coupling ([Local anesthesia 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/local_anesthesia_tbl_2025)).  
- Direct FC vs TBL comparison within the same prosthodontics course (2017) attempted to isolate effects on overlapping content; while the design is promising (with a faculty‑completed referential exam to benchmark question difficulty), the accessible abstract does not provide outcome direction, leaving the relative ranking of TBL vs FC inconclusive in that instance ([Prosth FC vs TBL 2017](https://example.org/local_corpus/prosth_fc_vs_tbl_2017)).  
- Dental pharmacology (FC vs lecture) and orthodontic RCTs (FC variants vs lecture) show FC can produce meaningful gains (e.g., +7% exam score), demonstrating that active alternatives beyond TBL can be effective comparators ([Dental pharmacology FC 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/dental_pharm_fc_2025); [Orthodontic emergencies RCT 2020](https://example.org/local_corpus/ortho_emergencies_fc_rct_2020); [Ortho live vs FC 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/ortho_live_vs_fc_2025)).

### Implementation fidelity of TBL

- High‑fidelity TBL prosthodontics courses included IRAT, GRAT, group applications, and peer evaluation; peer evaluation scores correlated with IRAT and term exam performance, supporting internal validity of TBL’s mechanisms ([Peer Eval 2017](https://example.org/local_corpus/peer_eval_tbl_prosth_2017)).  
- Where TBL implementations were described minimally (e.g., “tests and group discussion” without explicit IRAT/GRAT/peer evaluation), observed benefits were present but difficult to attribute solely to TBL design elements ([Local anesthesia 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/local_anesthesia_tbl_2025)).  
- Purposeful team formation, session duration (~2 hours), and explicit stakes were better detailed in classic TBL prosthodontics work than in newer reports; future studies should standardize fidelity reporting to enable moderator analyses.

### Moderators and contextual factors

- Discipline/topic: Benefits appear across cognitive disciplines (prosthodontics theory, local anesthesia principles) and may extend to integrated courses; complex psychomotor skills were not the focus of included TBL studies.  
- Assessment alignment: TBL appears most effective when summative assessments stress application/analysis consistent with 4S application exercises. Lecture‑style recall exams may attenuate observed TBL advantages.  
- Student cohort and class size: Studies span 3rd/4th year cohorts; sample sizes are modest (n≈36–256) and mostly single‑site.  
- Comparator strength: Active comparators (e.g., interactive FC) can also improve performance, reducing observable between‑group gaps relative to LBL.

### Risk of bias and certainty of evidence

- Dental TBL evidence remains dominated by single‑site quasi‑experimental and cross‑sectional designs with inherent risks (selection bias, sequence effects, historical confounding). Randomization and crossover designs are rare.  
- Outcome measures are often course‑specific exams with variable validation; only one study used faculty referential testing to benchmark difficulty across pedagogy blocks ([Prosth FC vs TBL 2017](https://example.org/local_corpus/prosth_fc_vs_tbl_2017)).  
- Nevertheless, convergent findings across multiple dental settings and supportive high‑quality meta‑evidence from allied health disciplines increase confidence in directionality (TBL ≥ LBL on Level 1–2 outcomes), while the precise magnitude in dental contexts warrants more robust trials.

## Interpretation and Opinion

Based on the dental studies reviewed—supported by rigorous syntheses in nearby health professions—TBL is a sound and often superior alternative to lectures for improving student satisfaction and, in most cases, academic performance in undergraduate dental education. The most convincing dental evidence comes from fixed prosthodontics courses that implemented TBL with high fidelity, demonstrating statistically significant exam gains and strong learner endorsement. Courses that only partially adopted TBL elements still showed positive trajectories, although attribution to canonical TBL features is less secure.

When TBL is pitched against other active learning strategies, especially well‑designed flipped classrooms, differences in performance may narrow; both pedagogies leverage pre‑class preparation and in‑class engagement. The thin and incomplete direct dental evidence comparing FC and TBL precludes definitive superiority claims; however, the mechanism‑rich structure of TBL (readiness assurance, team accountability, structured application) and the observed correlations between peer evaluation, IRAT, and summative performance suggest TBL provides a robust framework for consistent gains when implemented with fidelity and aligned assessment.

In concrete terms, for dental educators seeking to replace a portion of lectures, adopting high‑fidelity TBL in cognitively demanding topics (e.g., treatment planning in prosthodontics, local anesthesia decision‑making) is likely to yield measurable improvements in both satisfaction and exam performance. For topics emphasizing foundational knowledge transmission or procedural demonstration, flipped and blended approaches may offer similar benefits; hybrid designs (e.g., FC pre‑class with TBL in‑class applications) could be strategically leveraged.

## Implications for Dental Educators

- Adopt TBL with fidelity: Include IRAT/GRAT, 4S application exercises with significant problems and specific choices, peer evaluation, purposeful team formation, and meaningful grading stakes.  
- Align assessments: Emphasize application and analysis in summative exams to capture TBL’s strengths.  
- Consider blended models: Combine FC for content acquisition with TBL for in‑class application to maximize engagement and learning.  
- Monitor workload and readiness: Provide structured pre‑class materials and clear expectations; use readiness assurance data to calibrate.  
- Evaluate and report: Use validated satisfaction instruments and standardized performance metrics; report TBL fidelity to enable replication and meta‑research.

## Research Gaps and Recommendations

- Conduct multi‑site randomized or crossover trials in dental disciplines directly comparing TBL to FC and LBL with standardized outcomes (including common question banks) and blinded grading.  
- Report TBL implementation fidelity consistently to allow moderator analyses (IRAT/GRAT scores, peer evaluation policies, stakes).  
- Extend outcomes beyond Level 1–2: Assess clinical decision‑making performance, OSCEs, and longitudinal retention; explore Level 3–4 impacts (behavior and patient‑related educational proxies).  
- Explore equity: Examine how TBL impacts diverse learner subgroups and the role of team formation strategies.  
- Cost and feasibility: Analyze resource requirements and scalability in large dental cohorts.

## Limitations of This Review

- Reliance on a limited set of accessible abstracts constrains extraction of exact effect sizes for some dental studies.  
- Heterogeneity in study designs, outcomes, and TBL fidelity limits quantitative synthesis within dentistry.  
- The inclusion of supportive evidence from adjacent health professions, while helpful for triangulation, may not fully generalize to dental curricula and assessments.

## Conclusion

Within undergraduate dental education, TBL is a credible and frequently superior alternative to traditional lectures on student satisfaction and knowledge outcomes, provided it is implemented with core design elements and accompanied by aligned assessment. Direct comparisons with other active pedagogies, notably flipped classrooms, are sparse but suggest both can be effective; the choice may hinge on curricular goals, faculty expertise, and logistical context. Given consistent positive directions and the mechanistic plausibility borne out in dental prosthodontics, local anesthesia, and broader health professions, strategic incorporation of high‑fidelity TBL into dental curricula is warranted, alongside rigorous evaluation to refine best‑fit use cases.
