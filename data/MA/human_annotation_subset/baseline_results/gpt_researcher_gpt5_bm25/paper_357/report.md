# Postmortem Evidence of Cerebral Inflammation in Schizophrenia: A Systematic Review of Microglia, Astrocytes, Cytokines, and Related Immune Markers

## Abstract

Objective: To critically synthesize postmortem brain evidence for neuroinflammation in schizophrenia, focusing on microglia, astrocytes, cytokines/chemokines, complement and MHC pathways, and related immune-oxidative cascades, while appraising methodological rigor, brain-region specificity, and key confounders.

Methods: We evaluated postmortem studies from a curated corpus (1993–2026) spanning immunohistochemistry (IHC), stereology, in situ hybridization, qPCR, microarray/RNA-seq, and targeted proteomics. We prioritized recent, methodologically robust studies and systematic reviews/meta-analyses. We assessed findings by cell type and pathway, with attention to region specificity (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex [DLPFC], anterior cingulate cortex [ACC], hippocampus, thalamus, midbrain, white matter), and appraised confounding by antipsychotics, suicide, smoking, viral serostatus, substance use, age, PMI, pH, and agonal state.

Results: Across modalities, convergent evidence indicates increased microglial activation markers—particularly HLA‑DR/CD68 (MHC-II/lysosomal) and IBA1—in subsets of schizophrenia brains, especially in DLPFC, temporal cortex, ACC, and white matter; lateralization and disease-course effects are reported, and microgliosis is also associated with suicide (diagnosis-independent), underscoring a key confounder. Bulk transcriptomics and targeted assays demonstrate elevation of proinflammatory cytokine transcripts (IL1B, IL6, IL8) and SERPINA3 in DLPFC, upregulation of IL-23 pathway signatures, and increased complement (C1qA, C3, C4) and macrophage-related transcripts in midbrain, most pronounced in “high inflammation” biotypes. Astroglial findings are heterogeneous: region- and layer-specific GFAP decreases (e.g., subgenual cingulate; dlPFC layer V process area) contrast with reactive astrocyte signatures in a high-inflammation subset and robust evidence for S100 family upregulation (S100 meta-analysis and S100B-positive glia increases), suggesting astrocytic phenotypic diversity and immunophenotypic stratification. Complement/MHC and kynurenine pathway alterations align with immune activation and synaptic/circuit effects. White matter studies and systematic reviews link microglial activation to oligodendroglial/myelin abnormalities. Confounders (suicide, smoking, CMV serostatus, substance use) and methodological heterogeneity (marker selection, quantification, cell-type proportions, tissue quality) substantially impact interpretation.

Conclusion (opinion based on evidence): Postmortem data support a model of neuroinflammation in schizophrenia that is present in a molecularly and regionally defined subset (“high‑inflammation” immunophenotypes), characterized by microglial activation (antigen presentation and phagolysosomal programs), astrocyte reactivity/dysregulation (including S100 family upregulation), cytokine pathway activation (IL1/IL6/IL8 and IL‑23), and complement engagement. These processes are not ubiquitous across all cases or regions and are sensitive to major confounders—particularly suicide, smoking, and latent infections—necessitating rigorous stratification in future studies. Integration of spatially resolved and single-nucleus approaches with detailed clinical/biological metadata is recommended to refine causal inference and therapeutic targeting.

## Introduction

Neuroinflammation—an orchestrated set of immune processes within the CNS involving microglia, astrocytes, cytokines/chemokines, complement, MHC pathways, and oxidative stress—has been implicated in schizophrenia pathophysiology. Yet, postmortem studies have historically yielded heterogeneous findings due to region- and cell-type specificity, analytic variability, and confounding exposures near death. Here, we systematically review postmortem evidence for cerebral inflammatory alterations in schizophrenia relative to healthy controls, emphasizing microglia, astrocytes, cytokines, complement/MHC, and related pathways, along with methodological quality and confounders. The central question is not whether “neuroinflammation exists” in schizophrenia, but where, in whom, and by which mechanisms—an inquiry that informs immunophenotyping, biomarker development, and therapeutic strategies. Recent multi-omic and clustering approaches enable sharper resolution of “high-inflammation” subgroups in schizophrenia, making a contemporary synthesis timely ([Neuroinflammation in Schizophrenia: An Overview of Evidence and Implications for Pathophysiology, 2025](local_corpus://neuroinflammation_scz_overview_2025)).

## Methods: Scope, Modalities, Regions, and Appraisal Criteria

- Study scope: Postmortem human brain studies in schizophrenia vs. controls reporting immune/neuroinflammatory markers (microglia, astrocytes, cytokines/chemokines, complement/MHC, kynurenine pathway, acute-phase/serpins), with emphasis on robust quantitation and region specificity.
- Modalities: IHC (HLA‑DR, IBA1, CD68; GFAP; S100 family; QUIN); stereology; in situ hybridization; qPCR; microarray; RNA‑seq; gene set enrichment; proteomics.
- Regions: DLPFC/BA9, ACC (including subgenual cingulate), superior temporal gyrus (STG), hippocampus (subfields), mediodorsal thalamus, midbrain (ventral tegmental/substantia nigra), and adjacent white matter.
- Appraisal: We prioritized systematic reviews/meta-analyses and recent high‑quality studies; assessed confounding by antipsychotic exposure, suicide, smoking, viral serostatus (e.g., CMV), substance use, age, sex, PMI, brain pH, and agonal factors; and noted retracted reports.

## Results

### Microglia: Elevated Activation Markers with Regional and Phenotypic Specificity

- Cortical microglial activation: Early IHC reports found increased HLA‑DR+ microglial densities in chronic schizophrenia in DLPFC (BA9) and STG, with a 28% increase in area 9 versus controls (HLA‑DR; antigen-presenting MHC‑II), indicating enhanced immune vigilance in select neocortical regions ([Increase in HLA‑DR immunoreactive microglia…, 2000](local_corpus://hla_dr_increase_cortex_scz_2000)). Subsequent work demonstrated region-specific HLA‑DR+ microglial distributions across DLPFC, ACC, hippocampus, and mediodorsal thalamus, with hemispheric lateralization patterns, suggesting microglial responses are not uniform but anatomically organized in schizophrenia brains ([Distribution of HLA‑DR‑positive microglia…, 2006](local_corpus://steiner_hladr_lateralization_2006)).
- Morphological activation classes: Quantitative analysis in frontal/temporal lobes identified differences in ramified (RM) and degenerative/damaged (DM) microglial morphologies in schizophrenia, consistent with activation and process injury; however, sample sizes were modest and classification criteria varied ([Quantitative analysis of activated microglia…, 2005](local_corpus://microglia_quant_frontal_temporal_2005)).
- Suicide confounding: A critical study associated elevated HLA‑DR+ microglial densities with suicide across diagnoses (schizophrenia and affective disorders), suggesting microgliosis can reflect acute terminal pathophysiology independent of schizophrenia per se and must be controlled analytically ([Immunological aspects in the neurobiology of suicide…, 2008](local_corpus://suicide_microglia_2008)).
- White matter predilection: A systematic review collating neuropathology and neuroimaging linked schizophrenia with microglial activation preferentially in white matter over gray matter (and ultrastructural myelin abnormalities), highlighting glial-immune contributions to connectivity pathologies ([Neuroinflammation and white matter pathology…, 2015](local_corpus://neuroinflammation_white_matter_scz_2015)).
- Hippocampal functional specificity: Notably, microglial immunoreactivity for quinolinic acid (QUIN; an NMDA receptor agonist produced by microglia) was reduced in CA1 in schizophrenia, implying regional functional heterogeneity of microglial metabolism and signaling even amid activation signals elsewhere ([Reduced microglial immunoreactivity for QUIN…, 2014](local_corpus://quinolinic_hippocampus_scz_2014)).
- Disease course: In hippocampus, distributions of lymphocytes and microglia differed between residual vs paranoid schizophrenia, indicative of immune alterations related to symptom course and chronicity ([Different distribution patterns of lymphocytes and microglia…, 2012](local_corpus://hippocampus_lymph_microglia_course_2012)).

Overall, microglial findings converge on increased antigen-presenting/lysosomal phenotypes (HLA‑DR/CD68) and altered morphological states in select cortical and white matter regions, modulated by hemispheric lateralization and clinical factors (e.g., suicide), supporting a model of region- and state-dependent microglial activation rather than a pan-brain uniform process.

### Astrocytes: Divergent GFAP Signals and Consistent S100 Family Upregulation in Subgroups

- GFAP heterogeneity: Layer-specific analyses reported reduced GFAP area fraction in dlPFC layer V in schizophrenia, accompanied by increased density of GFAP+ somata—suggesting thinner or retracted processes rather than loss of astrocyte bodies ([Layer‑specific reductions in GFAP‑reactive astroglia…, 2002](local_corpus://dlpfc_gfap_layer_v_2002)). Independent studies documented decreased astrocyte densities in the subgenual cingulate (gray and white matter) and genu of the corpus callosum in schizophrenia, and fewer fibrillary astrocytes in subgenual white matter, suggesting regional astrocyte paucity in key limbic/callosal tracts ([Astrocyte decrease in the subgenual cingulate…, 2013](local_corpus://astrocyte_decrease_subgenual_2013); [Fibrillary astrocytes are decreased…, 2014](local_corpus://fibrillary_astrocytes_subgenual_2014)). Conversely, in the DLPFC, a transcriptomic study identified individuals with schizophrenia exhibiting increased GFAP expression and reactive astrocyte morphology within a broader “high-inflammation” subgroup, underscoring immunophenotypic heterogeneity ([Increased expression of astrocyte markers in schizophrenia…, 2014](local_corpus://astro_markers_inflammation_scz_2014)).
- S100 family evidence: A 2023 PRISMA meta-analysis of 12 microarray datasets (511 samples) found upregulation of 9/21 S100 genes (none downregulated), with per-sample analyses indicating changes concentrated in a subset of patients; ANXA3 (Annexin 3), linked to neuroinflammation, was co-upregulated and correlated with S100 expression. These data robustly support astrocyte-related S100 dysregulation as a marker of inflammatory astroglial activation in a schizophrenia subgroup ([Up-Regulation of S100 Gene Family…, 2023](local_corpus://s100_meta_scZ_2023)). Morphometric data also showed higher densities of S100B-immunopositive glia in cortical regions of paranoid vs residual schizophrenia, aligning with state-dependent astroglial variation ([S100B-immunopositive glia is elevated…, 2008](local_corpus://s100b_paranoid_residual_2008)).

Taken together, astrocytic alterations are regionally bidirectional for GFAP (reductions in limbic/callosal sites; reactive increases in high-inflammation DLPFC subgroup) but show more consistent S100 family upregulation in a molecularly defined subset, supporting astrocyte phenotype diversity within schizophrenia.

### Cytokines, Chemokines, and Acute-Phase Markers: DLPFC and Pathway-Level Signatures

- DLPFC cytokine upregulation: Next-generation RNA‑seq in DLPFC (n=20 pairs) reported differential expression of 798 transcripts with pathway enrichment for inflammation and confirmed elevations of IL1B, IL6, IL8, and SERPINA3 (alpha‑1 antichymotrypsin), alongside increased density of MHC‑II+ microglia-like cells, demonstrating convergent cell and transcript markers of neuroinflammation ([Increased inflammatory markers identified in the DLPFC…, 2013](local_corpus://dlpfc_rnaseq_inflam_2013)).
- Cross-region gene-set analysis: A unified re-analysis across seven regions found upregulation of interleukin‑23 signaling in DLPFC and metal-ion response/RUNX1 in parietal cortex, indicating selective, modest effect-size inflammatory pathway activation after adjusting for confounders ([Analysis of global gene expression at seven brain regions…, 2020](local_corpus://seven_region_transcriptomics_2020)).
- Kynurenine pathway coupling to cytokines: Postmortem PFC enzyme mRNAs and metabolites differed when schizophrenia cases were stratified by elevated inflammatory cytokine mRNAs, consistent with immune–metabolic crosstalk (increased KYNA reported in schizophrenia brain) and supporting the concept of high‑inflammation biotypes with distinct neurochemical profiles ([Dysregulation of kynurenine metabolism…, 2020](local_corpus://kp_cytokines_pfc_scz_2020)).

### Complement and MHC Pathways: Midbrain and Hippocampal Immune Signatures

- Midbrain complement/macrophage activation: In the dopamine neuron–rich ventral midbrain, schizophrenia cases—especially within “high inflammatory” biotypes—exhibited increased transcripts for complement activators/mediators (C1qA, C3, C4), perivascular and parenchymal CD163+ macrophages, and adhesion molecules, with correlations to glial markers, pointing to innate immune engagement at dopaminergic hubs ([Increased Macrophages and C1qA, C3, C4 Transcripts in the Midbrain…, 2020](local_corpus://midbrain_complement_macrophage_scz_2020)).
- MHC class I modulation and smoking: In hippocampus, expression of 6p21.3–22.1 immune genes was altered; notably, HLA‑B mRNA was increased in nonsmoking schizophrenia subjects, whereas smokers resembled controls, highlighting smoking as a major modifier of CNS immune gene expression and a critical confounder ([Expression of immune genes on chromosome 6p21.3–22.1…, 2013](local_corpus://mhc6p_hippocampus_smoking_scz_2013)).

### White Matter and Oligodendroglia: Immune–Myelin Interactions

- Synthesis across methods: The 2015 review concluded that microglial activation frequently co-occurs with white matter pathology in schizophrenia, consistent with a mechanistic interface between neuroinflammation and connectivity deficits ([Neuroinflammation and white matter pathology…, 2015](local_corpus://neuroinflammation_white_matter_scz_2015)). Complementary morphometric studies reported glial alterations in prefrontal white matter across schizophrenia and bipolar disorder (IBA1 microglia, GFAP astrocytes), further tying immune changes to myelin/oligodendrocyte disturbances ([Evidence for morphological alterations in prefrontal white matter glia…, 2014](local_corpus://white_matter_glia_ba9_2014)).
- Oligodendroglia: Stereology in hippocampus (CA4) found reduced oligodendrocyte numbers in schizophrenia; subcortical oligodendrocyte-associated gene expression also tended to be lower across regions, consistent with glial–immune contributions to myelin integrity ([Stereologic investigation of the posterior hippocampus…, 2009](local_corpus://hippocampus_stereology_2009); [Subcortical oligodendrocyte- and astrocyte-associated gene expression…, 2009](local_corpus://subcortical_oligo_genes_2009)).

### Heterogeneity and Immunophenotypes

- High-inflammation subgroups: Multiple lines—DLPFC cytokine/SERPINA3 elevations, midbrain complement/macrophage signatures, S100 family meta-analysis with per-sample clustering, and cytokine-stratified KP differences—support discrete immunophenotypes within schizophrenia rather than uniform neuroinflammation. Machine-learning studies using peripheral markers similarly identify inflammatory subgroups with distinct neuroanatomical profiles, further validating heterogeneity ([Up-Regulation of S100 Gene Family…, 2023](local_corpus://s100_meta_scZ_2023); [Increased inflammatory markers identified in the DLPFC…, 2013](local_corpus://dlpfc_rnaseq_inflam_2013); [Increased Macrophages and C1qA, C3, C4 Transcripts…, 2020](local_corpus://midbrain_complement_macrophage_scz_2020); [Dysregulation of kynurenine metabolism…, 2020](local_corpus://kp_cytokines_pfc_scz_2020); [Inflammatory subgroups of schizophrenia…, 2023](local_corpus://hydra_inflammatory_subgroups_2023)).
- Regionality: DLPFC, midbrain, white matter, and ACC/subgenual cingulate emerge as loci of robust change; hippocampus shows mixed signals (reduced QUIN microglia; MHC modulation by smoking; disease-course immune differences).

### Confounders and Modifiers

- Suicide: Strongly associated with higher microglial activation independent of diagnosis, requiring subgroup analyses or covariate control ([Immunological aspects in the neurobiology of suicide…, 2008](local_corpus://suicide_microglia_2008)).
- Smoking: Normalizes some hippocampal MHC class I upregulation, potentially masking disease effects ([Expression of immune genes on chromosome 6p21.3–22.1…, 2013](local_corpus://mhc6p_hippocampus_smoking_scz_2013)).
- Viral serostatus (CMV): Anti‑CMV antibodies associate with psychiatric diagnoses, suicide, and higher DLPFC inflammation clusters/microglial activation markers in a large postmortem cohort, implicating latent herpesviruses as contributors to brain immune activation and an important covariate ([Cytomegalovirus antibodies are associated with mood disorders…, 2023](local_corpus://cmv_serostatus_stanley_2023)).
- Substance use: Postmortem DLPFC extracellular vesicles from SUD cases carry inflammatory signatures, indicating potential glial activation effects of substances and a critical confounder for brain immune readouts ([Lipidomic and proteomic insights from extracellular vesicles…, 2024](local_corpus://ev_dlpfc_sud_2024); [2025 version](local_corpus://ev_dlpfc_sud_2025)).
- Antipsychotics: While most postmortem studies attempt to control for exposure, effects on glia are mixed; careful stratification by drug exposure and blood levels remains necessary ([The Janus face of antipsychotics in glial cells…, 2023](local_corpus://janus_antipsychotics_glia_2023)).
- Age/region/tissue quality: Microglial methylome is strongly influenced by age and brain region; tissue pH, PMI, and agonal state impact RNA/protein integrity and should be modeled ([Contribution of Age, Brain Region, Mood Disorder Pathology, and Interindividual Factors on the Methylome of Human Microglia, 2022](local_corpus://microglia_methylome_2022)).

## Methodological Appraisal

- Marker specificity and cell states: HLA‑DR/CD68 mark antigen presentation and lysosomal activation but are not exclusive to “neurotoxic” microglia. IBA1 labels microglia/macrophages broadly. GFAP reflects intermediate filaments, variably expressed across astrocyte subtypes; S100B/NDRG2 mark complementary astrocyte pools, and S100 family proteins participate in alarmin signaling. Using multiple markers and morphometrics improves interpretability over single‑marker studies.
- Quantification rigor: Design-based stereology and 3D morphometry reduce bias versus density-by-area fraction alone; nonetheless, cell shrinkage/swelling, regional sampling error, and layer misidentification can mislead estimates, especially in allocortex (e.g., hippocampus) with fine cytoarchitectonic gradients.
- Transcriptomics: RNA‑seq affords sensitivity over microarrays and can detect subtle pathway-level changes; however, bulk tissue signals are confounded by cell-type composition. Pairing with deconvolution or cell-type–specific profiling (laser capture/single-nucleus RNA‑seq/spatial transcriptomics) is essential to ascribe signals to glia vs neurons and to identify plaque/niche-specific crosstalk, as shown in AD literature and increasingly feasible in psychiatric postmortem cohorts.
- Tissue quality: pH, PMI, and agonal factors affect RNA/protein integrity and immune gene expression; models must include these as covariates. Brain region and age exert large effects on microglial methylomes and transcriptional states, requiring age- and region-matched designs ([Human microglia methylome 2022](local_corpus://microglia_methylome_2022)).
- Confounder control: Suicide status, smoking (notably normalizing hippocampal MHC‑I), latent viral infections (e.g., CMV), and substance use are strong modifiers of brain immune profiles; antipsychotic exposure may alter glial biology. Studies explicitly stratifying by these factors (and blood verification of exposure/serostatus) provide more reliable inferences ([Suicide 2008](local_corpus://suicide_microglia_2008); [MHC6p 2013](local_corpus://mhc6p_hippocampus_smoking_scz_2013); [CMV 2023](local_corpus://cmv_serostatus_stanley_2023); [EV-SUD 2024/2025](local_corpus://ev_dlpfc_sud_2024)).

Importantly, a 2013 report claiming widespread frontal cortical inflammatory cascade upregulation was retracted for data falsification and should not inform conclusions, illustrating the necessity of cross-validating key claims across independent datasets ([RETRACTED 2013](local_corpus://retracted_rao_2013)).

## Interpretation and Opinion

The weight of methodologically sound postmortem evidence supports a model in which neuroinflammation is present in schizophrenia but concentrated in a subset of cases and regions:

- A microglia-centered signature—elevated HLA‑DR/CD68/IBA1 indices, regional morphological activation, and complement engagement—is most consistently observed in DLPFC, temporal cortex, ACC, white matter, and midbrain, aligning with circuits subserving cognition, sensory integration, and dopaminergic modulation.
- Astrocyte changes are heterogeneous: regionally decreased GFAP (subgenual cingulate/callosum; possible process retraction in dlPFC) contrasts with reactive astrocytosis within high‑inflammation DLPFC subgroups and robust S100 family upregulation in transcriptomic meta-analysis. This pattern fits the emerging concept of diverse astrocyte “reactive states” with context-dependent functions (metabolic, synaptic, BBB, immune).
- Cytokine pathway activation (IL1B, IL6, IL8; IL‑23 axis) and immune–metabolic coupling (kynurenine pathway) co-localize with microglial activation and complement changes, bolstering biological coherence.
- Findings are sensitive to clinical/biological confounds—particularly suicide (increasing microgliosis independent of diagnosis), smoking (masking hippocampal MHC‑I changes), and latent infections (CMV associated with higher DLPFC inflammatory clusters)—and to substance use. Therefore, the “average case” analysis can obscure clear neuroinflammatory signals that emerge upon stratification.
- White matter predilection for microglial activation and frequent oligodendroglial/myelin abnormalities suggest that immune processes interface with connectivity pathology in schizophrenia, potentially contributing to dysconnectivity and cognitive symptoms.

In sum, cerebral inflammation in schizophrenia is neither ubiquitous nor epiphenomenal: it defines a biologically coherent, clinically relevant immunophenotype characterized by glial activation and innate immune pathway engagement. This has implications for precision therapeutics (e.g., complement modulation, microglia-targeting agents, cytokine pathway inhibitors) and for biomarker strategies that integrate peripheral and central immune measures to identify “high‑inflammation” patients most likely to benefit.

## Recommendations for Future Research

- Systematic stratification: Prospectively classify postmortem cohorts by suicide status, smoking, antipsychotic exposure (with blood levels), viral serostatus (CMV/HSV), SUD, and systemic inflammatory illness to delineate diagnosis-specific effects from modifiers.
- Spatially resolved, cell-type–specific profiling: Combine single-nucleus RNA‑seq, spatial transcriptomics, and multiplex IHC to map microglia–astrocyte–neuron–vascular crosstalk in schizophrenia-relevant circuits (DLPFC layer V/III, ACC/subgenual, hippocampal subfields, midbrain, white matter tracts).
- Complement and synapse biology: Integrate complement pathway readouts with synaptic protein and EM ultrastructure to test hypotheses of immune-mediated synaptic remodeling in high‑inflammation biotypes.
- Longitudinal integration: Link postmortem phenotypes to antemortem inflammatory biomarkers and imaging (e.g., free-water MRI, TSPO PET) where available, to triangulate in vivo–postmortem concordance and improve patient selection for immunomodulatory trials.
- Standardization and replication: Adopt harmonized protocols for tissue handling, marker panels, and quantitative analysis to enable meta-analytic synthesis and reduce between-study heterogeneity.

## Conclusion

Postmortem evidence substantiates cerebral inflammatory alterations in schizophrenia, but not as a uniform hallmark. Rather, a subset of patients exhibits microglial activation (antigen presentation, phagolysosomal activity), astrocyte reactivity/dysregulation (salient S100 upregulation), cytokine pathway activation, and complement engagement in specific cortical, subcortical, and white matter regions. These immunophenotypes are clinically meaningful yet easily obscured by potent confounders. Future progress hinges on rigorous stratification, cell-type–resolved spatial analyses, and translational bridges between in vivo and postmortem biology to inform targeted immunotherapies.
