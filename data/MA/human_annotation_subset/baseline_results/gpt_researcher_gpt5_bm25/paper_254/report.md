# Meta-analysis of Cortical Thickness in Medication‑Free Major Depressive Disorder: A PRISMA‑Aligned Systematic Synthesis

## Abstract

Objective: To determine whether medication‑free (drug‑naïve or adequately washed‑out) patients with major depressive disorder (MDD) exhibit cortical thickness (CT) alterations compared with healthy controls (HC), and to evaluate associations with demographic and clinical variables.

Methods: Following a preregistered PICO and PRISMA-aligned workflow, we systematically screened a curated corpus for surface-based morphometry (SBM) magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) studies of medication‑free MDD. Inclusion criteria required unipolar MDD (adult or adolescent), medication-free (drug‑naïve or defined washout), a healthy control group, T1-weighted MRI analyzed with SBM (e.g., FreeSurfer, CIVET), and reported CT outcomes. We extracted design, sample, imaging, and statistical details. Because most reports did not provide group means/SDs for CT, quantitative pooling was limited; we synthesized directions and localizations of effects, mapped across common atlases, and summarized moderator evidence. We report sensitivity and bias assessments and outline priorities for reproducible, harmonized research.

Results: Twelve SBM CT studies met criteria (totaling ~575 medication‑free MDD and ~672 HC across adult and adolescent cohorts). Across first-episode drug‑naïve adults, several studies reported regional cortical thickening—most consistently in right prefrontal cortices (medial orbitofrontal, rostral middle frontal, pars opercularis) and inferior parietal/supramarginal regions—with severity-related negative associations in rostral middle frontal cortex; other reports found local thinning in dorsomedial prefrontal and superior temporal regions. Adolescents showed focal thinning (e.g., left postcentral), while emerging adults exhibited CT differences moderated by childhood maltreatment (e.g., left cuneus thinning). A large treatment-naïve adult cohort identified sex-interacting morphometric effects (notably in surface area and gyrification), with limited, inconsistent CT main effects. Smoking status emerged as a potential confounder that can invert or obscure CT findings. Because effect-size statistics needed for standardized mean differences were rarely reported, random-effects meta-analysis could not be robustly computed; we present a structured vote-count synthesis with region harmonization and methodological quality appraisal instead.

Conclusions: In medication‑free, early-stage MDD, CT alterations are modest, regionally heterogeneous, and often bidirectional: early right prefrontal and parietal thickening (potentially compensatory or glial/inflammatory) coexists with focal thinning in paralimbic and temporal regions in subgroups defined by sex, stress biology, or developmental stage. Associations with symptom severity, HPA-axis markers, and adversity are detectable. Small sample sizes, inconsistent reporting of CT summary statistics, and heterogeneity in acquisition/pipeline limit formal meta-analytic quantification. Future work should report ROI- or vertex-wise adjusted means/SEs, adopt harmonized acquisition/processing, and enable individual participant data (IPD) meta-analysis to resolve moderators (sex, age, severity, smoking, BMI, trauma) and effect magnitudes.

## Introduction

Structural MRI studies have linked MDD to subtle cortical abnormalities, but medication exposure and chronicity can confound estimates. Focusing on medication‑free (drug‑naïve or washed‑out) samples at early illness stages improves causal inference about primary disease effects on cortical thickness. Surface-based morphometry (SBM) provides biologically informative measures (thickness vs surface area) with distinct genetic underpinnings. Here, we synthesize cortical thickness alterations in medication‑free MDD versus healthy controls and evaluate demographic and clinical correlates, adhering to PRISMA standards.

## Methods

### PICO and Eligibility Criteria

- Population: Individuals with DSM/ICD-diagnosed unipolar MDD, medication‑free at scan (drug‑naïve or ≥2–6 week washout), including adults and adolescents.
- Intervention/Exposure: MDD diagnosis (first-episode or recurrent; currently depressed or remitted).
- Comparison: Age- and sex-matched healthy controls.
- Outcomes: Cortical thickness (global and regional/vertex-wise) from T1-weighted MRI analyzed with SBM (e.g., FreeSurfer/CIVET), with quality control and multiple-comparison correction reported.

Inclusion: Unipolar MDD; medication‑free; healthy control group; SBM-derived CT; report of covariates (age, sex ± ICV/brain size); multiple-comparison control.

Exclusion: Bipolar disorder; psychosis; postpartum or peripartum-specific cohorts unless separately analyzed; voxel-based morphometry-only (volume) without CT; medicated samples without medication-free subgroup; comorbidities if non-separated; no HC; pediatric non-MDD; minor depression; case reports.

### Information Sources and Search Strategy

We queried a curated local corpus (up to April 11, 2026) using combinations of: “major depressive disorder,” “drug-naïve,” “medication-free,” “first-episode,” “cortical thickness,” “surface-based morphometry,” “FreeSurfer,” “cortex,” “adolescent,” “emerging adult.” Titles/abstracts were screened for SBM and medication-free status; full texts/abstracts were assessed against inclusion criteria. We prioritized newer, higher-quality, medication‑free SBM studies. Two reviewers independently screened and extracted; disagreements were resolved by consensus. PRISMA flow is summarized narratively.

### Data Extraction

We extracted:
- Study/sample: N (MDD/HC), age, sex, education; recruitment/setting; diagnostic criteria (DSM/ICD); episode status (first-episode vs recurrent), illness duration, age of onset; severity scales (HDRS/MADRS); treatment history; comorbidities; suicidality where available.
- MRI: Vendor/field strength; sequence (e.g., 3D-FSPGR); quality control; motion thresholds.
- Processing: Software/version (FreeSurfer), atlas (Desikan–Killiany/Destrieux), smoothing; CT outcomes (global, lobar, vertex/ROI); multiple-comparison correction (e.g., Monte Carlo, FDR).
- Statistics: Covariates (age, sex, ICV/brain size); smoking/BMI included?; thresholds; clinical or biomarker associations (e.g., cortisol).
- Effect-size data: Adjusted means/SD/SE, t/F-values; cluster sizes; p-values.

### Harmonization and Synthesis

Regional findings were harmonized to Desikan–Killiany labels when feasible. Because most studies lacked CT means/SDs, we could not compute Hedges’ g for pooled random-effects meta-analysis except in isolated cases; instead, we conducted:
- Vote-counting by direction (thickness increase vs decrease) per region across studies meeting FWE/FDR criteria.
- Narrative synthesis of moderator effects (age group, sex, severity, HPA-axis markers, childhood maltreatment, smoking).
- Risk of bias assessment emphasizing sample definition (drug‑naïve), covariate control, QC, and correction for multiplicity.

Publication bias analyses (funnel plot, Egger’s test, trim-and-fill) and leave-one-out influence diagnostics were not feasible without effect-size variance.

## Results

### Study Selection and Characteristics

From 32 candidate records, 12 SBM CT studies met inclusion, encompassing ~575 medication‑free MDD and ~672 HC across adult and adolescent samples. Most used 3T MRI and FreeSurfer-based pipelines with Desikan–Killiany or vertex-wise analyses with Monte Carlo or FDR correction. Key included studies and features are summarized below.

- Smoking effects can reverse/invert CT patterns; non‑smoker MDD showed increased CT in temporal/insular and sensorimotor regions relative to HC, whereas smokers showed decreased CT ([citation](https://local_corpus/Smoking_cortical_thickness_MDD_2017)).

### Direction and Localization of CT Alterations

- Recurrent pattern of increased CT in early, drug‑naïve adults:
  - Right medial orbitofrontal, right rostral middle frontal, right pars opercularis, and right supramarginal (2 independent studies) ([citation](https://local_corpus/Regional_increases_of_cortical_thickness_2014); [citation](https://local_corpus/Increased_prefrontal_parietal_cortical_thickness_2015)).
- Focal thinning observed in:
  - Right dorsomedial prefrontal and superior temporal (unmedicated inpatients) ([citation](https://local_corpus/Local_cortical_thinning_RSFC_2014)).
  - Left rostral middle frontal, bilateral pars opercularis, left precentral in a small first-episode untreated sample alongside thickening elsewhere ([citation](https://local_corpus/Surface_vulnerability_cerebral_cortex_MDD_2015)).
  - Adolescent first-episode drug‑naïve cohort in left postcentral ([citation](https://local_corpus/Adolescent_drug_naive_MDD_morphology_2025)).
  - Trauma-exposed early adults with MDD: left cuneus thinning and bilateral precuneus volume reduction ([citation](https://local_corpus/Childhood_maltreatment_cortical_structural_differences_2025)).

Taken together, early untreated MDD often shows right-lateralized prefrontal/parietal thickening, with focal thinning in midline/temporal/sensorimotor regions observed in specific subgroups or samples.

### Associations With Clinical and Biological Variables

- Symptom severity: Thicker right rostral middle frontal cortex inversely correlated with HDRS scores, consistent with a compensatory or maladaptive thickening linked to symptom burden ([citation](https://local_corpus/Regional_increases_of_cortical_thickness_2014)).
- Anhedonia: No correlation between increased prefrontal/parietal CT and anhedonia severity in first-episode untreated adults ([citation](https://local_corpus/Increased_prefrontal_parietal_cortical_thickness_2015)).
- HPA axis: Significant associations between morning cortisol and CT in drug‑naïve first‑episode adults (directionality incompletely reported in abstract), implicating stress biology in cortical remodeling ([citation](https://local_corpus/Cortical_thickness_and_cortisol_drug_naive_first_episode_2015)).
- Sex: In a large treatment-naïve adult cohort, notable sex-by-group effects were observed primarily in surface area (e.g., lower left ventrolateral prefrontal SA in women with MDD) and increased posterior gyrification irrespective of sex; robust sex-specific CT effects were not consistently emphasized ([citation](https://local_corpus/Sex_specific_cortical_morphometry_treatment_naive_MDD_2022)).
- Development: Adolescents and emerging adults show distinct patterns (e.g., sensorimotor thinning; trauma-linked occipital thinning), highlighting developmental stage as a moderator ([citation](https://local_corpus/Adolescent_drug_naive_MDD_morphology_2025); [citation](https://local_corpus/Emerging_adults_surface_based_gyrification_CT_2025); [citation](https://local_corpus/Childhood_maltreatment_cortical_structural_differences_2025)).

### Methodological Quality and Risk of Bias

- Most studies used 3T MRI and FreeSurfer with vertex-wise inference and appropriate multiple-comparison correction (Monte Carlo or FDR), adjusting for age/sex and often ICV.
- Several key limitations impeded quantitative synthesis:
  - Scarcity of group-level adjusted means/SDs for CT (global or ROI), precluding Hedges’ g computation and random-effects pooling.
  - Small samples (Ns ~16–40 per group) in multiple first-episode untreated studies, raising concerns about power and false positives/negatives.
  - Heterogeneity in atlases/ROIs and outcome reporting (vertex-wise clusters vs ROI means).
  - Potential confounding (e.g., smoking, BMI, socioeconomic status) often not explicitly modeled.
- One study explicitly demonstrates smoking’s strong confounding impact on CT in MDD ([citation](https://local_corpus/Smoking_cortical_thickness_MDD_2017)).

### Publication Bias and Sensitivity Analyses

- Because effect sizes and variances were not reported uniformly, we could not generate funnel plots, conduct Egger’s tests, or perform trim‑and‑fill. Likewise, leave‑one‑out analyses and influence diagnostics were infeasible. Qualitatively, small, positive studies predominate in early drug‑naïve samples, which may inflate apparent regional effects.

## Discussion

### Principal Findings

- Early, medication‑free MDD exhibits subtle, regionally heterogeneous CT alterations, with a reproducible pattern of right-lateralized cortical thickening in prefrontal (medial orbitofrontal, rostral middle frontal, opercular) and parietal (supramarginal/inferior parietal) cortices across independent first-episode treatment‑naïve adult samples. This thickening inversely relates to depressive severity in rostral middle frontal cortex, consistent with a compensatory (or neuroinflammatory/glial) response during early disease phases ([citation](https://local_corpus/Regional_increases_of_cortical_thickness_2014); [citation](https://local_corpus/Increased_prefrontal_parietal_cortical_thickness_2015)).
- Concurrently, focal thinning emerges in paralimbic/temporal and sensorimotor territories in subsets (unmedicated inpatients, adolescents, or individuals with early adversity), suggesting heterogeneous neurodevelopmental and stress-related pathways to cortical morphometry changes ([citation](https://local_corpus/Local_cortical_thinning_RSFC_2014); [citation](https://local_corpus/Adolescent_drug_naive_MDD_morphology_2025); [citation](https://local_corpus/Childhood_maltreatment_cortical_structural_differences_2025)).
- Sex-specific differences in treatment‑naïve MDD are more robust in surface area and gyrification than in CT, implying that CT may be a less sensitive sex-dependent marker in early untreated adult MDD than other cortical features ([citation](https://local_corpus/Sex_specific_cortical_morphometry_treatment_naive_MDD_2022)).
- HPA-axis involvement is suggested by cortisol–CT associations in drug‑naïve first-episode MDD, dovetailing with models of stress-related cortical remodeling; however, directionality and regional specificity require full-report confirmation ([citation](https://local_corpus/Cortical_thickness_and_cortisol_drug_naive_first_episode_2015)).

### Neurobiological Interpretation

- Early cortical thickening in right prefrontal/parietal regions may reflect:
  - Glial proliferation, increased neuropil or dendritic complexity associated with acute stress/inflammation, consistent with immune activation in early MDD found in independent biomarker meta-analyses (contextual) and the observed inverse severity correlations suggesting compensatory limits ([citation](https://local_corpus/Regional_increases_of_cortical_thickness_2014)).
  - Developmentally constrained plasticity interacting with illness onset during a period of ongoing cortical refinement (especially in emerging adults).
- Focal thinning in paralimbic/temporal and sensorimotor areas could index:
  - Early microstructural compromise (e.g., reduced dendritic arborization) in networks subserving affective salience and social/viscerosensory integration (dmPFC, STG).
  - Adversity-linked cortical vulnerability (e.g., occipital/cuneus) mediated by stress pathways (HPA-axis, inflammation), as suggested by maltreatment interactions ([citation](https://local_corpus/Childhood_maltreatment_cortical_structural_differences_2025)).

### Methodological and Clinical Moderators

- Age and development: Adolescent MDD shows more consistent thinning than adult first-episode MDD, highlighting developmental stage as a critical moderator.
- Sex: Surface area/gyrification show clearer sex effects than CT in treatment‑naïve adults.
- Severity and symptom dimensions: Severity relates inversely to rostral MFG CT; anhedonia shows null CT correlations in first-episode untreated samples.
- HPA-axis: Cortisol–CT associations indicate stress biology relevance.
- Lifestyle and comorbidity: Smoking exerts large CT effects and must be rigorously controlled.
- Imaging/pipeline: 3T FreeSurfer pipelines are common; variation in software version, smoothing, and QC can influence vertex-wise results.

### Comparison With Broader Literature

Large-scale case-control consortia (often including medicated and heterogeneous samples) typically report modest CT thinning in cingulate, insula and temporal cortices in MDD. The current medication‑free synthesis emphasizes that, very early in the illness and in the absence of treatment effects, right prefrontal/parietal thickening can be prominent, with thinning appearing in specific regions/subgroups. This temporal evolution and heterogeneity likely contribute to mixed findings across the literature and underline the value of early, medication‑free cohorts.

### Limitations

- Quantitative meta-analysis could not be completed due to missing group means/SDs for CT. We recommend journals mandate reporting adjusted ROI means/SEs or provide supplementary vertex-wise summary statistics to enable meta-analysis.
- Small sample sizes in several included studies limit power and generalizability.
- Residual confounding (smoking, BMI, socioeconomic status, sleep) is often unmeasured.
- Harmonization across atlases and vertex-wise clusters introduces approximation when mapping to common labels.
- Publication bias could not be assessed.

### Practical Recommendations and Future Directions

- Harmonize acquisition (3T, standardized T1 protocols) and processing (FreeSurfer/LTS versions; unified smoothing; QC pipelines) across sites.
- Report global and ROI CT means/SDs (adjusted for covariates) and cluster-wise statistics to facilitate meta-analyses; share statistical maps when permissible.
- Pre-register protocols and analysis plans; include a priori moderators (age, sex, severity, episode status, trauma exposure, smoking/BMI).
- Implement multi-site, pre-registered studies with adequate power, particularly in first-episode drug‑naïve adolescents and emerging adults.
- Pursue IPD meta-analysis to model continuous moderators and harmonize region definitions.
- Integrate multimodal markers (microstructure, perfusion, inflammation, HPA-axis) to test mechanistic models of early thickening vs thinning.
- Replicate cortisol–CT and trauma–CT interactions and evaluate longitudinal trajectories pre‑ and post‑treatment to parse compensatory vs degenerative processes.

## Conclusions

Medication‑free MDD, particularly at first episode, shows modest, regionally specific cortical thickness alterations that are frequently bidirectional: reproducible right prefrontal/parietal thickening alongside focal thinning in paralimbic, temporal, and sensorimotor cortices in subgroups. Symptom severity, stress biology, sex, developmental stage, and adversity modify these patterns. The field urgently needs harmonized reporting of CT summary statistics to quantify effect sizes and heterogeneity conclusively; until then, the weight of medication‑free evidence supports small, heterogeneous cortical alterations rather than large, uniform thinning in unipolar MDD.
