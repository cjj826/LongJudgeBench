# Influences on Engagement With Synchronous Remote Weight Management Interventions for Adults With Excess Weight: A COM‑B and TDF–Informed Synthesis

## Background and rationale

Engagement is a critical determinant of effectiveness for behaviorally oriented weight management programs. As health systems increasingly deploy synchronous (real‑time) remote delivery (eg, video or telephone) to expand access and reduce burden, understanding what helps or hinders engagement for adults living with excess weight is essential for implementation, equity, and outcomes. A 2025 systematic review specifically examined barriers and facilitators to engagement among adults with BMI ≥27.5 kg/m² participating in synchronous, remote weight management interventions, using inductive thematic synthesis mapped to the Capability, Opportunity, Motivation–Behavior (COM‑B) model and the Theoretical Domains Framework (TDF) (eg, knowledge, skills, social influences) (A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025). Complementary evidence from telehealth weight management and allied domains (eg, synchronous group lifestyle interventions; synchronous motivational interviewing; patient perspectives on GP teleconsultations) helps triangulate and contextualize the determinants identified in the review (Design and Methods of a Synchronous Online MI Intervention for Weight Management, 2016; Weight loss in videoconference and in-person iDiet…, 2017; Patients’ perspective about synchronous teleconsultation with a general practitioner…, 2025).

This evidence synthesis integrates findings from the 2025 review and related high‑relevance studies, organized using COM‑B and TDF to deliver actionable insights for intervention design, implementation, and policy.

## Scope and methods (overview)

- Population: Adults with excess weight (BMI ≥27.5 kg/m²).
- Interventions: Synchronous, real‑time remote weight management (eg, telephone or videoconferencing groups or one‑to‑one sessions).
- Outcomes of interest: Barriers and facilitators to “engagement” (uptake, attendance, sustained participation, active involvement).
- Methods: The primary 2025 review conducted a 12‑database search (inception to Oct 2023), performed narrative synthesis with inductive thematic analysis, and deductively mapped influences to COM‑B and TDF (A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025).

Given that full quantitative details (eg, counts of included studies) are not reported in the accessible abstract, we emphasize convergent themes corroborated by related synchronous or near‑equivalent telehealth evidence where relevant and recent.

## Detailed findings by COM‑B component

### Psychological capability

- Digital and health literacy: Participants who lack familiarity with video platforms, secure links, or basic troubleshooting are more likely to disengage. Brief, structured onboarding and simple platform choices mitigate this barrier ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]). In synchronous primary care teleconsultation evidence, clear instructions and streamlined pathways improved perceived usability and satisfaction, reinforcing the need for upfront guidance (Patients’ perspective about synchronous teleconsultation…, 2025).
- Cognitive load and session design: Complex instructions, lengthy didactics, or dense slides reduce attention and recall. Conversational formats, limited topics per session, and repetition support memory and learning ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]).

### Physical capability

- Functional limitations: Joint pain, mobility constraints, or incapacity to perform live demonstrations may reduce confidence and willingness to participate. Adapting demonstrations (eg, seated options) and offering audio‑only participation lower barriers (Patients’ perspective about synchronous teleconsultation…, 2025).
- Self‑monitoring skills: Some participants struggle to set up connected scales or trackers in real time. Coach‑assisted setup outside live sessions helps sustain in‑session focus ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]).

### Physical opportunity

- Technology reliability and access: Connectivity issues (bandwidth, device sharing in households), platform complexity, and authentication hurdles impede joining or staying connected. Equivalence between phone and video modalities as a backup strategy can prevent missed sessions ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]; Patients’ perspective about synchronous teleconsultation…, 2025).
- Timing and convenience: Flexible scheduling (evening/lunch), short session durations (~30–60 minutes), and time‑zone sensitive calendars are associated with higher attendance, paralleling favorable retention in videoconference‑delivered group programs (Weight loss in videoconference and in-person iDiet…, 2017).
- Privacy and environment: Lack of a private space exacerbates stigma concerns and reduces sharing during live sessions. Normalizing cameras‑off or audio‑only participation and providing asynchronous summaries can accommodate household constraints ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]).

### Social opportunity

- Therapeutic alliance and group dynamics: Participants often report concerns about rapport and reduced social presence online. Evidence from weight‑loss groups shows similar short‑term weight loss in videoconference vs in‑person delivery when skilled facilitation and small group sizes are used, suggesting that social opportunity can be preserved with appropriate design (Weight loss in videoconference and in-person iDiet…, 2017).
- Cultural tailoring and peer support: Engagement improves when facilitators reflect participants’ cultural/linguistic context and when programs incorporate structured peer support, accountability, and recognition of sociocultural norms relevant to food, exercise, and body weight ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]).

### Reflective motivation

- Beliefs about efficacy: Skepticism that “remote doesn’t work” reduces initial uptake. Early demonstration of benefit (eg, progress dashboards, testimonials) and personalized outcomes can shift beliefs (Design and Methods of a Synchronous Online MI…, 2016).
- Goal clarity and relevance: Personalized goal setting (eg, SMART targets) and aligning content with personal values (eg, energy for caregiving, symptom relief) increase perceived relevance and sustained participation ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]).
- Competing priorities: Work, caregiving, and health comorbidities compete for time and attention. Programs that offer flexible attendance and catch‑up options retain participants better (Patients’ perspective about synchronous teleconsultation…, 2025).

### Automatic motivation

- Affective experience and shame: Concerns about appearance on camera, or fear of judgment during weigh‑ins, can deter participation. Facilitators can normalize cameras‑off choices, emphasize non‑scale victories, and cultivate a nonjudgmental climate ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]).
- Reinforcement and accountability: Regular positive reinforcement, recognition of small wins, and structured accountability (eg, weekly check‑ins) increase habit formation and sustained attendance, consistent with MI‑informed synchronous support (Design and Methods of a Synchronous Online MI…, 2016).

## Synchronous modality–specific insights

- Videoconferencing vs telephone: Video may enhance nonverbal cues and group cohesion but increases bandwidth demands and camera‑related discomfort. Telephone parity reduces access barriers and sustains engagement when connectivity falters (Patients’ perspective about synchronous teleconsultation…, 2025). Evidence from hybrid comparisons suggests that, when delivered with fidelity, videoconference groups can achieve weight loss comparable to in‑person formats (Weight loss in videoconference and in-person iDiet…, 2017).
- Group size and facilitation skills: Small groups (≤8–10) and trained facilitators adept in online engagement, turn‑taking, and inclusive practices are consistently associated with higher engagement ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]; Weight loss in videoconference and in-person iDiet…, 2017).

## Equity and context considerations

- Digital divide: Rural residents, older adults, and lower‑income participants face distinct connectivity and device barriers. Real‑time telephone pathways, loaner devices, and low‑bandwidth platforms are critical equity strategies (Feasibility and acceptability of a rural, pragmatic, telemedicine‑delivered healthy lifestyle programme, 2019).
- Cultural relevance: Programs for Black or Latino adults have historically experienced lower engagement and outcomes in traditional formats; synchronous telehealth with culturally tailored content and options (eg, language concordance, family‑oriented goals) can mitigate disparities and improve acceptability (Examining preferences for weight loss interventions among Black men and women…, 2025; Examining the potential public health impact of Vida Sana…, 2025).
- Comorbidity and medication contexts: For remote programs incorporating pharmacotherapy (eg, GLP‑1/GIP agonists), engagement with synchronous coaching and monitoring supports adherence and augments outcomes, with sustained engagement associated with better weight loss trajectories in remote services (Semaglutide and Tirzepatide in a Remote Weight Management Program…, 2025).

## Implementation determinants (aligned with TDF/CFIR)

Cross‑cutting implementation factors from adjacent telehealth literature reinforce the engagement findings:

- Inner setting readiness: Clear workflows, protected time for facilitators, and reliable platforms are prerequisites for stable delivery (Patients’ perspective about synchronous teleconsultation…, 2025).
- Characteristics of individuals (staff): Training in virtual rapport, group facilitation, and troubleshooting increases confidence and reduces session disruption (Endocrinologists’ Experiences With Telehealth…, 2023).
- Process: Proactive onboarding, reminders, and rapid tech support address drop‑off risk during early sessions ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]).

## Quality and risk‑of‑bias appraisal (narrative)

- Study types and heterogeneity: The primary 2025 review used qualitative synthesis with COM‑B/TDF mapping; included studies likely varied in design (qualitative, mixed‑methods, pragmatic trials), intervention content, and populations—raising clinical and methodological heterogeneity. This constrains quantitative generalization but strengthens transferability of convergent qualitative themes.
- Selection and reporting biases: Engagement factors are often reported in completer subgroups; those disengaging early may be underrepresented, potentially underestimating barriers.
- Context dependence: Many studies derive from high‑income settings; generalizability to low‑resource contexts may be limited without adaptations (eg, telephone‑first, SMS reminders).
- Mitigation: Convergence of themes across multiple recent sources and modalities (weight management and primary care teleconsultations) increases confidence in key determinants; nonetheless, future studies should apply standardized engagement measures and report on non‑attenders.

Overall, the certainty of evidence is moderate for central themes (tech reliability, facilitator skill, flexible scheduling, culturally responsive design) and lower for nuanced subgroup effects due to limited comparative data.

## Actionable recommendations for design and delivery

- Reduce cognitive and technical burden
  - Provide a pre‑program “tech check” and simple “how‑to” guides; standardize platforms; automate calendar invites and reminders ([A systematic review of influences on engagement…, 2025]).
- Build capability and confidence
  - Offer digital literacy support and coach‑assisted setup for devices; start with low‑stakes sessions to boost self‑efficacy (Patients’ perspective about synchronous teleconsultation…, 2025).
- Maximize physical opportunity
  - Ensure telephone equivalence when video fails; schedule at accessible times; keep sessions concise (30–60 minutes); embed rapid tech support.
- Strengthen social opportunity
  - Train facilitators in online rapport and inclusive group management; keep groups small; match facilitators/participants by language and, where possible, culture (Weight loss in videoconference and in-person iDiet…, 2017; Examining the potential public health impact of Vida Sana…, 2025).
- Enhance reflective and automatic motivation
  - Use motivational interviewing principles; set personalized SMART goals; highlight early wins and non‑scale outcomes; normalize cameras‑off to reduce shame (Design and Methods of a Synchronous Online MI…, 2016).
- Equity‑first implementation
  - Provide phone‑first pathways, data subsidies or loaner devices; tailor content to cultural foodways and social norms; facilitate family involvement where desired (Examining preferences for weight loss interventions among Black men and women…, 2025; Feasibility of a rural telemedicine lifestyle programme, 2019).
- Fidelity and monitoring
  - Track engagement (attendance, participation) and reasons for missed sessions; collect rapid feedback to iterate formats; report disaggregated engagement by gender, age, race/ethnicity, and socioeconomic status.

## Research gaps and future directions

- Comparative effectiveness of synchronous modalities: Rigorous head‑to‑head trials comparing telephone vs videoconference for engagement and outcomes in diverse populations are needed (ongoing non‑inferiority trials comparing synchronous vs asynchronous may inform modality trade‑offs; protocol evidence suggests feasibility).
- Standardized engagement metrics: Agreement on core engagement measures (uptake, attendance, active participation) would enable meta‑analytic synthesis and equity analyses.
- Equity‑focused implementation trials: Pragmatic trials testing device/data support, phone‑first approaches, and culturally tailored content with disaggregated outcomes are priorities.
- Mechanisms of engagement: Mediation analyses to examine how alliance, self‑efficacy, and social support drive adherence and outcomes in synchronous formats.

## Conclusion

Across recent evidence, engagement with synchronous, remote weight management for adults with excess weight is shaped by modifiable determinants spanning capability (digital literacy and clear session design), opportunity (reliable, flexible access; small groups; culturally responsive facilitation), and motivation (beliefs about efficacy; supportive accountability; stigma‑reducing practices). Programs that proactively address technology barriers, invest in facilitator skills for online rapport, offer flexible schedules and telephone equivalence, and emphasize early, personalized wins are more likely to retain participants and realize effectiveness at scale. Embedding equity‑first design—through phone‑ready pathways, device/data supports, and culturally tailored content—appears essential to avoid widening disparities as remote services expand.
