# Effects of Time-Restricted Eating Combined with Exercise on Body Composition in Adults: An Evidence Synthesis

## Abstract
Time-restricted eating (TRE) is frequently paired with structured exercise to improve body composition, but its added value over unrestricted eating when exercise is held constant remains debated. Synthesizing the most recent meta-analytic and randomized controlled trial (RCT) evidence, TRE combined with aerobic, resistance, or concurrent training produces small but statistically significant reductions in fat mass (FM) and body fat percentage (BF%) compared with unrestricted eating alongside exercise, while generally preserving fat-free mass (FFM). Effects appear larger in adults with overweight/obesity and when interventions last 8–12 weeks, with preservation (and sometimes modest gain) of FFM most consistently observed in concurrent training programs among previously inactive adults. In trained populations and hypercaloric contexts, TRE maintains FFM but may constrain training volume and hypertrophy potential. Overall, the signal favors TRE+exercise for adiposity reduction with minimal risk of lean mass loss, but effects are modest, heterogeneous across populations and protocols, and often confounded by spontaneous energy restriction under TRE. The certainty of evidence is moderate for FM/BF% and low-to-moderate for FFM. Future trials should tightly control energy intake, extend duration, and stratify by sex, training status, and TRE window timing.

## Background
TRE restricts the daily eating window (often 8–10 hours) without mandating macronutrient composition or explicit calorie targets. Exercise training is a cornerstone of obesity management and body composition optimization. Whether TRE confers additional body composition benefits—beyond those provided by exercise—has practical implications for athletes and the general population. A recent systematic review and meta-analysis directly targeted this combined paradigm, supplemented by multiple RCTs across diverse populations (inactive adults with overweight/obesity, recreationally trained runners, resistance-trained men, and free-living middle-aged women). This report integrates those findings to provide a consolidated conclusion focused on FM, BF%, and FFM outcomes.

## Methods Summary of the Available Evidence
- The most recent meta-analysis (15 studies; n = 338; up to May 2023) pooled trials comparing TRE versus unrestricted eating among adults engaged in aerobic, resistance, or concurrent training, using random-effects models to estimate standardized effects on FM and BF%. Across trials, exercise exposure was broadly comparable between groups; dietary intake was variably controlled, with some trials using ad libitum TRE and others using matched or hypocaloric diets ([Effects of time-restricted eating with exercise on body composition in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](https://local-corpus/effects-of-time-restricted-eating-with-exercise-2025)).
- Additional RCTs since 2020 evaluated TRE during resistance, aerobic, or concurrent training across varying windows (typically 8 h) and durations (4–12 weeks), in both trained and untrained participants and in caloric deficit, maintenance, or surplus contexts. Collectively, these trials inform moderators such as exercise modality, TRE window length, energy control, intervention duration, training status, sex, and BMI category.

## Results

### Pooled Evidence (Meta-analysis)
- The 2025 meta-analysis reported small but statistically significant pooled reductions favoring TRE+exercise over unrestricted eating+exercise:
  - FM: effect size (ES) −0.20 (95% CI −0.28 to −0.13; p < 0.001).
  - BF%: ES −0.23 (95% CI −0.35 to −0.11; p < 0.001).
  - The abstract truncation did not report FFM explicitly; however, patterns across included trials suggest FFM was generally preserved with TRE when paired with structured exercise ([Effects of time-restricted eating with exercise on body composition in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](https://local-corpus/effects-of-time-restricted-eating-with-exercise-2025)).

Interpretation: TRE+exercise confers a small additional loss of body fat relative to unrestricted eating+exercise. Though numerically modest, these effects are consistent and statistically robust.

- FM/BF%: TRE+exercise frequently yields additional FM/BF% reductions relative to habitual or unrestricted eating with equal training exposure.
- FFM: Most trials report preservation of FFM under TRE+exercise; concurrent training in overweight/obese, inactive adults shows potential FFM gains. In trained individuals or under hypercaloric conditions, TRE does not reliably enhance FFM and may reduce training volume, potentially attenuating hypertrophy.

### Subgroup and Moderator Signals
- Exercise modality:
  - Concurrent training in overweight/obese adults: favorable for FM reduction and lean mass gain under TRE, plausibly via synergistic anabolic stimuli and improved adherence within structured programs ([Time-restricted eating and concurrent exercise training..., 2021](https://local-corpus/tre-concurrent-exercise-2021)).
  - Resistance training in trained adults: FM decreases similarly across TRE and non-TRE; FFM preserved in both. When calories are matched or in surplus, TRE provides little advantage for FFM and may slightly hinder training volume or explosive performance metrics ([Four Weeks of TRF + RT..., 2020](https://local-corpus/four-weeks-trf-rt-2020); [Effect of TRE and RT on High-Speed Strength..., 2023](https://local-corpus/tre-rt-high-speed-2023); [Hypercaloric TRE during RT, 2025](https://local-corpus/hypercaloric-tre-rt-2025)).
  - Aerobic training in women with overweight/obesity: TRE plus aerobic exercise appears additive for FM loss and waist reduction relative to either alone ([Flexible time-restricted eating combined with exercise..., 2025](https://local-corpus/flex-tre-ex-women-2025)).

- TRE window length and timing:
  - Most effective protocols used an 8-hour eating window (16:8). Evidence directly contrasting different windows within exercise trials is sparse; however, non-exercise TRE trials suggest that earlier windows may confer metabolic advantages. Within athletic cohorts, 8 h windows consistently reduce FM without harming FFM or performance over 4–8 weeks ([Four Weeks of 16:8 TRF..., 2021](https://local-corpus/trf-endurance-runners-2021); [TRE in Recreational Runners, 2024](https://local-corpus/tre-recreational-runners-2024)).

- Energy intake control:
  - When calories and protein are matched between TRE and control, differences in FM are small to null, indicating much of TRE’s advantage may stem from spontaneous energy restriction and/or improved alignment of intake with circadian rhythms. Preservation of FFM hinges on adequate protein (≥1.6–1.8 g/kg/d) and progressive resistance training ([Four Weeks TRF + RT under caloric deficit, 2020](https://local-corpus/four-weeks-trf-rt-2020); [High-Protein TRE + RT in women, 2025](https://local-corpus/high-protein-tre-rt-women-2025)).

- Intervention duration:
  - Short-term (4 weeks) studies frequently show small FM decreases (~0.5–1.0 kg) with TRE and stable FFM. Twelve-week, free-living trials in women demonstrate larger, clinically relevant FM reductions when TRE is combined with exercise ([Flexible time-restricted eating combined with exercise..., 2025](https://local-corpus/flex-tre-ex-women-2025)).

- Training status:
  - Overweight/obese, previously inactive individuals exhibit the clearest additive benefits on FM and lean mass with TRE+concurrent training.
  - Trained individuals typically maintain FFM and performance while reducing FM modestly; in surplus conditions, TRE may not optimize muscle accretion ([Hypercaloric TRE during RT, 2025](https://local-corpus/hypercaloric-tre-rt-2025)).

- Sex and BMI category:
  - Women with overweight/obesity in free-living settings benefit meaningfully in FM and central adiposity from combining TRE with exercise over 12 weeks ([Flexible time-restricted eating combined with exercise..., 2025](https://local-corpus/flex-tre-ex-women-2025)).
  - Data in men and mixed trained cohorts also show FM decreases without FFM loss across 4–8 weeks ([Endurance runners, 2021–2024](https://local-corpus/trf-endurance-runners-2021); [https://local-corpus/tre-runners-cardiometabolic-2023](https://local-corpus/tre-runners-cardiometabolic-2023); [https://local-corpus/tre-recreational-runners-2024](https://local-corpus/tre-recreational-runners-2024)).

- Caloric matching:
  - Trials with matched caloric deficits and protein show no meaningful FFM disadvantage with TRE and largely similar FM reductions, underscoring the importance of dietary control in interpreting TRE’s unique contribution ([Four Weeks TRF + RT under caloric deficit, 2020](https://local-corpus/four-weeks-trf-rt-2020)).

### Consistency With Related Syntheses
A 2025 meta-analysis focusing on overweight/obese women (not limited to exercise) found TRE significantly reduced body weight and fasting insulin, supporting the notion that TRE facilitates energy deficit and metabolic improvement; effects on body composition components varied by protocol and control condition ([Effects of time-restricted eating on body composition and metabolic parameters in overweight and obese women, 2025](https://local-corpus/tre-women-meta-2025)). While not directly the same PICO, these findings align with the exercise literature that much of TRE’s adiposity benefit is driven by modest reductions in energy intake.

## Heterogeneity, Bias, and Certainty
- Heterogeneity: The 2025 exercise-focused meta-analysis used random-effects modeling, indicating expected diversity in populations, modalities, windows, and energy control. Although exact I²/τ² were not reported in the abstract, cross-trial differences in training status and dietary control likely contributed materially to heterogeneity ([Effects of time-restricted eating with exercise..., 2025](https://local-corpus/effects-of-time-restricted-eating-with-exercise-2025)).
- Publication bias: Formal assessments (funnel plots, Egger’s tests) were not available in the abstract. Small-study effects cannot be excluded; several trials are modest in sample size and short duration.
- Risk of bias: Exercise trials frequently manage training well but vary in allocation concealment, adherence verification for eating windows, and blinding (often infeasible). Body composition assessments are generally robust when using DXA; however, some studies used bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA), introducing measurement variability.
- Certainty (GRADE, narrative): 
  - FM and BF% reductions with TRE+exercise: moderate certainty (consistent direction of effect, precise pooled estimates, but short durations and heterogeneity limit upgrading).
  - FFM preservation: low-to-moderate certainty (consistent maintenance in most trials; one RCT shows gain in concurrent training; limited by short durations, trained vs untrained differences, and protocol variability).

## Interpretation and Practical Implications
- For adults engaging in structured exercise, adopting an 8-hour eating window modestly augments fat loss versus unrestricted eating, with general preservation of FFM—especially when protein is adequate and resistance or concurrent training is included.
- The incremental benefit of TRE appears larger in previously inactive adults with overweight/obesity and when combined with concurrent training, where lean mass may increase.
- In trained individuals seeking maximal hypertrophy, strict TRE may not offer advantages under hypercaloric conditions and could reduce training volume; careful planning (e.g., distributing protein across the window, aligning post-exercise feeding, considering slightly longer windows) may mitigate potential downsides.
- Where calories and protein are matched, body composition differences narrow, implying TRE’s practical benefit often derives from a mild spontaneous energy deficit and possibly circadian alignment.

## Limitations of the Evidence Base
- Most trials are short (4–12 weeks), with small samples. Long-term sustainability and durability of FFM outcomes remain uncertain.
- Dietary intake is variably measured and often not tightly controlled, complicating attribution of effects to timing per se.
- Few trials stratify or power analyses by sex, menopausal status, or training history.
- Timing of TRE (early vs late windows) relative to exercise sessions is insufficiently explored in controlled designs.

## Conclusion
Based on the current body of evidence—including a 2025 random-effects meta-analysis and multiple RCTs—time-restricted eating combined with exercise produces small, statistically significant improvements in fat mass and body fat percentage versus unrestricted eating with matched exercise, while generally preserving fat-free mass. The additive benefit is most evident in adults with overweight/obesity engaged in concurrent training over 8–12 weeks. In trained populations, TRE reliably lowers fat mass without compromising lean mass over short time frames, but it may not optimize hypertrophy under surplus conditions. Overall, TRE+exercise is a reasonable, low-complexity strategy to enhance adiposity reductions with minimal lean mass risk; expectations should remain modest, and protocols should be tailored to goals, training status, and protein targets.
