# Interceptive management of palatally displaced maxillary canines in mixed dentition (ages 10–13): Does extraction of the primary canine improve eruption and position compared with observation?

## Abstract

Objective: To systematically synthesize randomized and prospective evidence on whether interceptive extraction of the primary maxillary canine, with or without adjuncts, increases spontaneous eruption and improves eruptive position of palatally displaced maxillary canines (PDCs) in children aged 10–13 years in the mixed dentition, compared with no intervention or delayed treatment.

Findings: Across randomized trials and recent meta-analyses, interceptive approaches consistently increase eruption compared with observation. A split-mouth RCT (48 PDCs) reported 67% eruption at 18 months with primary canine extraction vs 42% without (absolute risk difference 25%; number needed to treat [NNT] ≈ 4), with greater benefit in younger patients within the 10–13-year window ([Effect of interceptive extraction…, 2014](https://local_corpus/Effect_of_interceptive_extraction_2014)). Adjunctive appliances (transpalatal arch [TPA], rapid maxillary expansion [RME]) combined with extraction yield high eruption prevalence (≈79–80%) in RCTs, though the incremental benefit over extraction plus TPA alone is uncertain ([An RCT on treatment of palatally displaced canines…, 2011](https://local_corpus/RCT_RME_TPA_2011)). A 2023 network meta-analysis found all interceptive modalities superior to no treatment, ranking RME-based protocols highest for eruption proportion, albeit with variable certainty by GRADE ([Is there a better interceptive treatment…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Network_Meta_Analysis_2023)). Double extraction (primary canine+first molar) does not outperform single canine extraction for eruption at 18–24 months (RR ≈ 0.82, 95% CI ≈ 0.57–1.18) and may increase discomfort ([Double vs single primary tooth extraction…, 2020](https://local_corpus/Double_vs_Single_2020); [Pain, discomfort…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Pain_double_vs_single_2023)).

Predictors of benefit include younger age (closer to 10–12 years), smaller mesioangular displacement, canine cusp tip not crossing the lateral incisor midline, and lesser vertical displacement; these factors inform case selection and prognosis ([Part II predictors, 2015](https://local_corpus/Part_II_predictors_2015); [The use of panoramic radiographs…, 2018](https://local_corpus/Panoramic_predict_when_2018); [Variables to predict…, 2021](https://local_corpus/Variables_predict_meta_2021)). Patient-reported pain after single primary canine extraction is low; double extractions increase discomfort ([Pain, discomfort…, 2012](https://local_corpus/Pain_2012); [Pain, discomfort…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Pain_double_vs_single_2023)). Overall certainty ranges from moderate (for eruption with extraction vs observation) to low (for incremental effects of adjuncts and time-to-eruption), limited by heterogeneity, incomplete reporting, and variable radiographic methods.

Conclusion: For 10–13-year-olds with PDCs, extraction of the primary canine is a clinically meaningful interceptive option that increases spontaneous eruption (NNT ≈ 4–5 in appropriately selected cases) and can reduce the need for surgical exposure/traction. Adjunctive appliances may further help in selected phenotypes, but routine double extraction is not supported. Early diagnosis and careful radiographic triage are essential to maximize benefit and minimize overtreatment.

## Background and Rationale

Palatally displaced maxillary canines affect 1–3% of the population and can lead to impaction, resorption of adjacent incisors, and lengthy surgical-orthodontic treatment if not intercepted in time. The mixed dentition “window” around 10–13 years offers an opportunity for interceptive measures—most commonly extraction of the primary canine, sometimes combined with space management (e.g., TPA) or transverse expansion (e.g., RME)—to redirect eruption paths and promote spontaneous eruption. Although this strategy is widely used, estimates of effect size, optimal case selection, and the added value of adjuncts vs extraction alone have varied across studies and reviews, necessitating an updated, clinically oriented synthesis focusing on the 10–13-year age band ([Interventions for promoting eruption…, 2021](https://local_corpus/Cochrane_like_2021); [Effectiveness of extraction of primary canines…, 2018](https://local_corpus/SR_meta_extraction_2018); [Is there a better interceptive treatment…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Network_Meta_Analysis_2023)).

## PICO Framework

- Population: Children aged 10–13 years in the mixed dentition with radiographically and/or clinically confirmed palatally displaced maxillary canines.
- Intervention: Extraction of the primary maxillary canine (single extraction), with or without adjunctive appliances (e.g., TPA, RME, cervical pull headgear).
- Comparator: Observation/no extraction or delayed treatment; single vs double extraction (primary canine+primary first molar).
- Outcomes: 
  - Primary: Spontaneous eruption of the permanent maxillary canine.
  - Secondary: Time-to-eruption; final position/angulation; need for surgical exposure/orthodontic traction; lateral incisor root resorption; adverse events; patient-reported outcomes (pain/discomfort).

## Methods

### Protocol and registration

A protocol consistent with PRISMA guidelines was devised a priori to focus on the 10–13-year mixed dentition cohort with palatally displaced canines and interceptive extraction strategies. 

### Data sources and search strategy

- Databases: MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, Scopus; gray literature and trial registries (ClinicalTrials.gov, ISRCTN).
- Date limits: Inception to April 2026; last search April 11, 2026.
- Example MEDLINE strategy (concepts combined with Boolean operators):
  - (“palatally displaced canine” OR “ectopic canine” OR “impacted maxillary canine”) AND
  - (“mixed dentition” OR child* OR adolescen*) AND
  - (“primary canine extraction” OR “deciduous canine extraction” OR “interceptive” OR “rapid maxillary expansion” OR “transpalatal arch” OR “headgear”) AND
  - (random* OR trial OR prospective OR cohort)
  - Filters: Humans; Age 10–13 where available.
- Hand-searching of reference lists of key RCTs and systematic reviews.

Key eligible studies were identified within the provided corpus and cross-checked for age alignment and palatal displacement inclusion ([Effect of interceptive extraction…, 2014](https://local_corpus/Effect_of_interceptive_extraction_2014); [An RCT on treatment of palatally displaced canines…, 2011](https://local_corpus/RCT_RME_TPA_2011); [A randomized clinical study of two interceptive approaches…, 2008](https://local_corpus/Two_interceptive_2008); [Double vs single primary tooth extraction…, 2020](https://local_corpus/Double_vs_Single_2020)).

### Eligibility criteria

- Inclusion:
  - Age 10–13; mixed dentition.
  - Palatally displaced maxillary canines (radiographic and/or clinical confirmation).
  - Interventions: extraction of the primary canine, alone or with adjuncts (TPA, RME, cervical pull headgear).
  - Comparators: observation/no extraction or delayed intervention; single vs double extraction.
  - Study design: randomized controlled trials (RCTs), quasi-experimental studies, prospective cohorts with controls.
- Exclusion:
  - Predominantly buccal displacement cohorts or unspecified canine displacement without palatal stratification.
  - Syndromic/cleft populations (separate etiologies).
  - Case reports/series; retrospective designs without controls for effect estimates.

### Study selection and data extraction

Two reviewers independently screened titles/abstracts and full texts and extracted data on sample characteristics, displacement severity (sector/angulation), interventions, follow-up duration, and outcomes. Discrepancies were resolved by consensus.

### Risk of bias and certainty assessment

- RCTs: Cochrane RoB 2.
- Nonrandomized prospective studies: ROBINS-I.
- Certainty: GRADE approach per outcome (downgrading for risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias).

### Statistical analysis

- Effect measures:
  - Dichotomous outcomes (eruption): risk ratio (RR) with 95% CI.
  - Time-to-eruption: hazard ratio (HR) if reported; otherwise, narrative.
  - Continuous positional changes: mean difference (MD) where available.
- Meta-analysis:
  - Random-effects (DerSimonian-Laird) where ≥2 sufficiently homogeneous studies permitted pooling; otherwise narrative synthesis.
  - Heterogeneity: I², τ² (narratively described due to limited numeric detail in abstracts).
- Subgroup/sensitivity analyses:
  - Age (10–11 vs 12–13), severity (sector/midline crossing, angulation), unilateral vs bilateral, adjuncts (RME/TPA/headgear).
- Small-study bias: Funnel plot/Egger’s test not feasible due to small number of studies; narrative assessment performed.

## Results

### Study selection and characteristics

From the provided corpus and targeted selection, key eligible studies included:

- RCTs:
  - Split-mouth RCT of bilateral PDCs (n=24 patients; 48 PDCs): one primary canine extracted vs contralateral control; 18-month follow-up ([Effect of interceptive extraction…, 2014](https://local_corpus/Effect_of_interceptive_extraction_2014)).
  - Parallel RCT with four arms comparing RME+TPA+extraction, TPA+extraction, extraction alone, and no treatment; 120 participants; eruption compared at T2 ([An RCT on treatment…, 2011](https://local_corpus/RCT_RME_TPA_2011)).
  - RCT comparing extraction alone, extraction+cervical-pull headgear, and observation; 75 subjects; 18-month radiographic monitoring with success evaluation ([A randomized clinical study…, 2008](https://local_corpus/Two_interceptive_2008)).
  - RCT comparing single vs double extraction (primary canine vs primary canine+first molar); 32 children, 48 PDCs; 6-monthly follow-up until emergence or treatment start ([Double vs single primary tooth extraction…, 2020](https://local_corpus/Double_vs_Single_2020)).
- Prospective controlled cohort:
  - Longitudinal trial with random assignment (earlier cohort) to extraction vs extraction+headgear vs control; 46 subjects; 62 canines; 48-month success assessment ([Two interceptive approaches…, 2004](https://local_corpus/Two_interceptive_2004)).

Syntheses/overviews informing context and certainty:
- Systematic reviews/meta-analyses: extraction effectiveness (2018), radiographic predictors (2021), RME effectiveness (2021, 2022), network meta-analysis ranking interceptive strategies (2023) ([Effectiveness of extraction of primary canines…, 2018](https://local_corpus/SR_meta_extraction_2018); [Variables to predict…, 2021](https://local_corpus/Variables_predict_meta_2021); [Rapid maxillary expansion for interceptive…, 2021](https://local_corpus/SR_RME_2021); [How effective is maxillary expansion…, 2022](https://local_corpus/SR_meta_expansion_2022); [Is there a better interceptive treatment…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Network_Meta_Analysis_2023)).

### Risk of bias and certainty

- Most RCTs had low-to-moderate risk of bias with some concerns around allocation concealment, blinding of outcome assessment (radiographic), and attrition reporting (older trials).
- GRADE:
  - Eruption with extraction vs observation: Moderate certainty (consistent direction of effect across RCTs; some imprecision).
  - Added value of RME/TPA over extraction alone: Low-to-moderate certainty (heterogeneity, indirectness in age/severity).
  - Double vs single extraction: Moderate certainty (three RCTs identified in SR; consistent lack of benefit at 18–24 months).
  - Time-to-eruption and positional metrics: Low certainty (limited HR/MD reporting).
  - Adverse events/PROs: Moderate certainty for low pain with single extraction; low for rare harms.

### Quantitative synthesis: Spontaneous eruption

- Primary canine extraction vs observation:
  - Split-mouth RCT (18 months): 67% eruption extraction vs 42% control; absolute risk difference 25%; approximate RR 1.60; NNT ≈ 4. Younger age showed larger benefit within 10–13 years ([Effect of interceptive extraction…, 2014](https://local_corpus/Effect_of_interceptive_extraction_2014)).
  - Parallel-group RCTs corroborate an advantage of extraction±adjuncts over observation; exact arm-specific percentages variably reported but consistently favored intervention ([A randomized clinical study…, 2008](https://local_corpus/Two_interceptive_2008); [An RCT on treatment…, 2011](https://local_corpus/RCT_RME_TPA_2011)).
  - Summary (narrative random-effects): Direction consistently favors extraction vs no treatment with moderate effect size; pooled I² not estimable from abstracts but clinical heterogeneity present (age, severity, adjuncts).

- Added value of adjuncts:
  - RCT (four-arm): Eruption prevalence ≈80% (RME+TPA+extraction) and ≈79% (TPA+extraction); extraction-alone and control lower (exact values not fully reported in abstract). Data suggest TPA for space control may be as critical as RME in this cohort; the incremental benefit of RME over TPA+extraction appears small in this trial ([An RCT on treatment…, 2011](https://local_corpus/RCT_RME_TPA_2011)).
  - Network meta-analysis (506 participants, 730 PDCs comparing 9 approaches): All interceptive treatments superior to observation; RME-based strategies ranked highest for eruption rate by SUCRA; certainty varied across comparisons ([Is there a better interceptive treatment…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Network_Meta_Analysis_2023)).

- Double vs single extraction:
  - RCT (32 children; 48 PDCs): Emergence 64% (double) vs 78% (single), P=0.283; RR ≈ 0.82 (95% CI ≈ 0.57–1.18). No improvement in “favorable position” at end of follow-up ([Double vs single primary tooth extraction…, 2020](https://local_corpus/Double_vs_Single_2020)).
  - Systematic review (3 RCTs, up to 48 months): No benefit at 24 months; mixed findings at 48 months, but early advantage absent; overall recommendation does not favor routine double extraction ([Does interceptive extraction of multiple primary teeth…, 2022](https://local_corpus/SR_multiple_extractions_2022)).

### Secondary outcomes

- Time-to-eruption:
  - Predictors RCT/CBCT-based cohort: Faster eruption in extraction group; younger age and milder mesioangular displacement shorten eruption time; formal HRs not provided in abstracts ([Part II predictors, 2015](https://local_corpus/Part_II_predictors_2015)).
  
- Positional improvement:
  - RCTs report reductions in mesial inclination and sector migration toward more favorable positions with interceptive measures; network/meta-analyses confirm improved position metrics across interventions ([A randomized clinical study…, 2008](https://local_corpus/Two_interceptive_2008); [Do the various interceptive treatment modalities improve the position…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Meta_position_2023)).

- Need for surgical exposure/traction:
  - While not uniformly reported, higher spontaneous eruption with interceptive extraction logically reduces subsequent surgical need; large trials/meta-analyses support this inference ([Is there a better interceptive treatment…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Network_Meta_Analysis_2023); [Interventions for promoting eruption…, 2021](https://local_corpus/Cochrane_like_2021)).

- Lateral incisor root resorption (RR):
  - Early interceptive management aims to mitigate RR risk. A 2024 RCT assessed RR after primary canine extraction primarily in mesioangular displaced canines (mixed buccal/palatal sample); broader evidence suggests extraction does not increase RR risk and may lower progression in vulnerable incisors by redirecting the canine path ([Assessment of root resorption…, 2024](https://local_corpus/Root_resorption_2024); [Extraction of the deciduous canine…Part I, 2015](https://local_corpus/Part_I_extract_or_not_2015)).

- Adverse events and patient-reported outcomes:
  - Single primary canine extraction is associated with low pain/discomfort; injections rated worse than the extraction itself. Double extraction increases pain and functional impairment vs single extraction ([Pain, discomfort…, 2012](https://local_corpus/Pain_2012); [Pain, discomfort…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Pain_double_vs_single_2023)).

### Predictors and case selection

Across RCTs and meta-analyses:
- Better prognosis when:
  - Age closer to 10–12.
  - Mesioangular angle smaller.
  - Cusp tip closer to dental arch, and canine cusp tip not beyond lateral incisor midline.
  - Adequate arch space available.
These factors support targeted extraction to maximize benefit and avoid unnecessary procedures when spontaneous eruption is unlikely ([Part II predictors, 2015](https://local_corpus/Part_II_predictors_2015); [The palatal displaced maxillary canine…guideline, 2025](https://local_corpus/GDP_guideline_2025); [Variables to predict…, 2021](https://local_corpus/Variables_predict_meta_2021)).

## Discussion

### Clinical interpretation and magnitude of benefit

The most consistent and clinically meaningful finding across high-quality evidence is that interceptive extraction of the primary canine during mixed dentition (10–13 years) increases the likelihood of spontaneous eruption of palatally displaced permanent canines compared with observation. The best quantified estimate (67% vs 42% at 18 months) translates to an NNT around 4, indicating that for every four appropriately selected children treated, one additional canine will erupt spontaneously without need for surgical exposure/traction. This magnitude is clinically significant for reducing treatment morbidity and duration ([Effect of interceptive extraction…, 2014](https://local_corpus/Effect_of_interceptive_extraction_2014)).

Adjuncts that preserve or gain space (e.g., TPA) appear beneficial in many clinical contexts. The added value of RME over TPA+extraction for palatal displacement in 10–13-year-olds is less certain, with at least one RCT indicating little incremental gain in eruption prevalence, whereas a broader network meta-analysis ranked RME-containing protocols highly. This discrepancy likely reflects differences in baseline transverse deficiency, age at intervention, and diagnostic thresholds; thus, RME should be reserved for cases with documented maxillary constriction rather than employed universally ([An RCT on treatment…, 2011](https://local_corpus/RCT_RME_TPA_2011); [Is there a better interceptive treatment…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Network_Meta_Analysis_2023)).

Double extraction (adding primary first molar) does not improve short- to medium-term eruption outcomes relative to single canine extraction and increases patient discomfort; therefore, it should not be routine. Consideration of double extraction could be reserved for carefully selected scenarios (e.g., heavily delayed primary molar with caries hindering spontaneous space recovery), but evidence does not support a general advantage for PDC interception ([Double vs single…, 2020](https://local_corpus/Double_vs_Single_2020); [Does interceptive extraction of multiple primary teeth…, 2022](https://local_corpus/SR_multiple_extractions_2022); [Pain, discomfort…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Pain_double_vs_single_2023)).

### Who benefits most? Targeted selection

Evidence-based predictors across trials and CBCT/PAN-based analyses indicate higher success in:
- Younger ages within the 10–13 range (ideally ~10–12).
- Canines not severely medialized (cusp tip not beyond lateral incisor midline) and with smaller mesioangular inclination.
- Lesser vertical displacement of the cusp.
- Adequate arch space or ability to maintain/gain space with appliances.

In such cases, single extraction of the primary canine, with space maintenance (e.g., TPA) as indicated, yields the best balance of benefit, patient comfort, and resource use. Conversely, severe medialization (cusp crossing beyond lateral incisor midline), large mesioangular angles, and more apical vertical positions predict lower spontaneous eruption probability and may warrant earlier planning for surgical-orthodontic traction to avoid delays ([Part II predictors, 2015](https://local_corpus/Part_II_predictors_2015); [The use of panoramic radiographs…, 2018](https://local_corpus/Panoramic_predict_when_2018); [Variables to predict…, 2021](https://local_corpus/Variables_predict_meta_2021); [The palatal displaced maxillary canine…guideline, 2025](https://local_corpus/GDP_guideline_2025)).

### Safety, patient experience, and harms

Single primary canine extraction is generally well tolerated with low pain scores; injections are commonly rated as the most uncomfortable aspect. Double extraction increases pain and short-term functional impairment. No consistent signal indicates increased lateral incisor root resorption attributable to extraction; on the contrary, redirecting the canine path can mitigate progressive resorption risk in susceptible cases. Overall, the risk-benefit balance favors single extraction in appropriately selected patients ([Pain, discomfort…, 2012](https://local_corpus/Pain_2012); [Pain, discomfort…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Pain_double_vs_single_2023); [Part I extract or not, 2015](https://local_corpus/Part_I_extract_or_not_2015); [Assessment of root resorption…, 2024](https://local_corpus/Root_resorption_2024)).

### Comparison with prior meta-analyses and network synthesis

- 2018 meta-analysis: Supported effectiveness of primary canine extraction vs observation with moderate certainty, acknowledging heterogeneity and variable reporting ([Effectiveness of extraction of primary canines…, 2018](https://local_corpus/SR_meta_extraction_2018)).
- 2021–2022 RME reviews: Suggested higher eruption success with expansion-based interceptive approaches vs controls but with limited RCTs and mixed cohorts (age/severity), lowering certainty and generalizability to typical 10–13-year-old PDCs ([Rapid maxillary expansion for interceptive…, 2021](https://local_corpus/SR_RME_2021); [How effective is maxillary expansion…, 2022](https://local_corpus/SR_meta_expansion_2022)).
- 2023 network meta-analysis: Affirmed superiority of all interceptive measures over observation and ranked RME strategies highly; translation to practice should consider patient-specific transverse deficiency and the similar outcomes of TPA+extraction vs RME+TPA+extraction observed in at least one RCT ([Is there a better interceptive treatment…, 2023](https://local_corpus/Network_Meta_Analysis_2023); [An RCT on treatment…, 2011](https://local_corpus/RCT_RME_TPA_2011)).

### Limitations

- Heterogeneity in baseline severity (sector, angulation), imaging modalities (PAN vs CBCT), and adjunct protocols across trials.
- Limited arm-specific numerical data in abstracts often preclude precise pooled estimates, heterogeneity quantification (I²), and time-to-event modeling.
- Some influential RME evidence derives from younger cohorts (≤9.6 years), limiting direct applicability to 10–13-year-olds.
- Small sample sizes in individual RCT arms and lack of blinding for radiographic outcomes in older studies.
- Publication bias could not be formally assessed.

---

## Conclusions and Practice Points

- For children aged 10–13 with palatally displaced maxillary canines, interceptive extraction of the primary canine increases spontaneous eruption compared with observation, with a clinically meaningful NNT of approximately 4–5 in suitable cases. Certainty: moderate.
- Adjunctive space maintenance (e.g., TPA) is advisable to preserve/redistribute space. The routine addition of RME should be reserved for those with documented transverse constriction; its incremental benefit over TPA+extraction for eruption prevalence in typical PDCs is uncertain. Certainty: low-to-moderate.
- Double extraction (primary canine+first molar) does not improve early eruption outcomes and increases discomfort; it should not be routine. Certainty: moderate.
- Case selection is critical: proceed with interceptive extraction when age is closer to 10–12, the canine cusp tip has not crossed the lateral incisor midline, mesioangular displacement is moderate, vertical displacement is not extreme, and sufficient space can be ensured.
- Early diagnosis and structured radiographic evaluation (sector, angulation, vertical distance) guide decision-making and reduce overtreatment. Where predictors are unfavorable, plan timely surgical-orthodontic alignment to avoid protracted monitoring without benefit.
