# Effectiveness of Internet- and Mobile-Based Interventions for Reducing Depressive Symptoms in Adults With Overweight or Obesity: Systematic Review and Evidence Appraisal

## Background and Rationale

Overweight and obesity are highly prevalent globally and commonly co-occur with depressive symptoms, forming a bidirectional association mediated by behavioral, biological, and psychosocial pathways. Adults with elevated BMI (≥25 kg/m²) experience higher rates of depression and worse functional outcomes, yet access to integrated care for co-occurring depression and weight management is limited. Internet- and mobile-based interventions (IMIs)—including self-guided and guided digital cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), behavioral activation (BA), and blended digital lifestyle programs—offer scalable, low-barrier options that could address both conditions concurrently.

While numerous reviews show that eHealth and mHealth interventions can produce small-to-moderate weight loss in adults with overweight and obesity, mental health outcomes are often underreported in weight-focused trials, and conversely, depression-focused digital interventions rarely target populations selected for overweight/obesity. This review evaluates whether IMIs are effective in reducing depressive symptom severity specifically among adults with overweight/obesity who present with comorbid depressive symptoms and appraises the certainty of this evidence ([Mobile and Web Apps for Weight Management…](local_corpus); [Behavior Change Resources in Mobile App-Based Interventions…](local_corpus)).

## Objectives and PICO

- Population: Adults (≥18 years) with overweight/obesity (BMI ≥25 kg/m²) and comorbid depressive symptoms (e.g., PHQ‑9 ≥5 or clinician-diagnosed depressive disorder).
- Intervention: Internet- or mobile-based psychological/behavioral interventions targeting depressive symptoms (e.g., iCBT, BA, mindfulness, blended weight-loss plus mental health modules), with or without guidance.
- Comparator: Any (waitlist/usual care, minimal intervention, or active control).
- Primary Outcome: Depressive symptom severity post-intervention (validated scales, e.g., PHQ‑9, BDI-II).
- Secondary Outcomes: Anxiety, quality of life, weight/BMI, engagement/adherence, acceptability, and adverse events.

## Methods

### Protocol and Reporting

The review followed PRISMA and Cochrane Handbook guidance. A protocol detailing eligibility, search, and analysis plans was developed a priori. Given data access constraints, the synthesis emphasizes studies identified within the provided corpus and transparently reports evidence limitations.

### Eligibility Criteria

- Designs: Randomized controlled trials (RCTs); nonrandomized comparative studies considered for context.
- Population: Adults with BMI ≥25 kg/m²; depressive symptoms thresholded by validated screening or diagnosis.
- Interventions: Internet- or mobile-based psychological interventions (e.g., iCBT/BA/mindfulness/digital coaching); weight-only digital programs without a mental health component were excluded unless depressive outcomes were measured a priori.
- Outcomes: Validated depressive symptom measures post-intervention; studies reporting only weight outcomes without mental health measures were excluded from effectiveness synthesis.

### Information Sources and Search Strategy

Planned databases: MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, PsycINFO, Cochrane CENTRAL, CINAHL, and Web of Science; registries: ClinicalTrials.gov and WHO ICTRP. A sample MEDLINE (PubMed) strategy (to be adapted per database) included:

- (“depress*” OR “PHQ-9” OR “Beck Depression” OR “depressive symptoms”) AND
- (“internet-based” OR “web-based” OR “online” OR “mobile app” OR “mHealth” OR “eHealth” OR “digital”) AND
- (“obesity” OR “overweight” OR “BMI” OR “weight management”) AND
- (adult* OR “≥18”)

Searches would be limited to studies from inception through April 11, 2026. In this report, studies are drawn from the provided corpus using analogous logic.

### Study Selection, Data Extraction, and Risk of Bias

Two reviewers were planned to independently screen titles/abstracts and full texts, extract data (population, BMI, baseline depression, intervention features, comparators, outcomes, and results), and assess risk of bias using RoB 2 for RCTs and ROBINS‑I for nonrandomized studies. Disagreements resolved by consensus.

### Data Synthesis and Analysis

Where ≥2 trials reported comparable continuous outcomes, we planned random-effects meta-analysis using standardized mean differences (Hedges’ g), heterogeneity (I², τ²), and small-study bias tests (funnel plot, Egger’s). Planned subgroup analyses: guidance (guided vs unguided), intervention type (CBT/BA vs mindfulness), baseline depression severity, BMI class, comparator type, and risk of bias. With limited eligible trials found, we conducted narrative synthesis.

### Certainty Assessment

The GRADE framework was used to rate certainty (high, moderate, low, very low) for each outcome domain considering risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias.

## Results

### Study Selection

Within the provided corpus, we identified:
- 1 completed RCT directly meeting inclusion criteria (adult BMI ≥25 with depressive symptoms): SHED‑IT: Recharge (men with overweight/obesity and elevated PHQ‑9) ([Impact of a self-guided, eHealth program…](local_corpus)).
- 1 pilot RCT in young adults at risk for both depression and obesity factors (not uniformly overweight/obese): Vira digital BA with and without coaching (supportive but partially indirect) ([Vira Pilot RCT](local_corpus)).
- 1 retrospective cohort of a therapist-supported digital CBT (polychronic sample including obesity and type 2 diabetes) with depression/anxiety outcomes (supportive but nonrandomized and indirect) ([Digital CBT—Polychronic Population](local_corpus)).

We also identified two ongoing RCT protocols targeting depression in adults with obesity (no results yet): LightMood and Fit4Mood ([LightMood protocol](local_corpus); [Fit4Mood protocol](local_corpus)).

Given the scarcity of trials meeting the narrow PICO, quantitative meta-analysis was not feasible.

### Risk of Bias

- SHED‑IT: Recharge RCT: Likely some concerns due to self-reported outcomes and waitlist control; randomization and ITT analyses described; insufficient abstract detail to fully rate domains (overall: some concerns) ([SHED‑IT](local_corpus)).
- Vira Pilot: Pilot scale with small n; risk of bias due to limited power, potential performance bias between coached and self-guided arms; overall: high/some concerns for efficacy inference ([Vira Pilot RCT](local_corpus)).
- Digital CBT—Polychronic: Nonrandomized, self-selected completers; high risk of bias (confounding, selection bias), indirect for BMI-defined populations ([Digital CBT—Polychronic](local_corpus)).

### Effects on Depressive Symptoms (Primary Outcome)

- SHED‑IT: Recharge RCT reported reductions in PHQ‑9 among men with overweight/obesity and low mood compared to waitlist at 3 months (primary endpoint), with follow-up at 6 months. The abstract indicates improvement in both weight and depressive symptoms, but effect sizes and exact between-group differences were not reported in the abstract. Direction of effect supports benefit of a self-guided program integrating weight-loss content with mental fitness modules. Without numeric data, we cannot compute SMDs; however, the trial demonstrates proof-of-concept that a minimally supported eHealth intervention tailored for men with overweight/obesity can reduce depressive symptom severity ([SHED‑IT](local_corpus)).

- Vira Pilot RCT found improvements in mental health over 12 weeks, with feasibility and acceptability demonstrated. Coach-supported BA showed signals of greater engagement; specific between-group depression effect sizes are not reported in the abstract. Because participants were characterized by obesity risk rather than confirmed overweight/obesity by BMI, this evidence is indirect for the PICO ([Vira Pilot RCT](local_corpus)).

- Digital CBT—Polychronic cohort revealed significant pre–post reductions in depression and anxiety in a population with high prevalence of obesity and T2D. The lack of a parallel control and the mixed comorbidity profile limit causal inference and direct applicability to specifically overweight/obese adults with depression ([Digital CBT—Polychronic](local_corpus)).

Taken together, the single RCT directly addressing the PICO supports the effectiveness of a self-guided eHealth program in reducing depressive symptoms in overweight/obese adults (men). The broader digital mental health literature confirms that web/iCBT is effective for depression in general populations and in medical comorbidities such as diabetes and cardiovascular disease, but these trials did not specifically enroll based on BMI ([Web-Based CBT for Depression—Systematic Review](local_corpus); [iCBT in Diabetes](local_corpus); [iCBT in CVD](local_corpus)).

### Secondary Outcomes

- Weight/BMI: SHED‑IT: Recharge’s co-primary outcome included weight loss; the abstract indicates efficacy for weight alongside mood improvements, consistent with umbrella review findings that mobile/web interventions yield modest weight loss (∼1–1.5 kg) across heterogeneous populations ([SHED‑IT](local_corpus); [Umbrella Review—Mobile/Web for Weight](local_corpus)). Direct numeric results for the RCT were not available in the abstract.

- Anxiety and Quality of Life: Not consistently reported in the BMI-specific RCT abstract. Digital mental health interventions in mixed or medical samples often reduce anxiety and improve well-being, suggesting potential ancillary benefits ([Digital CBT—Polychronic](local_corpus); [iCBT in CVD RCT](local_corpus)).

- Engagement and Acceptability: SHED‑IT: Recharge was self-guided; retention and adherence details are not provided in the abstract. The Vira pilot reported feasibility and acceptability, with coaching improving engagement signals ([Vira Pilot RCT](local_corpus)).

- Adverse Events and Acceptability: Not systematically reported in the BMI-specific RCT abstract. Generally, digital interventions show favorable safety and acceptability profiles; however, specific adverse event monitoring in overweight/obesity with depression is sparse.

### Heterogeneity and Publication Bias

Due to the small number of directly eligible trials, we could not estimate heterogeneity or assess small-study/publication bias using funnel plots or regression tests.

## Certainty of Evidence (GRADE)

- Depressive Symptom Severity: Low certainty for benefit. Rating reflects:
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns (self-reported outcomes, waitlist comparator).
  - Inconsistency: Not assessable (single direct trial).
  - Indirectness: Evidence applies to men; generalizability to women and diverse BMI classes uncertain; some supportive but indirect studies.
  - Imprecision: Effect sizes unspecified; sample relatively small for firm precision.
  - Publication bias: Not assessable.

- Secondary Outcomes (weight, anxiety, QoL, adherence): Very low to low certainty specific to this PICO due to sparse BMI-and-depression co-targeted data and incomplete reporting.

Overall, current direct evidence supports a likely beneficial effect of IMIs on depressive symptoms in adults with overweight/obesity, but confidence is limited by scarce trials, narrow demographics, and incomplete reporting.

## Equity, Accessibility, and Digital Literacy Considerations

- IMIs can reduce access barriers for populations facing stigma, time constraints, or cost, which are common in both obesity and depression care. However, digital literacy, device access, and language/cultural tailoring can impact uptake and outcomes. Men-focused tailoring (as in SHED‑IT) may enhance engagement in historically under-engaged groups. Coaching support may improve adherence among users with lower digital self-efficacy ([Vira Pilot RCT](local_corpus)).

## Limitations

- Evidence scarcity: Only one completed RCT directly enrolling adults on the basis of both BMI and depressive symptoms was identified within the corpus.
- Reporting gaps: Lack of numeric effect size data in abstracts precluded meta-analysis.
- Generalizability: The primary RCT focused on men; applicability to women and older adults is uncertain.
- Indirect evidence: Several supporting studies involved related but not strictly BMI-selected populations (e.g., polychronic/diabetes/CVD samples), reducing directness.
- Risk of bias: Waitlist controls and self-reported outcomes are common and may inflate effects.

## Implications for Practice

- Clinicians and health systems may consider offering or recommending self-guided digital programs that integrate weight management with “mental fitness” or iCBT components for adults with overweight/obesity and mild-to-moderate depressive symptoms, particularly where access to in-person care is limited. Given the low-to-very low certainty, such use should be framed as part of stepped or blended care with outcome monitoring and the option to step up support.

## Recommendations for Future Research

- High-quality, adequately powered RCTs that:
  - Enroll adults based on both BMI ≥25 kg/m² and clinically relevant depressive symptoms (including diverse genders, ages, and BMI classes).
  - Compare guided versus unguided IMIs, and CBT/BA versus mindfulness-based modalities.
  - Report prespecified mental health and weight outcomes with sufficient detail for effect size calculation.
  - Include equity-focused analyses (digital literacy, socioeconomic status, language/culture).
  - Evaluate durability (≥6–12 months) and relapse prevention.
  - Monitor adverse events, including potential disordered eating or mood worsening.
- The ongoing LightMood and Fit4Mood trials are well positioned to help fill these gaps; their completion and publication should be prioritized ([LightMood protocol](local_corpus); [Fit4Mood protocol](local_corpus)).

## Conclusions

Direct evidence that internet- and mobile-based interventions reduce depressive symptoms among adults selected for overweight/obesity with comorbid depressive symptoms is presently limited but promising. One RCT in men with overweight/obesity demonstrated improvements in both depression and weight with a self-guided eHealth program tailored to mental fitness, supporting feasibility and potential effectiveness. Complementary pilot and observational studies suggest broader applicability, but indirectness and methodological limitations constrain certainty. On balance, the GRADE certainty is low for depressive symptom reduction and very low to low for secondary outcomes. More rigorous, diverse, and transparently reported trials are needed to inform clinical guidelines and implementation.
