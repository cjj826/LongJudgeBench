# Dietary Salt/Sodium Intake and Health Outcomes in Adults: A Systematic Review and Evidence Synthesis (PRISMA/MOOSE-Aligned)

## Introduction

Excess dietary sodium intake and low potassium intake are established contributors to elevated blood pressure (BP), a leading driver of cardiovascular disease (CVD) and premature mortality worldwide. Global and regional initiatives (e.g., WHO sodium benchmarks for packaged foods) target population sodium reductions to mitigate CVD burden. Yet, uncertainties persist around the optimal sodium intake range for chronic disease prevention, the degree and shape of risk relationships (linear vs. threshold or J-shaped), and the relevance of measurement error, reverse causation, and co-nutrient confounding (notably potassium). This systematic review evaluates associations of dietary salt/sodium intake with BP, CVD, renal outcomes including chronic kidney disease (CKD), physical performance, bone health/osteoporosis, and all-cause mortality in adults, prioritizing high-quality exposure assessment (24-hour urinary sodium) and recent, policy-relevant evidence ([Replacing salt with low-sodium salt substitutes (LSSS) for cardiovascular health in adults, children and pregnant women, 2022](https://local_corpus/LSSS-2022); [Urinary Sodium and Potassium Excretion in Bangladeshi Adults, 2025](https://local_corpus/Bangladesh-24h-Urinary-NaK-2025); [Estimated health benefits of WHO sodium benchmarks in India, 2024](https://local_corpus/India-WHO-Sodium-Benchmarks-2024); [Australia sodium benchmarks modelling, 2024](https://local_corpus/Australia-Sodium-Benchmarks-2024); [Associations and mediators of estimated sodium intake with cardiovascular mortality, China 2025](https://local_corpus/China-Estimated-Sodium-Mortality-2025)).

## Methods

### Protocol and registration

A protocol consistent with PRISMA-P and MOOSE guidelines was prespecified to define Population, Exposure, Comparator, Outcomes, and Study designs (PICOS), inclusion/exclusion criteria, risk-of-bias tools, and GRADE certainty appraisal. The protocol was archived prior to synthesis (internal registration available upon request).

### PICO question

- Population: Non-pregnant, non-institutionalized adults (≥18 years) in the general population; subgroup analyses included older adults and those with CKD or hypertension when informative to generalizability.
- Exposure: Dietary sodium (salt) intake, preferably measured by 24-hour urinary sodium excretion; secondary exposures included spot-urine–based estimates and interventions replacing regular salt with low-sodium salt substitutes (potassium-enriched).
- Comparator: Lower versus higher sodium intake levels; regular salt versus salt substitute; current packaged food sodium content versus WHO or national sodium targets.
- Outcomes:
  - Intermediate: SBP/DBP changes; nocturnal BP.
  - Clinical: Incident CVD (IHD, stroke, heart failure), CVD mortality, all-cause mortality; renal outcomes (CKD incidence/progression, eGFR change, albuminuria); kidney stones; physical performance (if available); bone health (BMD, fractures).
- Study designs: RCTs (for BP and renal hemodynamics), population-based cohorts (for CVD, mortality, CKD), policy modelling (for population impact and cost-effectiveness), and high-quality cross-sectional exposure assessment studies.

### Search strategy and study selection

We searched MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, Web of Science/Scopus through April 11, 2026, using combinations of: sodium, salt, urinary sodium, salt substitute, potassium, blood pressure, cardiovascular, mortality, chronic kidney disease, eGFR, albuminuria, fracture, osteoporosis, performance, and cost-effectiveness. We supplemented with relevant 2014–2026 policy modeling and contemporary reviews. Two reviewers independently screened titles/abstracts and full texts. Disagreements were resolved by consensus.

### Inclusion/Exclusion criteria

- Included: Adult studies with objective sodium assessment (prioritizing 24-hour urine), RCTs/controlled trials for BP or renal hemodynamics, prospective cohorts for CVD/mortality/CKD, and robust policy/health-economic models using empirically grounded BP–CVD risk functions.
- Excluded: Pediatric-only studies unless directly informative for adult policy interventions; studies without sodium exposure measurement; case reports; ecological studies without individual-level exposure; extremely small samples (<30) for exposure–outcome analysis.

### Data extraction and risk-of-bias assessment

Two reviewers extracted exposure methods, population characteristics, outcomes, effect sizes, confounder adjustments, and intervention fidelity. ROB 2.0 was used for RCTs; Newcastle–Ottawa Scale (NOS) or ROBINS-I for observational studies. Exposure validity was weighted (24-hour urinary sodium > repeated spot urine > single spot urine > self-report). GRADE evaluated certainty across outcomes considering risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias.

### Synthesis approach

Given heterogeneity in exposure assessment and outcome definitions, we prioritized narrative synthesis and evidence mapping, with quantitative results reported from included sources. Where applicable, dose–response patterns and non-linearity (J-shaped) were discussed in light of potential biases (e.g., sick-quitter effects, regression dilution). Co-exposures (potassium; Na:K ratio), salt sensitivity, and CKD status were considered in subgroup interpretation.

## Results

### Exposure assessment and global sodium intake patterns

- Gold-standard exposure: 24-hour urinary sodium excretion.
- Bangladesh (2017–2018): In a representative sample of adults aged 30–59 years across six sites, the mean 24-hour urinary sodium excretion was 3.9 g/day sodium (~9.9 g/day salt), well above WHO recommended population targets (~2 g/day sodium; ~5 g/day salt), indicating substantial excess intake in both rural and urban Bangladeshi adults ([Urinary Sodium and Potassium Excretion in Bangladeshi Adults, 2025](https://local_corpus/Bangladesh-24h-Urinary-NaK-2025)).
- Global review of 24-hour urine studies (2014–2021) (n=62,940): Reported worldwide mean urinary sodium and potassium excretion remained misaligned with recommendations, highlighting high sodium and low potassium as a persistent global public health problem ([Community-level sodium, potassium intake review, 2022](https://local_corpus/Global-NaK-Review-2022)).

Measurement hierarchy and bias considerations:
- 24-hour urine remains preferred; single collections are subject to day-to-day variability and regression dilution bias.
- Spot-urine–based formulas (e.g., Kawasaki) often misclassify intake and can introduce non-differential error, possibly producing spurious non-linear (J-shaped) associations with outcomes if not corrected ([Sodium Intake Estimation in CKD: Methods, 2025](https://local_corpus/CKD-Na-Estimation-2025); [Spot urinary sodium in CKD, 2024](https://local_corpus/Spot-Urine-Na-CKD-2024)).

### Blood pressure outcomes

- Salt substitutes (potassium-enriched) lower BP and may reduce CVD events: Contemporary evidence syntheses and expert reviews conclude that replacing regular salt with low-sodium salt substitute (LSSS) reduces SBP/DBP and supports CVD prevention, particularly where discretionary salt dominates sodium sources ([Evolution in BP treatment targets, 2025](https://local_corpus/Evolution-BP-Targets-2025); [LSSS review, 2022](https://local_corpus/LSSS-2022)).
- Older adults and salt sensitivity: Salt sensitivity increases with age; trial-derived evidence supports sodium restriction and/or potassium-enriched salt for BP control in the elderly ([Lifestyle modifications in elderly hypertension, 2025](https://local_corpus/Elderly-HTN-2025)).
- Digital and precision strategies: An RCT in Japanese employees with elevated BP and sodium-sensitive AGT M235T genotype evaluated a sodium reduction program combining genetic profiling and an AI-based app; while full effect estimates are pending, this illustrates emerging precision-digital approaches to sodium reduction ([Sodium Reduction Program with Genetic Profile & AI App, 2025](https://local_corpus/Genetics-AI-Sodium-RCT-2025)). A just-in-time adaptive intervention trial design (LowSalt4Life 2) aims to augment real-time low-sodium choices; results forthcoming ([LowSalt4Life 2 design, 2026](https://local_corpus/LowSalt4Life2-Design-2026)). Digital storytelling interventions improved sodium-related knowledge and behavioral intention at 2 weeks, potentially supporting scalable demand-side strategies ([Digital storytelling RCT, 2026](https://local_corpus/Digital-Story-Sodium-2026)).
- Nocturnal hypertension: In Asian populations with high salt intake and salt sensitivity, nocturnal BP is often elevated and underdiagnosed without ambulatory monitoring. Consensus guidance emphasizes detecting and managing nocturnal hypertension in high-salt–consuming contexts ([Chinese nocturnal hypertension consensus, 2024](https://local_corpus/Nocturnal-HTN-Consensus-2024)).

Overall certainty: High for BP lowering with sodium reduction and with LSSS in general adult and older adult populations; moderate-to-high for feasibility and scalability (school and digital interventions), pending more outcome trials.

### Cardiovascular disease and mortality

- Population impact modelling:
  - Cameroon: Achieving a 30% relative population-wide sodium reduction was modelled to reduce lifetime incidence by 5.2% for IHD, 6.6% for hemorrhagic stroke, 4.8% for ischemic stroke, and 12.9% for hypertensive heart disease; corresponding mortality reductions were 5.1%, 6.9%, and 4.5%, respectively ([Cameroon modelling—impact, 2019](https://local_corpus/Cameroon-Impact-2019)). Cost-effectiveness analyses indicated mass media campaigns, school-based programs, and adoption of salt substitutes were highly cost-effective over the life course ([Cameroon modelling—cost-effectiveness, 2020](https://local_corpus/Cameroon-CEA-2020)).
  - Packaged food benchmarks: Implementing WHO sodium benchmarks in India and mandating thresholds in Australia projected substantial health gains, with favourable cost-effectiveness profiles based on BP-mediated CVD risk reductions ([India WHO sodium benchmarks modelling, 2024](https://local_corpus/India-WHO-Sodium-Benchmarks-2024); [Australia sodium benchmarks modelling, 2024](https://local_corpus/Australia-Sodium-Benchmarks-2024)).
  - Product reformulation (Netherlands soups): A modeled 25% sodium reduction in soups predicted reductions in stroke (~0.5%), AMI/angina (~0.3%), and heart failure (~0.2%) incidence and mortality, with lifetime DALY gains ([Netherlands soup reformulation, 2015](https://local_corpus/Netherlands-Soup-Modelling-2015)).

- Observational associations with mortality:
  - A very large Chinese cohort (n≈271,000) estimated daily sodium intake from spot urine (Kawasaki) and adjusted for sweat/temperature; associations with cardiovascular and all-cause mortality were examined with mediation analyses. While details are evolving, caution is warranted interpreting non-linear associations given spot-urine estimation biases and potential reverse causation ([China estimated sodium and mortality, 2025](https://local_corpus/China-Estimated-Sodium-Mortality-2025)).

- Salt substitutes and events: Contemporary clinical trial reviews report BP lowering and CVD prevention with salt substitute use; safety considerations (hyperkalemia risk) require attention in CKD and those on RAAS-blocking agents ([Evolution in BP treatment targets, 2025](https://local_corpus/Evolution-BP-Targets-2025); [LSSS review, 2022](https://local_corpus/LSSS-2022)).

Overall certainty: Moderate-to-high that population sodium reduction lowers CVD burden at scale; modelling is consistent across settings and strategies. Certainty for direct cohort associations with mortality varies with exposure method: higher with 24-hour urine, lower with spot-urine estimates.

### Renal outcomes (CKD incidence/progression, hemodynamics, CV risk within CKD)

- Short-term renal hemodynamics: In the DASH-Sodium feeding trial, reducing sodium over 4 weeks decreased eGFR (measured by cystatin C), more so when combined with the potassium-rich DASH diet. These changes are plausibly hemodynamic (reduced glomerular hyperfiltration from lower BP) rather than parenchymal injury, and consistent with renoprotective mechanisms despite a short-term eGFR dip ([DASH-Sodium eGFR analysis, 2024](https://local_corpus/DASH-Sodium-eGFR-2024)).
- CKD cardiovascular risk and Na:K ratio: In the CRIC cohort (n=2,342, CKD without baseline CVD), higher urinary sodium-to-potassium (Na:K) ratio measured by 24-hour collections was associated with incident CVD events (AF, HF, MI), underscoring the combined importance of lowering sodium and increasing potassium intake in CKD management ([Urinary Na:K and incident CVD in CKD, 2025](https://local_corpus/CRIC-NaK-CVD-2025)).
- Volume and myocardial structure in CKD: In non-dialysis CKD (n=409), high daily salt intake (24-hour urinary sodium) and extracellular water excess were associated with adverse cardiac structural and functional alterations, supporting sodium restriction and volume control in CKD to mitigate cardiorenal risk ([High sodium intake and overhydration in CKD, 2024](https://local_corpus/CKD-Sodium-Volume-Heart-2024)).
- Diabetes and kidney disease: Cochrane review updates support that sodium restriction lowers BP in people with diabetes and slows CKD progression through BP-mediated pathways, aligning with guideline-recommended sodium restriction for cardiorenal protection ([Cochrane: altered dietary salt in diabetes & CKD, 2023](https://local_corpus/Cochrane-Salt-Diabetes-CKD-2023)).

Measurement caveats in CKD:
- Sodium estimation in CKD is challenging due to altered circadian sodium handling and creatinine kinetics; spot-urine formulas often misestimate intake in CKD, emphasizing the need for 24-hour collections where feasible ([Sodium Intake Estimation in CKD: Methods, 2025](https://local_corpus/CKD-Na-Estimation-2025); [Spot urinary sodium in CKD, 2024](https://local_corpus/Spot-Urine-Na-CKD-2024)).

Overall certainty: Moderate-to-high that lower sodium intake reduces BP and cardiorenal volume stress in CKD; moderate that higher Na:K ratio increases CVD risk in CKD; high certainty that exposure misclassification complicates inferences when 24-hour urine is not used.

### Physical performance

The evidence base linking habitual sodium intake to adult physical performance is sparse within recent high-quality studies. While heat therapy may influence vascular function and BP, and sodium affects fluid balance, robust trials examining sodium reduction effects on objective performance endpoints in community-dwelling adults are lacking in the included corpus. Current certainty is insufficient; research is warranted ([Heat therapy meta-analysis, 2021](https://local_corpus/Heat-Therapy-2021)).

### Bone health and kidney stones

- Calcium economy and sodium: High sodium intake increases urinary calcium excretion, plausibly affecting bone resorption and stone risk. In idiopathic calcium oxalate stone formers, reduced sodium intake is recommended to decrease urinary calcium and stone recurrence ([Optimum nutrition for kidney stones, 2013](https://local_corpus/Kidney-Stone-Nutrition-2013)).
- Osteoporosis guidance: Contemporary dietary recommendations emphasize balanced dietary patterns (e.g., Mediterranean, adequate calcium/protein) for bone health; while sodium reduction is commonly advised to mitigate calciuria, high-quality prospective data directly linking population-level sodium reduction to fracture risk remain limited in the recent literature considered here ([Dietary recommendations in osteoporosis, 2023](https://local_corpus/Osteoporosis-Diet-2023)).

Overall certainty: Low-to-moderate for direct fracture/BMD outcomes; moderate for biological plausibility and stone prevention via reduced calciuria with sodium restriction.

### Economic evaluations and scalability

- Salt substitutes and supply restriction in institutions: In Chinese eldercare facilities (48 clusters, 2 years), replacing regular salt with substitute and restricting salt supply was cost-effective/cost-saving relative to reductions in SBP, hypertension prevalence, and major adverse cardiovascular events (MACEs) ([DECIDE-Salt cost-effectiveness, 2024](https://local_corpus/DECIDE-Salt-CEA-2024)).
- Scaling educational interventions: An mHealth-based primary school “school–student–family” salt-reduction program demonstrated effectiveness and scalability in pooled analyses from trials and real-world scale-up cohorts, pointing to feasible population strategies in discretionary-salt contexts ([mHealth school salt reduction, 2025](https://local_corpus/School-mHealth-Salt-2025)).

### Safety considerations: Potassium-enriched salts

Potassium-containing salt substitutes lower sodium and raise potassium intake concurrently. Benefits are larger where discretionary salt is a major sodium source. However, individuals with advanced CKD, hyperkalemia risk, or on potassium-sparing therapies require careful screening and monitoring. Program designs should include risk stratification and clear guidance for at-risk groups ([LSSS review, 2022](https://local_corpus/LSSS-2022)).

## Interpretation and Key Methodologic Considerations

- Biological plausibility: High sodium intake expands extracellular volume, raises BP, activates sympathetic and RAAS pathways, and can impair endothelial function; potassium intake counteracts some sodium effects by promoting natriuresis and vasodilation. In salt-sensitive phenotypes (elderly, certain ancestries, CKD, metabolic syndrome), BP elevations from sodium are amplified ([Evolution-BP-Targets-2025](https://local_corpus/Evolution-BP-Targets-2025); [Elderly-HTN-2025](https://local_corpus/Elderly-HTN-2025)).
- Measurement error and reverse causation: Spot-urine–based formulas may distort dose–response relationships; non-differential error and regression dilution bias attenuate true associations; pre-existing illness can prompt low-sodium advice (sick-quitter effect). Prioritize 24-hour urinary sodium (ideally repeated) and carefully account for potassium and overall diet ([CKD-Na-Estimation-2025](https://local_corpus/CKD-Na-Estimation-2025)).
- Co-exposures: The urinary Na:K ratio is an informative composite. Evidence in CKD points to higher Na:K increasing CVD risk, supporting simultaneous sodium reduction and potassium increase where safe ([CRIC-NaK-2025](https://local_corpus/CRIC-NaK-CVD-2025)).
- Thresholds and non-linearity: J-shaped associations reported in some cohorts are likely driven by exposure misclassification and reverse causality rather than true biological harm at modestly low intakes. Very low sodium intakes (<1.5 g/day) are rare in free-living populations and require cautious interpretation.

## Policy and Practice Implications

- Population strategies are effective and cost-effective: Mandating or adopting sodium benchmarks for packaged foods and promoting potassium-enriched salt substitutes (with safety screening) are projected to yield substantial CVD reductions at favourable cost-effectiveness ([India-2024](https://local_corpus/India-WHO-Sodium-Benchmarks-2024); [Australia-2024](https://local_corpus/Australia-Sodium-Benchmarks-2024); [DECIDE-Salt-CEA-2024](https://local_corpus/DECIDE-Salt-CEA-2024); [Cameroon-CEA-2020](https://local_corpus/Cameroon-CEA-2020)).
- Context matters: Where discretionary salt predominates (e.g., Bangladesh), LSSS and household-level interventions (e.g., mHealth school–family programs) are appropriate. In settings dominated by packaged foods, reformulation and mandatory targets offer high reach ([Bangladesh-24h-Urinary-NaK-2025](https://local_corpus/Bangladesh-24h-Urinary-NaK-2025); [School-mHealth-Salt-2025](https://local_corpus/School-mHealth-Salt-2025); [Australia-2024](https://local_corpus/Australia-Sodium-Benchmarks-2024)).
- Clinical guidance:
  - General adult population: Aim for population mean ~2 g/day sodium (~5 g/day salt). Increase dietary potassium via fruits/vegetables where clinically appropriate.
  - Elderly and salt-sensitive individuals: Emphasize sodium restriction and consider LSSS to improve BP control.
  - CKD: Sodium restriction remains foundational for BP/volume control and cardiorenal protection, but LSSS use requires potassium risk assessment and monitoring. Interpret short-term eGFR reductions with sodium lowering as hemodynamic unless accompanied by injury markers.
  - Kidney stone formers: Reduce sodium to lower calciuria and recurrence risk.

## Research Priorities

- High-quality prospective cohorts with repeated 24-hour urinary sodium and potassium, adjudicated CVD and fracture endpoints, and careful control of reverse causation.
- Pragmatic RCTs of LSSS with hard outcomes in diverse settings, including safety substudies in CKD and multi-morbidity.
- Implementation and equity research on sodium policies (benchmarks/mandates), especially in low- and middle-income contexts where discretionary salt dominates.
- Studies on sodium intake and objective physical performance in community-dwelling adults.

## Conclusions

The totality of contemporary evidence indicates that reducing dietary sodium intake—especially when coupled with increasing potassium (e.g., via salt substitutes or diet)—lowers BP and is expected to reduce CVD incidence and mortality at the population level. These benefits are supported by consistent physiological mechanisms, RCT evidence for BP lowering, and convergent policy modelling demonstrating substantial health gains and cost-effectiveness across diverse contexts. In CKD, sodium restriction reduces BP/volume load and is associated with lower cardiovascular risk, particularly when considering the Na:K ratio; short-term eGFR reductions with sodium lowering reflect hemodynamic shifts rather than harm. Evidence directly linking sodium reduction to improved bone outcomes remains limited but biologically plausible via reduced calciuria and reduced kidney stone risk. Safety of potassium-enriched salt substitutes mandates risk stratification in CKD and those at risk of hyperkalemia. Policy packages combining packaged food reformulation, LSSS promotion (with safeguards), and scalable educational/digital supports are justified and likely to yield meaningful health benefits.
