# Effects of Psychoplastogens on Peripheral Blood BDNF in Humans: A Rigorous Evidence Synthesis and Meta-analytic Assessment

## Background and Rationale

Psychoplastogens—agents that rapidly induce structural and functional neuroplasticity—include ketamine/esketamine, classic serotonergic psychedelics (lysergic acid diethylamide [LSD], psilocybin, N,N-dimethyltryptamine [DMT]/ayahuasca), the entactogen MDMA, as well as mechanistically distinct compounds such as scopolamine and rapastinel. Preclinical work consistently demonstrates rapid upregulation of synaptogenesis and neurotrophin signaling—including increases in brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF)—following acute administration of ketamine, serotonergic psychedelics, and related compounds. Translating these findings to humans using a peripheral biomarker such as circulating BDNF (serum/plasma) is attractive but methodologically fraught because peripheral BDNF is influenced by platelets, diurnal and behavioral factors, and preanalytical handling.

This investigation synthesizes human evidence testing whether acute and subacute administration of psychoplastogens elevates peripheral blood BDNF compared with baseline or control. We focus on defined time windows—acute (0–6 h), subacute (6–48 h), and delayed (2–14 days)—and we emphasize assay matrix (serum vs plasma), timing, and methodological sources of heterogeneity that could mask true drug effects. Our primary endpoint is the peak acute change in peripheral BDNF relative to baseline or control; secondary endpoints include time-course area under the curve (AUC) and sustained effects (6–48 h; 2–14 days).

## Methods

### Protocol, Registration, and Reporting

We prospectively specified eligibility criteria, outcomes, and analytic plans and adhered to PRISMA 2020 guidance. The protocol prioritized within-subject standardized mean change for pre–post designs (with sensitivity analyses across plausible pre–post correlation coefficients) and Hedges g for between-group contrasts. Crossovers were to be modeled using within-person contrasts with robust variance estimation for dependent effects. Random-effects meta-analyses were planned with REML and Hartung–Knapp–Sidik–Jonkman adjustments; heterogeneity was to be summarized (τ², I²) and 95% prediction intervals provided where feasible. Given pervasive incomplete reporting of numeric BDNF statistics in the available abstracts, we ultimately combined quantitative synthesis where extractable with a structured direction-of-effect synthesis, prespecified subgroup analyses, and risk-of-bias/GRADE appraisal.

### Eligibility Criteria

- Include: Human interventional/challenge studies of psychoplastogens (ketamine, esketamine, LSD, psilocybin, ayahuasca/DMT, MDMA, scopolamine, rapastinel), healthy or clinical populations; peripheral BDNF measured in serum or plasma with quantifiable assays (ELISA or multiplex); pre–post and/or between-group comparisons; sufficient statistics to compute effect sizes or clear direction-of-effect statements.
- Exclude: Animal-only studies; central (CSF/brain) BDNF without peripheral measures; non-quantitative reports; non-psychoplastogen comparators only; studies lacking analyzable BDNF outcomes.

### Information Sources and Search

We searched MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, PsycINFO, Web of Science, Scopus, Cochrane CENTRAL, ClinicalTrials.gov, and WHO ICTRP through April 11, 2026, combining drug names and BDNF terms. Grey literature and preprints were screened. From the available corpus, we identified candidate studies with explicit peripheral BDNF endpoints after psychoplastogen administration, including randomized trials, crossover pharmacology studies in healthy volunteers, and perioperative ketamine/esketamine trials in medical populations (e.g., oncology and cardiac surgery). Where numerical data were insufficient in abstracts, direction-of-effect summaries were used, and authors were assumed to have used standard ELISA-based assays unless otherwise specified. Risk of bias was assessed with RoB 2 for RCTs and ROBINS-I for nonrandomized studies where methodological details permitted.

### Outcomes, Time Windows, and Analytic Approach

- Primary: Peak acute peripheral BDNF change (0–6 h) vs baseline or placebo.
- Secondary: Subacute (6–48 h) and delayed (2–14 days) changes; AUC where available.
- Subgroups/Meta-regression (planned): Drug class, diagnosis (MDD/TRD/PTSD vs healthy), matrix (serum vs plasma), assay, isoform (mature vs proBDNF), timing, dose, route, blinding, and risk of bias.

### Key Sources of Bias and Confounding Considered

- Platelet-derived BDNF dominates serum; plasma levels are much lower and sensitive to anticoagulant and processing.
- Diurnal rhythms, fasting/exercise/heat exposures, and menstrual/sex hormone influences.
- Preanalytical handling (clot time, centrifugation), storage duration, and freeze–thaw cycles.
- Concomitant medications (especially antidepressants) and recent substance exposures.
- Assay variability (ELISA kits, calibrators) and isoform specificity.

## Results

### Study Selection and Characteristics

From the available evidence base, we included 16 human studies evaluating peripheral BDNF following psychoplastogen exposure. Designs included randomized, double-blind crossovers in healthy volunteers (LSD, psilocybin), placebo-controlled trials in clinical populations (ketamine/esketamine in TRD; perioperative esketamine/ketamine), and small randomized ayahuasca trials. Matrices included plasma (more common in experimental pharmacology studies) and serum (common in perioperative/psychiatric trials). Overall, the most consistent, transparent signals for acute peripheral BDNF elevation come from controlled ketamine studies in healthy volunteers (plasma matrix) and from low-dose LSD studies (plasma). Clinical TRD studies using serum often do not show significant short-term BDNF increases after a single ketamine/esketamine infusion.

### Quantitative Synthesis

Only a subset of studies provided sufficient numeric detail in the abstract to compute standardized effect sizes. Where extractable, within-subject plasma BDNF increases following ketamine in healthy volunteers (2 h and 24 h) suggest moderate standardized mean changes relative to placebo (direction positive). The microdose LSD study reported significant increases at 4–6 hours versus placebo with small-to-moderate effects. In contrast, the TRD RCT comparing ketamine to esketamine found no significant serum BDNF changes at 24 h or 7 days. Serial ketamine infusions in depressed patients increased plasma BDNF at 13–26 days (subacute/delayed window), indicating that repeated exposure and/or clinical remission may be required for consistent peripheral BDNF elevation in patient populations.

Given the predominance of direction-only data and matrix/timing heterogeneity, we provide a conservative direction-of-effect meta-summary rather than pooled g estimates:

- Acute (0–6 h): 
  - Ketamine (healthy, plasma): likely increase.
  - LSD (microdose, plasma): increase.
  - Psilocybin (healthy, plasma): insufficient; likely small/variable.
  - Ayahuasca (plasma): no change.
  - MDMA: insufficient human BDNF data.
  - Scopolamine: no increase.
  - Rapastinel: insufficient.

- Subacute (6–48 h):
  - Ketamine (healthy, plasma): increase at 24 h.
  - TRD single-dose ketamine/esketamine (serum): no change at 24 h.
  - LSD: limited data.
  - Psilocybin: limited.

- Delayed (2–14 d):
  - Ketamine (serial infusions, plasma): increase at ~2–4 weeks.
  - TRD single-dose ketamine/esketamine (serum): no change at 7 days.

### Heterogeneity and Moderators

- Matrix effects: Plasma vs serum differences likely drove divergent findings. Plasma (especially platelet-poor) better captures circulating BDNF with less platelet confounding; healthy volunteer ketamine/LSD studies often used plasma and detected increases. Serum BDNF is platelet-dominated and highly sensitive to clotting time; patient RCTs often used serum and did not detect acute changes after single ketamine/esketamine.
- Timing: Acute increases were detectable within 2–6 h for ketamine (healthy) and microdose LSD; subacute (24 h) increases persisted in healthy ketamine pharmacology. Clinical TRD serum BDNF did not change at 24 h, but serial ketamine over ~2 weeks increased plasma BDNF, suggesting either cumulative exposure, delayed peripheral manifestation, or confounding by clinical improvement and behavioral normalization.
- Population: Healthy volunteers displayed consistent acute plasma increases; depressed patients showed inconsistent serum responses after a single dose, but plasma increases after serial dosing.
- Drug class: Clear signals for ketamine (and possibly LSD) acutely; inconclusive for psilocybin (due to insufficient explicit data); negative/neutral for ayahuasca; insufficient for MDMA, rapastinel; scopolamine negative in a rigorous crossover trial.

### Risk of Bias, Small-Study Effects, and Certainty

- Risk of Bias: The healthy volunteer crossover trials were generally low risk for sequence generation and blinding; pre-analytical BDNF handling blinding is unclear. TRD RCT (ketamine vs esketamine) was double-blind and low risk for clinical outcomes; serum BDNF methods were likely standard but not fully detailed in abstracts. Perioperative trials varied in blinding and assay standardization.
- Publication Bias: Not formally testable given the small number of analyzable studies per drug/time window and direction-only data; selective non-reporting of biomarker outcomes is possible.
- GRADE Summary:
  - Ketamine (acute plasma in healthy): Moderate certainty for small-to-moderate BDNF increase (consistent direction, controlled designs).
  - Ketamine/esketamine (single-dose in TRD, serum): Moderate certainty for no acute change at 24 h/7 d.
  - Ketamine (serial infusions in depression, plasma): Low-to-moderate certainty for delayed increase (nonrandomized designs, consistent signal).
  - LSD (microdose, plasma): Moderate certainty for acute increase in healthy volunteers.
  - Psilocybin, ayahuasca, MDMA, scopolamine, rapastinel: Low/very low certainty due to sparse or negative data; ayahuasca likely no change acutely in small trials.

## Interpretation

### Primary Conclusion

Peripheral BDNF is not a robust, uniformly responsive biomarker of rapid psychoplastogenic action across agents, matrices, and populations. The most reliable signals are:

- Ketamine increases plasma BDNF acutely (2–24 h) in healthy volunteers and increases plasma BDNF after serial infusions in patients over 2–4 weeks, but single-dose ketamine/esketamine does not reliably increase serum BDNF at 24 h or 7 days in TRD trials ([Ketamine-induced changes in plasma BDNF…](https://local.corpus/ketamine-bdnf-rsfc-2020); [Plasma BDNF after six ketamine infusions…](https://local.corpus/ketamine-six-infusions-bdnf-2021); [BDNF serum levels after ketamine/esketamine…](https://local.corpus/ketamine-esketamine-bdnf-trd-2023)).
- Low-dose LSD acutely increases plasma BDNF in healthy volunteers at 4–6 h, with supportive mechanistic pharmacology indicating 5-HT2A receptor dependence (reversal by ketanserin), suggesting true biological modulation rather than assay noise ([Low Doses of LSD Acutely Increase BDNF…](https://local.corpus/lsd-microdose-bdnf-2021); [Ketanserin Reverses the Acute Response to LSD…](https://local.corpus/ketanserin-reverses-lsd-2023); [Acute dose-dependent LSD…](https://local.corpus/lsd-dose-bdnf-2021)).
- Data for psilocybin remain insufficiently explicit to conclude, though parallels with LSD are plausible; small ayahuasca trials do not show significant plasma BDNF changes acutely; scopolamine did not elevate plasma BDNF in a negative antidepressant trial; for MDMA and rapastinel, human BDNF evidence is sparse or indeterminate in the present corpus ([Direct comparison of LSD and psilocybin…](https://local.corpus/lsd-psilocybin-comparison-2022); [Effects of Ayahuasca on REFE…](https://local.corpus/ayahuasca-refe-bdnf); [Negative trial of scopolamine…](https://local.corpus/scopolamine-bdnf-2019); [Rapastinel and BDNF in OCD…](https://local.corpus/rapastinel-ocd-bdnf-2018)).

### Biological and Methodological Considerations

- Platelet confounding: Serum BDNF derives largely from platelet degranulation during clotting. Ketamine-related increases in plasma BDNF (healthy studies) may reflect acute release from vascular/endothelial compartments or PBMCs, while serum BDNF may be insensitive over short intervals or confounded by platelet counts and release kinetics. Differences in anticoagulant (EDTA vs citrate), clot times, and spin protocols can alter BDNF by >2-fold.
- Timing: Rapid central plasticity may precede peripheral biomarker changes, particularly in illness. The observation that serial ketamine raises plasma BDNF over weeks aligns with a model whereby sustained neuroplastic remodeling and behavioral activation (e.g., increased activity, sleep normalization) secondarily elevate peripheral BDNF.
- Diagnosis and concomitants: Antidepressants, inflammation, and metabolic states modulate BDNF. In TRD, blunted BDNF reactivity or elevated platelet confounding may attenuate detectability. Healthy volunteer studies—stringently controlled for diet, activity, and timing—are better placed to detect acute plasma signals.
- Isoforms and compartments: Most studies measured total BDNF without distinguishing mature BDNF from proBDNF, which have opposing neural effects. Emerging work with cell type–specific extracellular vesicles (e.g., astrocyte-derived) suggests peripheral fractionation may better track central plasticity than bulk plasma/serum.

### Clinical and Translational Implications

- Peripheral BDNF should not be relied upon as a surrogate biomarker of acute clinical response to a single ketamine/esketamine dose in TRD, especially when measured in serum at 24 h. Lack of increase does not falsify central neuroplastic engagement.
- In healthy mechanistic studies, plasma BDNF provides a sensitive readout of acute psychoplastogenic action for ketamine and LSD, suitable for pharmacodynamic/pharmacogenomic studies and receptor-level dissection (e.g., 5-HT2A dependence for LSD).
- For trials aiming to link neuroplastic biomarkers to outcomes, standardized plasma protocols (platelet-poor or platelet-free), multiple acute and subacute timepoints, fasting morning draws, activity control, and mature/proBDNF differentiation are recommended.

## Recommendations for Future Studies

1. Matrix and assay standardization:
   - Use platelet-poor plasma (double spin) with a standardized anticoagulant (e.g., citrate), fixed draw-to-spin times, clotting control (for serum), and documented freeze–thaw cycles.
   - Measure both mature BDNF and proBDNF; consider PBMC gene expression and extracellular vesicle cargo as complementary indices.

2. Timing and design:
   - Sample across acute (1–6 h), subacute (24–48 h), and delayed (7–14 d) windows post-dose; in crossovers, ensure sufficient washout and pre-dose baselines.
   - Incorporate within-subject designs in healthy volunteers to maximize power for pharmacodynamic detection and receptor mechanism testing.

3. Population considerations:
   - Evaluate whether illness state blunts acute peripheral BDNF responses; stratify by diagnosis and inflammation; document concomitant medications and behavioral factors (sleep, exercise).

4. Multimodal integration:
   - Pair peripheral BDNF with central readouts (e.g., 7T MRS glutamate, resting-state connectivity, sleep slow-wave activity) to triangulate neuroplastic processes.

5. Reporting and data sharing:
   - Provide full descriptive statistics (means/SDs, change scores, pre–post correlations) to enable meta-analyses and robust variance modeling for crossover trials.

## Balanced Summary and Opinion

The human evidence indicates that some psychoplastogens—most reproducibly ketamine and low-dose LSD—acutely increase plasma BDNF in healthy individuals within hours of administration. However, this effect does not generalize across matrices, populations, and agents. Specifically, single-dose ketamine or esketamine does not reliably increase serum BDNF at 24 h in TRD, while repeated ketamine infusions elevate plasma BDNF over weeks. Ayahuasca does not appear to acutely alter plasma BDNF in small randomized trials. Data for psilocybin, MDMA, rapastinel, and scopolamine are sparse or inconsistent in the present corpus.

My overall conclusion is that peripheral BDNF—particularly when measured in plasma and with careful preanalytical control—can reflect acute psychoplastogenic pharmacodynamics in healthy volunteers but is an unreliable standalone biomarker of rapid antidepressant action in clinical settings. Heterogeneity in biological state, matrix choice (serum vs plasma), and timing strongly modulates detectability. Thus, claims that peripheral BDNF universally tracks rapid neuroplasticity in humans are not supported across agents and contexts. Future clinical trials should either adopt standardized plasma protocols with dense sampling and isoform-specific assays or pursue more specific peripheral compartments (e.g., extracellular vesicles) and pair them with central neuroimaging/EEG markers of plasticity.
