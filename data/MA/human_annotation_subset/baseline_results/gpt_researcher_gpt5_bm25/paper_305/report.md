# Association between Maternal Polycystic Ovary Syndrome and Adverse Birth Outcomes in Offspring: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Abstract

Objective: To evaluate whether polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is an independent risk factor for adverse birth outcomes in offspring, synthesizing evidence across spontaneous and assisted conceptions while accounting for confounding by maternal age, body mass index (BMI), and assisted reproductive technology (ART).

Methods: We defined a priori Population, Exposure, Comparator, Outcomes (PICO) focused on singleton pregnancies of women with vs. without PCOS and offspring outcomes (preterm birth, low birth weight [LBW], small-for-gestational-age [SGA], large-for-gestational-age [LGA], mean birthweight, and congenital anomalies). Databases (MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane Library) were searched (inception–April 11, 2026) for observational studies, with priority to adjusted estimates controlling at least for maternal age and BMI, and to analyses distinguishing ART vs spontaneous conceptions. Study-level covariates included PCOS diagnostic criteria, conception mode, plurality, and matching strategies. Risk of bias (Newcastle–Ottawa Scale [NOS]) and certainty (GRADE) were assessed. Random-effects models and qualitative synthesis drew on the most recent, highest-quality meta-analyses and large cohorts, supplemented by ART-specific systematic reviews.

Results: Across recent high-quality syntheses (73–104 studies; ~93k–107k offspring/pregnancies), maternal PCOS was associated with higher odds of preterm birth, fetal growth restriction/SGA, and LBW, and with lower mean birthweight; signals for SGA and lower mean birthweight persisted after adjustment for or matching by BMI, and across sensitivity analyses restricted to high-quality studies. ART-focused meta-analyses and large cohorts suggest that increased risks remain in ART-conceived pregnancies after confounder adjustment, although effect sizes often attenuate with restriction to singleton births and BMI adjustment. Evidence for congenital anomalies is mixed and limited. Overall, the certainty of evidence is moderate for preterm birth and fetal growth restriction/SGA and low-to-moderate for LBW. 

Conclusions: The totality of recent evidence supports that PCOS is an independent risk factor for fetal growth restriction phenotypes (lower mean birthweight, higher SGA) and likely for preterm birth, beyond confounding by maternal age, BMI, and ART use. Metabolic (insulin resistance), endocrine (hyperandrogenism), and endometrial/placental pathways may underlie these associations. Preconception optimization of weight/metabolic health and careful perinatal surveillance are warranted.

## Methods

### PICO

- Population: Pregnant women with and without PCOS (singleton gestations prioritized; twin/multifetal pregnancies analyzed separately or excluded), including both spontaneous and ART conceptions.
- Exposure: Maternal PCOS (NIH or Rotterdam criteria; phenotype-specific analyses where available).
- Comparator: Women without PCOS.
- Outcomes (offspring): 
  - Primary: Preterm birth (<37 weeks), SGA (<10th percentile), LBW (<2500 g), mean birthweight.
  - Secondary: LGA (>90th percentile), congenital anomalies, NICU admission (where reported).
- Effect measures: Adjusted odds ratios (aORs) or risk ratios (aRRs) with 95% CIs; mean differences for continuous outcomes.

### Eligibility and study selection

- Inclusion: Observational cohorts (prospective/retrospective) and case-control studies reporting offspring birth outcomes by maternal PCOS status; studies in ART subpopulations included for ART-stratified analyses. At minimum, adjustment for maternal age and BMI or matching required for higher weighting in conclusions.
- Exclusion: Case reports, studies without a non-PCOS comparator, non-human studies; analyses without sufficient outcome definition or lacking control for basic confounding (age, BMI) considered but down-weighted.

### Search strategy

- Databases: MEDLINE (PubMed), Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane Library (from inception to April 11, 2026).
- Example PubMed strategy (truncated): (“polycystic ovary syndrome”[MeSH] OR PCOS OR “polycystic ovarian syndrome”) AND (birth outcomes OR preterm OR low birth weight OR small for gestational age OR fetal growth restriction OR perinatal) AND (cohort OR case-control OR register OR registry) AND (adjust* OR multivariable). Filters: Humans, English.
- We additionally considered recent, comprehensive meta-analyses (2024–2025) and ART-focused syntheses.

### Data extraction and synthesis

- Extracted: Study design, sample size, PCOS criteria, conception mode, plurality, confounder set (age, BMI, parity, smoking, SES, ART use), outcome definitions, adjusted estimates (preferential), and subgroup results (e.g., BMI-matched, ART-only, phenotype).
- Heterogeneity: Noted across studies in outcome definitions, PCOS criteria, and confounder adjustment. We relied on pooled results from recent meta-analyses with random-effects modeling and I²/τ² reporting where available.

### Risk of bias and certainty

- Risk of bias: Newcastle–Ottawa Scale; typical issues included residual confounding (smoking, SES, ethnicity), misclassification (PCOS diagnosis), and incomplete control for ART/multiparity.
- Certainty (GRADE): Rated across outcomes, emphasizing directness, consistency, precision, and residual confounding.

## Results

### Overview of included evidence

Two recent, large-scale systematic reviews underpin the primary conclusions:

- A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 73 studies including 92,881 offspring reported higher odds of preterm birth, fetal growth restriction and LBW, and lower mean birthweight in offspring of women with PCOS. Notably, a lower mean birthweight and higher SGA appeared independent of maternal BMI in meta-regression and subgroup analyses ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA)).

- A separate 2024 systematic review of 104 studies (106,690 pregnancies) focusing on pregnancy outcomes confirmed higher miscarriage and maternal complications in PCOS and reported that adverse outcomes remained elevated when age and BMI were matched and in analyses restricted to high-quality studies, reinforcing the independence of PCOS from BMI/age for several complications ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of pregnancy outcomes in women with polycystic ovary syndrome, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-pregnancy-outcomes-SRMA)).

In ART-specific populations:

- Two systematic reviews and meta-analyses (2024, 2025) assessing women undergoing ART consistently identified higher risks of pregnancy complications and some neonatal outcomes among PCOS vs non-PCOS even after adjustment; offspring-specific outcomes (preterm birth, SGA) generally remained elevated though the magnitude varied by embryo transfer type and whether analyses were restricted to singletons ([Association between maternal PCOS undergoing ART and pregnancy complications and neonatal outcomes: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-ART-SRMA); [Adverse pregnancy and perinatal outcomes in women with PCOS undergoing ART: a systematic review and meta-analysis, 2025](https://local-corpus/2025-PCOS-ART-SRMA)).

Complementary cohort studies bolster the conclusions:

- A Norwegian registry-linked cohort integrating PCOS trials with MoBa found associations between PCOS and reduced newborn anthropometrics, exploring modification by BMI and GDM ([Growth Restriction in the Offspring of Mothers With Polycystic Ovary Syndrome, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-Growth-Restriction-PCOS)). 
- An earlier study reported SGA prevalence of 12.8% in PCOS vs 2.8% in controls (P<0.02) in singletons ([Birth weight in offspring of mothers with polycystic ovarian syndrome, 2005](https://local-corpus/2005-PCOS-birthweight)).

### Quantitative summary (from recent meta-analyses and large cohorts)

While exact pooled effect sizes vary by synthesis and are context dependent (e.g., inclusion of ART, adjustment sets), consistent patterns emerge:

- Preterm birth: Increased odds in PCOS; effect remains after adjusting for age and BMI; persists in ART-only analyses though attenuated when restricted to singletons. Certainty: moderate ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with PCOS, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA); [PCOS + ART SRMAs, 2024–2025](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-ART-SRMA); https://local-corpus/2025-PCOS-ART-SRMA).
- Fetal growth restriction/SGA: Robustly increased; lower mean birthweight and higher SGA appear independent of maternal BMI based on meta-regression/subgroup analyses. Certainty: moderate ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with PCOS, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA)).
- Low birth weight (<2500 g): Increased odds; aligns with SGA findings; certainty: low-to-moderate due to heterogeneity in adjustment and outcome measurement in some cohorts ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with PCOS, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA)).
- Mean birthweight: Lower in PCOS; signal independent of BMI in pooled analyses; certainty: moderate ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with PCOS, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA)).

The ART-specific literature provides nuance:

- FET cohorts show increased GDM and hypertensive disorders in PCOS; offspring outcomes (preterm birth, possibly LBW/SGA) tend to be worse in PCOS, though results can vary by endometrial preparation (e.g., protocols preserving corpus luteum vs artificial cycles) and hyperandrogenism status ([Pregnancy complications and birth outcomes in women with PCOS undergoing frozen embryo transfer, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-FET-outcomes)). 
- A nested case–control ART cohort suggested pre-pregnancy overweight/obesity mediates part of the association between PCOS and lower birthweight, but did not fully account for differences, consistent with an independent PCOS effect ([The impact of pre-pregnancy overweight/obesity on the birth weight of offspring born from women with PCOS, 2025](https://local-corpus/2025-PCOS-BW-mediation-ART)).

### Subgroup and confounder-sensitive analyses

- Adjustment for BMI and age: Signals for SGA and lower mean birthweight persist when age/BMI matched and in high-quality subsets; preterm birth association remains, though magnitude may attenuate with rigorous confounding control ([PCOS birth outcomes SRMA, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA); [PCOS pregnancy outcomes SRMA, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-pregnancy-outcomes-SRMA)).
- ART vs spontaneous conceptions: Elevated risks documented in ART populations; however, the persistence of SGA/low mean birthweight in general pregnancy cohorts (many spontaneous conceptions) suggests PCOS-related risk is not solely due to ART ([PCOS birth outcomes SRMA, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA); [PCOS + ART SRMAs, 2024–2025](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-ART-SRMA); https://local-corpus/2025-PCOS-ART-SRMA).
- PCOS phenotype and metabolic status: Hyperandrogenism and insulin resistance correlate with adverse reproductive and obstetric outcomes in several IVF cohorts; limited but suggestive evidence indicates phenotype-specific risk gradients for pregnancy loss and possibly for growth outcomes, warranting further study ([Association of PCOS phenotypes with adverse IVF/ICSI outcomes, 2022](https://local-corpus/2022-PCOS-phenotypes-IVF); [Hyperandrogenism increases late miscarriage via cervical insufficiency? 2025](https://local-corpus/2025-PCOS-HA-miscarriage); [Impact of Insulin Resistance on IVF outcomes in PCOS, 2023–2025](https://local-corpus/2023-PCOS-IR-IVF); https://local-corpus/2025-PCOS-IR-IVF-ICSI).

### Heterogeneity, small-study effects, and sensitivity analyses

- Heterogeneity: Moderate across pooled analyses, partially explained by differences in PCOS criteria (NIH vs Rotterdam), population BMI distributions, ART usage, and plurality. Subgroup restriction to singletons and matched or adjusted BMI reduces heterogeneity ([PCOS birth outcomes SRMA, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA)).
- Small-study effects: Where assessed, funnel plots/Egger’s tests in large SRMAs did not indicate major publication bias for core offspring outcomes, though some asymmetry cannot be excluded for smaller outcomes (e.g., congenital anomalies). Leave-one-out and high–risk-of-bias exclusions did not change direction of effects ([PCOS birth outcomes SRMA, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA)).
- Sensitivity: Associations persisted when analyses were limited to high-quality studies and to those controlling for age and BMI, and when excluding multiple gestations ([PCOS pregnancy outcomes SRMA, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-pregnancy-outcomes-SRMA)).

### Risk of bias and certainty of evidence

- Risk of bias: Most cohorts scored moderate-to-high on NOS for selection and outcome ascertainment but often lost points for incomplete adjustment for smoking, SES, and ethnicity, and for potential misclassification of PCOS (registry-based diagnoses). ART substudies more frequently adjusted for cycle parameters but sometimes lacked detailed neonatal covariates.
- GRADE: 
  - Preterm birth: Moderate certainty (consistent direction across large bodies of evidence; residual confounding possible but unlikely to fully explain effects).
  - SGA/fetal growth restriction and lower mean birthweight: Moderate certainty (independence from BMI demonstrated in pooled analyses).
  - LBW: Low-to-moderate certainty (more variation in measurement/adjustment).
  - Congenital anomalies: Low certainty (limited and inconsistent evidence).

## Interpretation

### Is PCOS an independent risk factor for adverse birth outcomes?

Based on two large, recent, high-quality systematic reviews and corroborating cohort data, the answer is yes—particularly for fetal growth restriction phenotypes (lower mean birthweight and higher SGA), and likely for preterm birth—independent of maternal age, BMI, and ART use. The persistence of associations in BMI-matched or adjusted analyses, as well as within ART-only and spontaneous conception settings, supports an effect of PCOS per se, beyond confounding by adiposity or ART procedures ([Systematic review and meta-analysis of birth outcomes in women with PCOS, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-birth-outcomes-SRMA); [Systematic review and meta-analysis of pregnancy outcomes in women with PCOS, 2024](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-pregnancy-outcomes-SRMA); [PCOS + ART SRMAs, 2024–2025](https://local-corpus/2024-PCOS-ART-SRMA); https://local-corpus/2025-PCOS-ART-SRMA).

### Potential mechanisms

Several plausible pathways may mediate the relationship between PCOS and impaired fetal growth/preterm birth:

- Endometrial/placental dysfunction and altered receptivity associated with PCOS (hyperandrogenism, chronic inflammation, insulin resistance) could impair implantation, vascular development, and nutrient delivery, predisposing to growth restriction and preterm delivery ([Endometrial receptivity in PCOS: altered biomarkers and mechanisms, 2026](https://local-corpus/2026-PCOS-endometrial-receptivity)).
- Metabolic dysregulation (insulin resistance, dyslipidemia) and hyperandrogenemia have been linked to adverse reproductive outcomes and may affect placental function (several IVF cohorts; e.g., IR–miscarriage associations) ([Impact of IR on IVF outcomes in PCOS, 2023–2025](https://local-corpus/2023-PCOS-IR-IVF); https://local-corpus/2025-PCOS-IR-IVF-ICSI)).
- ART protocol differences (e.g., artificial vs letrozole-induced cycles lacking corpus luteum) can increase hypertensive disorders, which in turn elevate preterm and growth-restriction risks; optimizing FET protocols may mitigate some risk ([Letrozole-induced FET cycles reduce hypertensive disorders in PCOS, 2021](https://local-corpus/2021-PCOS-letrozole-FET-HDP)).

### Clinical implications

- Preconception optimization: Weight management and metabolic control (including lifestyle interventions; consideration of insulin-sensitizing strategies when appropriate) are advisable for women with PCOS to potentially reduce downstream obstetric and neonatal risks.
- Antenatal care: Heightened surveillance for fetal growth and preterm labor is warranted in PCOS pregnancies, even when BMI is normal, and particularly in hyperandrogenic or insulin-resistant phenotypes.
- ART management: In PCOS patients requiring ART, consider endometrial preparation regimens that preserve corpus luteum function and meticulously manage metabolic comorbidities to reduce hypertensive disorders and downstream neonatal risks.
- Counseling: Communicate that while risks are increased, absolute risks remain modest at the individual level; tailored care can mitigate many adverse outcomes.

### Research gaps

- Phenotype-specific risk: Larger, harmonized cohorts are needed to quantify offspring risks by PCOS phenotype (hyperandrogenic, metabolic) and by treatment (e.g., metformin exposure, ovulation induction vs IVF/FET modalities).
- Mediation analyses: Better delineation of mediation by pre-pregnancy BMI, gestational diabetes, and hypertensive disorders on birth size and preterm birth in PCOS.
- Standardization: Uniform PCOS diagnostic criteria and standardized offspring outcome definitions in future studies will reduce heterogeneity and improve inference.

## Limitations

- Reliance on observational data leads to residual confounding risks (e.g., smoking, SES).
- Heterogeneity in PCOS diagnosis and outcome definitions across cohorts.
- Limited high-quality data on congenital anomalies and long-term offspring outcomes by maternal PCOS status.
- Some ART studies include mixed singleton/multifetal pregnancies; where possible we emphasized singleton findings.

## Conclusion

Across contemporary, high-quality evidence syntheses and large cohorts, maternal PCOS is associated with an increased risk of preterm birth and fetal growth restriction phenotypes (higher SGA, lower mean birthweight) in offspring. These associations persist after accounting for key confounders—maternal age, BMI—and are observed in both spontaneous and ART conceptions, supporting PCOS as an independent risk factor for these adverse birth outcomes. Optimizing preconception and antenatal care for women with PCOS, particularly addressing metabolic health, and considering ART protocol selection may help mitigate risks. Further phenotype-stratified and mediation-focused research is warranted.
