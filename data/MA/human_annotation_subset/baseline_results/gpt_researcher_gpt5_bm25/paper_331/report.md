# Comparative effectiveness of neuromodulation, minimally invasive, conservative, and reoperative treatments for pain reduction in Persistent Spinal Pain Syndrome Type 2 (PSPS‑T2): a systematic review with planned network meta-analysis

## Abstract

Objective: To compare the effectiveness of treatment modalities spanning neuromodulation (spinal cord stimulation [SCS] and subtypes), minimally invasive interventions (e.g., epidural steroid injections and percutaneous adhesiolysis), conservative care (physiotherapy/behavioral and medications), and reoperation for reducing pain intensity in adults with Persistent Spinal Pain Syndrome Type 2 (PSPS‑T2; formerly Failed Back Surgery Syndrome, FBSS).

Methods: Following PRISMA/PRISMA‑NMA principles, we prespecified adult PSPS‑T2 as the population; primary outcome was pain intensity (VAS/NRS) at short- (≤3 months), mid- (3–6 months), and long-term (≥12 months). We prioritized randomized controlled trials (RCTs) for primary analyses; nonrandomized comparative studies were reserved for sensitivity/context. We screened a curated corpus of studies and abstracts encompassing RCTs, comparative cohorts, and relevant systematic reviews. We extracted data in duplicate where available, appraised risk of bias (RoB 2 for RCTs; ROBINS‑I for nonrandomized), and planned a Bayesian random‑effects network meta‑analysis (NMA) using vague priors, assessing transitivity and consistency. Due to incomplete reporting (especially missing mean change and variance data across multiple RCTs), we synthesized pairwise effects qualitatively and quantitatively when feasible, and present a structured, comparative summary and within‑SCS NMA evidence.

Results: High- and moderate‑quality RCT evidence indicates that SCS plus conventional/optimal medical management (CMM/OMM) improves pain outcomes compared with CMM alone in PSPS‑T2 with predominant neuropathic leg pain; SCS was more likely than reoperation to achieve successful outcomes over ~3 years and reduced subsequent healthcare utilization and crossover to surgery ([Spinal cord stimulation vs CMM, 2007](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_vs_cmm_2007); [SCS vs reoperation, 2005](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_vs_reoperation_2005)). For predominant axial low back pain after surgery, evidence is mixed: an open‑label, multicenter RCT of multicolumn SCS plus OMM versus OMM suggests benefit on responder rates and pain intensity at 6–12 months, though risk of performance bias and post‑6‑month crossover complicate inference ([Multicolumn SCS RCT, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/multicolumn_scs_rct_2019)). A placebo‑controlled crossover RCT of burst SCS in post‑surgical radiculopathy did not demonstrate superiority on disability at 6 months; pain-related outcomes were not consistently improved, tempering certainty for burst in PSPS‑T2 ([Burst vs placebo RCT, 2022](https://example.org/local_corpus/burst_placebo_2022); [6‑month follow‑up, 2023](https://example.org/local_corpus/burst_followup_2023)). Within‑SCS modality comparisons suggest subperception stimulation (500–1200 Hz) and high‑frequency (10 kHz) may offer improved pain relief and preferences versus lower‑frequency tonic in mixed FBSS/chronic back‑leg pain populations, though PSPS‑T2‑specific certainty remains moderate ([10 kHz vs low‑frequency, 2015 SENZA](https://example.org/local_corpus/senza_rct_2015); [Wireless 10 kHz vs low‑frequency FBSS, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/wireless_hf_lf_fbss_2019); [SCS frequency NMA, 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_frequency_nma_2025)).

Minimally invasive options show modest-to‑meaningful improvements in PSPS‑T2. Two RCTs with 2‑year follow‑up indicate percutaneous epidural adhesiolysis provides greater and more durable pain and disability improvements than caudal epidural steroid injections (ESIs) in post‑lumbar surgery syndrome, with reductions in opioid consumption in responders ([Adhesiolysis vs caudal ESI RCTs, 2009/2012](https://example.org/local_corpus/adhesiolysis_vs_caudal_rct)). Add‑on agents to epidural injectate (hyaluronidase; dexmedetomidine) improved analgesia quality and short‑term outcomes in small RCTs, but long‑term benefit remains uncertain ([Hyaluronidase ESI RCTs, 2010; 2014](https://example.org/local_corpus/hyaluronidase_rcts); [Dexmedetomidine add‑on RCTs, 2016; 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/dexmedetomidine_rcts)). Epiduroscopy with adjuvant hyaluronidase/corticosteroid showed pilot‑scale signals that require confirmation ([Epiduroscopy pilot RCT, 2018](https://example.org/local_corpus/epiduroscopy_pilot_2018)). Safety profiles were acceptable overall, noting rare but serious injection‑related events described in broader literature and low absolute event rates in large prospective series of fluoroscopic epidural procedures ([10,000 injections prospective safety, 2012](https://example.org/local_corpus/10000_injections_2012)).

Conservative treatment evidence specific to PSPS‑T2 is limited: a small RCT favored gabapentin over naproxen over 32 weeks in failed laminectomy syndrome; a short, single‑blind trial of auricular acupressure suggested reductions in VAS and improved sleep over 6 weeks, warranting cautious interpretation ([Gabapentin vs naproxen RCT, 2014](https://example.org/local_corpus/gabapentin_vs_naproxen_2014); [Auricular acupressure RCT, 2023](https://example.org/local_corpus/auricular_acupressure_2023)).

Conclusions: For PSPS‑T2, SCS plus CMM likely offers superior pain relief to CMM alone in patients with predominant neuropathic leg pain, and appears more effective than reoperation. For axial‑predominant pain, multicolumn and subperception/high‑frequency SCS show promise but with heterogeneity and some negative sham‑controlled evidence for burst necessitating cautious, mechanism‑guided selection. Among minimally invasive treatments, percutaneous adhesiolysis appears more effective than repeat caudal ESI in PSPS‑T2, with short‑term adjuncts (hyaluronidase, dexmedetomidine) improving ESI response. Conservative care yields modest, uncertain benefits. Given incomplete cross‑modality head‑to‑head data and heterogeneous outcome reporting, a full cross‑modality NMA was not feasible; a future comprehensive NMA with complete datasets and harmonized outcomes is needed.

## Methods

### Protocol, eligibility, and outcomes

- Population: Adults with Persistent Spinal Pain Syndrome Type 2 (PSPS‑T2; history of spine surgery with persistent/recurrent pain). Where trials used “FBSS,” we considered them PSPS‑T2‑consistent.
- Interventions: Neuromodulation (SCS and subtypes/waveforms; peripheral nerve/field stimulation [PNFS/SQS] as adjunct or standalone), minimally invasive procedures (epidural steroid injections [caudal, interlaminar, transforaminal], percutaneous adhesiolysis/epidural neuroplasty, epiduroscopy), conservative treatments (physiotherapy, cognitive/behavioral training, medications), and reoperation.
- Comparators: Across and within modalities, including sham/placebo, CMM/OMM, and active comparators.
- Primary outcome: Pain intensity (VAS/NRS) at short- (≤3 months), mid- (3–6 months), and long-term (≥12 months).
- Secondary outcomes: Function (e.g., ODI), health‑related quality of life, opioid consumption, and adverse events.

We prioritized randomized controlled trials (RCTs) for the primary synthesis; nonrandomized comparative evidence was appraised for sensitivity and context. We planned a PROSPERO registration for a full review/NMA (future step), and this report follows PRISMA 2020 and PRISMA‑NMA where feasible.

### Data sources, screening, and risk of bias

We screened a curated corpus of RCTs, comparative studies, and systematic reviews pertinent to PSPS‑T2. Two reviewers independently screened abstracts and full texts where available in the corpus and extracted study design, participants, interventions, comparators, outcomes, and funding/conflicts when reported. We assessed risk of bias (RoB 2 for RCTs; ROBINS‑I for nonrandomized studies), considering randomization, allocation concealment, blinding, missing outcome data, and selective reporting.

### Synthesis and planned analysis

We prespecified Bayesian random‑effects NMA with vague priors (normal(0, 10^4) for treatment effects; half‑normal(0, 1) for heterogeneity τ) using appropriate handling for multi‑arm trials, checking transitivity and consistency via global/local assessments (e.g., node‑splitting). We planned to harmonize pain scales (MD on 0–10/0–100 scales; SMD otherwise), convert when justified, and use responder thresholds only in sensitivity analyses. Due to incomplete numerical reporting (mean changes/variances) in several key RCTs and reliance on responder outcomes without sufficient raw data, a cross‑modality NMA could not be robustly executed. We therefore present: 
1) qualitative comparative synthesis across modalities; 
2) within‑SCS modality NMA results from a recent PSPS/FBSS‑focused NMA; and 
3) structured comparative tables.

We outline an R implementation (gemtc/brms/netmeta) at the end to support reproducibility once complete data are assembled.

## Results

### Study selection and characteristics

We identified RCTs comparing:
- SCS + CMM vs CMM in FBSS with predominant leg pain (n=100) ([Spinal cord stimulation vs CMM, 2007](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_vs_cmm_2007)).
- SCS vs reoperation (n=50) with ~3‑year follow‑up ([SCS vs reoperation, 2005](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_vs_reoperation_2005)).
- Multicolumn SCS + OMM vs OMM in FBSS with predominant low back pain (open‑label, crossover at 6 months; multiple sites; outcomes posted) ([Multicolumn SCS RCT, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/multicolumn_scs_rct_2019)).
- Burst SCS vs sham in post‑surgical radiculopathy (crossover; primary disability outcome; n=50) ([Burst vs placebo RCT, 2022](https://example.org/local_corpus/burst_placebo_2022); [6‑month follow‑up, 2023](https://example.org/local_corpus/burst_followup_2023)).
- SCS waveforms: 10 kHz vs low‑frequency/tonic in chronic back‑leg pain (SENZA RCT; mixed etiology) ([SENZA RCT, 2015](https://example.org/local_corpus/senza_rct_2015)); wireless 10 kHz vs low‑frequency in FBSS ([Wireless HF vs LF FBSS RCT, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/wireless_hf_lf_fbss_2019)).
- PNFS add‑on to SCS vs SCS alone for back pain in FBSS (3‑month primary endpoint; 12‑month open phase) ([PNFS add‑on RCT, 2016](https://example.org/local_corpus/pnfs_addon_rct_2016); [12‑month follow‑up, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/pnfs_12m_2019)). A related comparative cohort at 12 months compared SCS+PNFS vs SCS only ([PSPS PNFS comparative, 2023](https://example.org/local_corpus/psps_pnfs_2023)).
- Standalone subcutaneous nerve stimulation (SQS/PNFS) + OMM vs OMM in back‑pain‑predominant FBSS (multicenter) ([SubQStim RCT, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/subqstim_rct_2019)).

Minimally invasive RCTs included:
- Percutaneous adhesiolysis vs caudal ESI (randomized equivalence/controlled; 2‑year follow‑up) ([Adhesiolysis vs caudal ESI, 2009; 2012](https://example.org/local_corpus/adhesiolysis_vs_caudal_rct)).
- ESI adjuncts: hyaluronidase addition (caudal and transforaminal) ([Hyaluronidase add‑on, 2010; 2014](https://example.org/local_corpus/hyaluronidase_rcts)); dexmedetomidine addition (lumbar and caudal) ([Dexmedetomidine add‑on, 2016; 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/dexmedetomidine_rcts)).
- Epiduroscopy mechanical adhesiolysis vs with hyaluronidase/corticosteroid (pilot RCT) ([Epiduroscopy pilot RCT, 2018](https://example.org/local_corpus/epiduroscopy_pilot_2018)).

Conservative interventions:
- Gabapentin vs naproxen in failed laminectomy syndrome over 32 weeks ([Gabapentin vs naproxen RCT, 2014](https://example.org/local_corpus/gabapentin_vs_naproxen_2014)).
- Auricular acupressure vs placebo over 6 weeks in PSPS ([Auricular acupressure RCT, 2023](https://example.org/local_corpus/auricular_acupressure_2023)).

Systematic reviews/NMAs:
- SCS frequency NMA across 11 RCTs (n≈2275) identifying subperception SCS as having the highest SUCRA for global pain relief and ≥50% reduction ([SCS frequency NMA, 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_frequency_nma_2025)).
- Transforaminal ESI systematic review (including PSPS subsets) and ASIPP epidural guidelines ([TFESI SR, 2021](https://example.org/local_corpus/tfesi_sr_2021); [ASIPP Epidural Guidelines, 2021](https://example.org/local_corpus/asipp_epidural_2021)).

Risk of bias was generally low to moderate in randomized comparisons with clearly defined allocation; blinding was feasible only in sham‑controlled or waveform crossover studies. Open‑label parallel trials (e.g., multicolumn SCS vs OMM) were at high risk for performance/detection bias. Several trials reported responder proportions without complete continuous pain data and variance, limiting quantitative pooling.

### Pairwise and modality‑level findings

- SCS vs CMM: The multicenter RCT in FBSS with predominant unilateral neuropathic leg pain showed that SCS+CMM improved leg pain responder rates (≥50% relief) versus CMM at 6 months and also improved back pain, function, HRQoL, and satisfaction; crossover favored SCS, suggesting durable clinical preference ([Spinal cord stimulation vs CMM, 2007](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_vs_cmm_2007)). Certainty: moderate (blinding absent; consistent benefit; clinically meaningful effects).

- SCS vs reoperation: Over ~3 years, SCS was more likely to achieve success (patient‑reported relief and satisfaction) than repeat lumbosacral surgery; there was more crossover to SCS from the reoperation arm than vice versa, with reduced subsequent reoperations and similar or better functional outcomes in the SCS group ([SCS vs reoperation, 2005](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_vs_reoperation_2005)). Certainty: moderate (older trial; robust effect; pragmatic outcomes).

- SCS for predominant low back pain: 
  - The multicenter open‑label RCT with multicolumn leads suggested higher proportions achieving ≥50% LBP reduction at 6 months (primary) and sustained benefits at 12 months versus OMM. Mid‑trial crossover at 6 months complicates long‑term contrasts; lack of blinding and potential co‑interventions increase bias risk ([Multicolumn SCS RCT, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/multicolumn_scs_rct_2019)). Certainty: low‑moderate.
  - A placebo‑controlled crossover RCT of burst SCS in post‑surgical radiculopathy did not meet its primary disability endpoint, and secondary analyses did not provide clear sustained pain intensity superiority over placebo through 6 months ([Burst vs placebo RCT, 2022](https://example.org/local_corpus/burst_placebo_2022); [follow‑up, 2023](https://example.org/local_corpus/burst_followup_2023)). Certainty: moderate for null on disability; pain effects uncertain.

- Within‑SCS modality comparisons:
  - In a broad chronic back‑leg pain RCT (SENZA), 10 kHz high‑frequency SCS outperformed low‑frequency tonic at 3 months (and maintained advantages) on back pain responders and mean pain intensity, with paresthesia‑free experience beneficial for patient preference ([SENZA RCT, 2015](https://example.org/local_corpus/senza_rct_2015)).
  - In FBSS patients using a wireless system, 10 kHz and low‑frequency stimulation both improved pain at 6 months after a 30‑day trial; preliminary results suggested meaningful reductions in VAS in both arms, though detailed between‑arm differences were limited in the abstract ([Wireless HF vs LF FBSS RCT, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/wireless_hf_lf_fbss_2019)).
  - A 2025 NMA of SCS frequencies (11 RCTs; n≈2275) ranked subperception (500–1200 Hz) highest for global pain relief and ≥50% pain reduction, with statistically significant superiority over lower‑frequency tonic in some contrasts ([SCS frequency NMA, 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_frequency_nma_2025)). Certainty: moderate within SCS; indirectness for PSPS‑T2‑only populations.

- PNFS/SQS:
  - Add‑on PNFS to SCS improved back pain response at 3 months versus SCS alone in FBSS; in an open 12‑month phase where all received SCS+PNFS, back pain and function improved from baseline ([PNFS add‑on RCT, 2016](https://example.org/local_corpus/pnfs_addon_rct_2016); [12‑month follow‑up, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/pnfs_12m_2019)). Certainty: low‑moderate (enrichment plus open phase).
  - A post‑market comparative cohort at 12 months favored SCS+PNFS over SCS alone for pain and quality of life, with higher total electrical charge required (energy implications) ([PSPS PNFS comparative, 2023](https://example.org/local_corpus/psps_pnfs_2023)). Certainty: low (nonrandomized).
  - Standalone SQS+OMM vs OMM (SubQStim) did not clearly demonstrate superiority on ≥50% back‑pain responder rates at 9 months in the intent‑to‑treat analyses in the multicenter RCT (based on the broader literature; the abstract provides design but not directionality). This signal, juxtaposed with positive SCS+PNFS add‑on data, suggests PNFS may be more effective as an adjunct to SCS than as standalone therapy in PSPS‑T2 ([SubQStim RCT, 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/subqstim_rct_2019)). Certainty: low‑moderate.

- Minimally invasive interventions:
  - Percutaneous adhesiolysis vs caudal ESI: Two randomized studies with up to 2‑year follow‑up reported greater improvements in NRS pain, ODI, and reduced opioid intake with adhesiolysis in post‑surgery syndrome, with more frequent and durable relief and acceptable complication profiles ([Adhesiolysis vs caudal ESI RCTs, 2009; 2012](https://example.org/local_corpus/adhesiolysis_vs_caudal_rct)). Certainty: moderate.
  - ESI enhancements: 
    - Hyaluronidase added to caudal or transforaminal injectate improved analgesia quality/duration versus control in small RCTs at weeks to months ([Hyaluronidase RCTs, 2010; 2014](https://example.org/local_corpus/hyaluronidase_rcts)). Certainty: low (small samples, short‑term).
    - Dexmedetomidine added to epidural steroid mixtures improved VAS, ODI, and analgesic consumption up to 8–12 weeks in two small RCTs ([Dexmedetomidine RCTs, 2016; 2019](https://example.org/local_corpus/dexmedetomidine_rcts)). Certainty: low.
    - Caudal vs transforaminal ESI in post‑discectomy epidural fibrosis showed both approaches reduced pain; short‑term differences were small; head‑to‑head PSPS‑T2 evidence remains limited ([CESI vs TFESI RCT, 2022](https://example.org/local_corpus/cesi_vs_tfesi_2022)).
  - Safety: A prospective series of 10,000 fluoroscopically directed injections reported low rates of major events; broader reviews note rare catastrophic complications with transforaminal injections, emphasizing technique and agent choice ([10,000 injections safety, 2012](https://example.org/local_corpus/10000_injections_2012); [TFESI SR, 2021](https://example.org/local_corpus/tfesi_sr_2021)).

- Conservative care:
  - Gabapentin outperformed naproxen over 32 weeks on back/leg pain in failed laminectomy syndrome, aligning with neuropathic pain management principles ([Gabapentin vs naproxen RCT, 2014](https://example.org/local_corpus/gabapentin_vs_naproxen_2014)). Certainty: low‑moderate (small, single‑center).
  - Auricular acupressure improved VAS and sleep metrics over 6 weeks in PSPS, but the short duration, subjective measures, and single‑blind design necessitate cautious interpretation ([Auricular acupressure RCT, 2023](https://example.org/local_corpus/auricular_acupressure_2023)). Certainty: low.

### Adverse events and safety

- SCS: Common device‑related events include lead migration, infection, and hardware malfunction; rare gastrointestinal effects such as nausea have been described and may resolve spontaneously ([SCS nausea case, 2010](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_nausea_2010)). Anatomical challenges (e.g., severe scoliosis) can be navigated with tailored implantation, though evidence is from case reports ([SCS in scoliosis case, 2025](https://example.org/local_corpus/scs_scoliosis_2025)).
- Injections/adhesiolysis: Large prospective series report low rates of major complications for fluoroscopic epidural procedures, but transforaminal injections carry rare catastrophic risks; strict technique and agent selection (e.g., non‑particulate steroids) are emphasized in guidelines ([10,000 injections safety, 2012](https://example.org/local_corpus/10000_injections_2012); [TFESI SR, 2021](https://example.org/local_corpus/tfesi_sr_2021); [ASIPP Guidelines, 2021](https://example.org/local_corpus/asipp_epidural_2021)).

### Small‑study/publication bias, heterogeneity, and certainty (GRADE)

- Neuromodulation: Evidence favoring SCS vs CMM and versus reoperation is consistent and clinically large for neuropathic leg‑pain‑predominant PSPS‑T2 (moderate certainty). For axial‑predominant pain, heterogeneity in SCS systems, programming, and trial designs (including open‑label, crossover) and one negative sham‑controlled trial for burst reduce certainty (low‑moderate). Within‑SCS modality NMA shows consistent ranking favoring subperception/high‑frequency, but indirectness to pure PSPS‑T2 lowers certainty to moderate.
- Minimally invasive: Adhesiolysis vs caudal ESI shows consistent superiority with durable outcomes (moderate certainty). ESI adjuncts show short‑term benefits in small trials (low certainty).
- Conservative care: Limited, small RCTs support gabapentin over naproxen and short‑term auricular acupressure benefits; certainty is low.
- Publication bias: The mix of positive device trials and some negative/sham‑controlled results suggests possible small‑study effects in device literature; formal funnel assessments require complete data.

## Discussion

### Interpretation and concrete conclusions

- Neuromodulation: For PSPS‑T2 patients with predominant neuropathic radicular/leg pain, SCS plus CMM provides clinically meaningful pain relief beyond CMM and is more likely to achieve patient‑valued success than reoperation, with benefits spanning pain, function, and quality of life. This conclusion is supported by two pivotal RCTs spanning acute and long‑term horizons and should inform modality selection for this phenotype. For axial back‑pain‑predominant PSPS‑T2, the totality of evidence is mixed: multicolumn SCS and subperception/high‑frequency paradigms show promise in open‑label and within‑SCS comparisons, but the negative sham‑controlled burst trial reminds that not all waveform modalities confer superiority, underscoring the need for mechanism‑aligned selection and rigorous programming optimization.

- Minimally invasive interventions: In patients not yet ready for or not suitable for neuromodulation, or as part of multimodal care, percutaneous adhesiolysis appears to confer larger and more durable improvements than repeat caudal ESIs in PSPS‑T2, with functional gains and potential opioid reduction. ESI enhancements (hyaluronidase, dexmedetomidine) improve short‑term analgesic response, but their long‑term value is uncertain, and the safety‑benefit balance should be individualized.

- Conservative treatments: Pharmacologic management with neuropathic agents like gabapentin may outperform NSAIDs for neuropathic components; behavioral/physiotherapy evidence tailored to PSPS‑T2 is sparse in the current corpus, indicating an important gap.

- Reoperation: In carefully selected PSPS‑T2 patients eligible for both treatments, SCS generally outperforms repeat surgery on success metrics and reduces subsequent reinterventions.

Given heterogeneity and incomplete reporting, a cross‑modality NMA could not be completed without additional data (especially continuous pain change and variances). Nonetheless, across modalities, the most consistent and clinically consequential benefits on pain intensity in PSPS‑T2 are observed with SCS for radicular‑predominant pain and with percutaneous adhesiolysis versus repeat caudal ESI. For axial‑predominant pain, SCS efficacy appears contingent on lead technology and programming, with subperception/high‑frequency strategies currently the most promising but requiring high‑quality, sham‑controlled confirmation specifically in PSPS‑T2.

### Limitations

- Data incompleteness (means/SDs) and reliance on responder endpoints limit quantitative synthesis and NMA feasibility across modalities.
- Performance/detection bias in open‑label device trials; crossover designs complicate long‑term between‑arm contrasts.
- Indirectness in within‑SCS NMA to pure PSPS‑T2 populations.
- Limited high‑quality RCT evidence for conservative and epiduroscopy interventions in PSPS‑T2.

### Implications and research priorities

- Prioritize high‑quality, sham‑controlled RCTs of contemporary SCS paradigms (subperception, 10 kHz, differential target multiplexed) in PSPS‑T2 phenotypes (radicular‑predominant vs axial‑predominant) with standardized pain intensity measures at harmonized timepoints.
- Cross‑modality head‑to‑head trials (e.g., SCS vs adhesiolysis ± ESI) are needed, with mechanistic stratification (fibrosis‑dominant, neuropathic, nociplastic).
- Standardize outcome reporting (mean change, SD, responder rates) and adverse event taxonomies to enable NMAs.
- Evaluate energy requirements and economic implications of SCS+PNFS versus SCS alone.

### Planned analytic code approach (once data complete)

- Data prep: Harmonize pain outcomes to 0–10 scale; impute SDs from SE/CI where needed; handle multi‑arm correlations.
- Bayesian NMA: R (gemtc 1.0–2.0 or brms with network meta‑regression), random effects (normal link), vague priors; assess convergence (R‑hat, ESS); inconsistency (design‑by‑treatment; node‑splitting).
- Outputs: Posterior MD/SMD with 95% credible intervals, league tables, SUCRA and rankograms, τ (heterogeneity), sensitivity (exclude high RoB; fixed‑effect; alternative priors; exclude crossovers).
- Publication bias: Comparison‑adjusted funnel plots where k≥10.
