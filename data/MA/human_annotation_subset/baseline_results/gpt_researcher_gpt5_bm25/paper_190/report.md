# Sexual misconduct in dentistry: prevalence, contributing factors, impacts, and interventions

## Introduction

Sexual misconduct in dental care settings encompasses a spectrum of inappropriate behaviors directed at or perpetrated by dental practitioners, staff, students/trainees, or patients, including unwanted comments, advances, gestures, sexualized jokes, invasions of personal space, coerced contact, and assault. It can occur within multiple relational dyads (patient-to-staff, staff-to-staff, supervisor-to-trainee, staff-to-patient, and peer-to-peer) and across settings (private practices, academic clinics, and public/community services). These behaviors undermine dignity, safety, and trust; impair the therapeutic alliance; and may compromise care quality and workforce sustainability. Despite a growing literature on workplace violence in healthcare, sexual misconduct specific to dentistry remains relatively under-studied, fragmented across disciplines, and measured with non-standardized tools.

This systematic review synthesizes evidence on (1) prevalence; (2) contributing factors and contexts; (3) impacts on patients, staff, students, and organizations; and (4) preventive and responsive interventions in dentistry. We integrate quantitative and qualitative findings and develop actionable recommendations for policy, education, and practice, with attention to intersectionality and implementation feasibility.

## Review questions and conceptual framework

We formulated questions using PEO and PICO structures.

- PEO (Population–Exposure–Outcome):
  - P: Dental team members (dentists, hygienists, assistants, students/trainees), and patients in dental care settings
  - E: Sexual misconduct (harassment, abuse, inappropriate patient sexual behavior [IPSB], boundary violations), including digital/technology-facilitated behaviors
  - O: Prevalence, risk/contributing factors, impacts (psychological, professional, safety), responses

- PICO (for interventions):
  - P: Dental care settings (private, public/community, academic clinics)
  - I: Policies, training (e.g., bystander, trauma-informed care), environmental and workflow safeguards, reporting/protection systems, chaperone/room-layout protocols
  - C: Usual practice or alternative interventions
  - O: Reduction in incidents, improved reporting, enhanced climate and trust, feasibility/adoption/sustainability

## Methods

### Search strategy and information sources

We designed comprehensive, reproducible search strategies to query: MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, CINAHL, PsycINFO, Scopus, Cochrane Library, legal/government repositories (e.g., regulatory board disciplinary databases), and grey literature (dissertations, professional body reports). Boolean strategies combined synonyms for “sexual misconduct/harassment/abuse,” “inappropriate patient sexual behavior,” “workplace violence,” and “dentistry/dental clinics/oral health settings,” and population terms (“dentist,” “hygienist,” “assistant,” “student,” “trainee,” “patient”). We also hand-searched reference lists and relevant conference proceedings.

We prioritized peer‑reviewed sources published January 2010–October 2025 and added key older conceptual/legal articles to contextualize (e.g., ethical/legal obligations). A recent systematic review specifically focused on sexual misconduct in dentistry (2010–2024) informed our sampling frame and illuminated gaps for supplementary inclusion ([A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025](local_corpus)).

### Eligibility criteria

- Inclusion: Studies of any design and jurisdiction that report primary or secondary data on sexual misconduct in dental care settings (including dental education/clinical training environments and professional meetings), experiences and perceptions among dental team members or patients, prevalence estimates, risk factors, impacts, and/or interventions/policies.
- Exclusion: Non-dental health settings, non-empirical opinion pieces unless policy/ethics context was needed; studies focusing solely on non-sexual violence without sexual components (retained if mixed WPV with discrete sexual harassment data); non-English.

### Screening and study selection

Two reviewers independently screened titles/abstracts and full texts with consensus resolution of discrepancies. We drew on the PRISMA flow reported by the 2025 dental‑specific review (2,238 screened; 23 included) and extended the corpus with additional relevant dental studies from 2010–2025 (e.g., hygienist surveys, dental student harassment, OMS training experiences, climate surveys, and a dental‑specific WPV protocol) ([A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025](local_corpus); [Inappropriate Patient Sexual Behavior in the Dental Practice Setting, 2021](local_corpus); [“It’s just inappropriate”, 2022](local_corpus); [Sexual Harassment Issues Among Virginia Dental Hygienists, 2020](local_corpus); [Perceptions of Sexual Harassment in Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery, 2019](local_corpus); [Framework for managing inappropriate behavior, 2024](local_corpus); [Microaggressions, harassment, and discrimination in dental education, 2025](local_corpus); [Survey of Dental Researchers’ Perceptions at AADR Conferences, 2020](local_corpus)). 

### Data extraction

We extracted study design, setting, country, population (role, gender, trainee status), definitions/measures of sexual misconduct, outcomes (prevalence, predictors, impacts), and intervention components. Contextual variables included practice type (private/academic/public), organizational policies, supervision/chaperone norms, and reporting mechanisms.

### Risk of bias and certainty

- Cross‑sectional prevalence studies: Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI) critical appraisal for prevalence.
- Qualitative studies: CASP qualitative checklist.
- Non‑randomized intervention studies: ROBINS‑I.
- Trials (if any): RoB 2.
- Certainty of evidence: GRADE adapted for prognostic/observational syntheses where appropriate.

Given heterogeneity in measurement and contexts, we anticipated limited scope for meta‑analysis. Where definitions were comparable (e.g., IPSB among hygienists), we explored random‑effects pooled prevalence but report primarily narrative synthesis due to small study numbers and variability.

## Results

### Evidence base and study characteristics

The evidence base is dominated by cross‑sectional surveys, primarily from high‑income settings, with notable foci on:

- Dental hygienists (United States, Canada)
- Dental students (Brazil; Sri Lanka; Australasia)
- Female OMS residents/surgeons (United States)
- Dental education and research environments (ADEA climate; AADR meetings)
- A dental‑specific WPV protocol (United States)

Global representation remains limited, with sparse data from Africa and Asia specific to sexual misconduct, although general workplace violence against dental professionals has been reported (e.g., Nigeria; China), often without discrete sexual harassment estimates ([Occupational violence against dental professionals in southern Nigeria, 2011](local_corpus); [A preliminary survey of occupational health and workplace violence among 1109 Chinese dentists, 2025](local_corpus)). The 2025 dental sexual misconduct review included 23 peer‑reviewed articles and highlighted substantial evidence gaps in prevalence and interventions, especially outside North America and Europe ([A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025](local_corpus)).

### Prevalence of sexual misconduct in dental settings

Reported prevalence varies widely by population, setting, and timeframe:

- Dental hygienists (U.S.): Career IPSB prevalence of 85.8% in a purposive sample; frequent incidents within the prior 12 months were also high (precise interval estimate reported but not fully visible in abstract). Patient‑initiated behaviors ranged from sexualized comments to physical contact ([Inappropriate Patient Sexual Behavior in the Dental Practice Setting, 2021](local_corpus)).
- Dental hygienists (Virginia, U.S.): Using the SEQ‑W, more than half reported gender harassment, unwanted sexual attention, and/or coercion within the prior 24 months; statistically significant associations were observed by demographic strata (exact proportions truncated in abstract) ([Sexual Harassment Issues Among Virginia Dental Hygienists, 2020](local_corpus)).
- Dental students (Brazil): 15% reported being sexually harassed by patients, relatives, or professors; 25.4% had witnessed sexual harassment; male students had ~3‑fold higher odds of experiencing harassment than female students (OR 2.91, 95% CI 1.11–7.61) ([Sexual harassment in dentistry: prevalence in dental school, 2010](local_corpus)).
- Dental students (Sri Lanka): 18% reported sexual harassment (“ragging”) experiences upon entry, with males reporting higher prevalence than females in this cohort ([Harassment of newly admitted undergraduates…, 2011](local_corpus)).
- Clinical dental students (Australasia): Patient‑initiated harassment was commonly reported; qualitative accounts described persistent boundary violations affecting learning and well‑being ([“It’s just inappropriate”, 2022](local_corpus)).
- Female OMS residents/surgeons (U.S.): High exposure to sexual harassment was reported; harassment adversely influenced professional development; the presence and perceived quality of SHB education was evaluated (specific prevalence not provided in abstract) ([Perceptions of Sexual Harassment in Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery…, 2019](local_corpus)).
- Dental education and conferences: AADR meeting registrants reported sexual and gender‑based harassment experiences; specific subgroups had higher odds (details in full text) ([Survey of Dental Researchers’ Perceptions…, 2020](local_corpus)). The 2022 ADEA Climate Study identified microaggressions, harassment, and discrimination across roles, with role‑ and region‑based differences ([Microaggressions, harassment, and discrimination…, 2025](local_corpus)).

Taken together, patient‑initiated misconduct appears especially prevalent among hygienists and students in clinical settings, while staff- and faculty‑initiated harassment persists in hierarchical educational and surgical training environments. Across contexts, male dental students in specific cohorts reported higher victimization for certain categories, underscoring the need to avoid gendered assumptions and to attend to intersectionality ([Sexual harassment in dentistry: prevalence in dental school, 2010](local_corpus); [Harassment of newly admitted undergraduates…, 2011](local_corpus)).

### Contributing factors and contexts

Across studies and related workplace‑violence literature in dentistry, multiple modifiable determinants emerge:

- Power and hierarchy: Supervisory relationships, gatekeeping of evaluations, and dependence on faculty/senior clinicians heighten vulnerability for students and residents; similar dynamics occur in small private practice teams with single‑owner dentists ([Perceptions of Sexual Harassment…, 2019](local_corpus); [Sexual boundaries in dental practice: Part 2, 1999](local_corpus)).
- Patient contact patterns: Close physical proximity, intra‑oral procedures, and constrained patient/provider movement can facilitate IPSB; hygienists’ longer chairside time may increase exposure ([Inappropriate Patient Sexual Behavior…, 2021](local_corpus)).
- Organizational culture and policy gaps: Lack of explicit codes of conduct, unclear reporting pathways, or fear of retaliation inhibit reporting and corrective action; sensationalized media narratives may distort risk perception without fostering constructive change ([Exploring dentists’ professional behaviours in media, 2020](local_corpus); [A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025](local_corpus)).
- Environmental design and privacy: Single‑provider rooms without windows, limited chaperone availability, and closed doors may elevate risk; conversely, visibility and clear sightlines can deter misconduct ([Framework for managing inappropriate behavior…, 2024](local_corpus)).
- Workload and scheduling: Staffing shortages and time pressures can isolate staff and reduce oversight, exacerbating exposure to patient‑initiated events ([Framework for managing inappropriate behavior…, 2024](local_corpus)).
- Reporting mechanisms and protections: Absence of confidential reporting, weak whistleblower protections, and inadequate follow‑through degrade trust and deter disclosures ([Survey of Dental Researchers’ Perceptions…, 2020](local_corpus)).
- Training gaps: Limited de‑escalation/bystander training, lack of trauma‑informed and survivor‑centered communication skills, and inconsistent sexual harassment education are common ([Perceptions of Sexual Harassment…, 2019](local_corpus); [Framework for managing inappropriate behavior…, 2024](local_corpus)).
- Digital communication and boundaries: Though under‑studied in dentistry, technology‑facilitated sexual harassment is rising in other sectors; boundary issues in appointment messaging and social media warrant attention ([A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025](local_corpus)).

Intersectionality matters: Gender, trainee status, and minority identities intersect with power and culture to create differential risk and reporting burdens, as evidenced in ADEA climate analyses and cross‑national student surveys ([Microaggressions, harassment, and discrimination…, 2025](local_corpus); [An International Survey of Female Dental Students…, 2018](local_corpus)).

### Impacts

- Psychological and physical health: Anxiety, fear, anger, sleep problems, depressive symptoms, and stress are commonly reported after harassment or broader WPV; qualitative accounts among students describe lasting distress and diminished confidence ([“It’s just inappropriate”, 2022](local_corpus); [Healthy and Respectful Workplaces: Experiences of Dental Hygienists in Canada, 2022](local_corpus)).
- Professional development and career trajectories: Harassment contributes to burnout, job dissatisfaction, absenteeism, intent to leave, and diminished learning; OMS trainees reported adverse consequences for professional development ([Perceptions of Sexual Harassment…, 2019](local_corpus); [Healthy and Respectful Workplaces…, 2022](local_corpus)).
- Patient safety and care quality: Psychological distress and burnout among clinicians correlate with lower preparedness and potential safety risks; hostile environments erode the therapeutic alliance and may reduce access for vulnerable patient groups ([Healthy and Respectful Workplaces…, 2022](local_corpus); [A review of sexual misconduct in dentistry, 2025](local_corpus)).

### Interventions and safeguards

Evidence specific to dentistry is emerging but remains limited regarding effectiveness testing. A 2024 protocol developed through the PRECEDE‑PROCEED model for a dental school setting illustrates a structured pathway from needs assessment to a comprehensive policy and management approach ([Framework for managing inappropriate behavior…, 2024](local_corpus)). Interventions drawn from the dental and wider health literature include:

- Policy and codes of conduct
  - Clear definitions, zero‑tolerance statements, scope covering patient‑initiated and staff‑initiated behaviors, and anti‑retaliation clauses.
  - Transparent, confidential reporting pathways with protections and restorative/disciplinary processes ([Sexual boundaries in dental practice: Part 2, 1999](local_corpus); [Survey of Dental Researchers’ Perceptions…, 2020](local_corpus)).

- Environmental and workflow design
  - Clinic layout with windows/visible corridors; panic buttons; chaperone availability for selected procedures; scheduling strategies to avoid single‑staff isolation ([Framework for managing inappropriate behavior…, 2024](local_corpus)).

- Training
  - Bystander intervention; de‑escalation and safety planning; trauma‑informed, survivor‑centered communication; consent and boundary training for all team members, including strategies for addressing patient‑initiated behaviors in real time ([Perceptions of Sexual Harassment…, 2019](local_corpus); [Framework for managing inappropriate behavior…, 2024](local_corpus)).

- Reporting and support systems
  - Confidential, multi‑modal reporting (including anonymous); case triage; psychosocial support; linkage to occupational health; data monitoring with feedback loops and continuous improvement ([Survey of Dental Researchers’ Perceptions…, 2020](local_corpus); [Framework for managing inappropriate behavior…, 2024](local_corpus)).

- Education and culture change
  - Integration of harassment and professionalism content into curricula and CPD; leadership role‑modeling; reinforcement through clinical supervision; alignment with wider institutional climate initiatives (e.g., ADEA climate assessments) ([Microaggressions, harassment, and discrimination…, 2025](local_corpus)).

Implementation science lenses (RE‑AIM/CFIR) suggest key determinants: leadership engagement, clarity of workflows, compatibility with existing practices, staff self‑efficacy, and adequate resourcing. The dental‑specific WPV protocol demonstrates feasibility and face validity; rigorous evaluation of effectiveness, adoption, and maintenance is a priority ([Framework for managing inappropriate behavior…, 2024](local_corpus)).

### Risk of bias and certainty of evidence

- Most prevalence estimates come from cross‑sectional, convenience samples with self‑report measures. Selection bias (e.g., recruitment via social media or CE events), recall bias, and measurement heterogeneity (varied instruments and definitions) limit precision and generalizability. Using JBI tools, many studies would rate as moderate risk of bias due to sampling frames and response rates.
- Qualitative studies are generally coherent and credible (CASP), offering depth on mechanisms and impacts.
- Intervention studies specific to dentistry are scarce and largely descriptive; ROBINS‑I would identify serious risk of bias due to confounding and lack of comparison groups.
- Overall, certainty (GRADE) is low to very low for pooled prevalence across settings due to inconsistency and indirectness, but moderate for the conclusion that IPSB against hygienists and students is common, given consistent signals across multiple studies and countries.

## Discussion

### Interpretation

Within dentistry, sexual misconduct most frequently manifests as patient‑initiated inappropriate behaviors toward hygienists and students, with career prevalence approaching or exceeding levels reported in other health professions. Staff‑initiated harassment persists in academic and surgical training contexts where power imbalances and hierarchical cultures may normalize or conceal misconduct. The impacts are substantial—psychological distress, burnout markers, impaired learning, and potential downstream effects on patient safety.

Variation in prevalence estimates likely reflects both true differences (settings, roles, exposure patterns) and methodological heterogeneity. Notably, several studies identify higher victimization among male dental students in specific contexts (e.g., ragging), cautioning against simplistic gender narratives and reinforcing the need for inclusive, role‑sensitive prevention. Intersectional factors—sexual orientation, race/ethnicity, and minority status—interact with climate and power to shape risk and reporting, as evidenced by ADEA climate analyses.

### Strengths and limitations of the evidence

Strengths include multiple consistent signals across roles and regions; use of validated harassment instruments in some studies (e.g., SEQ‑W); and the emergence of dental‑specific protocols. Key limitations include:

- Under‑representation of low‑ and middle‑income countries and private practice contexts.
- Inconsistent definitions and recall windows.
- Limited longitudinal and intervention studies; minimal rigorous outcome evaluation.

### Quantitative synthesis

Due to heterogeneity of definitions and sampling, we did not compute a pooled prevalence across all settings. For IPSB among hygienists, study numbers and measures were insufficient to support a robust random‑effects meta‑analysis. Nonetheless, convergent high career prevalence in U.S. hygienist samples strengthens confidence that IPSB is a common occupational hazard.

## Recommendations

Drawing from the synthesis and implementation frameworks (RE‑AIM/CFIR), we propose the following:

- Policy and governance
  - Mandate practice‑level codes defining sexual misconduct and IPSB, inclusive of patients and staff, with zero‑tolerance statements and anti‑retaliation protections.
  - Establish confidential, multi‑channel reporting and tracking systems; standardize procedures for triage, support, investigation, and outcomes.
  - Incorporate periodic climate assessments (e.g., adapted from ADEA) and publish de‑identified dashboards to promote transparency and learning ([Microaggressions, harassment, and discrimination…, 2025](local_corpus)).

- Environment and staffing
  - Improve clinic visibility (windows, open sightlines), install duress alarms, and ensure chaperone availability for specified procedures and providers at higher risk (e.g., hygienists during prolonged procedures).
  - Schedule and staffing policies to minimize lone‑worker exposure.

- Education and training
  - Embed de‑escalation, bystander intervention, trauma‑informed and survivor‑centered communication, and boundary/consent modules in predoctoral curricula and CPD; include scenario‑based practice (e.g., role‑play) ([Perceptions of Sexual Harassment…, 2019](local_corpus); [Framework for managing inappropriate behavior…, 2024](local_corpus)).
  - Orient students/trainees explicitly on rights, reporting pathways, and protections; train supervisors in supportive responses and responsibilities.

- Support and aftercare
  - Provide timely psychosocial support, occupational health referral, and adjustments to duties or patient assignments where indicated.
  - Utilize restorative processes where appropriate and disciplinary action where necessary, in line with due process.

- Digital boundaries
  - Develop policies on professional digital communications (e.g., messaging platforms, social media) and responses to technology‑facilitated harassment.

- Evaluation
  - Apply RE‑AIM to monitor reach, adoption, implementation fidelity, and maintenance of policies and training; link incident data to process and outcome metrics (e.g., reporting rates, staff well‑being indices).

## Priorities for future research

- Standardize definitions and measurement (e.g., validated, dentistry‑adapted harassment instruments with clear recall periods) to enable pooling and benchmarking.
- Conduct longitudinal cohort studies to assess trajectories of impacts (psychological, academic/career) and the effects of organizational responses.
- Design and evaluate multicomponent interventions in pragmatic trials (e.g., cluster‑randomized or stepped‑wedge) with outcomes including incident rates, reporting behavior, well‑being, and care quality.
- Examine intersectionality explicitly (e.g., gender identity, sexual orientation, race/ethnicity, disability) in exposure and outcomes.
- Expand representation in LMICs and private practice settings; include patient perspectives and safeguards.

## Conclusion

Evidence indicates that sexual misconduct in dentistry—particularly patient‑initiated behaviors toward hygienists and clinical students—is common and consequential. Hierarchical cultures and policy gaps contribute to staff‑initiated harassment in training and academic environments. Impacts span psychological distress, professional development, and potential degradation of patient safety. While high‑quality intervention trials are sparse, a coherent suite of feasible, multi‑level safeguards is available: explicit codes and confidential reporting, environmental design, chaperoning, targeted training (bystander, trauma‑informed, de‑escalation), and supportive aftercare, underpinned by leadership and continuous learning. Implementing and rigorously evaluating such programs is an urgent priority to protect patients and the oral healthcare workforce.
