# Cellular calcium abnormalities in bipolar disorder: a systematic review and meta-analytic synthesis of intracellular Ca2+ concentration and signalling

## Abstract

Objective: To evaluate whether intracellular calcium ion concentration ([Ca2+]i) and calcium-signalling responses are altered in individuals with bipolar disorder (BD) relative to healthy controls, whether abnormalities vary by mood state (mania, depression, euthymia), and whether they differ from other psychiatric conditions.

Methods: We synthesize findings from the most comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis available alongside primary studies quantifying [Ca2+]i and Ca2+-signalling in peripheral blood cells, lymphoblastoid lines, and neuronal preparations from BD. We prioritize methodologically robust and recent sources and integrate older, foundational reports to appraise trait/state effects, diagnostic specificity, assay heterogeneity, and medication influences. Primary outcomes include basal/resting [Ca2+]i and agonist-stimulated responses; where available we cite standardized effect sizes.

Results: Convergent evidence indicates moderately increased basal [Ca2+]i in BD in platelets and lymphocytes (Hedges g ≈ 0.55; ~29% elevation), present in medication-free patients and in acute mood states (mania and bipolar depression), while data for euthymia remain equivocal. Stimulated [Ca2+]i responses to 5‑HT and thrombin are also increased (~25%; g ≈ 0.63). Mechanistic cellular studies point to dysregulated endoplasmic reticulum (ER) Ca2+ store handling and store‑operated calcium entry (SOCE) in BD lymphoblasts and altered neuronal Ca2+ dynamics in olfactory neurons. Comparative data suggest partial diagnostic specificity: baseline and stimulated platelet [Ca2+]i elevations are robust in BD and not consistently seen in schizophrenia, whereas 5‑HT–elicited platelet Ca2+ responses can be elevated in melancholic major depression as well as BD, indicating overlap at the level of serotonergic Ca2+ signalling. Lithium and carbamazepine tend to normalize elevated [Ca2+]i; valproate effects vary with baseline cellular calcium status, and calcium‑channel antagonists have mixed clinical efficacy despite a biologically plausible target.

Conclusions: Intracellular calcium abnormalities constitute a replicated cellular phenotype in BD, particularly in acute states, with suggestive (but not definitive) evidence for trait involvement in subgroups. Heterogeneity by cell type, assay modality, illness phase, and medication exposure is substantial and merits deliberate control in future studies. Mechanistic and genetic findings implicate ER‑mitochondrial Ca2+ handling and calcium‑channel pathways as convergent substrates linking cellular and clinical phenotypes.

Keywords: bipolar disorder, intracellular calcium, [Ca2+]i, platelets, lymphocytes, store‑operated calcium entry, thapsigargin, serotonin, thrombin, lithium, valproate

## Background and Rationale

Intracellular calcium is a ubiquitous second messenger governing excitability, secretion, gene transcription, and circadian regulation. The hypothesis that BD involves Ca2+ dysregulation dates back decades and gained traction with reports of elevated [Ca2+]i in peripheral cells during acute illness, normalization with some mood stabilizers, and genetic associations with calcium-channel loci (e.g., CACNA1C). A recent systematic review and meta-analysis provides the most rigorous quantitative estimate of the magnitude and consistency of these alterations, warranting integration with mechanistic and comparative evidence across diagnoses and illness phases ([Cellular calcium in bipolar disorder: systematic review and meta-analysis, 2021](https://local_corpus/Cellular_calcium_2021)).

## PICO and Outcomes

- Population: Individuals with DSM/ICD-diagnosed BD (I/II) across mood states; comparison groups include healthy controls and, when available, schizophrenia and major depressive disorder (MDD).
- Index measures: Basal/resting [Ca2+]i; agonist-evoked Ca2+ responses (e.g., 5‑HT, thrombin); pharmacologic probes (thapsigargin, lysophosphatidic acid) indexing ER store content and SOCE; neuronal measures in biopsy-derived olfactory neurons and iPSC-derived neurons.
- Comparators: Healthy controls; psychiatric comparators (MDD, schizophrenia).
- Outcomes: Standardized mean differences (Hedges g) where available; percentage differences; presence/absence of differences by mood state; medication effects.

Primary outcomes prioritized a priori are basal [Ca2+]i and stimulated peak amplitude in peripheral blood cells.

## Methods Summary

We prioritized the 2021 PRISMA-conformant meta-analysis as the quantitative backbone and triangulated it with earlier and later primary reports addressing mood state, specificity, and mechanisms. We extracted: diagnostic subtypes and mood states, medication status, cell type, assay modality (fura‑2 microfluorometry predominates), stimuli, and outcomes (basal [Ca2+]i, peak, ER store, SOCE proxies). We emphasize higher-quality and more recent sources where available and present effect sizes reported in the meta-analysis. Given reliance on provided sources, we narratively synthesize heterogeneity and bias considerations.

## Results

### Quantitative summary (meta-analysis)

- Basal [Ca2+]i is elevated in BD relative to controls in platelets and lymphocytes: Hedges g ≈ 0.55, corresponding to ~29% higher resting free [Ca2+]i. The effect persists in medication‑free samples and is evident in mania and bipolar depression; euthymic data are inconclusive ([Cellular calcium in bipolar disorder: systematic review and meta-analysis, 2021](https://local_corpus/Cellular_calcium_2021)).
- Stimulated [Ca2+]i responses are increased: 5‑HT and thrombin evoke ~25% higher responses in BD (g ≈ 0.63) ([Cellular calcium in bipolar disorder: systematic review and meta-analysis, 2021](https://local_corpus/Cellular_calcium_2021)).

These estimates were constructed from up to 642 BD patients and 404 controls across 21 studies, pointing to a moderate and consistent effect size at the cellular level.

### Foundational case–control evidence

Multiple earlier case–control studies reported elevated basal and stimulated [Ca2+]i in BD:

- Elevated basal platelet and lymphocyte [Ca2+]i in mania and bipolar depression; no effect of BD plasma ultrafiltrate on control platelets; carbamazepine lowered [Ca2+]i in patient lymphocytes but not controls ([Intracellular calcium signalling in peripheral cells…, 1994](https://local_corpus/Intracellular_calcium_1994)).
- Elevated baseline and thrombin-stimulated platelet [Ca2+]i in untreated bipolar depression vs untreated unipolar depression and controls; euthymic treated BD approximated controls, suggesting possible state-dependence in that sample ([Elevated platelet intracellular calcium concentration in bipolar depression, 1991](https://local_corpus/Elevated_platelet_Ca_1991)).
- Fluorometric microassay in 30 affective disorder patients found significantly higher basal platelet/lymphocyte [Ca2+]i in BD; BD platelet responses to thrombin, 5‑HT, thapsigargin were greater; lymphocyte peaks greater only to thapsigargin; BD vs unipolar differences significant for basal and thapsigargin‑stimulated measures; BD I vs BD II did not differ ([Elevated basal and thapsigargin-stimulated intracellular calcium…, 1999](https://local_corpus/Thapsigargin_1999)).
- Early small samples replicated elevated basal [Ca2+]i and higher interindividual variability in BD ([Abnormal intracellular calcium ion concentration…, 1992](https://local_corpus/Abnormal_Ca_1992)).

### Mood state (trait vs state) considerations

The 2021 meta-analysis concludes that elevations are present in mania and bipolar depression, with equivocal findings in euthymia, implying a robust state effect and a possible trait component in subgroups ([Cellular calcium in bipolar disorder: systematic review and meta-analysis, 2021](https://local_corpus/Cellular_calcium_2021)). Consistent with state sensitivity:

- Elevated [Ca2+]i reported during mania and bipolar depression ([Intracellular calcium signalling in peripheral cells…, 1994](https://local_corpus/Intracellular_calcium_1994)).
- In one study, euthymic treated BD showed normalized platelet [Ca2+]i, suggestive of state/medication normalization ([Elevated platelet intracellular calcium concentration in bipolar depression, 1991](https://local_corpus/Elevated_platelet_Ca_1991)).

Notably, neuronal-level data in euthymic, medication-free BD reveal altered stimulus-induced Ca2+ dynamics (predominant decreases), pointing to trait-like neuronal signalling differences in some patients, mitigated by mood stabilizers ([Aberrant intracellular calcium signaling in olfactory neurons…, 2005](https://local_corpus/Olfactory_2005)).

### Diagnostic specificity: BD vs schizophrenia and unipolar depression

- BD vs schizophrenia: Baseline and agonist-stimulated platelet [Ca2+]i were significantly elevated in BD (including psychotic mania) but not in hospitalized schizophrenia patients; greater variability in BD; antipsychotic exposure may confound schizophrenia comparisons ([Increased platelet intracellular calcium ion concentration is specific to bipolar disorder, 2014](https://local_corpus/Specific_to_BD_2014)).
- BD vs MDD: 5‑HT–induced platelet Ca2+ responses are enhanced in BD and melancholic MDD, but not consistently in non‑melancholic MDD, highlighting overlap in serotonergic Ca2+ signalling between melancholic depression and BD ([Serotonin-induced platelet intracellular calcium mobilization in depressed patients, 1994](https://local_corpus/5HT_depressed_1994); [Enhanced calcium response to serotonin…, 1996](https://local_corpus/Enhanced_5HT_1996); [Serotonin-induced platelet intracellular calcium mobilization in various psychiatric disorders, 2001](https://local_corpus/5HT_various_2001)). In contrast, basal and thapsigargin‑stimulated measures have discriminated BD from unipolar depression in several reports ([Elevated basal and thapsigargin-stimulated intracellular calcium…, 1999](https://local_corpus/Thapsigargin_1999)).

Overall, basal and ER/SOCE-linked indices appear more BD‑specific than 5‑HT–elicited responses, which may reflect shared serotonergic signalling abnormalities in melancholic states.

### Mechanistic insights: ER stores, SOCE, and mitochondria

- BD lymphoblasts show heightened thapsigargin‑induced cytosolic Ca2+ elevations, consistent with altered ER store content and/or SOCE; mitochondrial uncoupling (CCCP) probes indicate concurrent mitochondrial Ca2+ handling differences ([Mechanisms of altered Ca2+ signalling in transformed lymphoblastoid cells…, 2003](https://local_corpus/Lymphoblast_mechanisms_2003)).
- Larger samples of BD‑I BLCLs confirm higher basal [Ca2+]i and faster receptor‑stimulated rise rates (e.g., LPA), indicating hyperactive intracellular Ca2+ dynamics ([Hyperactive intracellular calcium dynamics in B lymphoblasts…, 2008](https://local_corpus/BLCL_2008)).
- Genetic modulation of Ca2+ homeostasis is implicated by BCL2 rs956572 associations with disrupted intracellular Ca2+ in BD and by calcium‑channel genes influencing lithium’s amplification of circadian rhythms in fibroblasts ([Bcl‑2 SNP rs956572 associates…, 2011](https://local_corpus/Bcl2_2011); [Calcium channel genes associated with bipolar disorder modulate lithium's amplification of circadian rhythms, 2016](https://local_corpus/LTCC_circadian_2016)). Transcriptomic changes in iPSC‑derived neurons from BD patients emphasize increased expression of membrane receptors and ion channels, supporting a cell‑autonomous predisposition to altered excitability and Ca2+ signalling ([Transcripts involved in calcium signaling…, 2014](https://local_corpus/iPSC_2014)).

### Medication effects on cellular Ca2+ metrics

- Lithium and carbamazepine: Multiple studies indicate normalizing or lowering effects on elevated [Ca2+]i in BD cells, aligning with their antimanic/mood‑stabilizing profiles ([Intracellular calcium signalling in peripheral cells…, 1994](https://local_corpus/Intracellular_calcium_1994)). Lithium’s complex modulation of IP3 signalling and circadian amplification may underlie indirect effects on Ca2+ dynamics ([Effects of lithium on platelet ionic intracellular calcium…, 1990](https://local_corpus/Li_platelet_1990); [Calcium channel genes associated with bipolar disorder modulate lithium's amplification…, 2016](https://local_corpus/LTCC_circadian_2016)).
- Valproate: Results are inconsistent. Experimental work in healthy platelets shows dose‑dependent attenuation of agonist‑induced Ca2+ rises; lithium exerts reductions only under specific kinase-inhibition conditions ([Effects of lithium and valproate on agonist-induced platelet intracellular calcium mobilization, 2004](https://local_corpus/ML9_Li_VPA_2004)). In mildly to moderately ill BD patients with relatively low baseline platelet [Ca2+]i, ex vivo valproate incubation increased baseline [Ca2+]i, suggesting that valproate’s direction of effect may depend on initial cellular status and illness severity ([Effects of Valproate on Intracellular Calcium Ion Signaling, 2025](https://local_corpus/Valproate_2025)).
- Calcium channel blockers (CCBs): Despite biological plausibility and historical use (e.g., verapamil), a systematic review of randomized trials found no convincing efficacy of verapamil in acute mania; data for other L‑type antagonists and illness phases remain observational and inconclusive ([A systematic review of calcium channel antagonists in bipolar disorder, 2016](https://local_corpus/LTCC_review_2016)). Contemporary reviews highlight CCBs as promising but unproven, pending better targeting and trials ([Applications of calcium channel blockers in psychiatry, 2019](https://local_corpus/CCB_review_2019)). Levetiracetam, an anticonvulsant with calcium‑antagonist properties, did not correct elevated platelet [Ca2+]i and lacks controlled evidence as a mood stabilizer ([Levetiracetam, Calcium Antagonism, and Bipolar Disorder, 2015](https://local_corpus/Levetiracetam_2015)).

### Neuronal evidence

- Olfactory receptor neurons from euthymic, medication‑free BD subjects demonstrated atypical Ca2+ responses (predominant decreases upon stimulation) vs matched controls; mood stabilizer‑treated patients’ neurons were less responsive, suggesting treatment-related normalization and underscoring neuronal relevance of Ca2+ dysregulation ([Aberrant intracellular calcium signaling in olfactory neurons…, 2005](https://local_corpus/Olfactory_2005)).

## Risk of Bias, Heterogeneity, and Methodological Considerations

- Assay heterogeneity: Studies used fura‑2 microfluorometry, spectrofluorometry, and varied calibration protocols, buffers, and temperatures, contributing to between‑study variance. Outcomes included basal levels, peaks, and kinetic parameters, sometimes within the same cohort, risking non‑independence if not modeled appropriately in meta‑analysis.
- Sample characteristics: Diagnostic heterogeneity (BD I/II), variable mood states and psychosis, and inconsistent reporting of smoking, age, and sex introduce confounding. Medication status is a major determinant; the 2021 meta-analysis addressed medication‑free subsets but primary studies vary in controlling for exposure.
- Cell types and culture: Peripheral cells (platelets, lymphocytes), transformed lymphoblastoid lines, and small neuronal samples from olfactory epithelium differ in biology and assayability; culture conditions can affect Ca2+ handling.
- Publication bias: Smaller, early studies may overestimate effects. The 2021 meta-analysis did not report funnel or Egger statistics in the abstract; thus remaining uncertainty about small‑study effects persists.

Despite these limitations, the moderate effect sizes and replication across methods and laboratories increase confidence in a real signal.

## Interpretation and Implications

- Strength of evidence: Moderate. A PRISMA‑based meta-analysis quantifies consistent elevations in basal and stimulated [Ca2+]i, with corroborating mechanistic studies strengthening biological plausibility.
- Trait vs state: Elevations appear robust in acute mood states; euthymic findings are mixed, yet neuronal and genetic data (e.g., olfactory neurons, BCL2, CACNA) support a latent trait component in subgroups. A parsimonious model is a state‑amplified trait: a predisposing Ca2+‑handling imbalance that is exacerbated during mania/depression and moderated by treatment.
- Specificity: Basal and store‑related indices may be more specific to BD than 5‑HT–evoked responses. Schizophrenia does not demonstrate the same platelet [Ca2+]i elevations in controlled comparisons, albeit confounded by antipsychotics. Overlap with melancholic MDD in 5‑HT responses suggests that serotonergic calcium signalling is a transdiagnostic dimension linked to melancholic/psychomotor features rather than diagnostic category per se.
- Mechanisms: Convergent evidence implicates ER store content/SOCE dysregulation and altered mitochondrial buffering. Genetic and transcriptomic signals (BCL2, CACNA genes) and lithium’s IP3/clock interactions integrate with cellular phenotypes, offering mechanistic pathways from molecular risk to cellular excitability and network behaviour.
- Treatment translation: Lithium and carbamazepine’s normalizing cellular effects align with clinical efficacy; valproate’s effects depend on baseline cellular physiology, offering a testable stratification hypothesis. The mixed clinical efficacy of L‑type CCBs despite a clear calcium phenotype may reflect targeting of the wrong channels, suboptimal CNS penetration, or the primacy of ER/SOCE over plasma‑membrane L‑type influx in the BD cellular phenotype. Precision trials stratified by calcium‑pathway genotypes (e.g., CACNA1B/C) and baseline cellular [Ca2+]i may clarify responders.

## Recommendations for Research

- Standardization: Adopt harmonized protocols for [Ca2+]i calibration, temperature, buffers, and readouts (basal, peak, AUC, time constants), with a priori primary outcomes and robust variance estimation to handle multiple outcomes.
- Phenotyping: Systematically record mood state, psychosis, rapid cycling, BD subtype, and medication exposure, including washout when ethical and feasible.
- Specificity tests: Include MDD (melancholic vs non‑melancholic) and schizophrenia comparators with careful control for antipsychotic exposure.
- Mechanistic assays: Expand measures of ER Ca2+ load (e.g., thapsigargin protocols), SOCE (e.g., Mn2+ quench), and mitochondrial Ca2+ buffering; integrate pharmacologic dissection (IP3, PKC inhibitors) to map pathway nodes.
- Longitudinal designs: Track within‑person [Ca2+]i across mood states and pre/post treatment with lithium, carbamazepine, valproate, and targeted Ca2+ modulators.
- Neuronal models: iPSC‑derived neurons and ex vivo neuronal biopsies (olfactory) should assess whether peripheral findings translate to neuronal excitability, synaptic function, and network oscillations relevant to BD.
- Stratified trials: Test calcium‑modulatory treatments (including SOCE modulators) in genetically or cellularly stratified BD subgroups, with cellular normalization as a pharmacodynamic biomarker.

## Conclusion

The cumulative evidence supports intracellular calcium dysregulation as a reproducible cellular phenotype in BD, characterized by moderately elevated basal and stimulated [Ca2+]i in peripheral cells, present in acute mood states and possibly in subsets during euthymia. Mechanistic studies implicate ER store handling, SOCE, and mitochondrial buffering, while genetic and neuronal findings align with a biologically coherent pathway. Diagnostic specificity is partial: BD shows stronger basal/ER-linked abnormalities than schizophrenia, whereas 5‑HT‑elicited Ca2+ responses overlap with melancholic MDD. Clinically, mood stabilizers with calcium‑modulating properties tend to normalize cellular abnormalities, but translation of calcium‑channel antagonism to efficacy remains inconsistent, underscoring the need for mechanistically refined targets and stratified designs. Future standardized, longitudinal, and mechanistically integrated studies are warranted to solidify trait/state inferences, enhance diagnostic specificity, and guide precision therapeutics.
