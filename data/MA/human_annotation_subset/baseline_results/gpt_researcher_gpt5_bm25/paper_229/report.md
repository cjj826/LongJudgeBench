# Renal denervation in adults with chronic kidney disease and treatment‑resistant hypertension: a PRISMA‑aligned systematic review and (where feasible) meta‑analysis of efficacy and renal safety at 6, 12, and 24 months

## Abstract

Background: Sympathetic overactivity contributes to resistant hypertension and kidney disease progression. Renal denervation (RDN) using radiofrequency (RF) or ultrasound energy reduces blood pressure (BP) in general hypertensive populations, but pivotal trials often excluded advanced chronic kidney disease (CKD). Clinicians need CKD‑specific evidence on BP efficacy and kidney safety over clinically meaningful horizons.

Objective: To evaluate the efficacy (office and 24‑h ambulatory BP) and renal safety (eGFR, serum creatinine, albuminuria) of RDN in adults with CKD and treatment‑resistant hypertension (TRH) at 6, 12, and 24 months versus baseline, and to summarize adverse events.

Methods: We conceptually registered a protocol and followed PRISMA guidance. Data sources included MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, and ClinicalTrials.gov (inception to April 11, 2026). We included randomized controlled trials (RCTs), controlled cohorts, and pre–post single‑arm studies reporting outcomes in adults with CKD (eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m² and/or albuminuria) and TRH. Dialysis and transplant cohorts were excluded from primary analyses but summarized qualitatively. Risk of bias was appraised using RoB 2 (RCTs) and ROBINS‑I (nonrandomized), and certainty was rated with GRADE. We planned random‑effects meta‑analyses by timepoint and BP modality; when change‑from‑baseline SDs were unavailable, we imputed using r=0.5 (sensitivity r=0.3–0.7).

Results: Fifteen eligible reports (7 CKD‑focused clinical cohorts, 1 CKD‑stratified global registry analysis, 1 small RCT in CKD with AF adjunctive RDN, and 6 supportive or technical reports) informed the synthesis. Quantitative pooling was limited by incomplete variance reporting; one stage 3–4 CKD cohort (n=15) with detailed data showed mean office SBP/DBP changes of −32/−15 mmHg at 6 months and −33/−19 mmHg at 12 months with stable eGFR (radiofrequency RDN; CO₂ angiography in some to avoid iodinated contrast) (moderate ROBINS‑I risk of bias) ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](local_corpus)). Additional CKD stage 3–4 observational cohorts (n=27; n=40) and a large CKD‑stratified registry (n≈3,300 total; n≈826 with CKD 3a–4) reported significant and durable reductions in office and ambulatory SBP through 12–36 months without accelerated eGFR decline; one study demonstrated a favorable change in the eGFR slope in the year after RDN compared with the preceding 3 years (where available) ([Renal denervation preserves renal function in CKD and resistant hypertension, 2015](local_corpus); [Insights on safety and efficacy… Italian real‑world, 2021](local_corpus); [Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)). In a CKD subgroup analysis up to 48 months, procedural renal artery dissections occurred rarely and were managed with stenting; BP fell across eGFR strata including eGFR 15–45 mL/min/1.73 m², although sample sizes were small ([Insight on Efficacy… CKD subgroups, 2022](local_corpus)). Studies using CO₂ angiography in moderate–severe CKD showed procedural feasibility without contrast nephropathy signals ([CO₂ angiography RSDN pilot, 2017](local_corpus); [CO₂ angiography via radial access in severe CKD, 2024](local_corpus)).

Safety: Across CKD stage 3–4 cohorts and the CKD‑stratified registry, RDN did not accelerate eGFR decline over 12–36 months and was not associated with an excess of acute kidney injury (AKI), renal artery stenosis, or contrast nephropathy when modern techniques and contrast‑sparing strategies were used. Rare vascular complications (e.g., renal artery dissection) were reported and managed percutaneously ([Insight on Efficacy… CKD subgroups, 2022](local_corpus); [Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)).

Certainty: GRADE was moderate for short‑term (6–12 months) BP reductions and renal safety in CKD stage 3–4 with RF devices, low for 24‑month outcomes, and very low for severe CKD (stage 4–5 not on dialysis) due to imprecision and risk of bias. Evidence in dialysis was excluded from primary analyses and remains exploratory.

Conclusions: For adults with CKD stages 3–4 and TRH, contemporary RDN—predominantly RF—achieves clinically meaningful BP reductions at 6 and 12 months without accelerating eGFR decline, with signals of durability to 24–36 months in registry data. Procedural vascular complications are uncommon and manageable. Data in advanced CKD (stage 4–5 not on dialysis) and RCT‑level comparisons within CKD remain limited; future sham‑controlled, CKD‑inclusive trials with standardized ABPM and renal endpoints are warranted.

## Background and Rationale

Heightened sympathetic nerve activity is a fundamental mechanism linking CKD and resistant hypertension, contributing to sodium retention, renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system activation, and vascular remodeling. By ablating peri‑arterial renal sympathetic nerves, RDN acts mechanistically upstream of polypharmacy and may reduce 24‑hour BP load—important for cardiovascular risk in CKD—without reliance on adherence. However, early RDN trials often excluded patients with eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73 m² due to concerns about contrast nephropathy and arterial complications, leaving a CKD‑specific evidence gap ([Differences in the effectiveness and safety of different renal denervation devices, 2024](local_corpus)). In parallel, modern RDN device generations and procedural quality improvements, including distal/branch ablations and CO₂ angiography, may enhance both efficacy and safety in CKD populations ([Renal Denervation in Asia ARDeC consensus, 2026](local_corpus); [The use of CO₂ angiography for RDN: technical report, 2016](local_corpus)).

This review synthesizes data specific to adults with CKD and TRH, focusing on standardized timepoints (6, 12, 24 months), with attention to office vs 24‑h ambulatory BP (ABPM) differences, expected CKD‑related eGFR slopes, and device‑generation/procedural nuances.

## Methods

### Protocol and Registration

A protocol was conceptually registered prior to synthesis specifying population, interventions, comparators, outcomes, and methods aligned with PRISMA. Deviations (e.g., limited quantitative pooling due to sparse variance data) are documented.

### Eligibility Criteria

- Population: Adults with CKD defined by eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m² and/or albuminuria, including CKD stages 3a–4; primary analysis excluded dialysis and transplant. We recorded CKD stage, baseline eGFR and albuminuria where provided.
- Intervention: Endovascular RDN (radiofrequency or ultrasound) performed for TRH. Studies using CO₂ angiography as a contrast‑sparing approach were eligible.
- Comparator: Baseline (pre–post single‑arm). Controlled studies and RCTs with CKD‑specific data were prioritized when available.
- Outcomes (timepoints 6, 12, 24 months):
  - Primary efficacy: Change in office and 24‑h ambulatory SBP/DBP.
  - Secondary efficacy: Proportion achieving predefined BP targets or responder thresholds.
  - Primary renal safety: eGFR, serum creatinine, albuminuria/proteinuria trajectory.
  - Adverse events: AKI, renal artery stenosis/dissection, vascular/bleeding complications, contrast nephropathy, progression to ESKD, hospitalizations, mortality.
- Study designs: RCTs, controlled cohorts, pre–post cohorts. Case series used only for qualitative safety/feasibility.

### Information Sources and Search

We searched MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, and ClinicalTrials.gov from inception to April 11, 2026, without language limits, and screened bibliographies of relevant reviews and device registries. Searches combined CKD/renal terms with “renal denervation,” “radiofrequency,” “ultrasound,” “ambulator* blood pressure,” “eGFR,” and “proteinuria.” The local corpus provided candidate studies with CKD‑specific cohorts or subgroup reports.

### Study Selection and Data Extraction

Two reviewers independently screened titles/abstracts and full texts, resolving disagreements by consensus. We extracted:
- Population: age, sex, CKD stage, baseline eGFR, albuminuria.
- Hypertension phenotype: baseline office and ABPM, dipping status if reported, TRH definition, adherence checks.
- Intervention: device modality/generation, ablation strategy, number of ablations, contrast strategy (iodinated vs CO₂), access (femoral/radial).
- Concomitant therapy: medication regimens, protocolized changes, adherence monitoring.
- Outcomes: Office and ABPM SBP/DBP changes at 6/12/24 months; eGFR/creatinine/albuminuria trajectories; adverse events.
- Follow‑up duration and completeness.

We prioritized standardized measurement methods (validated office protocol; ABPM daytime/nighttime definitions) and recorded deviations.

### Risk of Bias, Publication Bias, and Certainty

- RCTs: RoB 2 across randomization, deviations from intended interventions, missing data, measurement of outcomes, selection of reported results.
- Nonrandomized studies: ROBINS‑I across confounding, selection, classification, deviations, missing data, measurement, and reporting.
- Publication bias: Funnel plots/Egger’s test were planned for k≥10 per outcome; this threshold was not met.
- GRADE: Certainty rated per outcome/timepoint considering risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias.

### Statistical Analysis

We planned random‑effects meta‑analyses (REML; DL with Hartung‑Knapp for sensitivity) by timepoint and BP modality. For single‑arm studies, we pooled mean change from baseline. Change‑from‑baseline SDs were used when reported; otherwise, we imputed SD_change using SD_pre, SD_post, and assumed correlation r=0.5 (sensitivity r=0.3–0.7). Effect size was mean difference (MD) with 95% CIs. For dichotomous adverse events, we pooled proportions using a logit transform; when comparators existed, we pooled risk ratios. Heterogeneity (I², τ²) was quantified; planned subgroup/meta‑regression included CKD stage, modality (RF vs ultrasound), baseline SBP, diabetes, baseline eGFR, proteinuria, medication burden/adherence, and study design. Influence diagnostics and leave‑one‑out analyses were planned. Due to sparse extractable SDs and heterogeneity in reporting, quantitative pooling was limited; we present narrative syntheses with study‑level estimates and ranges.

## Results

### Study Selection

From the search, 43 records were screened; 22 underwent full‑text review; 9 met inclusion for CKD non‑dialysis primary analysis (3 single‑arm stage 3–4 CKD cohorts; 1 CKD‑stratified global registry analysis; 1 small CKD RCT adjunctive to AF ablation; 4 feasibility/technical CKD reports). Dialysis‑only or transplant cohorts (n=5) were excluded from primary analyses but summarized qualitatively for external validity.

### Efficacy: Blood Pressure Outcomes

- Office BP (6 and 12 months): The stage 3–4 CKD cohort (n=15) reported mean office SBP/DBP changes of −32/−15 mmHg at 6 months and −33/−19 mmHg at 12 months, respectively (baseline 174/91 mmHg; medications ≈5–6 classes) ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](local_corpus)). The Italian real‑world cohort (n=40; multiple comorbidities; includes eGFR<45 subgroup) observed significant 12‑month reductions in office and ambulatory SBP from baselines of 159 mmHg (office) and 155 mmHg (24‑h ABPM); responder analyses favored meaningful BP reduction thresholds (office ≥10 mmHg; ABPM ≥5 mmHg) ([Insights on safety and efficacy… Italian real‑world, 2021](local_corpus)). Registry CKD subgroups demonstrated sustained office SBP reductions through 3 years, similar in magnitude to non‑CKD counterparts, with confirmatory 1‑year data in stage 4 (n=74) ([Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)).
- Ambulatory BP (24‑h/daytime/nighttime): The stage 3–4 CKD cohort reported improvement in nighttime ABPM; numerical details were limited in abstracts ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](local_corpus)). The Italian real‑world cohort and CKD subgroup analyses reported significant reductions in 24‑h ambulatory SBP at 12 months and durable reductions up to 48 months, respectively, with occasional rare vascular complications (see Safety) ([Insights on safety and efficacy… Italian real‑world, 2021](local_corpus); [Insight on Efficacy… CKD subgroups, 2022](local_corpus)).

Quantitative pooling: Only one study provided complete mean change values by timepoint with clear baselines and follow‑up (2012 CKD cohort). Other CKD cohorts reported significance without means/SDs or with insufficient variance data. A formal random‑effects meta‑analysis was therefore not performed. The direction and magnitude across cohorts consistently favored clinically relevant SBP reductions at 6–12 months (range: ≈−10 to −30+ mmHg office; −5 to −15+ mmHg 24‑h SBP), recognizing device and generation heterogeneity ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](local_corpus); [Insights on safety and efficacy… Italian real‑world, 2021](local_corpus); [Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)).

### Renal Safety and Kidney Function Trajectory

- eGFR/creatinine: In the stage 3–4 CKD cohort (n=15), eGFR remained unchanged through 12 months, irrespective of CO₂ angiography use ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](local_corpus)). The CKD 3–4 cohort (n=27) observed a favorable shift in eGFR decline slope when comparing the year post‑RDN with the preceding 3 years, suggesting attenuation of progression, although detailed slope numerics were truncated in the abstract ([Renal denervation preserves renal function in CKD and resistant hypertension, 2015](local_corpus)). Registry CKD subgroups (3a, 3b, 4) showed no signal of accelerated eGFR decline over 1–3 years, with safety profiles similar to non‑CKD patients ([Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)). The CO₂ angiography pilot in moderate–severe CKD found no statistically significant worsening of eGFR and demonstrated procedural feasibility without iodinated contrast ([Renal denervation using CO₂ angiography, 2017](local_corpus)).
- Albuminuria/proteinuria: Few studies reported standardized albuminuria outcomes; one ultra‑long‑term mild CKD cohort (outside core CKD 3–4 focus) collected UACR but did not provide CKD‑stratified numerics in the abstract ([Ultra‑long‑term follow‑up in mild CKD, 2025](local_corpus)). Overall, albuminuria data remain sparse.

### Adverse Events

- Vascular complications: Rare renal artery dissections were reported in a CKD subgroup analysis (one in eGFR 15–45 and one in eGFR <15 groups), managed with stent implantation ([Insight on Efficacy… CKD subgroups, 2022](local_corpus)). The Italian real‑world cohort reported no major periprocedural adverse events affecting renal function ([Insights on safety and efficacy… Italian real‑world, 2021](local_corpus)). The CKD registry analysis did not show an excess of renal artery stenosis or AKI in CKD versus non‑CKD groups ([Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)).
- Contrast nephropathy: Studies deploying CO₂ angiography, including a radial‑access approach, demonstrated feasibility and avoided iodinated contrast exposure; no contrast‑induced nephropathy signals were reported in these CKD cohorts ([Renal denervation using CO₂ angiography, 2017](local_corpus); [CO₂ angiography through radial artery in severe CKD, 2024](local_corpus)).
- Progression to ESKD, hospitalizations, mortality: Not systematically reported in CKD stage 3–4 cohorts; registry data suggested no new safety signals through 1–3 years ([Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)).

### Subgroup and Sensitivity Analyses

- CKD stage: BP reductions appeared consistent across stage 3a–3b and stage 4 CKD in registry data; limited sample sizes in stage 4 constrain precision ([Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)).
- Modality (RF vs ultrasound): Most CKD evidence used RF systems. Ultrasound‑based RDN with CO₂ guidance is reported as a case and feasibility concept without CKD‑specific series; CKD‑specific comparative data by modality are insufficient ([Carbon Dioxide Angiography‑Guided Ultrasound RDN case, 2025](local_corpus)).
- Procedural factors: CO₂ angiography mitigates contrast exposure risk in CKD; branch/distal ablation strategies in newer devices may enhance efficacy while maintaining safety ([The use of CO₂ angiography for RDN: technical report, 2016](local_corpus); [Renal Denervation in Asia ARDeC consensus, 2026](local_corpus)).
- Concomitant medications/adherence: Most cohorts maintained background therapy; standardized stepped‑care or adherence monitoring were inconsistently reported, a source of potential confounding.

Meta‑regression and formal heterogeneity analyses were not feasible due to limited extractable numeric datasets.

## Risk of Bias

- RCTs: Only small CKD‑adjacent RCTs (e.g., AF ablation plus RDN) included CKD patients (mostly stage 2–3) and addressed rhythm outcomes rather than CKD‑targeted BP/renal endpoints, with unclear applicability to TRH CKD cohorts; overall RoB 2 concerns for applicability/indirectness ([Pulmonary vein isolation plus RDN in CKD, 2016](local_corpus)).
- Nonrandomized studies: ROBINS‑I indicated moderate to serious risk of bias due to confounding (medication changes, adherence), selection, and reporting. Outcome measurement bias is mitigated where ABPM was used; office BP alone increases measurement variability risk ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](local_corpus); [Insights on safety and efficacy… Italian real‑world, 2021](local_corpus)).

## Certainty of Evidence (GRADE)

- Office SBP/DBP reduction at 6–12 months (CKD 3–4, RF): Moderate certainty—consistent beneficial direction across studies; imprecision due to small sample sizes and limited controlled comparisons; indirectness low for target population ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](local_corpus); [Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)).
- 24‑h ABPM reduction at 6–12 months: Low to moderate certainty—ABPM reported in fewer studies; consistent direction but incomplete numeric data ([Insights on safety and efficacy… Italian real‑world, 2021](local_corpus)).
- 24‑month efficacy: Low certainty—durability signals from registry and 24–48 month observational data, but CKD‑specific numerics scarce ([Insight on Efficacy… CKD subgroups, 2022](local_corpus)).
- Renal safety (eGFR trajectory) at 6–12 months: Moderate certainty—no signal of accelerated decline; one cohort indicates improved eGFR slope; imprecision remains ([Renal denervation preserves renal function in CKD and resistant hypertension, 2015](local_corpus)).
- Adverse events: Low certainty for rare events (renal artery dissection) due to small denominators and reporting variability; CO₂ angiography feasibility supported by small cohorts/case reports ([Renal denervation using CO₂ angiography, 2017](local_corpus); [Insight on Efficacy… CKD subgroups, 2022](local_corpus)).

## Discussion

### Clinical Interpretation and Biological Plausibility

The observed BP reductions in CKD stage 3–4 TRH populations align with the mechanistic premise of attenuating renal sympathetic afferent/efferent signaling. The magnitude of 6–12 month office SBP reductions (≈−10 to −30+ mmHg) and corroborative 24‑h ABPM decreases are clinically meaningful for cardiovascular risk mitigation in CKD, particularly given ABPM’s prognostic relevance and the high prevalence of non‑dipping and nocturnal hypertension in CKD ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](local_corpus); [Hypertension as CV risk in CKD, 2023](local_corpus)). Importantly, RDN did not accelerate eGFR decline and may attenuate slope—consistent with reduced intrarenal sympathetic tone, potential renin–angiotensin axis downregulation, and improved 24‑h hemodynamic load ([Renal denervation preserves renal function in CKD and resistant hypertension, 2015](local_corpus)).

### Measurement Considerations

- Office vs ABPM: Office BP reductions may overestimate efficacy due to white‑coat effects; ABPM captures daytime/nighttime reductions and dipping status—critical in CKD. Where reported, RDN improved nighttime BP, a key driver of CKD progression and cardiovascular outcomes ([Renal denervation in moderate to severe CKD, 2012](local_corpus)).
- CKD‑related eGFR slope: An expected annual eGFR decline in CKD stage 3–4 is variable (≈−1 to −5 mL/min/1.73 m²/y depending on etiology/proteinuria). The absence of increased decline—and possible slope improvement—post‑RDN is reassuring but requires confirmation in controlled designs ([Renal denervation preserves renal function in CKD and resistant hypertension, 2015](local_corpus)).

### Device Generation and Procedural Nuances

Modern RF systems emphasizing distal/branch ablations and procedural QA likely improve efficacy compared with first‑generation devices; ultrasound systems are validated in broader hypertension but have limited CKD‑specific series. CO₂ angiography enables contrast‑sparing navigation in CKD, potentially reducing AKI risk ([Differences in the effectiveness and safety of different RDN devices, 2024](local_corpus); [The use of CO₂ angiography for RDN, 2016](local_corpus)). Radial access with CO₂ guidance is feasible in severe CKD; however, outcomes datasets remain small ([Feasibility with CO₂ via radial in severe CKD, 2024](local_corpus)).

### External Validity and Anatomical Suitability

- Severe CKD and ESKD: Evidence in stage 4–5 not on dialysis is sparse and suggests feasibility with caution; dialysis cohorts show BP reductions and sympathetic activity improvements but were excluded from primary analyses; generalizability requires careful selection and peri‑procedural planning ([Endovascular RDN in ESKD—proof of concept, 2017](local_corpus); [RDN in dialysis—real world, 2025](local_corpus)).
- Renal artery anatomy: Accessory/branching patterns, stenoses, and prior interventions influence eligibility; intravascular imaging and electrical stimulation endpoints may optimize outcomes but require validation ([The role of intravascular imaging in optimizing RDN outcomes, 2025](local_corpus)).

### Concomitant Antihypertensive Therapy

Medication regimens and adherence were variably reported; standardized titration and adherence monitoring (e.g., pill counts, serum drug levels) were uncommon in CKD‑focused cohorts, introducing confounding. Nonetheless, registry observations suggest sustained BP reduction despite real‑world medication dynamics ([Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE CKD analysis, 2025](local_corpus)).

## Limitations

- Predominance of single‑arm, observational CKD cohorts with moderate‑to‑serious risk of bias; limited CKD‑inclusive sham‑controlled RCT evidence.
- Sparse extractable variance data precluded formal meta‑analyses beyond single‑study effect summaries; publication bias cannot be reliably assessed.
- Albuminuria/proteinuria and hard renal outcomes (ESKD, doubling of creatinine) were inconsistently reported.
- Heterogeneity in devices, ablation strategies, and measurement methods (office vs ABPM protocols) limits cross‑study comparability.
- Limited data beyond 24 months within CKD per se; registry follow‑up extends to 3 years but CKD‑specific ABPM reporting is limited.

## Conclusions

For adults with CKD stages 3–4 and treatment‑resistant hypertension, RDN—principally with contemporary RF systems—provides meaningful reductions in office and ambulatory BP at 6–12 months without evidence of accelerated eGFR decline; durability signals extend to 24–36 months in registry data. Procedural safety appears acceptable with low rates of manageable vascular complications, particularly when contrast‑sparing techniques (CO₂ angiography) are applied. Evidence in advanced CKD (stage 4–5 not on dialysis) remains limited and suggests cautious feasibility. High‑quality, CKD‑inclusive sham‑controlled trials with standardized ABPM and renal outcomes are needed to define long‑term renal benefits, modality differences (RF vs ultrasound), and optimal patient selection.

## Implications for Practice and Research

- Practice: Consider RDN for carefully selected CKD stage 3–4 adults with TRH after optimization of diuretics and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, particularly when medication intolerance/adherence issues persist. Favor centers with CKD‑savvy protocols using CO₂ angiography when iodinated contrast poses risk. Prioritize ABPM for assessment and follow‑up.
- Research: Conduct CKD‑inclusive, sham‑controlled RCTs powered for ABPM and renal endpoints (eGFR slope, albuminuria), with adjudicated adverse events and standardized medication/adherence protocols. Explore ultrasound‑based RDN in CKD and define procedural endpoints (e.g., electrical stimulation) and imaging guidance strategies tailored to CKD anatomy. Evaluate long‑term (>24 months) renal outcomes and hard endpoints (ESKD, mortality).
