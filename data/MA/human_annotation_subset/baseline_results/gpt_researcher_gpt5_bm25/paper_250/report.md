# Comparative effectiveness of antidepressant treatments for adult treatment‑resistant depression: an evidence‑based network meta‑analysis synthesis

## Introduction

Treatment‑resistant depression (TRD)—commonly defined by inadequate response to at least two adequate antidepressant trials—affects roughly one‑third of patients with major depressive episodes and is associated with substantial morbidity, functional impairment, and healthcare costs. Clinical decision‑making in TRD often requires choosing among pharmacological augmentation or switch strategies and neuromodulatory procedures. Yet, head‑to‑head randomized trials are sparse, and the relative efficacy of available options remains challenging to contextualize without a comparative framework focused on outcomes that matter clinically, such as treatment response (≥50% symptom reduction) (Treatment‑resistant depression: definition, prevalence, detection, management, and investigational interventions, 2023). Against this backdrop, network meta‑analysis (NMA) offers a coherent means to integrate direct and indirect evidence to rank treatments and guide practice.

This report synthesizes and interprets the most recent and robust comparative evidence—including a large 2025 TRD‑specific NMA of randomized controlled trials (RCTs), NMAs of augmentation agents and neuromodulation, and pivotal head‑to‑head trials—to appraise the relative acute‑phase effectiveness of key pharmacological (e.g., ketamine/esketamine, atypical antipsychotic augmentation, lithium, thyroid hormone, minocycline) and neuromodulatory treatments (electroconvulsive therapy [ECT], repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation [rTMS], theta‑burst stimulation [TBS]) for adults with TRD. The primary outcome is acute treatment response using intention‑to‑treat data where available (Relative effectiveness of antidepressant treatments in treatment‑resistant depression: a systematic review and network meta‑analysis of randomized controlled trials, 2025).

## Methods overview

- Population: Adults with nonpsychotic unipolar major depression who failed ≥2 adequate antidepressant trials in the current episode, consistent with regulatory criteria for TRD (Treatment‑resistant depression: definition, prevalence, detection, management, and investigational interventions, 2023).
- Interventions and comparators:
  - Pharmacological: augmentation with aripiprazole and other second‑generation antipsychotics, lithium, thyroid hormone (liothyronine), modafinil, lamotrigine, minocycline; switch strategies (e.g., venlafaxine/duloxetine); ketamine (IV racemic) and esketamine (intranasal or IV).
  - Neuromodulation: ECT, rTMS protocols (left‑sided 10 Hz, right‑sided 1 Hz, bilateral), TBS variants (intermittent [iTBS], continuous [cTBS], bilateral).
- Outcomes: Primary—treatment response rate at acute endpoint (typically 2–12 weeks depending on modality); Secondary—acceptability (all‑cause discontinuation), remission, and tolerability where available.
- Evidence sources: A 2025 TRD‑specific NMA of 69 RCTs (10,285 participants, 25 treatments), NMAs on augmentation (2022) and neuromodulation (2019–2025), and large head‑to‑head trials including ketamine versus ECT (NEJM) and individual patient data (IPD) comparisons of esketamine versus rTMS (Relative effectiveness…, 2025; Augmentation strategies for treatment resistant major depression: A systematic review and network meta‑analysis, 2022; Comparative efficacy and acceptability of non‑surgical brain stimulation…, 2019; Ketamine versus ECT for nonpsychotic treatment‑resistant major depression, 2023; Intravenous ketamine versus esketamine for depression: a systematic review and meta‑analysis, 2025; Intranasal esketamine versus rTMS IPD analysis, 2025).

This report does not re‑analyze study‑level data but integrates effect estimates and qualitative rankings where reported, emphasizing RCTs, TRD‑specific samples, and NMAs with robust methods. Where precise odds ratios (ORs) are not reported in the provided abstracts, we present directionality (superior vs placebo/sham) and relative strength based on available comparative findings.

## Evidence base and comparative effectiveness

### Overview of the 2025 TRD network meta‑analysis

A comprehensive TRD‑focused NMA (69 RCTs; n=10,285; 25 interventions) compared pharmacological and neuromodulatory acute‑phase treatments with response as the primary outcome. Six of the 25 treatments achieved significantly higher response rates than placebo/sham; ECT was among these top performers. The NMA used a random‑effects model and RoB 2 for bias assessment (Relative effectiveness of antidepressant treatments in treatment‑resistant depression: a systematic review and network meta‑analysis of randomized controlled trials, 2025).

While the abstract is truncated, its main contributions are: (1) a broad, TRD‑specific network across modalities; (2) response‑focused outcomes; and (3) a shortlist of effective options significantly outperforming sham, including ECT. These findings anchor the comparative landscape and are consistent with other lines of evidence summarized below.

### Pharmacological strategies

#### Ketamine and esketamine

- Ketamine vs ECT: In a large noninferiority trial in nonpsychotic TRD, IV ketamine was noninferior to ECT for symptom reduction (and broadly comparable response), a finding corroborated by a 2024 moderator analysis that explored clinical features guiding selection (e.g., anxiety, comorbidity) without overturning noninferiority (Ketamine versus ECT for nonpsychotic treatment‑resistant major depression, 2023; Ketamine vs Electroconvulsive Therapy for Treatment‑Resistant Depression: A Secondary Analysis, 2024).
- Ketamine vs esketamine: A 2025 meta‑analysis of head‑to‑head trials found both IV ketamine and esketamine (IN/IV) effective for TRD, with subgroup analyses examining diagnosis and design; while pooled ORs are not detailed in the abstract, no decisive superiority emerged across sensitivity analyses, suggesting both agents are viable with differences likely driven by route, logistics, and safety monitoring (Intravenous ketamine versus esketamine for depression: a systematic review and meta‑analysis, 2025).

Interpretation: Rapid‑acting NMDA‑targeting treatments (ketamine/esketamine) are among the most effective acute options in TRD, with IV ketamine matching ECT in nonpsychotic TRD and IN esketamine providing an on‑label alternative with clinic‑based administration and monitoring.

#### Atypical antipsychotic augmentation and other augmentations

A 2022 NMA of augmentation strategies (65 studies; N=12,415; up to 24 weeks) reported significantly higher response versus placebo for several agents, notably:
- Aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, quetiapine, lithium, liothyronine, modafinil, nortriptyline, and olanzapine (augmentation or OFC) (Augmentation strategies for treatment resistant major depression: A systematic review and network meta‑analysis, 2022).

Aripiprazole has consistent RCT evidence across adult and late‑life TRD, with remission benefits in elderly nonresponders to venlafaxine XR and acceptable tolerability at optimized dosing (Efficacy, safety and tolerability of adjunctive aripiprazole in late life TRD, 2015; Aripiprazole augmentation trials, 2009; 2013). Real‑world comparative cohorts and secondary analyses (ASCERTAIN‑TRD) suggest augmentation with aripiprazole or rTMS versus switching can be effective, with patient preference influencing engagement and outcomes (Patient treatment preference in ASCERTAIN‑TRD, 2025).

Lithium augmentation remains an evidence‑supported classic strategy with modest effect sizes but broad availability; small RCTs in deeply resistant cohorts indicate benefit, though modern data are fewer (Lithium augmentation RCT, 2003). Thyroid hormone (liothyronine) and modafinil also showed signal in the 2022 NMA; lamotrigine augmentation has mixed evidence in unipolar TRD; and minocycline remains investigational with pilot signals but insufficient confirmatory RCTs (Lamotrigine augmentation meta‑analyses, 2016/2019; Minocycline pilot and protocol, 2017/2020; Systematic review of adjunct minocycline, 2023).

Interpretation: Among pharmacologic augmentations, aripiprazole (and brexpiprazole) have the most consistent, high‑quality evidence for improving response in TRD, with lithium and liothyronine as viable alternatives. Minocycline remains promising but unproven in adequately powered TRD RCTs.

### Neuromodulatory procedures

#### Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT)

ECT remains one of the most effective acute treatments in TRD, consistently outperforming sham and often other comparators. The 2025 TRD NMA identified ECT among the few interventions significantly superior to sham for response. Observational comparisons with rTMS suggest higher completion and faster time‑to‑response/remission in real‑world TRD cohorts (Relative effectiveness NMA, 2025; Real‑world ECT vs rTMS in Taiwan, 2025). Noninferiority of ketamine to ECT in nonpsychotic TRD expands options in shared decision‑making (Ketamine vs ECT trial, 2023).

#### Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) and theta‑burst stimulation (TBS)

A high‑quality NMA (2019) across non‑surgical brain stimulation found rTMS protocols and ECT superior to sham for response, with acceptable discontinuation profiles. Later RCTs and pragmatic trials in TRD support: 
- Unilateral L‑DLPFC 10 Hz rTMS and bilateral protocols as effective, with bilateral and TBS variants offering shorter sessions (Comparative efficacy of non‑surgical brain stimulation, 2019).
- TBS (iTBS, cTBS, bilateral) achieves broadly similar antidepressant outcomes to standard rTMS in adults, including TRD subgroups; bilateral TBS has non‑inferiority to bilateral rTMS in late life and across broader ages (BEAT‑D), improving clinic throughput (Theta burst stimulation meta‑analysis, 2021; BEAT‑D RCT, 2025).
- Accelerated TBS schedules show increasing support for rapid symptom gains, though standardization and sham‑controlled data in TRD are still accruing (High‑dose accelerated iTBS protocol, 2024; Predictors of response analysis, 2025).

Interpretation: rTMS (10 Hz left, 1 Hz right, bilateral) and TBS are effective, well‑tolerated neuromodulatory options for TRD with moderate response rates and better acceptability than many drugs. TBS offers comparable efficacy with markedly reduced session times, improving accessibility.

## Consistency, transitivity, and risk of bias

- The 2025 TRD NMA employed RoB 2 and random‑effects modeling. While specific node‑splitting or design‑by‑treatment tests are not detailed in the abstract, typical concerns in TRD NMAs include variation in TRD definitions (e.g., number/adequacy of failed trials), inpatient vs outpatient settings, baseline severity, and differences between augmentation and monotherapy networks that can affect transitivity (Relative effectiveness NMA, 2025).
- The 2022 augmentation NMA carefully separated augmentation regimens and used pairwise and network methods with random effects, strengthening internal validity for pharmacologic strategies (Augmentation NMA, 2022).
- The 2019 and 2021 neuromodulation syntheses provide cross‑validation for rTMS/TBS findings, though not restricted to exclusively ≥2‑trial TRD samples. TRD subgroup analyses in later NMAs and RCTs (e.g., BEAT‑D) support generalizability to TRD populations (Comparative efficacy NMA, 2019; BEAT‑D, 2025).

Overall, findings converged across modalities: ECT and ketamine are highest for acute efficacy; rTMS/TBS provide effective, better‑tolerated neuromodulatory options; atypical antipsychotic augmentation (especially aripiprazole/brexpiprazole) and lithium/T3 are pharmacologically robust.

## Acceptability and safety

- Acceptability (all‑cause discontinuation) was broadly favorable for neuromodulation relative to antipsychotic augmentation in prior NMAs; TMS/TBS have low dropout rates and benign side‑effect profiles (headache, scalp discomfort). ECT requires anesthesia and carries cognitive side‑effect risks requiring informed consent and monitoring (Comparative efficacy NMA, 2019).
- Aripiprazole/brexpiprazole carry risks of akathisia, weight gain, and metabolic effects; quetiapine and OFC have higher metabolic/sedative burdens; lithium requires renal/thyroid monitoring; liothyronine requires cardiac/thyroid monitoring (Augmentation NMA, 2022).
- Ketamine/esketamine require monitoring for dissociation, blood pressure elevations, and potential urinary risks; pharmacovigilance suggests esketamine may have lower or comparable urologic risk than ketamine, though caution is warranted in long‑term or high‑frequency use (Comparative safety of esketamine and ketamine in renal/urinary disorders, 2025).

## Subgroups and effect modifiers

- Late‑life TRD: Aripiprazole augmentation improved remission in older nonresponders; bilateral TBS was non‑inferior to bilateral rTMS, supporting neuromodulation feasibility in geriatric TRD (Adjunctive aripiprazole in late‑life TRD, 2015; BEAT‑D, 2025).
- Modality history: Prior ECT during the episode may predict lower iTBS response (retrospective), highlighting potential sequencing considerations (Response to iTBS with vs without prior ECT, 2025).
- Treatment duration and early improvement: Early symptom change during TMS courses predicts eventual nonresponse, enabling adaptive strategies (Lack of early reduction predicts nonresponse in rTMS/TBS, 2025).
- Patient preference: In comparative effectiveness settings, baseline preference may influence adherence and outcomes (ASCERTAIN‑TRD preference analysis, 2025).

## Practical guidance and opinionated synthesis

Integrating the best available evidence, the following hierarchy balances efficacy, acceptability, feasibility, and certainty:

1. Highest acute efficacy for nonpsychotic TRD: ECT and IV ketamine are top‑tier. ECT remains a standard with robust response and remission rates; ketamine is noninferior for symptom reduction in nonpsychotic TRD and offers rapid relief without the cognitive profile of ECT. Choice can be individualized based on urgency, patient preference, medical comorbidity, and access (Ketamine vs ECT, 2023; Relative effectiveness NMA, 2025).

2. Effective and well‑tolerated neuromodulation with moderate efficacy: rTMS (unilateral or bilateral) and TBS offer clinically meaningful response with low discontinuation rates. Bilateral TBS provides session‑time efficiency with non‑inferiority to bilateral rTMS in TRD, supporting it as a first‑line neuromodulatory option when ECT/ketamine are declined or inaccessible (Comparative efficacy NMA, 2019; BEAT‑D, 2025).

3. Pharmacologic augmentation with strongest evidence: Aripiprazole (and brexpiprazole) augmentation has the most consistent RCT and NMA support in TRD; lithium and liothyronine are credible alternatives, particularly when antipsychotic AEs are limiting. Quetiapine/OFC can be effective but require careful metabolic risk management (Augmentation NMA, 2022; Adjunctive aripiprazole RCTs, 2009/2013/2015).

4. Esketamine (IN) is an effective, regulated option suitable for outpatient administration with monitoring, comparable to IV ketamine in available head‑to‑head syntheses and an alternative when IV access/logistics are barriers (IV ketamine vs esketamine, 2025; esketamine vs rTMS IPD, 2025).

5. Insufficient for routine TRD use: Minocycline remains investigational; use should be limited to trials or carefully selected cases pending confirmatory evidence (Minocycline RCTs, 2017/2020/2023).

In practice, a stepped, patient‑centered approach is reasonable: consider neuromodulation (rTMS/TBS) or evidence‑based augmentation (aripiprazole) after two failed trials; escalate to rapid‑acting agents (ketamine/esketamine) or ECT for higher severity, suicidality, or need for rapid response. Shared decision‑making should incorporate patient preference, prior treatment history (including ECT/rTMS exposure), comorbidity, and logistics.

## Limitations

- Heterogeneity in defining “adequate” prior trials and duration across studies can threaten transitivity in networks.
- Mixing augmentation and monotherapy/switch strategies in NMAs requires caution when interpreting indirect comparisons.
- Some recent accelerated TBS protocols and specific rTMS variants have limited TRD‑specific sham‑controlled evidence.
- Abstracts provided do not include all effect sizes or SUCRA ranks; quantitative rankings herein are therefore qualitative and triangulated across multiple syntheses.

## Conclusions

Across contemporary NMAs and pivotal RCTs in adult TRD (≥2 failed antidepressants), ECT and IV ketamine emerge as the most effective for acute response in nonpsychotic TRD, with ketamine noninferior to ECT and esketamine providing a practical, on‑label alternative. rTMS and TBS deliver clinically meaningful response with favorable tolerability and efficiency trade‑offs, particularly bilateral TBS. Among pharmacological augmentations, aripiprazole (and brexpiprazole) have the most consistent evidence, while lithium and liothyronine are validated alternatives. Minocycline remains investigational. Treatment should be individualized, integrating efficacy, safety, patient preference, and access, with early symptom trajectories used to adapt nonresponders promptly.
