# Associations between Posterior Pole Ocular Structures and Female Hormonal Fluctuations Across the Menstrual Cycle and Post-Menopause: A Scoping Review

## Abstract
Objective: To map and critically appraise evidence on associations between macular, choroidal, and optic nerve head structures and endogenous female hormonal fluctuations across the menstrual cycle and post-menopause in healthy adult, non-pregnant women not using hormonal contraception or hormone replacement therapy (HRT).

Methods: Following PRISMA-ScR guidance, we defined a PEO question, prespecified eligibility criteria, and charted data from multimodal ocular imaging studies. Priority outcomes included choroidal thickness (CT), choroidal vascularity index (CVI), choriocapillaris (CC) flow, macular vessel density (VD) in superficial/deep capillary plexuses (SCP/DCP), foveal avascular zone (FAZ), peripapillary VD, retinal nerve fiber layer (RNFL), ganglion cell layer (GCL), and optic nerve head (ONH) metrics. We emphasized methodological quality, hormonal-phase ascertainment, and control of confounding (age, axial length, intraocular pressure [IOP], blood pressure [BP], diurnal variation).

Findings: Across menstrual cycle phases, the most reproducible signal is a modest choroidal thinning in the mid-/late luteal phase versus follicular/ovulatory phases on EDI- or SD-OCT, corroborated by decreased CVI in mid-luteal phase in one study. OCTA studies generally do not show consistent phase-related changes in macular/peripapillary VD or FAZ, though small physiological variability is reported. Retrobulbar Doppler flow indices appear stable across the cycle. In post-menopausal women, cross-sectional studies suggest lower RNFL and peripapillary VD and a larger FAZ versus reproductive-age controls, while CT findings conflict across studies (thinner on SD-OCT versus thicker on SS-OCTA in one report), likely reflecting design, device, and confounder heterogeneity.

Conclusion: Current evidence supports luteal-phase choroidal thinning as the most consistent menstrual-cycle association, with limited and inconsistent evidence for retinal microvascular changes. Post-menopausal differences suggest reduced neural and peripapillary microvascular metrics, but choroidal structural findings are heterogeneous. Future rigor requires hormone-validated phase ascertainment, standardized diurnal control, axial length/IOP/BP adjustment, device-agnostic OCTA metrics, and preregistered analyses with effect sizes.

## Background and Rationale
Estrogen and progesterone cross the blood-retinal barrier and bind intraocular receptors in retina, retinal pigment epithelium, choroid, and ONH tissues, plausibly modulating vascular tone, fluid homeostasis, and neural integrity. The menstrual cycle introduces predictable fluctuations in estradiol (E2) and progesterone (P4), while menopause entails sustained E2/P4 decline. Yet, clinical correlations in posterior pole structures remain uncertain due to small samples, heterogeneous imaging methods, day-based phase proxies, and limited endocrine validation ([Macular, choroidal and disc associations… 2025](local-corpus://Macular-choroidal-and-disc-associations-2025)).

Physiological context relevant to ocular perfusion includes luteal-phase rises in BP and thermogenic/progesterone effects, potential RAAS modulation, and early follicular elevations of natriuretic peptides (e.g., proANP), potentially influencing vascular tone and tissue hydration ([Choroidal Morphology and Systemic Circulation Changes… 2023](local-corpus://Choroidal-Morphology-and-Systemic-Circulation-Changes-2023); [Menstrual cycle phase and cardiovascular biomarkers 2026](local-corpus://Menstrual-cycle-phase-cardiovascular-biomarkers-2026)).

## Review Question (PEO)
- Population: Healthy adult, non-pregnant women (reproductive-age and post-menopausal), not using hormonal contraception or HRT.
- Exposure: Endogenous hormonal fluctuations by menstrual cycle phase; post-menopausal status.
- Outcomes: Posterior pole structural and perfusion metrics (macular thickness/volume; choroidal thickness/volume; CVI; CC flow/perfusion; macular SCP/DCP VD; FAZ metrics; peripapillary VD; RNFL; GCL; ONH topography).

---

## Eligibility Criteria
- Inclusion:
  - Prospective or cross-sectional human studies imaging macula/choroid/ONH (SD-/EDI-OCT, SS-OCT, OCTA, fundus imaging, Doppler ultrasound, mfERG) across menstrual phases or comparing post-menopausal vs reproductive-age.
  - Healthy adult women; no hormonal contraception/HRT; non-pregnant.
  - Reporting at least one prespecified posterior pole outcome.
- Exclusion:
  - Contraceptive/HRT intervention cohorts for the main synthesis (allowed for contextual discussion only).
  - Pregnancy, systemic/ocular disease cohorts (unless providing a healthy control arm we could isolate).
  - Pediatric or male cohorts; animal studies.
  - Case reports, editorials lacking original data.

## Results: Evidence Map and Synthesis

### Menstrual Cycle: Structural and Vascular Metrics

- Choroidal Thickness (CT) and CVI
  - Multiple small prospective studies report significantly thinner subfoveal CT in the mid-/late luteal phase compared with early follicular or ovulatory phases, with concurrent luteal increases in systemic BP in at least one cohort ([Choroidal thickness changes during the menstrual cycle 2013](local-corpus://Choroidal-thickness-changes-2013); [Choroidal Morphology and Systemic Circulation Changes… 2023](local-corpus://Choroidal-Morphology-and-Systemic-Circulation-Changes-2023)). A study quantifying CVI found a significant mid-luteal decrease in CVI, aligning with luteal choroidal thinning ([Assessment of choroidal vascularity index during the menstrual cycle 2022](local-corpus://CVI-menstrual-cycle-2022)). Collectively, these results support a reproducible small-magnitude luteal choroidal thinning signal.
- OCTA microvasculature (SCP/DCP VD; FAZ; CC)
  - A prospective SS-OCTA study with standardized imaging times (11:00–12:00) at early follicular (D3), ovulatory (D14), and mid-luteal (D21) phases found no consistent or clinically meaningful differences in macular or peripapillary VD across phases; FAZ was manually delineated without major phase-related change ([OCTA-based assessment… 2025](local-corpus://OCTA-menstrual-2025)).
  - Another prospective OCTA study with five serial scans per cycle reported physiological variability in retinal hemodynamics but did not establish robust phase-locked trends, emphasizing small fluctuations within normal limits ([Variabilities in Retinal Hemodynamics… 2024](local-corpus://Variabilities-retinal-hemodynamics-2024)).
  - Color Doppler ultrasound of retrobulbar circulation (OA, CRA, PCAs) across six timepoints per cycle in healthy women found no significant differences in pulsatility/resistance indices or peak systolic velocity, suggesting maintained ocular blood flow homeostasis across phases ([Change in retrobulbar circulation… 2002](local-corpus://Retrobulbar-Doppler-menstrual-2002)).
- Optic nerve head metrics
  - Contemporary high-resolution OCT/OCTA studies specifically evaluating ONH structural/vascular phase changes in healthy women are sparse; older confocal scanning laser ophthalmoscopy studies targeted special populations (diabetes, migraine) and are not strictly generalizable.

Overall, the choroid shows the most consistent cycle-linked structural change (luteal thinning), whereas retinal microvascular density and retrobulbar flow appear relatively stable within physiological variability, likely reflecting robust autoregulation.

### Post-Menopause: Structural and Vascular Metrics

- Neural structure (RNFL, GCL) and peripapillary microvasculature
  - Cross-sectional OCT/OCTA evidence indicates lower RNFL thickness and reduced peripapillary VD in post-menopausal compared with premenopausal women, with a concomitant small rise in IOP and stronger negative correlation between IOP and VD than between IOP and RNFL, implying pressure-related and microvascular contributors ([Effect of postmenopausal hormonal drop on ONH and peripapillary perfusion 2022](local-corpus://Postmenopausal-ONH-perfusion-2022)).
- Macular microvasculature and FAZ
  - SS-OCTA results suggest larger FAZ area and reduced CC VD in post-menopausal women versus reproductive-age controls; SCP/DCP VDs were similar between groups ([Comparison of retinochoroidal microvascular circulation in menstrual and postmenopausal periods 2023](local-corpus://OCTA-menstrual-vs-postmenopausal-2023)).
- Choroidal thickness
  - SD-OCT studies reported thinner macular sectors and thinner choroid in post-menopause relative to reproductive-age controls ([Evaluation… postmenopausal women using SD-OCT 2014](local-corpus://SD-OCT-postmenopausal-2014)), whereas one SS-OCTA study paradoxically reported thicker CT in the post-menopausal group—this divergence may reflect device differences (SS vs SD), sample characteristics (age, axial length distributions), measurement locations, diurnal timing, or unmeasured confounding ([OCTA menstrual vs postmenopausal 2023](local-corpus://OCTA-menstrual-vs-postmenopausal-2023)).

Collectively, neural and peripapillary microvascular differences appear more consistent than choroidal structural differences across post-menopausal studies, though causality cannot be inferred in cross-sectional designs.

## Methodological Appraisal and Sources of Heterogeneity

- Phase ascertainment and endocrine validation:
  - Most menstrual-cycle imaging studies used day-based proxies (e.g., day 3, 14, 21) rather than hormone assays, with few measuring E2/P4/LH to confirm phases. This weakens causal attribution to specific hormonal milieus ([OCTA-based assessment… 2025](local-corpus://OCTA-menstrual-2025); [Variabilities… 2024](local-corpus://Variabilities-retinal-hemodynamics-2024)). Some historical ONH studies drew hormones but in non-healthy subgroups (e.g., diabetes/migraine), limiting generalizability.
- Control of confounders:
  - Axial length (AL) and myopia substantially affect CT and OCTA-derived VD and must be measured and adjusted; normative datasets show choroidal VD and peripapillary VD vary with AL and topographic sector ([SS-OCTA Peripapillary Normative 2025](local-corpus://SS-OCTA-Peripapillary-Normative-2025)). Few cycle/post-menopause studies explicitly adjusted for AL.
  - IOP and BP: luteal-phase BP increases were documented alongside choroidal thinning ([Choroidal Morphology… 2023](local-corpus://Choroidal-Morphology-and-Systemic-Circulation-Changes-2023)); post-menopausal studies found slightly higher IOP and lower peripapillary VD and RNFL ([Postmenopausal ONH perfusion 2022](local-corpus://Postmenopausal-ONH-perfusion-2022)).
  - Diurnal variation: CT and OCTA metrics fluctuate diurnally; one OCTA menstrual study standardized imaging to 11:00–12:00, a best practice not universal across studies ([OCTA-based assessment… 2025](local-corpus://OCTA-menstrual-2025)).
  - Hydration, caffeine, nicotine, exercise, and systemic meds may acutely alter perfusion; rarely reported or standardized.
- Imaging variability:
  - Device/platform heterogeneity (SD-OCT vs SS-OCT/SS-OCTA), scan sizes (3×3 vs 6×6 mm), segmentation algorithms, and projection artifact handling differ, impeding meta-analytic synthesis.
  - Manual CT measurement (caliper) invites intergrader variability; CVI may reduce sensitivity to hydration but requires standardized binarization pipelines.

- Study design and reporting:
  - Small sample sizes, multiple comparisons without correction, and lack of a priori power/effect size reporting are common. Cross-sectional post-menopausal comparisons limit causal inference and are vulnerable to age and AL confounding.

## Interpretation and Clinical Relevance

- Menstrual cycle:
  - The most consistent ocular correlate of cyclic hormonal changes is a small luteal-phase thinning of the choroid and decreased CVI, potentially reflecting progesterone-related systemic hemodynamic/oncotic shifts or RAAS modulation. Retinal microvascular density and retrobulbar flow are generally stable within physiological variability, indicating effective ocular autoregulation in healthy women. For clinical practice and research imaging, rigorous phasic scheduling is likely unnecessary for retinal OCTA endpoints, but diurnal control and awareness of small luteal CT shifts may improve repeatability in choroid-focused studies ([Macular, choroidal and disc associations… 2025](local-corpus://Macular-choroidal-and-disc-associations-2025); [OCTA-based assessment… 2025](local-corpus://OCTA-menstrual-2025)).
- Post-menopause:
  - Evidence suggests modest reductions in RNFL and peripapillary VD and enlargement of FAZ with menopause, consistent with microvascular and neural vulnerabilities in estrogen-deficient states, though cross-sectional designs preclude causal attribution. Divergent choroidal thickness findings across modalities underscore the need for standardized measurement and careful AL/IOP/BP control. Clinically, menopause status may be a relevant covariate in normative databases for RNFL/peripapillary VD and FAZ metrics, but current data do not support menopause-stage–specific diagnostic thresholds.

Overall opinion: The preponderance of reliable, phase-validated evidence supports a small, consistent luteal reduction in choroidal thickness/CVI without robust, reproducible changes in retinal OCTA microvasculature across the cycle in healthy women. Post-menopausal status is associated with measurable peripapillary microvascular and neural differences, while choroidal structural changes remain inconsistent across studies due to methodological heterogeneity.

## Recommendations for Future Research

1. Endocrine validation and phasic precision
   - Confirm menstrual phases with serum E2, P4, LH, and, when feasible, serial sampling to anchor imaging to peak/trough hormonal windows; adopt consensus definitions for post-menopausal status (e.g., >12 months amenorrhea ± FSH thresholds).
2. Standardization and preregistration
   - Predefine primary endpoints (e.g., SFCT, CVI, CC perfusion, peripapillary VD), diurnal windows (±1 hour), and analytic pipelines; preregister protocols with sample size justifications and correction for multiple comparisons.
3. Confounder control
   - Measure and adjust for axial length (and refractive error), IOP, BP/heart rate, hydration, caffeine/nicotine/exercise within 12–24 hours, and systemic medications; stratify or adjust for ethnicity given CT/FAZ differences across populations.
4. Device-agnostic quantification
   - Employ harmonized OCTA metrics (e.g., hardware-agnostic perfusion density, 3D CC topographic mapping) and choroidal metrics less sensitive to hydration (CVI, 3D CVV/CSV) to improve reproducibility across platforms.
5. Longitudinal and mechanistic designs
   - Dense-sampling within individuals across full cycles and menopausal transition; integrate systemic biomarkers (natriuretic peptides, adrenomedullin, copeptin) to link cardiovascular endocrine milieu with choroidal metrics ([Menstrual cycle phase and cardiovascular biomarkers 2026](local-corpus://Menstrual-cycle-phase-cardiovascular-biomarkers-2026)).
6. Reporting
   - Provide effect sizes with 95% CIs; share de-identified imaging/segmentation code; include outlier and quality control criteria.

---

## Limitations of the Evidence Base
- Small samples, limited racial/ethnic diversity, day-based phase assignment without hormone assays, cross-sectional post-menopausal comparisons, and heterogeneous imaging/analysis protocols limit generalizability and preclude meta-analysis across most outcomes. Publication bias cannot be excluded.

---

## Conclusion
In healthy women, luteal-phase choroidal thinning (and decreased CVI) is a reproducible structural correlate of cyclic endocrine changes; retinal OCTA metrics are comparatively stable across phases, aligning with robust ocular autoregulation. Post-menopausal changes in neural and peripapillary microvascular metrics are apparent, while choroidal structural findings conflict across modalities and cohorts. Methodological standardization, endocrine validation, and rigorous confounder control are essential to resolve remaining uncertainties and to determine whether these physiological associations have clinically meaningful implications for posterior pole assessments across women’s reproductive life stages.
