# Association between agricultural land-use exposure and infectious disease risks in Southeast Asia: An updated evidence synthesis

## Background and rationale

Rapid agricultural expansion and intensification across Southeast Asia (SEA) have transformed landscapes through oil palm and rubber plantations, livestock development, irrigation, and forest conversion. These changes alter ecological communities, vector habitats, and human–animal contact, potentially shifting infectious disease risk. A 2019 meta-analysis synthesizing 34 studies from SEA reported a substantial and consistent association: individuals living or working in agricultural land were on average 1.74 times as likely to be infected with a pathogen compared with unexposed populations (95% CI: 1.47–2.07) (Agricultural land-uses consistently exacerbate infectious disease risks in Southeast Asia, 2019). The largest effects were observed for hookworm, malaria, scrub typhus, and spotted fever group rickettsioses, whereas no change was detected for fecal–oral pathogens—implying ecological mechanisms beyond water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) as primary drivers ([Agricultural land-uses consistently exacerbate infectious disease risks in Southeast Asia, 2019](https://local_corpus/agri_disease_SEA_2019)).

Since 2019, additional entomological and eco-epidemiological evidence has clarified how land-use mosaics, deforestation, and plantation economies facilitate disease transmission—particularly zoonotic and vector-borne infections such as Plasmodium knowlesi malaria. This report synthesizes the best available evidence to address the question: does occupational or residential exposure to agricultural land use increase the risk of human infectious disease in SEA compared with unexposed populations? We summarize effect estimates from the 2019 meta-analysis and integrate post-2019 mechanistic and epidemiological findings to contextualize the magnitude, heterogeneity, and plausibility of associations relevant to policy and prevention.

## Methods (overview of evidence base)

- Population, Exposure, Comparator, Outcome (PECO):
  - Population: People living in or near agricultural land or working in agricultural occupations in SEA.
  - Exposure: Occupational or residential exposure to agricultural land uses (e.g., oil palm, rubber, livestock, rice cultivation, mixed agriculture).
  - Comparator: Populations without agricultural land-use exposure (non-agricultural workers, non-agricultural residents, forest or urban residents where applicable).
  - Outcomes: Laboratory- or clinically-confirmed infectious diseases (vector-borne, zoonotic, soil-transmitted helminths, fecal–oral infections).

- Evidence sources:
  - Pooled quantitative evidence from a SEA-focused meta-analysis (34 studies; 2019).
  - Regional observational and entomological investigations on malaria (including P. knowlesi), scrub typhus/rickettsial diseases, and helminthiases (2013–2025).
  - Reviews on environmental/occupational determinants of leptospirosis in tropical contexts and global ecological studies linked to human-dominated landscapes.

- Study designs: Observational (case–control, cross-sectional, cohort), entomological surveys, geospatial ecological studies.

- Analytic approach:
  - Report the pooled odds ratios (ORs) and subgroup findings from the 2019 meta-analysis.
  - Provide qualitative triangulation with later mechanistic and epidemiological studies to assess coherence and residual uncertainty.
  - Consider risk-of-bias (ROBINS-I domains) and applicability to current policy needs.

Because individual study-level data beyond the 2019 synthesis are not provided here, no de novo quantitative pooling is performed for post-2019 literature. Instead, we integrate these newer findings to appraise biological plausibility, differential risks by land-use type, and emergent zoonotic threats.

## Results

### Overall association and disease-specific effects

The 2019 meta-analysis demonstrated a robust overall association: agricultural exposure increased odds of infectious disease by 74% relative to unexposed groups (OR 1.74, 95% CI: 1.47–2.07). Effects varied by land-use and pathogen class, with strongest associations for vector-borne and soil-transmitted infections. Fecal–oral diseases showed no significant change, suggesting that agricultural exposure primarily amplifies risks mediated by vectors, zoonotic reservoirs, and soil contact rather than classic WASH pathways ([Agricultural land-uses consistently exacerbate infectious disease risks in Southeast Asia, 2019](https://local_corpus/agri_disease_SEA_2019)).

Land-use types with the largest observed effects included oil palm, rubber, and non-poultry livestock farming ([Agricultural land-uses consistently exacerbate infectious disease risks in Southeast Asia, 2019](https://local_corpus/agri_disease_SEA_2019)).

### Post-2019 evidence reinforcing ecological mechanisms

- Zoonotic malaria (Plasmodium knowlesi):
  - Land-use changes (deforestation, plantations) increase contact among macaque reservoirs, anopheline vectors, and forest-edge workers/residents, intensifying transmission risk. Climate variability modulates vector habitats, compounding land-use effects ([Plasmodium knowlesi and global changes: socio-ecological drivers and challenges, 2025](https://local_corpus/knowlesi_global_change_2025)).
  - In Malaysian Borneo (Sabah), fine-scale land-use and configuration are associated with more favorable breeding habitats for P. knowlesi vectors, explaining higher local risk in agricultural mosaics ([Environmental and spatial risk factors for the larval habitats of Plasmodium knowlesi vectors in Sabah, Malaysian Borneo, 2021](https://local_corpus/knowlesi_larval_habitats_sabah_2021)).
  - Entomological case–control work indicates that peri-domestic exposure can be substantial, pointing to household-adjacent agriculture as a risk context, not only deep-forest worksites ([Investigating the Contribution of Peri-domestic Transmission…, 2016](https://local_corpus/peridomestic_knowlesi_sabah_2016)).
  - Along the Thailand–Myanmar border and within Myanmar, very high human-biting rates by diverse Anopheles species can offset low sporozoite rates, sustaining transmission in agricultural/forest fringe communities ([Entomological determinants of malaria transmission in Kayin state, Eastern Myanmar, 2018](https://local_corpus/entomology_myanmar_2018)). Joint outbreak investigations in Thailand highlight forest-going occupations and mixed civilian–military exposures in highly forested/agricultural districts ([Civilian-military malaria outbreak response in Thailand, 2021](https://local_corpus/civilian_military_malaria_thailand_2021)).
  - Rubber plantation workers in Cambodia illustrate occupational risk stratification within plantation economies—relevant for vector exposure timing (night work) and forest-edge ecology ([Rubber plantations and drug resistant malaria: a cross-sectional survey in Cambodia, 2019](https://local_corpus/rubber_malaria_cambodia_2019)).

- Helminth and food-borne trematodes:
  - Hookworm risk coheres with agricultural soil contact (e.g., barefoot field work) and sanitation gaps on farm plots—consistent with the pooled OR of 2.42 from the 2019 meta-analysis ([Agricultural land-uses consistently exacerbate infectious disease risks in Southeast Asia, 2019](https://local_corpus/agri_disease_SEA_2019)).
  - In Northeast Thailand, Opisthorchis viverrini prevalence varies with village location, land-use composition, and landscape structure, implying that agricultural water management and habitat mosaics shape the transmission ecology of food-borne trematodes ([Environmental determinants of Opisthorchis viverrini prevalence in northeast Thailand, 2013](https://local_corpus/opisthorchis_env_thailand_2013)).

- Rickettsial infections:
  - Elevated odds for scrub typhus and spotted fever group rickettsioses in agricultural settings suggest increased exposure to chiggers and ticks in disturbed vegetation, field edges, and plantations—aligning with the large pooled effects observed ([Agricultural land-uses consistently exacerbate infectious disease risks in Southeast Asia, 2019](https://local_corpus/agri_disease_SEA_2019)).

- Leptospirosis:
  - Systematic review evidence links environmental and occupational factors to leptospirosis risk—especially agricultural tasks and wet-season field exposures—corroborating agricultural land-use as a key determinant in tropical, resource-limited settings ([Environmental and Occupational Factors Associated with Leptospirosis: A Systematic Review, 2024](https://local_corpus/leptospirosis_environment_occupational_2024)).

- Broader ecological context:
  - Across human-dominated ecosystems, wildlife hosts that carry human-shared pathogens increase in relative richness and abundance, supporting a global mechanistic basis for heightened zoonotic risk in agricultural landscapes ([Zoonotic host diversity increases in human-dominated ecosystems, 2020](https://local_corpus/zoonotic_host_diversity_2020)).

### Interpretation of heterogeneity

Although the 2019 synthesis did not report heterogeneity statistics here, variability is expected across:
- Land-use categories (e.g., oil palm vs rubber vs livestock),
- Pathogen groups (vector-borne > soil-transmitted > fecal–oral),
- Geographic and climatic subregions,
- Occupational roles (e.g., rubber tappers, plantation harvesters, livestock handlers),
- Exposure intensity and co-exposures (e.g., forest proximity, night work, housing quality).

Consistent elevation for vector and soil-transmitted infections, alongside null findings for fecal–oral pathogens, indicates that unmeasured confounding by general poverty or sanitation alone is unlikely to explain the association. Instead, vector ecology, wildlife host dynamics, and behavior-specific exposure (e.g., forest-edge work at dawn/dusk/night) appear central.

## Risk of bias and certainty of evidence

- Study designs were predominantly observational. Key ROBINS-I concerns include:
  - Confounding (e.g., socio-economic status, mobility, housing quality). Notably, disease-specific patterns and null effects for fecal–oral infections argue against uniform confounding.
  - Exposure misclassification (residential proximity vs task-specific occupational exposure; dynamic migration/seasonality).
  - Outcome misclassification (variability in diagnostics; strengthened by studies using laboratory confirmation).
  - Selection bias (healthy worker effect, clinic-based sampling).
- Publication bias/small-study effects cannot be excluded; vector-borne diseases are often prioritized, potentially overrepresenting positive associations.
- Certainty (GRADE): 
  - Overall: Moderate certainty that agricultural land-use exposure increases infectious disease risk in SEA. 
  - By disease: Moderate-to-high for malaria, scrub typhus/rickettsiae, and hookworm due to consistent magnitude and mechanistic coherence; low-to-moderate for leptospirosis and trematodes due to ecological complexity and potential exposure misclassification.
  - Fecal–oral infections: Low certainty of no effect overall due to heterogeneity in WASH contexts, but the best available pooled estimate suggests little or no association with agricultural exposure.

## Biological plausibility

- Vector ecology: Plantation understories, irrigation, and forest-edge mosaics create larval habitats and microclimates favoring Anopheles spp., chigger, and tick populations. Night work and forest/plantation shifts synchronize with vector biting times.
- Zoonotic interfaces: Deforestation and agricultural encroachment increase contact among humans, wildlife reservoirs, and vectors—especially for simian malaria in macaque territories.
- Soil exposure: Frequent skin contact with contaminated soil promotes hookworm transmission in agricultural workers.
- Hydrometeorology: Monsoon cycles, irrigation, and standing water in agricultural settings amplify leptospiral contamination and mosquito breeding.

## Policy and public health implications

- Target high-risk land-use settings: Oil palm, rubber plantations, and non-poultry livestock operations show the largest effect sizes; prioritize them for integrated interventions.
- Vector control tailored to plantations and forest edges:
  - Personal protection for night/early-morning workers (repellents, treated clothing, long sleeves, boots).
  - Targeted larval source management and environmental modifications compatible with plantation operations.
  - Enhanced surveillance for P. knowlesi and other vector-borne pathogens in agricultural districts.
- Occupational health measures:
  - Provision and enforcement of personal protective equipment for soil and water exposures (boots, gloves).
  - Health education addressing peri-domestic risk and work-related behaviors; scheduling adjustments to reduce peak vector exposure where feasible.
- Environmental management:
  - Landscape planning that reduces high-risk edge habitats; improved drainage and water management to limit vector breeding.
  - One Health approaches to wildlife–livestock–human interfaces, including surveillance for zoonotic spillover.
- Maintain WASH investments:
  - Although fecal–oral diseases were not elevated overall, WASH remains essential—especially for leptospirosis mitigation in wet agricultural environments.

## Limitations of the evidence base

- Temporal changes: Agricultural practices and vector control have evolved since some included studies; ongoing monitoring is required.
- Exposure granularity: Many studies used proxies (residence, occupation) instead of detailed task-specific exposure metrics.
- Generalizability: Findings apply most directly to SEA contexts; still, mechanistic insights may inform other tropical agricultural frontiers.

## Conclusions

The best available evidence supports a clear, quantitatively substantial association between occupational or residential exposure to agricultural land use and increased infectious disease risk in Southeast Asia. The 2019 meta-analysis indicates a 74% higher odds of infection among exposed populations, with particularly strong associations for malaria (including P. knowlesi contexts), hookworm, scrub typhus, and spotted fever group rickettsioses. Subsequent entomological and eco-epidemiological studies reinforce biological plausibility, demonstrating how plantations, deforestation, and agricultural mosaics enhance vector habitats and human–vector–reservoir contact. The absence of increased risk for fecal–oral diseases in pooled analyses suggests that the observed associations are driven primarily by vector-borne and soil-transmitted pathways rather than generalized sanitation differences.

In our judgment, the overall certainty of evidence is moderate and sufficient to justify targeted prevention in high-risk agricultural settings—especially oil palm and rubber plantations and livestock operations—through integrated vector management, occupational protections, and landscape-level interventions within a One Health framework.
