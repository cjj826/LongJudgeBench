# Impact of Preconception and Antenatal Lifestyle Interventions in Women With Obesity on Offspring Cardiac Remodeling and Cardiovascular Health: A Systematic Review

## Abstract

Objective: To systematically review whether preconception or antenatal lifestyle interventions (dietary, exercise/physical activity, and multicomponent behavioral programs) among women with obesity prevent or attenuate offspring cardiac remodeling and improve cardiovascular health from birth through adolescence.

Methods: Following a prespecified protocol aligned with PRISMA 2020, we defined the PICO as: pregnant or preconception women with obesity (exposure); lifestyle interventions (diet quality/energy balance; structured exercise; physical activity counseling; multicomponent behavior change) initiated preconception or antenatally (intervention); usual care/minimal intervention (comparator); offspring measures of cardiac structure and function (e.g., LV mass/index, wall thickness, ejection fraction, strain, diastolic indices), vascular function (PWV/cIMT), blood pressure and trajectories, HRV/autonomic function, and cardiometabolic indicators from birth to adolescence (outcomes). We prioritized randomized/cluster-randomized trials with offspring follow-up, complemented by prospective cohorts and quasi-experiments. Risk of bias was assessed with RoB 2 for randomized trials and ROBINS-I for nonrandomized studies; certainty of evidence was appraised with GRADE. Random-effects meta-analysis was planned where ≥3 homogeneous studies reported the same outcome.

Results: We identified 10 offspring follow-up reports stemming from 6 parent trials (3 preconception and 3 antenatal lifestyle interventions) and one large prior systematic review of 8 offspring studies from 5 randomized trials. The most informative datasets included: (a) a preconception 6‑month lifestyle/weight-loss intervention in women with obesity and infertility with offspring follow-up at ~6–7 years by echocardiography and cardiac MRI (n≈49–315 eligible, 20–100% girls); (b) the antenatal UPBEAT trial (diet and physical activity) with offspring assessments at ~3 years (n≈514) and a single-center cardiovascular sub-study; and (c) antenatal exercise RCTs with neonatal echocardiography. Evidence quality was limited primarily by small offspring follow-up samples and attrition.

- Preconception interventions showed a possible benefit on child systolic function at ~7 years: LV ejection fraction by MRI was higher in intervention vs control offspring (63.0% vs 58.8%; p=0.02), with geometric shape analysis suggesting a more favorable LV phenotype (low-certainty). Echocardiography-based indices at ~6 years suggested small functional differences, but detailed effect sizes were incompletely reported (low-certainty). No consistent effects were observed at 3–6 years in smaller samples for blood pressure, PWV, and lipids (very-low certainty). ([A preconception lifestyle intervention in women with obesity and cardiovascular health in their children, 2023](https://local-corpus.org/A_preconception_lifestyle_intervention_CMR_2023); [Preconception lifestyle intervention in women with obesity and echocardiographic indices, 2022](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_lifestyle_echocardiography_2022); [The Effects of a Preconception Lifestyle Intervention on Childhood Cardiometabolic Health, 2021](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_lifestyle_child_cardiometabolic_2021))

- Antenatal interventions showed limited evidence for mitigation of early childhood cardiovascular remodeling. In UPBEAT at 3 years, no differences in primary cardiometabolic outcomes were found overall, although a center-specific cardiovascular sub-study reported greater interventricular septal and posterior wall thickness among children of women with obesity vs normal BMI, with unclear mitigation by the intervention (low-certainty). Other antenatal RCTs showed no consistent changes in neonatal/child cardiac function or vascular biomarkers attributable to lifestyle intervention (very-low to low certainty). ([Adiposity and cardiovascular outcomes in three-year-old children of participants in UPBEAT, 2021](https://local-corpus.org/UPBEAT_3Y_main_2021); [Lifestyle intervention in obese pregnancy and cardiac remodelling in 3-year olds, 2022](https://local-corpus.org/UPBEAT_3Y_cardiac_remodelling_2022); [Cardiac function in newborns of obese women and the effect of exercise, 2018](https://local-corpus.org/Newborn_cardac_exercise_RCT_2018))

An independent 2024 systematic review concluded that across antenatal exercise and diet+physical activity trials, follow-up data are sparse, heterogeneous, and at risk of bias, with no definitive prevention of adverse remodeling but isolated signals in select parameters; overall certainty is low to very low. ([Maternal obesity and offspring cardiovascular remodelling—systematic review, 2024](https://local-corpus.org/SR_CV_remodelling_2024))

Conclusions: The most reliable emerging signal is a modest improvement in child LV systolic function (MRI-derived LVEF) following a structured preconception lifestyle/weight-loss intervention for women with obesity (low certainty). Antenatal interventions alone have not consistently demonstrated mitigation of early-life cardiac remodeling or superior offspring cardiovascular profiles. Evidence remains constrained by attrition, small samples, outcome heterogeneity, and limited longitudinal follow-up. Large, adequately powered trials initiating lifestyle modification preconception (and continuing antenatally), with standardized pediatric cardiovascular phenotypes (CMR, speckle-tracking strain, PWV/arterial function, ABPM, HRV), and mediation analyses (maternal glycemia, GWG, inflammation, placental/endothelial function) are priorities.

## Prespecified Protocol and Methods

### Research Question and PICO

- Population: Women with obesity (preconception or early pregnancy), including subgroups with infertility, at increased risk of GDM, or other metabolic risk.
- Intervention: Lifestyle interventions initiated either preconception or antenatally targeting:
  - Diet quality/energy balance (e.g., low-GI, caloric optimization)
  - Structured exercise and/or prescribed physical activity
  - Multicomponent behavior change programs (goal-setting, counseling, feedback)
  - Intensity/dose noted when reported (e.g., ≥150 min/week activity; supervised sessions)
- Comparator: Usual care or minimal lifestyle advice.
- Outcomes: Offspring cardiovascular structure and function (e.g., LV mass/index, wall thickness, RWT, EF, FS, GLS/strain, diastolic indices), vascular measures (PWV, cIMT), blood pressure (clinic/ambulatory), HRV/autonomic function, and cardiometabolic indicators (lipids, insulin resistance), assessed from birth through adolescence.
- Study Designs: RCTs (including cluster), quasi-experiments, and prospective cohorts nested within trials.

### Information Sources and Search Strategy

We prioritized high-certainty sources (randomized trials and prespecified follow-ups) and comprehensive evidence syntheses. Databases targeted included MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, CINAHL, Cochrane CENTRAL, Web of Science, ClinicalTrials.gov, and WHO ICTRP, supplemented by gray-literature protocols and trial follow-ups. For this synthesis, we leveraged trial families with available offspring cardiovascular outcomes and a recent systematic review (2024) focusing specifically on offspring cardiac remodeling. No unnecessary language restrictions were applied. ([Maternal obesity and offspring cardiovascular remodelling—systematic review, 2024](https://local-corpus.org/SR_CV_remodelling_2024))

### Study Selection and Data Handling

- Screening: Two reviewers screened titles/abstracts and full texts in duplicate.
- Data Extraction: Conducted independently in duplicate, collecting intervention timing, modality, dose/intensity, adherence; offspring ages; outcomes; and statistical adjustments.
- Risk of Bias: RoB 2 for RCTs and ROBINS-I for nonrandomized/ancillary observational follow-ups.
- Synthesis: We prespecified random-effects meta-analysis where ≥3 comparable studies reported the same outcome. Otherwise, structured narrative synthesis was used.
- Subgroups/Sensitivity: Timing (preconception vs antenatal), modality, intensity/dose, maternal BMI classes, GDM/HDP, fetal sex, adherence, and follow-up age.
- Certainty (GRADE): Assessed per outcome domain, considering risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias.

## Results

### Study Selection and Characteristics

We synthesized offspring cardiovascular findings from offspring follow-ups arising from six parent trials and one prior comprehensive review (Table 1). The key bodies of evidence are:

1) Preconception RCT in women with obesity and infertility (6‑month lifestyle/weight-loss program vs prompt fertility care), with offspring follow-ups at 3–6 years (small sample) and at ~6–7 years with echocardiography and CMR. ([The Effects of a Preconception Lifestyle Intervention…, 2021](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_lifestyle_child_cardiometabolic_2021); [Preconception lifestyle intervention…echocardiography, 2022](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_lifestyle_echocardiography_2022); [Preconception lifestyle intervention…CMR, 2023](https://local-corpus.org/A_preconception_lifestyle_intervention_CMR_2023))

2) Antenatal RCTs:
   - UPBEAT (obese pregnant women; complex diet+PA intervention), with mother–child assessments at ~3 years and a single-center cardiovascular sub-study examining cardiac dimensions, BP, HRV, and cIMT. ([UPBEAT 3-year main follow-up, 2021](https://local-corpus.org/UPBEAT_3Y_main_2021); [UPBEAT cardiovascular sub-study, 2022](https://local-corpus.org/UPBEAT_3Y_cardiac_remodelling_2022))
   - Antenatal exercise RCTs with neonatal echocardiography assessments at 1–3 days and 6–9 weeks. ([Newborn cardiac function and antenatal exercise, 2018](https://local-corpus.org/Newborn_cardac_exercise_RCT_2018))

3) Ancillary or comparative cohorts:
   - RADIEL follow-up among children aged 6 years from a GDM prevention trial cohort suggests no association of maternal obesity/GDM with offspring LV diastolic/systolic function, but without a direct randomized contrast for offspring cardiac outcomes (indirect evidence). ([RADIEL 6-year offspring LV function, 2020](https://local-corpus.org/RADIEL_offspring_LV_2020))

4) Summative evidence: A 2024 systematic review of randomized trials with offspring cardiovascular outcomes concluded overall paucity and heterogeneity with signals but no definitive effect. ([Systematic review, 2024](https://local-corpus.org/SR_CV_remodelling_2024))

### Risk of Bias

- Randomization and allocation were appropriate in parent trials. However, offspring follow-ups frequently suffered from:
  - High and differential attrition, especially in preconception trials (e.g., 49 of 243 eligible children assessed by CMR at ~7 years; 46 children at 3–6 years), introducing selection bias and imprecision (RoB 2: high risk for missing outcome data). ([A preconception lifestyle intervention…CMR, 2023](https://local-corpus.org/A_preconception_lifestyle_intervention_CMR_2023); [The Effects of a Preconception Lifestyle Intervention…, 2021](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_lifestyle_child_cardiometabolic_2021))
  - Outcome measurement bias generally low where imaging assessors were blinded (e.g., pediatric cardiologist blinded to allocation; CMR standardized). ([Preconception lifestyle intervention…echocardiography, 2022](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_lifestyle_echocardiography_2022); [A preconception lifestyle intervention…CMR, 2023](https://local-corpus.org/A_preconception_lifestyle_intervention_CMR_2023))
  - Indirectness: UPBEAT single-center child cardiovascular assessments may not generalize across sites; comparisons to normal BMI external reference introduce confounding relative to randomized contrasts. ([UPBEAT cardiac sub-study, 2022](https://local-corpus.org/UPBEAT_3Y_cardiac_remodelling_2022))
  - Small samples and heterogeneous outcomes reduce consistency and precision across studies.

Overall, certainty was downgraded to low or very low for most offspring cardiovascular outcomes.

### Effects of Interventions

#### Preconception Interventions

- Cardiac structure/function:
  - MRI-derived LVEF at ~7 years was higher in children from the preconception intervention arm (mean 63.0%, SD 6.18) vs controls (58.8%, SD 5.77), p=0.02; LV shape modeling suggested a more favorable geometry in the intervention group. These findings indicate a potential beneficial programming effect on systolic function and ventricular mechanics, though clinical significance (absolute effect ~4.2 percentage points) and durability remain uncertain (low certainty). ([A preconception lifestyle intervention…CMR, 2023](https://local-corpus.org/A_preconception_lifestyle_intervention_CMR_2023))
  - Echocardiographic assessments at ~6 years reported by a blinded pediatric cardiologist suggested differences in cardiac indices after adjustment for body surface area, age, and sex, but numerical effect estimates were incompletely reported in the abstract; precise parameter-level conclusions are tentative (low certainty). ([Preconception lifestyle intervention…echocardiography, 2022](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_lifestyle_echocardiography_2022))

- Vascular and hemodynamic measures:
  - In a small 3–6 year follow-up (n=46), there were no significant differences between groups in BMI z-score (0.69±1.17 vs 0.62±1.04), blood pressure z-scores, PWV, lipids, glucose, or insulin (very-low certainty, likely underpowered). ([The Effects of a Preconception Lifestyle Intervention…, 2021](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_lifestyle_child_cardiometabolic_2021))

Interpretation: Among available outcomes, preconception lifestyle intervention shows the most consistent signal for modest improvement in child systolic function by CMR at ~7 years, with supportive but incomplete echocardiographic data. No early differences were seen in small-sample cardiometabolic/vascular measures at 3–6 years. Evidence certainty is low to very low due to attrition and imprecision.

#### Antenatal Interventions

- UPBEAT (diet+PA):
  - At 3 years, there was no difference in the primary child adiposity outcome, blood pressure, or resting pulse rate between intervention vs usual care, despite antenatal improvements in maternal diet and metabolic profile and reduced infant adiposity at 6 months (low certainty for offspring CV outcomes). ([UPBEAT 3-year main follow-up, 2021](https://local-corpus.org/UPBEAT_3Y_main_2021))
  - A single-center cardiovascular sub-study reported evidence of cardiac remodeling (e.g., increased interventricular septum thickness by ~0.04 cm and posterior wall thickness) in children of women with obesity vs children of normal BMI women; whether the antenatal intervention attenuated these differences was not clearly established in the abstract (low certainty). ([Lifestyle intervention in obese pregnancy and cardiac remodelling…, 2022](https://local-corpus.org/UPBEAT_3Y_cardiac_remodelling_2022))

- Antenatal exercise RCT (neonatal echo):
  - Neonatal echocardiograms at 1–3 days and 6–9 weeks did not show a consistent effect of antenatal exercise on early cardiac function vs standard care (very-low certainty; sample sizes modest; short-term outcomes). ([Cardiac function in newborns of obese women…exercise RCT, 2018](https://local-corpus.org/Newborn_cardac_exercise_RCT_2018))

Interpretation: Antenatal lifestyle interventions in obese pregnancy have not consistently demonstrated improvements in early childhood cardiovascular structure or function, with isolated signals of remodeling in obesity not conclusively mitigated by the interventions assessed to date (low to very-low certainty).

#### Evidence Synthesis and Meta-analysis

Heterogeneity in outcome measures (CMR vs echo; diverse parameters), ages, and small sample sizes precluded quantitative meta-analysis. Effects appear time- and modality-dependent, with the most consistent signal emerging for improved LVEF after a preconception intervention at school age (low certainty). ([A preconception lifestyle intervention…CMR, 2023](https://local-corpus.org/A_preconception_lifestyle_intervention_CMR_2023))

### Certainty of Evidence (GRADE)

- LV systolic function (LVEF by CMR at ~7 years) after preconception intervention: Low certainty (downgraded for risk of bias—attrition, and imprecision—small sample), effect direction favors intervention.
- Early cardiometabolic/vascular measures (3–6 years) after preconception intervention: Very low certainty (serious imprecision and risk of bias), no difference detected.
- Antenatal interventions (UPBEAT; neonatal exercise) on offspring CV outcomes: Very low to low certainty; direction of effect inconsistent or null.

### Potential Mechanisms and Mediation

- Maternal glycemia and gestational weight gain are plausible mediators. In obese pregnancies, higher maternal fasting glucose was linearly associated with altered cord blood metabolite profiles (e.g., lysophosphatidylcholines, 3‑hydroxybutyrate), which have potential roles in cardiovascular programming; the antenatal lifestyle intervention itself did not alter cord metabolic profiles. ([Cord Metabolic Profiles in Obese Pregnant Women—UPBEAT, 2018](https://local-corpus.org/UPBEAT_cord_metabolites_2018))
- Preconception weight loss and improved diet/physical activity behaviors (documented in the preconception RCT) may beneficially influence oocyte/early embryonic environment and placentation, with downstream effects on cardiac development and function. ([Effects of a preconception lifestyle intervention on diet and physical activity, 2018](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_diet_PA_secondary_2018))
- Other pathways include reduced inflammation, improved endothelial/placental function, maternal hemodynamics, and epigenetic programming; however, direct mediation analyses linking these to child cardiac phenotypes in trials remain limited.

### Equity, Feasibility, and Adherence

- Equity/generalizability: Most evidence derives from high-income settings and specific populations (e.g., obese women with infertility), limiting external validity to broader, diverse populations, including non-infertile women with obesity. ([Healthy for My Baby protocol, 2021](https://local-corpus.org/Healthy_for_My_Baby_protocol_2021))
- Feasibility/adherence: Preconception interventions showed meaningful short-term behavior changes (reduced sugary drinks/snacks; increased activity), but sustaining engagement into pregnancy and through offspring follow-up proved challenging, contributing to attrition and selection bias. Digital and hybrid delivery models may enhance reach and adherence, but offspring cardiovascular benefits remain untested at scale. ([Effects on diet/PA, 2018](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_diet_PA_secondary_2018))

## Interpretation and Implications

### Overall Assessment

- The balance of evidence points to a modest, potentially clinically relevant improvement in child LV systolic function (LVEF) at early school age following a structured preconception lifestyle/weight-loss intervention in women with obesity (low certainty). This aligns with the developmental origins hypothesis that earlier (preconception) optimization of maternal metabolic health yields greater potential to influence fetal cardiac development and postnatal phenotypes. ([A preconception lifestyle intervention…CMR, 2023](https://local-corpus.org/A_preconception_lifestyle_intervention_CMR_2023))
- Antenatal-only interventions—despite improving maternal gestational weight gain, diet, and short-term infant adiposity—have not yet demonstrated consistent benefits on offspring cardiac structure/function or vascular health at 0–3 years. Single-center signals of remodeling in children of obese mothers underscore risk but do not confirm mitigation via antenatal lifestyle programs as currently designed. ([UPBEAT 3-year main and sub-study, 2021–2022](https://local-corpus.org/UPBEAT_3Y_main_2021))
- Null or inconsistent findings may reflect: (a) insufficient intensity/dose of antenatal interventions; (b) initiation too late to modify critical windows of cardiac development; (c) inadequate sample sizes and attrition; (d) outcome heterogeneity; and (e) relatively short follow-up.

### Clinical and Public Health Recommendations

- Prioritize preconception lifestyle optimization for women with obesity planning pregnancy. Programs targeting ≥5–10% weight loss with structured diet quality improvements and physical activity—ideally continued through pregnancy—are biologically plausible and now supported by preliminary child cardiac benefits (low certainty). Integrate behavior change techniques (goal setting, feedback, supervised components) and partner/family involvement to enhance adherence. ([A preconception lifestyle intervention…CMR, 2023](https://local-corpus.org/A_preconception_lifestyle_intervention_CMR_2023); [Preconception diet/PA secondary analysis, 2018](https://local-corpus.org/Preconception_diet_PA_secondary_2018))
- Embed standardized pediatric cardiovascular phenotyping into ongoing and future trials: CMR (volumes, mass, EF, strain), echocardiographic speckle-tracking (GLS), vascular function (PWV, cIMT), ABPM and BP trajectories, HRV/autonomic indices, and cardiometabolic biomarkers at multiple developmental stages (neonate, infancy, early school age, adolescence).
- Implement mediation analyses linking maternal glycemia, gestational weight gain, inflammatory and endothelial markers, and placental function to offspring CV outcomes to refine mechanistic targets.
- Expand to more inclusive populations (beyond infertility), ensure adequate sample size and retention strategies (e.g., digital hybrid follow-up), and pre-specify sex-stratified analyses and high-risk subgroups (e.g., maternal GDM/HDP, BMI class II–III).

## Research Gaps and Priorities

- Large, multicenter RCTs initiating preconception interventions and continuing antenatally with offspring CMR/advanced echocardiography at ≥2 time points through adolescence.
- Dose–response trials comparing intervention intensity, timing (preconception vs early pregnancy), and modality (diet-only, exercise-only, multicomponent).
- Trials powered for sex-specific effects and interactions with maternal complications (GDM, HDP).
- Pragmatic implementation studies leveraging digital platforms to enhance reach and adherence, with embedded offspring cardiovascular endpoints.
- Longitudinal cohorts to evaluate persistence of early cardiac functional differences (e.g., higher EF) and their relation to blood pressure trajectories, arterial stiffness, and cardiometabolic risk.

---

## Limitations of the Evidence Base

- Small offspring follow-up samples and high attrition limit precision and increase selection bias.
- Heterogeneity in outcomes and assessment ages precluded meta-analysis and complicates cross-study comparisons.
- Some analyses rely on observational contrasts (e.g., obese vs normal BMI comparators) rather than randomized comparisons for child outcomes, increasing risk of confounding.
- Limited neonatal/infant cardiovascular data may miss later-emerging phenotypes.

---

## Conclusion

Current evidence—while limited—suggests that preconception lifestyle interventions for women with obesity may beneficially influence offspring cardiac function in early childhood, with a low-certainty signal of higher LV ejection fraction and favorable LV shape at ~7 years. Antenatal lifestyle interventions alone have not consistently demonstrated improved cardiovascular structure or function in offspring by 0–3 years. To translate developmental origins theory into measurable intergenerational cardiovascular benefit, future research must initiate interventions earlier (preconception), intensify and sustain lifestyle change into pregnancy, and adopt standardized, longitudinal pediatric cardiovascular assessments with adequate power and retention. Until then, clinical programs should prioritize preconception metabolic optimization, recognizing its potential to improve offspring cardiac health while acknowledging the current low certainty and the need for more definitive evidence.
