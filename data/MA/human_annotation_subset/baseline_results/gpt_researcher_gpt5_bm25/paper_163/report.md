# Bisphosphonate Use and Risk of Overall and Site-Specific Cancers: Systematic Review and Meta-analytic Synthesis

## Executive Summary

- Question: Is bisphosphonate use associated with risk of overall and site‑specific cancers, and do class (nitrogen‑containing vs non‑nitrogen‑containing) and duration of use modify these associations?
- Bottom line:
  - Overall cancer: No clear association in pooled observational evidence; large cohorts show null or modest inverse associations subject to healthy‑user and time‑related biases ([Shah et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+incidence+of+cancers+in+patients+with+osteoporosis:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis); [Cardwell et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Exposure+to+oral+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancer)).
  - Colorectal cancer (CRC): Consistent, small inverse association (pooled RR ≈0.87–0.89) with stronger reductions in longer use categories in several analyses; one large prospective cohort showed null findings. Overall, evidence suggests a probable modest risk reduction, but residual confounding cannot be excluded ([Liu et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Reduced+risk+of+colorectal+cancer+with+use+of+oral+bisphosphonates:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis); [Zhou et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+the+risk+of+colorectal+cancer:+a+meta-analysis); [Oral BPs for CRC Prevention 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+Bisphosphonates+for+Colorectal+Cancer+Prevention:+A+Meta-Analytic+Reappraisal); [Newcomb et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=A+prospective+study+of+bisphosphonate+use+and+risk+of+colorectal+cancer); [Lee et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+colorectal+cancer)).
  - Endometrial and ovarian cancers: Meta-analyses indicate reduced risks for endometrial cancer (RR ~0.75) and suggestive reductions for ovarian cancer; protective signals appear stronger with ≥1 year of use ([Li et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonate+use+and+the+risk+of+endometrial+cancer:+a+meta-analysis+of+observational+studies); [Li et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Risk+reduction+of+endometrial+and+ovarian+cancer+after+bisphosphonates+use:+A+meta-analysis); [CITOUS 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=The+effect+of+bisphosphonates+on+the+risk+of+endometrial+and+ovarian+malignancies)).
  - Breast cancer: Several pooled analyses suggest a modest inverse association (RR ~0.87) for incidence, more apparent with nitrogen‑containing agents; confounding by bone health and prior estrogen exposure cannot be fully excluded ([Wang et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancers:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis); [Wright et al., 2013/2018 nested case‑control](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Exposure+to+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+common+non-gastrointestinal+cancers)).
  - Upper gastrointestinal (UGI: esophageal, gastric) cancers: Best‑quality evidence indicates no material increase in esophageal or gastric cancer risk; early signals of increased esophageal cancer in one UK GPRD analysis were not confirmed in larger, better‑adjusted or dose–response analyses; several cohorts show null or even lower gastric cancer risk ([Solomon et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonates+and+evidence+for+association+with+esophageal+and+gastric+cancer:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis); [Vinogradova et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancer+of+oesophagus,+stomach,+and+colorectum); [Green et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Esophageal+and+gastric+cancer+incidence+and+mortality+in+alendronate+users); [Abrahamsen et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Exposure+to+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+gastrointestinal+cancers:+QResearch+CPRD)).
  - Lung, renal cell carcinoma: No clear association with lung cancer in WHI; RCC evidence sparse and largely null ([WHI Lung 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonate+use+and+lung+cancer+incidence+among+postmenopausal+women); [Thorlacius-Ussing et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonate+use+and+risk+of+renal+cell+carcinoma)).
  - Class effects: Signals of protection against breast and possibly colorectal/endometrial cancer appear more consistent with nitrogen‑containing bisphosphonates (N‑BPs) versus non‑nitrogen‑containing agents, consistent with the FPPS‑inhibition mechanism ([Wang et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancers:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis)).
  - Duration: For CRC and endometrial cancer, longer use (≥1–3 years) is associated with stronger inverse associations; no positive duration‑response for UGI cancers; isolated elevated risks in “rare users” likely reflect protopathic bias or confounding ([Li et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonate+use+and+the+risk+of+endometrial+cancer:+a+meta-analysis+of+observational+studies); [Lin et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+esophageal+cancer:+a+dose-intensity+analysis)).

Overall certainty: Low to moderate for site‑specific inverse associations (CRC, endometrial, breast), downgraded for risk of bias and inconsistency; moderate for “no material increase” in esophageal/gastric cancer.

## Methods Overview

### PICO and Eligibility

- Population: Adults prescribed bisphosphonates (primarily postmenopausal women receiving oral therapy for osteoporosis; oncology populations excluded for cancer incidence questions unless explicitly reported).
- Intervention/Exposure: Any bisphosphonate use (oral or parenteral), with subgrouping by class (nitrogen‑containing [alendronate, risedronate, ibandronate, zoledronate, pamidronate] vs non‑nitrogen‑containing [etidronate, clodronate]) and duration/intensity.
- Comparator: Non‑use of bisphosphonates; in some analyses, users of other antiresorptives.
- Outcomes: Overall cancer incidence and site‑specific cancer incidence (esophageal, gastric, colorectal, breast, endometrial, ovarian, lung, renal).
- Study designs: Randomized trials with cancer outcomes, cohort, nested case‑control, and population‑based case‑control studies with adjusted estimates.

### Search Strategy and Study Identification

We prioritized recent, large, and methodologically rigorous systematic reviews and meta‑analyses (2012–2025) and high‑quality cohort/case‑control studies covering bisphosphonate exposure and cancer outcomes. Sources included Medline/PubMed, Embase, and registries as summarized in the included reviews. The included evidence synthesized in this report is derived from identified meta‑analyses and large database studies in the provided corpus ([Solomon et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonates+and+evidence+for+association+with+esophageal+and+gastric+cancer:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis); [Wang et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancers:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis); [Shah et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+incidence+of+cancers+in+patients+with+osteoporosis:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis); others as cited below).

### Data Handling and Prioritization

- Extracted adjusted RRs/HRs/ORs (treated OR≈RR for rare outcomes).
- Preferred time‑dependent/new‑user designs where reported.
- Subgrouped by bisphosphonate class and duration/intensity when available.
- Risk of bias: ROBINS‑I considerations for observational studies (confounding, selection, exposure misclassification, missing data, outcome misclassification, selective reporting). RoB 2 for any trials with long‑term cancer outcomes (few to none).

### Quantitative Synthesis

Given heterogeneity in exposure definitions and populations, we relied primarily on the pooled estimates from existing random‑effects meta‑analyses and highlighted large, adjusted cohort/case‑control studies that addressed key biases. Where possible, we provide pooled risks and heterogeneity (I²). Small‑study effects were considered qualitatively; one meta‑epidemiological study on publication bias for bisphosphonate adverse events provides context for safety literature but not site‑specific cancer incidence ([Meta‑epidemiology 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=How+publication+bias+overestimates+the+risk+of+atypical+femoral+fracture+and+osteonecrosis+of+the+jaw+associated+with+bisphosphonate+use)).

## Results

### Overall Cancer

- Large UK GPRD cohort showed a reduced risk of all cancers among oral bisphosphonate users vs non‑users (HR 0.87, 95% CI 0.82–0.92) ([Cardwell et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Exposure+to+oral+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancer)).
- A 13‑study cohort meta‑analysis (1.51 million participants) reported no significant association with all‑cause cancer (HR 0.97, 95% CI 0.80–1.18; I² 92.5%) ([Shah et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+incidence+of+cancers+in+patients+with+osteoporosis:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis)).

Interpretation: Given heterogeneity and potential healthy‑user bias, the most prudent interpretation is that there is no robust evidence for a material effect on overall cancer risk.

### Site-Specific Cancers

#### Colorectal Cancer (CRC)

- Multiple meta‑analyses (6–8 studies) found small, significant reductions in CRC risk:
  - Pooled OR 0.87 (95% CI 0.78–0.97) with inverse–dose relationship for ≥10 prescriptions (OR 0.71, 95% CI 0.55–0.93) ([Liu et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Reduced+risk+of+colorectal+cancer+with+use+of+oral+bisphosphonates:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis)).
  - Pooled OR 0.89 (95% CI 0.80–0.99), and stronger reduction for long‑term exposure (OR 0.73, 95% CI 0.57–0.93) ([Zhou et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+the+risk+of+colorectal+cancer:+a+meta-analysis)).
- A large nested case‑control study (Kaiser Permanente; 12,505 CRC cases) observed OR 0.82 (95% CI 0.74–0.89), similar across colon and rectum; stronger among men ([Lee et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+colorectal+cancer)).
- Prospective cohort (Nurses’ Health Study) reported null association (multivariable HR 0.92, 95% CI 0.73–1.14) ([Newcomb et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=A+prospective+study+of+bisphosphonate+use+and+risk+of+colorectal+cancer)).
- WHI cohort presented mixed findings across analyses; some WHI analyses found no association with CRC ([WHI CRC 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonate+use+and+colorectal+cancer+incidence+in+the+Women%27s+Health+Initiative)).

Interpretation: Consistent small inverse association with CRC in several pooled analyses and a large nested case‑control study; null results in a high‑quality prospective cohort suggest possible residual confounding in some settings. Overall, a modest protective association is plausible but not definitive.

#### Endometrial Cancer

- Meta‑analysis of seven observational studies (4 cohorts, 3 case‑controls): RR 0.75 (95% CI 0.60–0.94; I² 49.6%); stronger reductions with >1 year of use (e.g., 1–3 years RR 0.58, 95% CI 0.47–0.72) ([Li et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonate+use+and+the+risk+of+endometrial+cancer:+a+meta-analysis+of+observational+studies)). WHI cohort analyses support an inverse association ([WHI endometrial 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonate+use+and+risk+of+postmenopausal+endometrial+cancer)).

Interpretation: Likely modest reduction in endometrial cancer risk, enhanced with longer exposure; confounding by obesity and hormonal milieu must be considered.

#### Ovarian Cancer

- Case‑control evidence (CITOUS) showed OR 0.49 (95% CI 0.26–0.93) with >1 year use; a 2018 meta‑analysis found ~27% lower risk, though fewer studies and potential heterogeneity limit certainty ([CITOUS 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=The+effect+of+bisphosphonates+on+the+risk+of+endometrial+and+ovarian+malignancies); [Li et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Risk+reduction+of+endometrial+and+ovarian+cancer+after+bisphosphonates+use:+A+meta-analysis)).

Interpretation: Suggestive protective association; certainty low due to limited data.

#### Breast Cancer

- Pooled evidence indicates a modest inverse association with breast cancer incidence (RR 0.87, 95% CI 0.82–0.93), especially for nitrogen‑containing agents (RR 0.94, 95% CI 0.90–0.98, smaller effect) ([Wang et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancers:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis)). Observational design and confounding by bone density, prior HRT, and health‑seeking behaviors limit causal inference.

Interpretation: Modest inverse association; mechanistic plausibility exists (N‑BP inhibition of protein prenylation), but residual confounding remains a concern.

#### Esophageal and Gastric Cancers (UGI)

- Systematic review/meta‑analysis (11 studies): no increased risk of esophageal or gastric cancer overall in bisphosphonate users ([Solomon et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonates+and+evidence+for+association+with+esophageal+and+gastric+cancer:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis)).
- UK GPRD analysis reported increased esophageal cancer risk (RR 1.30, 95% CI 1.02–1.66), particularly with ≥10 prescriptions or >5 years, but other GPRD/QResearch/CPRD and Danish registry studies failed to confirm and found no dose–response; some found lower gastric cancer risk ([Vinogradova et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancer+of+oesophagus,+stomach,+and+colorectum); [Abrahamsen et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Exposure+to+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+gastrointestinal+cancers:+QResearch+CPRD); [Green et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Esophageal+and+gastric+cancer+incidence+and+mortality+in+alendronate+users)).
- Asian datasets (Taiwan/Korea) largely reported null associations and no positive duration–response; elevated odds among “rare users” likely reflect protopathic bias or diagnostic workup effects ([Lin et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+esophageal+cancer:+a+dose-intensity+analysis); [Kim et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+Bisphosphonates+and+Upper+Gastrointestinal+Cancer+Risks+in+Asians)).
- Medicare‑based marginal structural models adjusting time‑varying confounding found no UGI cancer association and even a negative association with Barrett’s esophagus ([Solomon et al., 2015 UGI toxicity](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+upper+gastrointestinal+toxicity:+a+study+of+cancer+and+early+signals+of+esophageal+injury)).

Interpretation: The totality of evidence indicates no material increase in risk of esophageal or gastric cancer with oral bisphosphonates.

#### Lung Cancer

- WHI cohort: no difference in lung cancer incidence among oral bisphosphonate users (adjusted HR 0.91, 95% CI 0.77–1.07) ([WHI Lung 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonate+use+and+lung+cancer+incidence+among+postmenopausal+women)).

Interpretation: No meaningful association.

#### Renal Cell Carcinoma

- Danish population‑based case‑control: overall null association between osteoporosis‑dose bisphosphonate use and RCC; no consistent dose‑response ([Thorlacius-Ussing et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonate+use+and+risk+of+renal+cell+carcinoma)).

Interpretation: No clear relationship.

### Class Effects: Nitrogen-Containing vs Non-Nitrogen-Containing

- A 2020 meta‑analysis reported protective associations primarily with nitrogen‑containing bisphosphonates (e.g., breast cancer RR ~0.94), whereas non‑nitrogen agents did not show consistent effects ([Wang et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancers:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis)).
- Mechanistic plausibility stems from N‑BP inhibition of farnesyl diphosphate synthase (FPPS), disrupting protein prenylation in osteoclasts and potentially tumor/stromal cells; non‑nitrogen agents act via different mechanisms and may lack comparable antitumor activity.

### Duration/Intensity of Use

- CRC: Lower risks with longer duration or higher prescription counts observed in several analyses (e.g., ≥10 prescriptions OR ~0.71); one nested case‑control and pooled analyses support duration‑response ([Liu et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Reduced+risk+of+colorectal+cancer+with+use+of+oral+bisphosphonates:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis); [Lee et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+colorectal+cancer)).
- Endometrial: Greater risk reduction with >1 year of use ([Li et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonate+use+and+the+risk+of+endometrial+cancer:+a+meta-analysis+of+observational+studies)).
- UGI: No positive duration‑response; elevated risks in very short‑term “rare users” with declining odds over longer windows suggest reverse causation or detection bias rather than causality ([Lin et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Oral+bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+esophageal+cancer:+a+dose-intensity+analysis)).

## Bias, Heterogeneity, and Certainty

- Confounding by indication and healthy‑user bias: Osteoporosis patients on bisphosphonates often have distinctive risk factor profiles (e.g., lower BMI, different HRT use, screening behaviors). Several studies adjusted for BMI, smoking, alcohol, and comorbidities; residual confounding likely persists, especially for breast/endometrial outcomes.
- Time‑related biases: Immortal time and time‑lag biases can spuriously inflate protective associations. New‑user, time‑dependent designs (e.g., Medicare marginal structural models; Kaiser nested case‑control) mitigate this and largely confirm null or modest associations for UGI and CRC.
- Detection bias: For UGI cancers, higher endoscopy rates among bisphosphonate users could influence cancer detection and site classification. Danish analyses explicitly adjusted for endoscopy frequency and found no increased esophageal cancer risk and lower gastric cancer risk.
- Heterogeneity: Moderate to high heterogeneity in several pooled analyses (e.g., overall cancer I² >90% in Shah 2018; endometrial I² ~50%) reflects differences in exposure definitions, confounder adjustment, and populations.
- Small‑study effects/publication bias: Limited evidence for publication bias in cancer incidence studies; a meta‑epidemiological review underscores bias in bisphosphonate adverse event literature but is less informative for cancer incidence ([Meta‑epidemiology 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=How+publication+bias+overestimates+the+risk+of+atypical+femoral+fracture+and+osteonecrosis+of+the+jaw+associated+with+bisphosphonate+use)).

GRADE certainty:
- UGI cancers: Moderate certainty for no increased risk (consistent nulls in well‑adjusted, large datasets).
- CRC and endometrial: Low‑to‑moderate certainty for small protective association (consistency in several analyses with plausible duration gradient; downgraded for residual confounding).
- Breast: Low certainty for protective association (consistent but susceptible to confounding).
- Ovarian, lung, RCC: Low certainty due to limited studies (ovarian) or consistent nulls (lung, RCC).

## Mechanistic Context

Nitrogen‑containing bisphosphonates inhibit farnesyl diphosphate synthase in the mevalonate pathway, disrupting protein prenylation and exerting anti‑resorptive and potential anti‑tumor effects (e.g., on macrophages, osteoclasts, and possibly tumor microenvironment), providing biological plausibility for modest chemopreventive signals seen for colorectal, breast, and endometrial cancers. Non‑nitrogen agents rely on alternative mechanisms and appear less likely to confer such effects. These mechanisms align with the observed class‑specific patterns in epidemiologic data ([Wang 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Bisphosphonates+and+risk+of+cancers:+a+systematic+review+and+meta-analysis)).

## Interpretation and Implications

- Clinical practice: Current evidence does not support avoiding bisphosphonates over fears of esophageal or gastric cancer. Conversely, while small reductions in CRC, endometrial, and perhaps breast cancer risk are observed, these should not drive prescribing outside established bone‑health indications.
- Patient counseling: For osteoporosis patients, bisphosphonates appear cancer‑neutral overall, with possible ancillary benefits for certain cancers and no convincing increase in UGI cancer risk.
- Research priorities:
  - High‑quality pharmacoepidemiology using incident‑user, time‑dependent exposure modeling with rich confounder adjustment (including BMD, diet, screening, HRT) to clarify causality.
  - Comparative effectiveness by class and dose, and Mendelian randomization or target trial emulation to reduce bias.
  - Exploration of effect modification by sex, obesity/metabolic health, and concurrent medications (e.g., PPIs, HRT).

---

## Limitations of the Evidence Base

- Predominance of observational designs with unavoidable residual confounding.
- Exposure misclassification (adherence, persistence) and heterogeneity in “any use” definitions.
- Limited data for some sites (ovarian, RCC) and for non‑nitrogen agents.
- Potential misclassification of UGI cancer subsites and detection bias due to differential endoscopy.

---

## Conclusion

Based on contemporary systematic reviews, meta‑analyses, and large registry studies, bisphosphonate use is not associated with increased overall cancer risk and does not materially raise esophageal or gastric cancer risk. There is a consistent, albeit modest, inverse association with colorectal and endometrial cancer incidence—particularly with longer use—and a small inverse association with breast cancer, patterns more apparent for nitrogen‑containing bisphosphonates. Given the potential for residual confounding, these protective associations should be interpreted cautiously. Clinically, bisphosphonates remain appropriate for bone indications without added concern for UGI malignancy risk, and any putative chemopreventive benefits should be considered hypothesis‑generating pending more robust causal evidence.
